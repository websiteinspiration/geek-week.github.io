Despite all the high achievements, new technologies "enter" medicine hard. AI is waiting for the same fate. We have prepared a list of the most probable problems that companies that will advance this direction will have to face. It will be interesting to see your versions in the comments.



1. Errors in diagnoses



One of the most important problems in the use of artificial intelligence in medicine is the risk of an erroneous diagnosis. This is directly stated in one of the latest studies.. Scientists warn that the use of methods of reconstruction and analysis of images using artificial intelligence to make a diagnosis and determine treatment can ultimately harm patients. According to them, even small errors ‚Äúat the entrance‚Äù can lead to fundamentally different diagnoses at the exit. And to whom to answer?



Fears have a right to exist. An experienced doctor, looking at the picture, will be able to distinguish the image defect from any education. Or ask to do the diagnosis again. AI can't do that. He will use the available information and make a diagnosis. Thus, possibly greatly influencing the further treatment procedure.



2. Insufficient recognition accuracy



Close in meaning to the first, but quite justifiable claim against the developers of ‚Äúsmart‚Äù systems for recognizing medical information. A simple example - cat recognition systems still do not work correctly. It is enough to add a couple of details, and the AI ‚Äã‚Äãwill think that in front of him is not a cat, but a truck, for example. When recognizing images, some details (for example, a tumor) may be omitted or vice versa added.



A problem can arise both at the stage of computer training, and during its operation by a medical institution. The slightest distortion in the image, which can be caused by a simple movement of the patient, greatly affects the results of image recognition. This has been proven through research .



3. Concerns from medical facilities



Society has always advocated the use of new technologies in our lives. But not everyone is ready to take risks and be the first to introduce them into their work processes. In medicine especially. Few people are ready to take responsibility to use new tools that are still not well understood and it is not known how effective they will be. Hospitals and officials are afraid of the risks posed by the use of new devices, as well as criticism from patients who do not want to become ‚Äúexperimental rabbits‚Äù and ask that they be treated in the old way. It turns out a vicious circle. To implement the technology, you need cases, but for cases, you need already implemented technologies.



4. The complexity of the "black box"



Neural networks, ML do not give an answer to the question why such a result was obtained. The logic underlying the conclusions is incomprehensible. And this leads to uncertainty about the correctness of the result. The way in which AI came to one or another conclusion is essential information that is important for the preparation of a treatment plan. And if this is not understood, skepticism will remain in relation to machine logic and its achievements.



5. Concern for data confidentiality.



Patient health data is personal data, extremely sensitive information. Accordingly, appropriate mechanisms must be in place to protect them. As we take care of the databases - telling, perhaps, it makes no sense. Few companies take this matter seriously. And in the end there are leaks about whichMany have reported , including us. So protection systems need to be further developed.



Anonymization of data can help in part, but we said that this does not always guarantee anonymity . Anyway, collecting in one base the medical history, images from imaging devices, and other medical documentation is an extremely difficult task, and the database itself will be a tidbit. So the fears are fair.



6. Conflict with stakeholders



A lot of money is spinning in medicine, although the doctors themselves sometimes get to the point of indecency a small salary. And they are unlikely to want to share a delicious pie with another member of the organization working in the healthcare sector. Pharmacists, insurers, health departments - everyone has their own interests. And to protect them, of course, certain steps will be taken. Technology resistance at any level will lead to problems with the inclusion of AI in medical processes.



7. Compliance with the law

The collection of patient data is regulated by a number of laws, including the same FZ-152, to ensure compliance with which the business often turns to us. The exchange of data between different databases for analysis using AI algorithms is a problem in terms of compliance with the requirements of current legislation. Considering that a significant part of the bills is written in the style of "prohibit" and "do not let go", legalizing such experiments will be awesome how difficult.



8. Geopolitical threats



You can laugh, but the state (and society) is able to discern the threat in combining medical databases. And what if AI finds some peculiarity in a certain category of people, and it will be possible to develop a point-acting biological weapon? Suddenly, a foreign country, using our databases, will be able to harm us? Suddenly make some important conclusions on the specifics of diseases in certain regions?



In general, such fears, no matter how strange they may sound, are arising now, when little is heard about AI in medicine. One can only guess what will happen when the actual introduction of technology begins. Will the protesters go to burn data centers and 5G towers (towers have nothing to do with it, but the habit remains!).



9. The need for new technologies



The use of deep learning models for the development and successful integration of artificial intelligence in the healthcare sector almost always includes volumetric tasks, such as teaching models to scan and analyze large volumes of medical imaging data. Powerful IT infrastructure is required for proper operation. In particular, high-performance graphics processors ( GPUs ). Accordingly, cloud platforms will be more actively used. They can provide the necessary computing power with proper data protection. And about how to relate to new technologies, we have already said.



10. The human factor



New technology requires new knowledge. How quickly will the doctor, who has treated the traditional method for 30-40 years, master the new tool? And how soon will the technology get to the district hospitals, where not every doctor has computers? Everything can turn out as it often happens: in the capital region the technology will work, and where it is needed, it will appear with a considerable delay.



Perhaps you see some other perspectives or problems of introducing artificial intelligence into medicine. It will be interesting to hear your arguments and objections. Thanks for your attention.
