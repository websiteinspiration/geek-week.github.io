I talked recently with the middle of the development team, which consisted of 6 seniors and one middle. According to the middle, growing up in this team was very difficult for a number of reasons:

  • lack of techlide. Technically, there was a techlide. With a very high technical level. But as a leader who could lead and develop his group, he was a complete zero: he did not know how to decompose tasks, distribute them according to the level of each member, he did not engage in group training, the activities of the group were controlled in a dictatorial mode, there were no soft skills, etc. .P.
  • a large gap between the skills of the middle and seniors. Basic guide to creating balanced development teams


According to a 2016 StackOverflow developer survey , participation in a mentoring program is more correlated with salary than having a Ph.D., and developers value learning new things more than other motivators, such as creating a new one, faith in a company's mission, and career advancement.

Good mentors can help solve most of the major problems facing developers, including:

  • Unrealistic expectations (due to an understanding of when and how to shift expectations and generally manage them when the management process stops working)
  • Bad documentation
  • Ineffective development processes
  • Weak code base

Senior mentors play an indispensable role in development teams. But it‚Äôs hard to find real, qualified senior developers with experience who can normally lead the team.

Take a good look at your team. Does anyone have more than 6 years of experience creating large-scale industrial applications? Do your team members feel that they have a reliable guide and mentor who constantly encourages them to improve their skills?

If the answers are no, then you really miss a lot.

There are many reasons why good mentors are hard to find and retain, but it all starts with team management. Much can be done to create a much more efficient and well-organized team, to which developers will be happy to join for many years.

In Silicon Valley, many engineers either take over or quit altogether. This is bad not only for senior developers. This is bad for employers.

This has a bad effect on team culture and attitude. This is bad for team performance. And this is bad for junior and mid-level developers who are deprived of great mentoring opportunities.

If you have been developing for a very long time, then you may have felt the pressure that forces you to take control. If you decide that you want to choose this particular route, this article will help you get an idea of ‚Äã‚Äãhow to build the best team and its culture.

Creating a well-balanced team is an important element in building a high-speed development team.

It all starts with people


If you want to create something great, then start with great people. Building a team is much more than just hiring people with the right skills. It also means finding people who will fit into the rest of the team and balance it.

For a manager, it can be tempting to consider other team members as machine cogs. It would be convenient if you could put them in any position and expect them to act in accordance with your expectations for this position.

Fortunately for everyone, real teams do not work that way, so your first task is to learn to recognize the temptation to evaluate team members by the number of tickets that they close.

The biggest mistake I observed with techlides was determining the value of individual team members by the number of tickets they closed.

This error is the root cause of the number one problem that developers face: Unrealistic expectations. When you focus on counting the number of closed tickets, you ignore the wider context of the project and the normal dynamics of the team, which can lead to disastrous results: moral depression, high turnover of developers and reduced productivity.

Not all individual developers are junior developers . Many of them are talented senior developers who simply prefer to focus on developing new features and fixing bugs, rather than plunging into the complex kitchen of mentoring, forming managerial expectations and leading people.

The startup culture in the San Francisco Bay area is trying to unite everyone in group 2. It's short-sighted and wasteful. Now let's see why.

Let's look at a couple of definitions:

  • Senior Developer (Senior) - A person with a deep understanding and proven experience in creating real large-scale applications.
  • Junior developer (junior) - A person with limited experience or knowledge who wants to learn and improve his skills.

"Senior" does not mean more in years. I met developers with 15 years of experience, whom I would classify as junior developers in terms of team building, and others who have 2-3 years of experience to whom I would entrust the creation of a new application from scratch.

Nevertheless, the ‚Äúsenior‚Äù is definitely not what happens in one night, and the skill scale is a wide gradient, not something binary. Nothing can replace the lessons learned over the years of work. Malcolm Gladwell‚Äôs book Outliers: The Story of Success assumes a 10_000 hours rulecan be applied to many skills: To achieve mastery in your craft, it takes 10_000 hours of practice. This means that a developer needs about 6 years of practice to master this craft. That sounds absolutely true to me.

However, just experience is not enough to ensure progress. In the article ‚ÄúPeak: Secrets from the New Science of Expertise‚Äù, Anders Erickson and Robert Poole explain that practitioners should often engage in conscious practice , in which the student is constantly working on the verge of their abilities - this is a pleasant point in which one can also feel complexity and confidence.

Plus, as a mentor and teacher, I discovered many other aspects that can drastically improve the effectiveness of developer training. In particular:

  • Collaborative / pair programming
  • Code review
  • One-on-one mentoring

If you want your team to improve quickly, you need to encourage these practices.

Why your team needs strong mentors


Some team members are distinguished by their knowledge and ability to create architecture and serve as a good example for the rest of the team. When they are involved in this activity, their contribution is a factor in multiplying efficiency , increasing the productivity of the entire team.

In addition, these participants can see more universal solutions to common problems and develop tools that will help the rest of the team be more productive. (Other team members will also be able, but perhaps not so often).

If in a team of 6 people, 1 employee is a strong mentor with a factor of multiplying efficiency = 2, then this means that the remaining 5 people will do the work of 10. You may have heard the term tenfold developer. Mentoring is the most realistic way to make a tenfold contribution .

Perhaps you think that my math is wrong now, because the other participants do not deserve praise for their one-time contribution? Perhaps some, but not so simple. The real number of developers who need help in order to achieve tenfold productivity lies somewhere between 5-10.

Let's dig a little deeper. Many people find it difficult to understand or appreciate this part:

Junior or middle developers are able to close tickets. Maybe even fast. However, the junior developer lacks the vision and experience to understand how the technical choices he makes today will affect the productivity of the entire team.in future. In other words, a neglected junior developer can be a fractional factor in efficiency , which interferes with the productivity of the entire team.

Unattended junior developers will issue quick fixes in a general direction for each problem they encounter, regularly missing root causes, often repeatedly causing problems and fires, and leaving behind a bunch of technical debts.

Younger developers also often struggle with complex problems, distracting other developers from their work to help.

Mid-level developers (usually with 2-5 years of experience. This is what is called "senior" in many jobs in the bay area) will come up with working solutions to complex technical issues, but these solutions will usually be difficult themselves on their own and difficult to understand by other team members, which also puts a strain on team productivity .

Experienced senior developers often come up with deceptively simple solutions to complex problems and teach other developers to do the same.

So, don‚Äôt have to hire junior developers, right? Not true. A junior developer can be very smart and be a very good student. Just a lack of experience and understanding creates negative resistance.

In a mentoring environment with frequent pair programming and code reviews, even novice developers quickly become great contributors.

But hiring junior developers without involving senior mentors in their orientation is like planting time bombs throughout the code base.

Real danger


When a development team is confronted with performance problems and big technical debts, it is tempting to blame individual developers for it.

‚ÄúThis developer closes 1/5 of those tickets that another closes, so it‚Äôs clear that this is the problem, right?‚Äù Not true.

The root cause of technical debt is management's focus on short-term microproductivity to the detriment of long-term macroproductivity. When you allow a by-product to accumulate long enough, it will eventually clog the mechanism.

Managers often make the mistake of thinking that the processing speed of tickets can be used to evaluate the effectiveness of individual participants. This is simply not true.

People do not work in a vacuum. They work with each other. Your most knowledgeable employees will quickly become those mentors to whom everyone addresses questions, and that‚Äôs good .

This mentor / senior developer is responsible for keeping the mechanism clean and well lubricated, preventing blockages that derail the entire team.

But senior mentors do not close many of their own tickets. Instead, they will focus on code review, unlocking the rest of the team when it encounters new challenges, and working to optimize the long-term productivity of the entire team.

The fact is, engineering leaders must understand that your most productive team members often close the fewest tickets.

Multipliers of team effort will lose time (both yours and the rest of the team) if they act as individual developers trying to quickly close their own tickets.

You do not need your mentors to quickly close tickets . You do not want your mentors to quickly close tickets. The last thing you want to do as an engineering manager is to put pressure on them to do this.

You need them to help other developers learn how to develop applications in such a way that the team as a whole maintains a smoother speed.

Gulf startups correctly give great value to speed and flexibility, but they don‚Äôt understand at all how to build a culture where speed and flexibility flourish naturally.

They are too focused on closing specific tickets today, instead of realizing that it is the culture of training and mentoring that will ensure the long-term health and stability of the team‚Äôs combined speed and productivity in the future.

If you want to determine the value of a senior developer or mentor, instead of looking at the individual processing speed of tickets, ask other developers in the team how useful this mentor is.

Another great way to make sure your mentors work productively is to look at their code review activities. They should do a lot of code review, full of great tips on how code can be improved in specific ways at the architectural level.

An inexperienced reviewer is more focused on non-essential stylistic issues (semicolons, capital letters, spaces, etc.). The great mentor will teach timeless software development concepts, design patterns, and how to write code that is easier to read and support for other developers.

Why every company wants to have senior developers


Each company wants to have "senior" developers, because it is correctly believed that senior developers will better understand how to write an application that in itself will be resistant to the changing requirements of customers and product managers.

Senior developers themselves are less likely to create mountains of technical debt, since they close tickets and most of them require less effort to quickly get used to the new code base.

Why few startups have real senior developers


The standards for what is meant by a ‚Äúsenior developer‚Äù have changed over the years. When I first started working as a professional programmer, very often I had to see vacancies for senior positions requiring a minimum of 5, 7 or 10 years of experience.

Today in San Francisco you will see similar positions that require a minimum experience of 2-3 years. Part of the reason for these changes is that there is a shortage of developers on the labor market who not only possess the required experience in years, but also the knowledge and skills necessary to carry out this work.

But the vacancies are still not closed. Therefore, we hire people with slightly less experience, call them ‚Äúsenior developers‚Äù and hope that everything works out. Will not work.

As I mentioned, experience is not everything, but when it comes to the role of a senior mentor, experience is still of great importance . There are things that cannot be learned, except living life.

There is really a big difference between a senior developer on paper and a really senior developer. A developer with 3 years of experience does not have real life experience creating several large-scale industrial applications. He simply did not work on enough roles long enough to see how this happens more than once or twice.

This means that he does not know all or even the most common problems faced by development teams.

You can fill the ocean with those things about which the 3-year-old developer does not even suspect that he does not know them.

But we still hire them. Because they do an excellent job of developing all kinds of pieces and fixing bugs, and this is almost always enough for a while.

Why are most companies struggling to keep senior developers


As I just explained, many of the ‚Äúsenior‚Äù talents hired in the San Francisco Bay area are not really senior. These are less experienced developers who are ready to work for a much lower salary than the real senior developers who in San Francisco expect to earn more than 150 thousand US dollars per year.

The three-year developer will be happy, earning 130 thousand US dollars per year. The median salary of a developer in San Francisco is just over 110 thousand US dollars per year.
(Note to non-native: it looks like a lot, but in San Francisco, you don‚Äôt take it easy after you pay rent.

Many technical founders in the Gulf area are young themselves. When you are young and free from attachments, you can allow yourself to share an apartment with four roommates and bring a small salary home while you are running around looking for financing and trying to hire other people.

It may seem crazy to a young founder to hire an ‚Äúunattainable‚Äù developer for the same price that he would pay for two less experienced ‚Äúsenior‚Äù developers, and for a CEO it might seem outrageous to pay a senior developer more than he would bring home. Young startups usually see the salary of a real senior developer as ‚Äúexpensive.‚Äù

San Francisco's corporate culture is often geared towards young workers, including an exhausting routine and frequent after-hours activities such as parties, dinners, drinks, evening quizzes, ping-pong and barbecue tournaments. Employees with families often do not feel part of the company when they decide to return home to families instead of attending events.

Work in a young startup is not the only option for senior developers. Experienced companies such as Adobe, Google, IBM and Microsoft appreciate the talents of senior developers and are willing to pay bonuses for this.

Google, as you know, lures developers of successful applications with compensations of millions of dollars.

Microsoft admits that it needs both senior and junior developers and aggressively recruits both.

Some senior developers are disappointed in the labor market in the Gulf region and are leaving there, preferring to create their own applications or small software development agencies.

Others simply wonder why startups in the Valley underestimate them and are struggling to find work.

Why every company needs junior developers


When you treat each developer as a senior developer whom you can trust in the conditions of his full independence, as a result you get a team in which employees cannot learn from each other and grow together in skills and productivity.

When you hire junior developers, you take for granted that you need to create a learning culture so that you can develop junior developers and share experience across the team.

This culture increases the volume of pair programming, leading to a more thorough and productive code review (as opposed to the trivial nit-picking), and much less isolation of knowledge.

When team members often train each other, it takes their individual work out of their heads, giving a better idea of ‚Äã‚Äãhow their work fits into the wider context of the application.

One of the big problems faced by development teams is that individual developers tend to specialize in a small area of ‚Äã‚Äãthe application (which leads to increased productivity when they work in this area). But they do it at the cost of reduced productivity when other team members try to work on this part of the application.

Beyond these benefits, teams need junior developers because no one told the junior developer that something was impossible. They will tackle the impossible and sometimes will be able to find a solution, and they will not be stuck with the words ‚Äúwe always did that.‚Äù

Perhaps the most important reason for hiring junior developers is that it‚Äôs hard to find senior developers who really know what they are doing. The hunt for a good senior developer can take many months and it is important to balance the costs of moving now compared to moving after six months.

Conclusion


Why is it so hard to find good senior developers?
Because we do not encourage enough junior developers on the system. What we are doing now is unacceptable in the long run.

If everyone hires only senior developers, where do the new senior developers come from?

And if you do not appreciate the real senior developers, who will instruct the recruits? How do we pass deep knowledge to the next generation?
