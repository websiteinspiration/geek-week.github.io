Civil War Financing (USA vs. USA)

Translation of an article by Michael A. Martorelli about sources of financing the US war against the United States - the history of the printing of dollars on both sides, counterfeiting of notes, loans and bonds, tax collection and much more.









Introduction



By the time of the inauguration of Abraham Lincoln as President of the United States (USA) in March 1861, seven breakaway southern states had already formed the Confederate States of America (CSA). The political leaders of this territory, as well as most of their opponents in the northern and border states, believed that no matter what the period of the struggle to establish the legitimacy of the new Confederate state, it would be quite short. The new US Treasury Secretaries (Salmon Portland Chase) and the United States (Christopher Gustav Memminger) did not expect them to raise several billion dollars for a four-year war. But even when solving the task of financing a short war, they both understood that states traditionally use three main sources of funds for conducting military operations:borrowed money, printing money and collecting money through taxes . From 1861 to 1865, both the United States and the United States used all these mechanisms, albeit in different proportions. Data from historical sources vary regarding the exact percentages of these military costs received from different sources.



In many ways, the description of these financial transactions reveals the history of the two countries.



Union Financing



Borrowed money



Until 1861, the bulk of US revenue was made up of duties and customs duties. The importance of the non-increase in debt was characterized by the presidential administration from Thomas Jefferson to Franklin Pierce. The federal government relied on the sale of bonds and credit notes to bankers, brokers, and their large customers only during the war. When James Buchanan became president in March 1857, federal debt was a modest $ 28 million. The financial panic that swept the country at the end of that year caused a sharp drop in revenue from duties and fees; To cover the deficit, the Treasury was forced to issue large volumes of bonds and credit notes. In addition, President Buchanan was forced to use borrowed funds to finance his operations in relation to Cuba and Mormon territory,as well as to expand the Navy and the postal service. Between 1857 and 1861, the Treasury issued bonds and notes worth more than $ 142 million; over the years, the government created a combined deficit of 76 million.













In February and March 1861, the split between the North and the South intensified, and the likelihood of war grew. Due to the very low level of funds in the Treasury, Congress approved two separate bills allowing the issuance of government bonds in the amount of $ 35 million. When Abraham Lincoln took the Presidential Oath in March 1861, federal debt was at an unusually high level of almost 77 million. In the early days of the Lincoln administration, interest in any new debt was fairly low; ordinary customers of bankers and brokers acquired a total of 16 million (45%) of the aforementioned ten-year and twenty-year bonds. Over the next few months, increased duties and new moderate sales of credit notes brought the Treasury another 15 million more.However, the initial costs of expanding the Federal Army and Navy in response to the undisguised aggressive actions of the United States Army totaled $ 24 million.



In order to enlist the support of Congress in funding for the war, in July 1861, the President convened the legislature for a special meeting. At this time, the Secretary of the Treasury Chase proposed raising funds for an unprecedented amount of $ 320 million to finance the war; this amount was to include the sale of government bonds (240 million), an increase in fees (57 million), new duties or fees (20 million), as well as the sale of public land (3 million).



In response to this proposal, Congress approved the sale of bonds for $ 250 million and the introduction of income tax. Both funding mechanisms have proven non-compliance. The introduction of income tax was not appointed until the middle of 1862, and Secretary Chase was able to agree on the sale of bonds for only 150 million group of banks in New York, Boston and Philadelphia. At the end of 1861, the Lincoln administration reevaluated the need for federal government funding for a conflict that was now considered long and costly by officials.



In February 1862, Congress approved the sale of bonds for another $ 500 million. But the Treasury was having trouble selling government bonds. In the second half of 1861, the Jay Cooke & Company provided the Treasury with quite a bit of assistance in selling bonds, so in February 1862, Secretary Chase authorized Jay Cook to distribute 500 million new approved twenty-year bonds. Over the next two years, the firm sold these securities in the amount of $ 511 million. She succeeded thanks to a new campaign to increase the popularity of bonds through advertising in newspapers and a network of 2.5 thousand sales agents working across the country. Cook wrote editorials in which he reportedthat the acquisition of such bonds (some of them had a face value of only $ 50) is a patriotic action and that every citizen who cares about preserving the Union should think about it. His tremendous success in selling these bonds forced Congress to approve another issue of bonds in the amount of 830 million at the beginning of 1865. By the summer of that year, Cook had sold them all. 20 1861



On the eve of the Civil War, the volume of funds in circulation in the United States consisted mainly of $ 200 million banknotes issued by more than 1.5 thousand state banks. Such a system with independent issuers of money had its drawbacks, but still made it possible to cope with the financial needs of the country. However, by the end of 1861, the Secretary of the Treasury Chase concluded that the existence of so many banknotes issued by state licensed banks, each of which had different denominations and degrees of protection, was unfavorable for financing the war. Even with the issue of the bonds mentioned above, he suggested that the federal government issue a new paper currency in the amount of $ 150 million without collateral in gold, but still an obligation of the state. These greenbacks printed on green papershould have been converted into an equal amount of government bonds and considered legal tender for all public and private debts. After two months of heated discussion, Congress approved the plan. The new currency has come to be seen as a fiat payment instrument accepted by both sellers and consumers. In July 1862, Congress approved another issue of "greenbacks" in the amount of 150 million, calling for the printing of approximately 25% of banknotes in face value from one to five dollars. The cost of the war was constantly growing, and the government was limited in the possibility of selling bonds, so Congress approved the sale of an additional 150 million greenbacks in early 1863. By the end of the war, approximately 450 million of this new national currency was in circulation in the Union.















The federal government and the states have long used various types of taxes, for example, state taxes, excise taxes, property taxes, business taxes and import duties. However, as mentioned above, the unusually large amount of income needed to finance the hostilities forced the government to think about additional taxes, despite the beginning of the search for other sources of funds. Treasury Secretary Chase persuaded Congress to introduce income taxes in August 1861, but this law did not require anyone to pay such taxes until mid-1862. In July of that year, Congress passed the new Domestic Income Act of 1861, setting a 3% tax rate on annual income of $ 600 to $ 10,000 and a 5% income tax on this amount. In 1863, revenue from this source amounted to less than 20 million.By mid-1864, Congress saw the need to raise income tax rates above $ 5,000, raise other taxes (inheritance tax, excise taxes, commercial gross income tax, licenses, personal property, etc.) and introduce an extra tax (in excess of new rates) for incomes above $ 600.



The income tax brought in only 2 million of the federal government‚Äôs total income from all taxes, amounting to 38 million at the end of the fiscal year on June 30, 1863. Income from income tax increased to 20 million in the fiscal year 1864 and to 32 million in the fiscal year 1865. In total, all taxes accounted for approximately 21% of the federal government‚Äôs income during all the years of the war.



Confederation Financing









KSA President Jefferson Davis



Borrowed money



Unlike the long-established USA, young CABGs did not yet have experience in raising money to support their actions. However, their competent founding political leaders definitely considered meeting the financial needs of the new state one of the most important tasks. On February 8, 1861, the Provisional Confederate Government accepted a loan of 500 thousand dollars from Alabama. On February 19, 1861, the day after his inauguration, Confederation President Jefferson Davis announced the appointment of Christopher G. Memminger as Secretary of the Treasury. The new government, which did not have its own currency, experience, and tax authorities, had a rather limited choice of financing options. In late February, the Confederation Congress agreed on the first Treasury bond issue to finance government operations.The government offered this so-called ‚Äúbanker loan‚Äù to regional commercial and banking circles, asking them to purchase bonds for cash. Congress established a small duty on cotton export, the income from which was intended to pay both the interest (8%) and the principal loan amount (for ten years). Over time, subscribers from leading commercial centers such as New Orleans and Charleston purchased all of the bonds.









1861



In May, the Confederation Congress allowed another 50 million bond issue. This time, the legislative act stated that bonds could be purchased for cash, military property, or for the expected proceeds from the sale of raw materials and industrial products. This so-called ‚Äúfood loan‚Äù took into account the shortage of a significant amount of hard currency among the classes of southern planters and farmers. It took into account that these people had large volumes of cotton, tobacco and other products that they would like to borrow from the government. Thus, thanks to the ingenious use of mortgages (in which the borrower provides collateral to secure the debt), the farmer, in exchange for receiving confederate bonds, could give the expected proceeds from the sale of his crop to the Treasury of the Confederation.In August, the Confederation Congress increased the volume and terms of this product loan to 100 million; in April 1862, he again expanded it to 250 million. After much initial success in collecting these funds, the military failures of the Confederate army had an overwhelming effect on the ability of the United States to finance itself with such tools. By the end of 1864, the Confederation managed to raise just 34 million with a grocery loan.



In October 1862, partially guided by the successes of the KSA armies on the battlefields, Paris Emile Erlanger and Company invited representatives of the Confederation to place a loan to its government. In March 1863, after lengthy negotiations, this company signed an agreement that it would become a 15 million bond broker for various subscribers in England and several European countries. Loans should be repaid in cotton, provided that the depositor accepts this product within the confederation. In the first few months of selling (i.e. reselling) Erlanger loans, it was quite successful. However, in August, after the battle of Gettysburg, Europeans' demand for these bonds began to fall. By then, Erlanger had stopped selling bonds, and the deal brought the Confederation just about 8.8 million instead of the expected 15 million dollars.



In March 1865, the Confederation Congress approved yet another $ 30 million bond issue that could only be purchased in cash. He promised that back bonds would also be redeemed for cash, and that this decision would be implemented within two years after the end of the war. There is no evidence that some volume of these bonds was sold. He also failed to sell a smaller volume of bonds worth 3 million, issued a little later that same month. In its last days, the KSA received 300 thousand in cash from a group of banks in Richmond.



In total, by the end of the war, the KSA had implemented more than fourteen bond issues, which made it possible to finance a little more than one third of their expenses.



Print money

Although the Treasury of the Confederation sought to sell bonds for cash in February 1861, its management understood that the potentially high costs of an imminent war would force it to seek other sources of financing. The issue of paper money in the form of Treasury banknotes solved several problems, the most practical of which was the creation of a medium of exchange for free circulation. Banknote printing also created ‚Äúmoney‚Äù for the formation of the financial infrastructure of the United States and for ‚Äúpayment‚Äù to suppliers of military products. The government began distributing these banknotes (some of them were promissory notes on which interest was paid) in March 1861 and printed them in denominations of five dollars or more. With an increase in military spending, the output of these banknotes also increased. The Confederation Congress approved their seal in several trenches.The total amount of banknotes issued increased by the end of the summer of 1861 to 300 million, and by the end of 1864 - to more than 1.5 billion.

Counterfeiting banknotes has become a serious problem. Trying to deal with this problem, the government several times, from the end of 1861 until the beginning of 1865, forced banknote holders to exchange one type of banknote for another, taking interest bills out of circulation and replacing them with interest-free banknotes. Officials hoped that depositors saving up bills of exchange would use banknotes as a means of calculating. The Confederation also issued new promissory notes with different conditions and repayment dates, and several times tried to turn all of these promissory notes into legal tender. Ultimately, most banks in the South began to consider them as an acceptable way to pay off all types of debts. Treasury Secretary Memminger said that the appointment of banknotes and bills ‚Äúlegal tender‚Äù (legal tender) is optional. Moreover, he statedthat such actions would harm the very essence of the Confederation, which is based on ‚Äústate rights,‚Äù and will raise suspicions that the national government is stronger than necessary. Other voices joined in his statement, doubting the ability to declare a ‚Äúlegal tender‚Äù to counteract high inflation and large amounts of fakes that were harmful to the South‚Äôs economy throughout the war.



The pressure on inflation was aggravated by printing and circulating ‚Äúmoney‚Äù in the form of banknotes issued by almost all the southern states, many large cities in the region, as well as private companies - railways, private highways, insurance companies, etc. In fact, the need for additional means of exchange was so strong that many independent businessmen also issued their own banknotes. These so-called ‚Äúshinplasters‚Äù allowed their holder to receive a certain amount of goods or services from grocers, carpenters, innkeepers, etc. Treasury Secretary Memminger officially acknowledged that such state / city / business banknotes were in circulation for approximately $ 20 million; however, historians have not been able to determine the true volumes of such collective types of money.



Increase in duties and taxes









Christopher Gustav Memminger



In May 1861, Treasury Secretary Memminger suggested that existing tariff laws and approved rate increases in February would bring the United States 25 million in revenue this year. However, due to the Union‚Äôs successful blockade of most of the South coast, the total amount received was closer to 1 million. For the entire period from 1861 to 1865, import duties totaled only 3.5 million. The bill, which approved a bank loan in February 1861, also imposed on all cotton shipped after August 1 an export duty paid in cash. The Union blockade also limited the export of cotton and many other products. Despite pre-war estimates of 20 million, the total revenue from this source was negligible.



Back in May 1861, Secretary Memminger began to suggest the need to impose taxes on property, capital, trade, stocks, etc. In August, Congress approved tax on a list of specific items, including real estate and the value of slaves. It was to be assembled in May 1862 on the basis of the estimated value at the end of 1861. However, at the time of payment, only two out of eleven states had completed the full assessment; six states did not even start this. Ultimately, the government began to collect these taxes; records show that by mid-1863 approximately 90% of the total estimated volume of 20 million was received. However, eight out of eleven states borrowed money (through state banknotes) to pay their tax debts for cash-strapped citizens.Therefore, the total amount of funds received from taxes was significantly lower than expected.



An acute shortage of finances forced the Confederation Congress in April 1863 to pass a bill introducing various new taxes on profits, agricultural products, business and income. A curious requisition on agricultural products was introduced: farmers were obliged to transfer the tithe of their harvest of wheat, corn, oats, etc. to government commissary depots. Although such requisitions made it possible to collect a large amount of food for the Confederate armies, they did not help to increase the funds of the Treasury.



In February 1864, the Confederation Congress was forced to pass another tax law introducing new taxes on most tangible assets of individuals and legal entities in the South. As in other years, the damage caused by battles, an inefficient collection system, large-scale fraud and flagrant disobedience led to the fact that the amount of taxes amounted to only 80% of the 145 million that were supposed to be introduced by the law. Over the course of the war years, despite many political, organizational, and infrastructural obstacles, the KSA collected more than 200 million in duties and taxes ‚Äî a little more than 9% of its total income.



Conclusion



The decisions taken by the United States and the United States on the sources of funding during the Civil War reflect the deep-rooted ideological views of these two territories. Northerners usually supported traditional ideas (increasing federal debt), as well as new concepts (national currency and federal income tax). The successful appearance of banker Jay Cook and his ingenious bond sale scheme made the diffusion of various government debt obligations much more efficient and effective than officials had expected at the start of the war. Southerners, fearing the national political and economic dominance of the central state, were not interested in issuing the national currency. However, and the central government,and the states themselves were quite willing to print various types of means of payment in the form of both interest-free banknotes and interest-bearing bills. Members of the Confederate Congress opposed national taxation and never sought to receive a significant percentage of the Confederation's income from this source. In the end, both US and US officials have made impressive strides in finding significant financial resources to support their armies for most of the four long and bloody years. The northern states were not the scene of serious battles, so they could maintain the productivity of their agricultural, industrial and commercial enterprises. The federal government had both the desire and the ability to use a high level of borrowed financial strength to finance military spending. Most businessmen, landowners, and merchants continued to lead a fairly productive economic life, and therefore could withstand a fairly high level of taxes imposed by the government to raise additional funds. An experiment in printing state-sponsored paper tickets representing money,also proved to be quite successful, mainly due to the faith of the population in the federal government. Ultimately, the United States was able to use borrowed funds and cash raised from tax increases to meet almost 90% of its financial needs.



The southern states, which with certainty made their contribution to the economic power of the nation before the war, were simply not able to maintain or expand their economic base in the face of hostilities. Unfortunately, with the growing needs of the Confederation's borrowed funds, its creditworthiness declined, so the loans secured at the beginning of the conflict, at its end, fell to par. Moreover, political leaders and powerful citizens feared giving the central government enough power to raise taxes or create a national currency. Although the KSA was able to borrow funds to cover just over a third of its financial needs, circumstances forced them to print and issue debt obligations (money) in the amount of a little more than half of their war-related costs.
