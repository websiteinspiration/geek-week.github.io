What is a Digital Substation?



Digital Substation is a substation, all systems of which have a very high level of automation. All secondary and primary equipment of such a substation is focused on digital data transmission. The data exchange is arranged in accordance with the transmission protocols described in the IEC 61850 standard.



Accordingly, all data is transmitted in digital form here:



  • Measurements.
  • Diagnostic information.
  • Management teams.


This trend has been very developed in the Russian energy sector and is now being introduced everywhere. In 2019 and 2020, a lot of regulatory documents appeared regulating the creation of the Digital Substation at all stages of development. For example, STO 34.01-21-004-2019 PJSC Rosseti defines the following definition and criteria for DSP:



Definition:



Digital substation is an automated substation equipped with digital information and control systems interacting in a single time mode and functioning without the presence of permanent duty personnel.



Criteria:



  • remote observability of parameters and operating modes of equipment and systems necessary for normal operation without the constant presence of duty and maintenance operational personnel;

To combine the described systems with each other and organize horizontal and vertical communication, as well as the transfer of measurements, buses are organized. For now, let's agree that each bus is just a separate LAN on industrial Ethernet switches.

Relay protection terminals participate in both horizontal and vertical communication, and also use measurements, so they are connected to all buses.

Features of Multicast transmission in Digital Substation



What data is transmitted using multicast?



Horizontal communication and transmission of measurements within the Digital Substation is performed using the Publisher-Subscriber architecture. Those. relay protection terminals use multicast streams to exchange messages with each other, and measurements are transmitted using multicast.



Prior to the digital substation in the energy sector, horizontal communications were implemented using point-to-point communications between terminals. As an interface, either copper or optical cable was used. Data was transmitted using proprietary protocols.



Very high demands were placed on this connection, as these channels transmitted protection actuation signals, the position of switching devices, etc. The operational blocking algorithm of the terminals depended on this information.



If the data will be transmitted slowly or unwarranted, it is likely that some of the terminals will not receive relevant information on the current situation and may give a signal to turn off or turn on the switching device when, for example, some work will be carried out on it. Or the URO will not work on time and the short circuit will spread to the rest of the circuit. All this is fraught with large financial losses and a threat to human life.



Therefore, the data should have been transmitted:



  • Reliable.
  • Guaranteed.
  • Fast.


Now instead of a point-to-point connection, the station / substation bus is used, i.e. LAN And the data is transmitted using the GOOSE protocol, which is described by the IEC 61850 standard (in IEC 61850-8-1, to be more precise).



GOOSE stands for General Object Oriented Substation Event, but this decryption is no longer very relevant and does not carry a semantic load.



Within the framework of this protocol, relay protection terminals exchange GOOSE messages among themselves.



The transition from point-to-point to LAN has not changed the approach. Data still needs to be transmitted reliably, guaranteedly and quickly. Therefore, for GOOSE messages, a somewhat unusual data transfer mechanism is used. About him a little later.



Measurements, as we have already discussed, are also transmitted using multicast streams. In DSP terminology, these streams are called Sampled Value streams.



SV streams are messages containing a specific data set and transmitted continuously with a certain period. Each message contains a measurement at a particular point in time. Measurements are taken with a specific frequency - the sampling frequency.



Sampling frequency - the sampling frequency of a time-continuous signal when it is sampled.





Sampling rate 80 samples per second.



The composition of SV streams is described in IEC61850-9-2 LE.



SV streams are transmitted through the process bus.



The process bus is a communication network that provides data exchange between measuring devices and devices of the connection level. The rules for exchanging data (instantaneous values ‚Äã‚Äãof current and voltage) are described in the IEC 61850-9-2 standard (the IEC 61850-9-2 LE profile is currently used).



SV streams, as well as GOOSE messages, must be transmitted quickly. If the measurements are transmitted slowly, the terminals may not receive the current or voltage value necessary for the protection to operate in time, and then a short circuit will spread to most of the electrical network and cause great damage.



Why is multicast necessary?



As mentioned above, to close the data transfer requirements for horizontal communications, GOOSEs are transmitted somewhat unusual.



Firstly, they are transmitted at the data link level and have their own Ethertype - 0x88b8. This provides a high data rate.



Now you need to close the warranty and reliability requirements.



Obviously, for warranty, it is necessary to understand whether the message has been delivered, but we cannot organize the sending of acknowledgment of receipt, as, for example, this is done in TCP. This will significantly reduce the data transfer rate.



Therefore, the Publisher-Subscriber architecture is used to transmit GOOSE.





Architecture "Publisher - Subscriber"



The device sends a GOOSE message to the bus, and subscribers receive this message. Moreover, the message is sent with a constant time T0. If an event occurs, a new message is generated, regardless of whether the previous T0 period has ended or not. The next message with the new data is generated after a very short period of time, then after a bit more and so on. As a result, time increases to T0.





Principle of transmitting GOOSE messages The



subscriber knows from whom he is receiving messages, and if he has not received a message from someone after T0, he will generate an error message.



SV streams are also transmitted at the channel level, have their own Ethertype - 0x88BA, and are transmitted according to the "Publisher - Subscriber" model.



The nuances of multicast transmission in a digital substation



But in the "energy" multicast'e has its own nuances.



Nuance 1. For GOOSE and SV, their multicast groups are defined.



For the "energy" multicast, their own groups are used for distribution.



Telecom uses the 224.0.0.0/4 range for multicast distribution (with rare exceptions, there are reserved addresses). But the IEC 61850 standard and the IEC 61850 corporate profile from PJSC FGC defines its own ranges of multicast distribution.



For SV streams: from 01-0C-CD-04-00-00 to 01-0C-CD-04-FF-FF.



For GOOSE messages: 01-0C-CD-04-00-00 to 01-0C-CD-04-FF-FF.



Nuance 2. Terminals do not use multicast protocols



The second nuance is much more significant - relay protection terminals do not support IGMP or PIM. Then how do they work with multicast? They just wait for the necessary information to be sent to the port. Those. if they know that they are subscribed to a specific MAC address, then they accept all incoming frames, but only process the necessary ones. The rest are simply discarded.



In other words, all hope rests with the switches. But how will IGMP or PIM work if the terminals do not send Join messages? The answer is simple - no way.



And SV streams are quite heavy data. One stream weighs about 5 Mbps. And if you leave everything as it is, it turns out that each stream will be broadcast. In other words, we will only pull 20 streams per 100 Mbit / s LAN. And the number of SV streams at a large substation is measured in hundreds.



What is the solution then?



Simple - use old trusted VLANs.



Moreover, IGMP in the LAN of a Digital Substation can play a trick, and vice versa, nothing will work. After all, switches without a request will not begin to transmit flows.



Therefore, we can distinguish a simple commissioning rule - ‚ÄúDoes the network not work? ‚ÄúDisable IGMP!‚Äù

But can it still be possible to somehow organize a LAN of a Digital Substation based on multicast? Let's try to turn now to the regulatory documentation on the LAN. In particular, I will provide extracts from the following service stations:



  • STO 34.01-21-004-2019 - DIGITAL SUPPLY CENTER. REQUIREMENTS FOR TECHNOLOGICAL DESIGN OF DIGITAL SUBSTATIONS WITH VOLTAGE OF 110-220 KV AND NODE DIGITAL SUBSTATIONS WITH VOLTAGE OF 35 KV.
  • STO 34.01-6-005-2019 - POWER SWITCHES. General technical requirements.
  • STO 56947007-29.240.10.302-2020 - Typical technical requirements for the organization and performance of technological LANs in the automated process control system of the substation of the UNEG.


Let's first see what you can find in these service stations about multicast? Mention is only in the fresh service station from PJSC FGC UES. The STO asks for LAN acceptance tests to verify that the VLANs are configured correctly and to verify that there are no multicast traffic in the switch ports that are not written in the working documentation.



Well, STO also prescribes that maintenance personnel should know what multicast is.



That's all about multicast ...



Now let's see what you can find in these service stations about VLAN.



Here, all three service stations agree that the switches must support VLAN based on IEEE 802.1Q.



STO 34.01-21-004-2019 says that VLANs should be used to control flows, and using VLAN, traffic should be divided into relay protection and automation systems, automated process control systems, AIIS KUE, video surveillance, communication, etc.



STO 56947007-29.240.10.302- 2020, in addition to this, it also requires during designing to prepare a VLAN distribution map. At the same time, STO offers its ranges of IP addresses and VLAN for DSP equipment.



The service station also provides a table of recommended priorities for different VLANs.

Conclusion



In the Digital Substation, despite the fact that a lot of multicast streams are transmitted, in fact standard multicast traffic control mechanisms (IGMP, PIM) are not used. This is because the end devices do not support any multicast protocols.



Good old VLANs are used to control flows. Moreover, the use of VLAN is regulated by regulatory documentation, which offers well-developed recommendations.
