I am sure that the vast majority of food companies face similar problems. As a working tool to solve these problems, the approach described in the article by Lenny Rachitsky, product lead Airbnb, is useful.



It was 2012 when our team just joined Airbnb. Our task was to create the experience of ‚Äúsocial travel‚Äù for Airbnb travelers. We proceeded from the fact that they are scattered around the city, and if we make it easier for them to meet and do something together, traveling with Airbnb will become much more meaningful for them.



Fast forward 6 months later when we launched the first version in San Francisco. The product turned out great, everything went like clockwork. The implementation was not so successful. A tiny percentage of travelers tried it. And all would be nothing, but it was absolutely not the result that we expected. We tried more than once, introduced some improvements, but a few months later completed this project.





The first developments of the launched product, enabling Airbnb travelers to find events in which they can participate with other travelers



Personally, I learned a great lesson from this. But most importantly, this experience developed a habit and understanding in me of the importance of correctly formulating problems. Among the many reasons that influenced the failure of the project, the main one was the initial misunderstanding of the problem, which was to be solved. We noticed too late that the real problem that we had to solve was not ‚Äútravelers want to hang out with other travelers‚Äù, but ‚Äútravelers want to find interesting, non-travel opportunities to spend time‚Äù. Hanging out with other travelers is just one of these opportunities, and not the most significant. Fortunately, another team identified this need and eventually launched the much better Airbnb Experiences solution a few years later.







As I noted in myprevious article , to solve any problem, you must clearly identify it. It sounds deceptively easy. Doing it well is the superpower of the best leaders. Fortunately, this includes three simple steps.



  • Step 1. Crystallization of the problem
  • Step 2. Coordination of the problem with the team and stakeholders
  • Step 3. Continuous return to the problem






First, a little context. This framework is most effective when applied to projects, especially new products or new features. For a project to succeed, complete these three steps before you get started.



If your team does not have a clear idea of ‚Äã‚Äãwhere you should go, or an understanding of the general strategy of how you get there, stop to deal with these things first. Here , here and here to help you.







Step 1. Crystallization of the problem



Start by answering these questions about your project.



  • Description: what is this project?
  • Problem: What problem does it solve?
  • Why: how do we understand that this is a real problem and needs to be addressed?
  • Success: how do we know that the problem is resolved?
  • Audience: for whom are we doing this?
  • What is it: what will the implementation in the product look like?


Description: what is this project?



This is a brief description of the idea. People reading it can quickly understand the point. Be brief.



Problem: What problem does it solve?



The very statement of the problem is so fundamental that it is worth spending extra time on this. Think about the problem hypothetically. What problem do you think you are solving and why? You will add more context later.



We name the key characteristics of the correct formulation of the problem.



  1. The problem is brief. The goal is to describe the problem in one sentence. The more explanations you need, the less clear the problem becomes in the end.
  2. Is focused. We have the only clear problem that can be solved by one team and in a reasonable time. It is often very helpful to give a few examples of other problems that you DO NOT solve.
  3. Associated with unmet need. Focus on the needs of the user or business. It is extremely useful here to use this framework.
  4. Includes answers to the question what and why. What is going wrong and why is this a problem? You may need to return here in the next section.
  5. It does not depend on the decision. Suppress the urge to go too quickly to solve the problem.


Good problem statement examples



  • Lyft taxi drivers often reject orders because they are too far from the customer.
  • Airbnb homeowners are disappointed because they want to grow, but they don‚Äôt know how to do it.
  • Users leave too often at the final registration stage.


Bad problem statement



  • The number of users is growing slowly. [What's wrong: the problem is too wide. You can read about the strategy for approximating the big picture here and here. In addition, user needs are not taken into account.]
  • Create a loyalty program. [What's wrong: this is a solution. What problem do we want to solve?]
  • Users do not reach the end of registration. [What's wrong: lack of focus, missing the alleged reason. You need to go down one level.]


Why: how do we understand that this is a real problem and needs to be addressed?



At this point, you gather evidence supporting your problem as a hypothesis. What initially convinced you that this was a problem? What makes obvious the need to solve this problem?



Often at this stage you understand that this problem is not at all priority now or you need to adjust your ideas about the problem. This is the meaning of the exercise - do not neglect it. The number of problems is endless. Your goal is to make sure that this problem requires a solution by your team right now.



A few tips for this step.



  1. Examine quantitative and qualitative evidence. Collect all the data that indicates that this is really a real and important problem.
  2. Quality is more important than quantity. 3 to 5 direct facts are better than a dozen indirect ones. In the second case, your conclusions will be weaker, since they will be based on secondary and irrelevant facts that look like a bunch of evidence. Extra perfectionism is also useless.
  3. Play the Devil's Advocate with yourself. Try to convince yourself that this is a completely insignificant problem. What gaps are there in your evidence? Is the evidence consistent with what you think? Check yourself.


In the end, you will receive a verdict based on many compromises. Your job is to make the best decision based on available data. Continue to refine the wording of the problem as you learn more.



Success: how do we know that the problem is resolved?



Have you achieved what you planned to achieve? How do you know? Answer this question and write down the answer.







This criterion becomes incredibly important throughout the project, as it helps to make decisions and prioritize. Does feature X increase the chances of reaching a goal? If not, do not do it.



Ideally, this is a specific metric that is easy to measure. Ideally, it is directly related to the KPI of your team. Ideally, it is based on reliable data on available resources, investments and the results of past experiments. Rarely is ideal.



Some tips for determining success criteria.



  • Try to get a specific number, for example: 10% - an increase in the indicator X; 50% - decrease in Y.
  • The goal must be achievable, but ambitious. Achieving what goal will delight your team and leaders?
  • If you could not come up with a metric for your purpose (you need to think long and hard), describe how the world will look if the project is successful. Make it a criterion for success.


Audience: for whom are we doing this?



Everything is pretty obvious here. We rarely do something right away for everyone. Is it for new or returning users? Is it for casual or regular users? Is this for site or mobile app users? Etc.



What: what will the implementation in the product look like?



At this stage, you need to briefly describe the solution to the problem. Depending on how your team works and how much is known about the project, this can be a description at a very high level or as detailed as possible. In my experience, the key point here is to synchronize with the performers, finding out how many details they need and what will be most useful in the process of work.



Step 2. Coordination of the problem with the team and stakeholders



Have you seen the Chiptolle billboards along the highway (picture below)? A year ago, my colleague Peter pointed out the trick of this ad - each of us presents his ideal burrito inside the foil. We all see what we want to see.







Understanding the problem is like burrito in foil. Each member of your team has its own version of the problem in its head. Sometimes they almost coincide. Sometimes they are completely different. The larger and more complex the project, the more often they differ. Your task is to eradicate these discrepancies as early as possible and as often as possible. Open the package and make sure that everyone agrees with the burrito inside. Fortunately, we have a great document that we created in the first step. It will make your work 10 times easier.

Step 3. Continuous return to the problem



The classic Seinfeld episode, where Jerry and Elaine try to get the car they reserved, is a great metaphor for the trap of project management.







We often start with obvious intentions and all the necessary approvals, but at the right time, when the work is in full swing, we are moving away from the problem that we planned to solve. And this is the most important part of the task.



I remember how several years ago I worked on a project to create an admin panel for homeowners in Airbnb. Initially, we solved the problem of reducing the response time from the host, namely, reducing the time the host responded to the guest‚Äôs message. Our hypothesis was as follows: the hosts would respond faster if unread messages were more visible to them. In addition, we would remind them that the speed of response to messages affects their search ranking. In the end, we were right. But throughout the project, with its scale and complexity, I constantly found myself reminding the team what problem we should solve. (Professional advice: admins are a classic burrito problem in foil.) Nothing helps from blurring the focus of the project better than a constant return to the original statement of the problem and success criteria.You can solve many problems in many ways, but you can also create a great product that does not solve any problems.



Good habits will help you avoid this trap.



  1. At each review of the project, make sure that the contractor begins by posing the problem. If this is not obvious, ask the question: ‚ÄúWhat problem are we trying to solve?‚Äù.
  2. Each time you report on the progress of the project, before the stakeholders return to the statement of the problem. Make sure everyone has the same understanding.
  3. Before finalizing the project, ask yourself: ‚ÄúDo I feel confident that we are ready to solve the problem that we planned to solve initially?‚Äù
