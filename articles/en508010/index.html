What else are stepper motors capable of? - Score goals in table hockey

Creating my first robot (Robot for table hockey), namely the mechanical part, I learned a lot of interesting things for myself, which I want to share.







I ‚Äúgoogled‚Äù about half of the knowledge or spied on Youtube, and received the other half in practice, by trial and error in numerous experiments.



I will tell you about 4 points that, in my opinion, deserve special attention:



  1. Acceleration of the stepper motor to maximum speed.
  2. Simultaneous operation of an array of stepper motors through one controller.
  3. Cooling stepper motors and drivers.
  4. How to get rid of the squeak of stepper motors.


There are 6 players on each side of the Stiga Play-Off table hockey (goalkeeper and 5 on the field). My table hockey robot, in accordance with the number of players, has 6 robots, one arm for each player, and 2 motors are driven by each arm. The first motor moves the player forward and backward, and the second motor rotates the club. Total involved 12 stepper motors.



Why stepper motors? I needed accuracy and accuracy, and the stepper motor just has the ability to rotate the shaft strictly by a given number of degrees / steps. But, besides accuracy, speed was needed, and here the problems began and the search for a solution.



1. Acceleration of the stepper motor to maximum speed



When searching the Internet, you can find a lot of information about 3D printers, CNCs, robot arms with 6 degrees of freedom, and similar devices. But, I need to move the player by ~ 40 cm in a split second or hit the club on the puck so that it flies into the goal through the entire playing field. And what to do? - set up experiments and squeeze out all the juices from the motors!



The stepper motor shaft rotates with pulses and the motors that I used for the robot (frame size Nema 17) make 1 full revolution in 200 steps, 1.8 degrees per 1 step.

As a beginner, I purchased the most common drivers for A4988 stepper motors, bought an Arduino Mega controller with the largest number of inputs / outputs, CNC Shield v3 for conveniently placing 4 drivers at once, a 12-volt power supply, connected everything according to instructions from numerous sites. I figured out how to write a sketch program for Arduino and ran it.



The motors began to spin, but I needed speed. If you apply 1000 pulses per second from the controller to the motor driver, the motor will rotate at a speed of 5 revolutions per second. Great, but that was not enough. He increased the number of pulses and the motor stopped moving - it beeps and does not spin, there is not enough strength to start immediately at such speeds. It‚Äôs like on a machine with mechanics to immediately engage 5th gear. I had to modify the sketch of the controller for a smooth, but if possible, quick start. Of course, there are ready-made AccelStepper classes, but somehow I mistook it, there will be limitations, as always, I thought and made my own class for a smooth start, in which I used the cubic parabola formula with some auxiliary coefficients.



First, the intervals between pulses must be done more so that the motor starts moving, and then smoothly reduce the intervals between pulses. The motor will smoothly accelerate thanks to such a software variator. For different motors, there may be their own conditions for accelerating as quickly as possible, you need to experiment, to understand where the ceiling of the motor's capabilities in voltage and current is.



With such overclocking, there are always missing steps, but the loss of several steps for the purposes that I pursued is not important, this is not 3D printing.

To summarize - for maximum RPM you need to ensure smooth acceleration.



2. Simultaneous operation of an array of stepper motors through one controller



As already stated above, the robot uses 12 stepper motors. This array of motors is controlled through 1 Arduino Mega controller, which was selected due to the presence of a large number of pins (inputs / outputs).







To control 12 motors I needed:



  • 12 controller pins for transferring steps to motor drivers
  • 12 pins for setting the direction of rotation
  • 12 pins for controlling on / off motor drivers (optional, but desirable, as it turned out to be important for cooling)


Additionally involved another 24 pins:



  • 12 ( , , ? , ?)
  • 12 RGB- ( , / , , , )


The robot‚Äôs brain is now ‚Äústaying‚Äù in the desktop computer, as a temporary solution, until it moves to the Rasperry Pi 4 microcomputer aboard the robot. The computer sends control commands via the USB port to the Arduino controller and here you need the ability to work all 12 motors simultaneously. In practice, during the game I did not see that all 12 motors worked at the same time, maybe 4, but this is technically provided.



In a sketch for Arduino, he created the Player class for controlling 2 motors (moving and rotating the club) and at launch 6 instances of this class are created. When the classes are initialized, the pin numbers are transferred to which each of the ‚Äúplayers‚Äù will be attached, that is, each of the 6 players is tied to its drivers / motors, sensors and LEDs.



In the loop of the sketch, the USB port is checked for the presence of a command from the computer and the Move and Rotate methods of 6 player objects are called in turn to perform movements or stops.



To summarize - the Arduino Mega controller is equipped with a very large number of inputs / outputs> 70. If you need to solve a certain problem, you can connect dozens of stepper motors.



3. Cooling stepper motors and drivers



If the stepper motor driver is energized to the VMOT pin and the driver is turned on (by default ENABLE = 0 - turned on), the motor may start to squeak a little (depending on the driver), and the motor windings and the driver will start to heat up. You can touch the driver heatsink and make sure of this. I started using the power of the 12V motor and quickly felt the heat, but at 36V I really did not like this effect.



I did not wait until the motor windings burn down or the driver burned and began to figure out what was wrong. All helped pin pin ENABLE. If rotation of the motor is not required, turn off the ENABLE = 1 driver, and when rotation is needed, you just need to turn on the ENABLE = 0 driver. Since the robot uses 12 motors, and they do not often rotate at the same time, it means that they can be allowed to cool when they are not needed.



I took control of the Enable pin of each motor driver through Arduino. It took an additional 12 pins to use the controller, but the drivers became cold and the motors no longer make noise, although 36V is used.



4. How to get rid of the squeak of stepper motors



First, I purchased drivers for the A4988 stepper motors, it took 12 pieces. They are popular, reliable, simple and straightforward, and also very cheap, for beginners it is the most. Everything worked almost well, everything is clear with steps, 1 impulse - 1 step, 200 impulses at 1.8 degrees per 1 step - 1 motor revolution.



But, there were 2 unpleasant moments. At low revs, especially when you need to slowly turn the player‚Äôs club, tremors set in at the motors. And yet, this squeak! When 12 motors were running, it felt like R2D2 was talking to me.



I began to look for a solution to the problem and found out about quiet drivers with German Trinamic TMC 2100, 2130, 2208 chips and other series. They cost several times more, but if you need peace and quietness, they are worth it.



TMC has a StelthChop mode with interpolation of 4 or 16 input pulses into 256 micro-steps. After testing, I selected 4-> 256 micro-steps.







The sketch for Arduino needed to be added a little, since now for a full revolution of the motor it was required not 200 steps, but 4 times more (800 per 1 full revolution) due to the StelthChop 4-> 256 mode used. More steps - smoother and quieter move. The rotation is now smooth, quiet and no squeak.
