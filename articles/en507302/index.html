Step 0. Old-school


Just “to be” and for those who have forgotten, let's recall what target marketing and segmentation are in general. For the definition we turn to the classic of marketing - Philip Kotler.

“ , . , , , . ”
.

This is Kotler, 1993. Although it’s a very long year, nothing has changed with technology and all this technological progress.

Kotler’s targeted marketing consists of three steps:

1. Market segmentation. We break the market into distinct groups of buyers, each of which will require its own product (or customization of the existing one) and its own marketing mix.

2. The choice of target market segments. We evaluate and select one or several segments for exit, compile a matrix of priorities for entering the target markets.

3. Positioning of goods in the market. For each segment we form our own marketing message and marketing mix (to whom, how, when, where, with what message we are promoting).

What is the visible result of segmentation?


And now seriously about step-by-step segmentation for an IT product.

In our case - and we do AIOps - the result of segmentation will be the matrix of priority for reaching the target segments.

It’s not enough to decide who we want to sell the product to, it’s also important to understand which markets we will go to now, and which ones we need to prepare for, and what exactly needs to be done.

Step 3. Formation of indicators for assessment


The following indicators of each segment are important for us to evaluate:

  1. Attractiveness in terms of the size of one transaction (“average check”).
  2. Attractiveness in terms of volume (number of potential customers).
  3. The technological maturity of the segment.
  4. “Availability of money” in the segment for the product.
  5. The speed of the transaction (from acquaintance with the product to its implementation).
  6. The presence of strong drivers for development (for example, legislation on the development of digitalization).
  7. The pace of development (digitalization) of the segment.
  8. Internal readiness of the company to enter the segment (including personnel, organizational).

You may have a different list. When forming or revising, try to answer the question “For what reasons did we decide that this segment is of interest to us?”

Step 4. Launch


After we have decided on a list of experts, a list of segments and indicators for evaluation, we start the process.

Download the survey. We used standard Google Forms.

A little tip. When you send a survey of experts, be sure to note that it can take from 30 minutes to 1 hour. Such estimates are also best done not from the phone, but from a large screen monitor.

Set a deadline for passing, especially if there are a lot of participants. Send reminders to those who drag out the process.

Step 5. Processing the results


You can upload survey results from Google Forms to Exel and work with them.

What results can be obtained?

  1. The total assessment of each segment by experts.
  2. The average attractiveness score of each segment.
  3. Ranking by attractiveness within each group (by field of activity, size, etc.)
  4. Ranking by indicators (which are the most attractive in terms of transaction size, segment maturity, company readiness, etc.).

You can always do something better, and there are flaws in the methodology we used.

So next time we:
