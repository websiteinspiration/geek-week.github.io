A new technology for laser-based wireless Internet under water: how it works and who needs it

Aqua-Fi is a new technology for high-speed wireless Internet under water. Normal Wi-Fi in these conditions is powerless.



Underwater telecommunications has always been a problem. Radio signals, the ubiquitous wireless standard, are unsuitable for use in this case, since they are completely absorbed by water. Acoustic transmitters (such as sonar) work better underwater, but they suffer from very low data rates. It would be nice to use Wi-Fi under water, right?











The Aqua-Fi system uses the Wi-Fi module of the Raspberry Pi computer, which converts the signal and transfers it to the laser. And the laser, in turn, transmits a signal to the repeater, which is located on the surface of the water.



Researchers at King Abdullah University of Science and Technology ( KAUST ) in Tuvalu (Saudi Arabia) have developed underwater Wi-Fi. The system, which they call Aqua-Fi, uses a combination of lasers and some off-the-shelf components to create a bidirectional wireless connection for underwater devices. The system is fully compliant with IEEE 802.11 wireless standards , which means that it can easily connect and function as part of a larger network.



Here's how it works. Suppose you have an underwater device that must transmit data (for KAUST researchers, these were waterproof smartphones). Then they used a regular Wi-Fi signal to connect this device to an underwater ‚Äúmodem‚Äù. As a modem, they used the Raspberry Pi , which converted the wireless signal into an optical (in this case, a laser). The laser sent its signal to a receiver (relay) attached to the buoy on the surface of the water.



How it all began?



Aqua-Fi is a continuation of the work that KAUST researchers began in 2017 when they used a blue laser to transfer a 1.2-gigabit file under water. But Basem Shihada , an assistant professor of computer science at KAUST and one of the participants in the Aqua-Fi project, decided that it wasn‚Äôt as large as we would like: ‚ÄúWho cares only about one file? Let's do something more. ‚Äù



And then the team began to study bidirectional communication to create a system capable of transmitting high-resolution video.



Shihada says that it was important for him to use ready-made components: "My first rule: I do not want something to be [made specifically for this]." The only exception was the circuit for the Raspberry Pi, which converts the wireless signal into an optical signal and vice versa.



At first, the team used LEDs instead of lasers, but found that the LEDs were not powerful enough for high data rates. With LEDs, the beam was limited by a distance of about 7 meters and a data transfer rate of about 100 kilobits per second. When they switched to blue and green lasers, they reached 2.11 megabits per second at a distance of 20 meters.









Two students at KAUST talk on Skype using Aqua-Fi. Each phone on the edge of the black box is connected to an underwater Raspberry Pi, and the green laser is reflected several times (multiple of the length of the box), passing a distance of 20 meters.



Even with the limitations of the Raspberry Pi, KAUST researchers were able to use Aqua-Fi for Skype calls and file transfers.



Any problems?



Shihada says the system is currently limited by the capabilities of the Raspberry Pi. The team burned the user circuit twice, which was responsible for converting optical and wireless signals, when trying to use an overly powerful laser. In order for this installation to include more powerful lasers that can transmit further (in meters) and more (in megabits), the Raspberry Pi must be replaced with a special optical modem.



But there is still a big problem that needs to be solved in order to make a system like Aqua-Fi commercially viable. No, this is not a replacement for the Raspberry Pi. It's not so simple: laser tuning remains the most difficult task. Because lasers are very accurate, even slight vibrations in the water can lead the beam off course.



KAUST researchers are considering two options for solving this problem. First, you can use a technique similar to the "photon fence" designed to kill mosquitoes. A low power guide laser will scan the relay. When the connection is established, it will inform another laser with a higher power that you can start sending data. If the waves move the system again, the high-power laser will turn off, the auxiliary guiding laser will be activated again, and a new search will begin.



Another option is a MIMO-like solution using a small set of relays. If the laser emitter is slightly shifted due to waves, it will still support the connection.



MIMO is a spatial coding method for a signal that allows you to increase the channel bandwidth in which data is transmitted and received by systems from multiple antennas (Wikipedia).



Who needs this?



Why would anyone need the Internet under water? Firstly, there is a great need to save and transfer large files, for example, in the field of remote monitoring of marine life and coral reefs. High-definition video collected and transmitted by wireless underwater cameras can be extremely useful for conservationists.



It is also useful for the high tech industry. Companies like Microsoft are studyingthe possibility of placing data centers in the sea and under water. Placing data centers on the ocean floor may possibly save money on both cooling equipment and energy costs. Especially if the kinetic energy of the waves can be collected and converted into electricity. Well, since the data centers will be under water, then the Internet should also be there.
