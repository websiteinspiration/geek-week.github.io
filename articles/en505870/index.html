How to promote mobile games and applications in Japan, Korea and China



Asia is a tidbit for application developers, because Western markets have long been oversaturated and slowed down, and the Asian market continues to grow rapidly. The question is how to approach him.

In this article, we will talk about trends and methods for promoting mobile games and applications in Korea, Japan and China, as well as other Asian countries.

What types of games and applications are most popular?


Games are incredibly popular in Asia: in 2019, residents of the Asia-Pacific region spent more than $ 70 billion on games, and this, for a minute, half the size of the global gaming market. Most of this revenue came from China, Japan, and Korea.

Here are the most popular mobile game genres by country:



RPG is a popular genre in many Asian countries. There is also great interest in hyper-casual games.

Chinese players are very fond of e-sports (eSports), regular offline competitions in esport games are regularly held in Shanghai and Hong Kong. This is due to the fact that in China there is a tradition of collectivity, they like to do something in the company.

As for the applicationsIn Asia, categories such as shopping and second-hand goods, food delivery, finance, streaming services, photo and video editors, and productivity apps are popular. There is also a growing interest in health and fitness applications and the study of foreign languages, especially English (this applies to Korea).

Interestingly, Japanese users are in no hurry to switch to mobile applications to increase productivity and simplify their lives: instead, they use sites and desktop applications. And the Japanese, known for their shyness, actively download dating applications: there it is easier for them to get acquainted and remain relatively anonymous.

Dominant Stores


With Western countries, everything is simple: there is Google Play and the App Store. In Asia, its own nuances. In China, for example, there is an App Store, but Google and all its services are prohibited. Place Google Play - namely 70% of the mobile market - took about 400 local Android-store. Interestingly, each of these stories has its own focus, and some of them publish only games. It is easiest to collaborate with them through local publishers, which we will talk about a little later.

In Japan, the App Store dominates, and in Korea - Google Play, but in both countries there are also alternative stores; for example, one of these major Android stores is the Korean OneStore. Taiwan and Hong Kong have both the App Store and Google Play.

User solvency


Japan is the most attractive country in terms of profit: more than 70 million smartphone owners, willingly paying users (almost half of the players are willing to spend money on mobile games) and high ARPU (average revenue per user) - $ 200. Compare with ARPU in the USA ($ 72), in China ($ 40) and South Korea - $ 108 ( source ).

Interestingly, in Japan, women are more likely to spend money on games than men, so many companies create games and marketing campaigns aimed specifically at the female audience.

As elsewhere in the world, players from Asia prefer the freemium model. However, users are willing to pay for quality applications and the ability to customize.

Speaking of solvency, it is worth noting that many users from Asia are not used to paying with a card. So, in China, Wechat Pay and Alipay payment systems are used for in-app purchases, in MoMo, ZaloPay and VTCPay e-wallets, and in Korea, in addition to card payments, Kakao Pay was recently added to the App Store in Korea.

Promotion channels


Social media



* Line messenger is also popular in Indonesia and Thailand.

As you can see, China has no western sites at all, because they are blocked. But this does not apply to Taiwan and Hong Kong, because they are considered separate countries. There is a tense political situation between mainland China and Taiwan and Hong Kong, which is why the super-popular Chinese application WeChat is not so widespread in Hong Kong and Taiwan.

The main messengers in Asia are super-ups, inside which you can not only communicate, but also post posts and photos, play games, order food, send money, call a taxi, apply for a loan, etc. It is not surprising that users willingly spend there is a lot of time.

And most importantly - almost the entire population of Japan, China and Korea is concentrated in such super-ups, that is, these are ideal platforms for promoting your applications. They have crazy coverage: 93% of Koreans use KakaoTalk, Line - 91% of Taiwanese and 65% of Japanese, WeChat - 78% of Chinese.

The wide functionality of local messengers allows you to attribute them to social networks. At the same time, although Western social networks are present in most Asian countries, local social networks come first: for example, Koreans use Facebook, but KakaoTalk and Line are much more popular. The exception is the ubiquitous YouTube, as well as Twitter, which is very popular in Japan.

Important!In Asia, they are used to registering in applications through an account in their favorite social network. Thus, Korean users expect 4 options from you: Google, Naver, Kakao and Facebook, while Chinese users expect WeChat and a phone number.

How to promote your application through super-ups?


You can advertise through opinion leaders (influencers) or create a corporate account and give followers coupons, gifts in your application or funny stickers. Asian users love them so much that a bunch of cool stickers really motivates them to subscribe.

Offline Advertising


In Asia, offline advertising of mobile games and applications is very common: it is seen every day in the subway, on buses, at stops, etc. Do not think that only large companies resort to offline marketing - this channel works for companies of all sizes, and such advertising will not always cost a pretty penny.

Why is offline advertising considered effective? In densely populated Asia, there are many cities with a high population density: Taipei (Taiwan), Seoul, Shenzhen ... In Hong Kong alone, the population density is almost 9 times higher than in Moscow! It is clear that in such cities a huge number of people will see advertising.

In China, promoters often distribute flyers with a QR code to download applications on university campuses. Sometimes for installing the application on this flyer offer bonuses in the application. The Chinese themselves believe that since the developers took care of offline advertising, the application is probably of high quality.

For offline marketing, you will need to contact your local advertising agency or publisher. WeQ recommends that you first evaluate the effect of online promotion, and then think about offline advertising.

We did not mention the super-popular promotion channel - influencers. This channel deserves special attention.

Although many educated users know English, they still prefer apps in their native language. But localization raises many questions.

Traditional Chinese or Simplified? WeQ advises both options. The main thing is not to confuse where it is: in mainland China they use simplified Chinese, and in Hong Kong and Taiwan - traditional. Although users from China generally understand traditional characters, reading them will be unusual and uncomfortable. And residents of Hong Kong and Taiwan may be offended at all by seeing simplified hieroglyphs (remember the negativity of Taiwanese and Hong Kong people in relation to mainland China).

Although there is a temptation to dwell on simplified Chinese, because there are almost 900 million users, however, more solvent and ‚ÄúEuropeanized‚Äù users live in Taiwan and Hong Kong. Application Design and Local Culture


The style of Asian applications is very different from what we are used to in the West. Cartoon and anime characters, children's design style (cute animals, characters with a huge head) and a screen overloaded with information. Usually they try to push all the information onto one screen of the application.

Bili bili is a popular Chinese site with a typical Asian design:



For Japanese players, design is more important than the essence of the game. A win-win option is a cute, anime style, because anime culture is incredibly developed among all ages. Many Japanese games are based on manga (comics), the most famous of them is Monster strike. This game is popular in both Japan and Taiwan.

Numerous holidays are a great occasion for new events. On holidays, people spend more time on smartphones than usual, so the income from in-game purchases grows several times. In addition to the New Year on the lunar calendar (in China, Korea, Vietnam, Thailand), in many Asian countries celebrate the Mid-Autumn Festival. In China there is the Dragon Boat Festival, and in Japan - the Golden Week, and many other holidays.

The number 4 is considered unlucky in China, Korea and Japan: the four are consonant with the word "death", and it is avoided. Also, in Chinese culture, a name written in red means a curse, so avoid the red design of character names in games. Good numbers are 8 in China and 7 in Korea. This may be relevant for gambling applications.

The tradition of giving money in a red envelope (hong bao) is widespread in China, and virtual hong bao can be sent to each other in the WeChat messenger. You can beat this idea and come up with an unusual bonus in your application. By the way, Japan also has such a tradition, but Japanese envelopes with money are white.
