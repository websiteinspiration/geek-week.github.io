Background


The displays of electronic readers are made using E Ink technology. The image is formed by black and white granules located inside the capsule balls with a clear liquid. They ‚Äúfloat‚Äù upward depending on the charge applied to the electrodes. The E Ink display does not have LEDs, as in monitors or tablets, so it is clearly visible in daylight. But there is a drawback - in the dark it is impossible to distinguish the text written by "electronic ink".

The first manufacturers of readers delivered their devices without backlights, so reading in the evening required a lamp - as is the case with a regular book. But the situation began to change in 2008. Then Sony releasedThe first front-lit backlit reader. Engineers placed eight blue diodes at the edges of the screen.


The idea was good, but its implementation left much to be desired - the screen was lit unevenly. The company received a huge number of negative reviews from both device users and the press. One of the residents of the thematic forum Mobileread called the Sony solution an excellent example of how you do not need to do highlighting in the readers. The editor of the publication Goodereader noted that the decision of the Japanese company left an unpleasant aftertaste and tarnished the whole concept of backlit screens. As a result, the Sony leadership decided to abandon the technology and no longer add it to the readers. Head of Sony France IT department Stephanie Lang admitted in one of the interviews that their highlighting really hinders reading.

For a while, there was a lull in the lighting industry for E Ink. Until 2012, Barnes and Noble introduced the Nook Simple Touch with Glowlight technology. Engineers added a special light-conducting layer, which scattered the radiation of the LEDs, but the ‚Äúbacklight‚Äù was still visible on the screen .

The problem was solved in the same year by Amazon and ONYX. The first introduced the Kindle Paperwhite reader in the USA, and the second - the Aurora reader in Russia. The developers directed the light of white LED-lights at the end of the fiber. As a result, the impression was that the display was illuminated by an external light source.

ONYX BOOX called MOON Light. She allowed to read books in any light and reduced eye strain, as reflected rather than direct light entered the pupil. According to BusinessInsider, the backlight for the screen has become one of the most popular features for readers. But the developers of ONYX BOOX and other electronic readers did not stop there and continue to introduce technologies that improve the user experience. One of them was the flickering backlight.

Flickering backlight


The luminance of LED displays is usually controlled by pulse width modulation (PWM). In this case, the method of pulsating switching on and off the device with a change in the pulse duration relative to the signal period is used to control the brightness. If you increase the duration of the pulses without changing their frequency, then a larger light flux will fall into the eye - it will seem that the brightness has increased. If the pulse duration is reduced, then the screen, on the contrary, will look darker.



The scheme is easy to implement, so it is used both in the production of monitors and smartphones, and for backlighting E Ink screens. If the repetition rate of PWM pulses is high enough, the human eye does not see flicker. But it still affects the nervous system - fatigue occurs faster, a feeling of "sand in the eyes" appears. According to an analysis by a team of specialists from the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE), flickering LEDs can cause headaches and dry eyes, especially when reading from a screen. Researchers at the Institute for Environment and Sustainable Development at the University of Exeter also note that in especially serious cases, flickering can cause epileptic seizures.

It is possible to exclude a negative effect if you refuse PWM. In this case, the LEDs supply constant voltage, and the brightness of the glow is determined by its level. One of the first such technology was introduced by manufacturers of monitors. For example, ViewSonic back in 2014 offered flicker-free displays.

The light of sources whose brightness is regulated by constant voltage is more comfortable for the eyes, which is why manufacturers of electronic readers have adapted it. The ONYX BOOX engineers were the first to do this - the solution was called MOON Light 2.



The ONYX BOOX Livingstone was the first reader with flicker-free technology.with an E Ink Carta Plus screen and a resolution of 1072x1448 pixels. Reading comfort is added by the SNOW Field function, which reduces the number of artifacts during partial redrawing of the image. Backlight temperature control


This is another technology of electronic readers that can reduce the load on the eyes and the human body as a whole. Specialists from Harvard Medical School say that in the evening, blue light shifts the circadian rhythms and suppresses melatonin, so reading from the screen before going to bed can prevent you from falling asleep at night. At the same time, their colleagues from the University of Oregon argue that prolonged exposure to blue light can damage the retina and even brain cells.

Modern readers allow you to adjust the temperature of the backlight. So, you can choose a white and yellowish light for reading during the day and evening, respectively.


Devices with this feature are offered by many manufacturers, such as Kobo and Amazon. But the first reader with a ‚Äúwarm‚Äù backlight in Russia was the ONYX BOOX Cleopatra 3 . The color scheme in the device is regulated by seven warm and eight cold diodes (their brightness is adjusted individually). Today, similar screens are installed in almost all ONYX BOOX gadgets - from budget James Cook 2 and Caesar 3 to premium models like Monte Cristo 4 and Note 2 .

To date, the backlight with the ability to adjust color (Color Temperature Modulation) and powered by a current source is the most progressive in the field of E Ink screens.
