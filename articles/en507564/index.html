Psychopathy as an adaptation



Some researchers believe that psychopathy is an adaptation , not a disorder. I was also inclined to count many years ago when I was in a relationship with a difficult young woman. She was treated for borderline personality disorder. She went from work to work, always blaming others for it. Her life was a long story, consisting of cruel parents, former partners and friends.



She lived in a cloud of chaos and emotional pain. However, others have always suffered. No matter how bad it was, she could always find a way out and attract new friends. She left a trail of injuries and injuries. She was prescribed medications that she rarely took. She had a therapist whom she later seduced. The "Sufferer" always got what he wanted.



Among other things, she was a predator in a very confident and innocent manner. It took me a long time to realize that I was sleeping with a psychopath. She wore a ‚Äúborderline personality disorder‚Äù mask to become a more successful victim. I took notes at that time. They documented my immersion in an alternate universe. In relations with a psychopath, the basic laws of social behavior disappear. Something foreign and ‚Äúhungry‚Äù appears in their place.



There are already a huge number of people studying psychopathy as a disorder. I wanted to study psychopathy as an adaptation, because it better fits my data. In addition, this seemed to lead to more positive and useful conclusions.



Modeling psychopathy as an adaptation rather than a disorder opens the door to a new world. Our questions are changing. We wonder what kind of struggle caused this evolution. We ask what specific adaptations they actually possess. Do psychopaths have longer teeth? Sharp talons? Or are their talents more abstract?



We are also wondering what kind of counter-adaptations can exist in social people (not psychopaths, the vast majority). We ask ourselves: ‚ÄúCan the relationship between a psychopath and a normal, social person really be a story of a predator and a victim?‚Äù And the answer is unequivocal: ‚ÄúYes, of course!‚Äù We ask ourselves: ‚ÄúHow old is this story?‚Äù Answer: "Millions of years."



Predator model



The author of Dracula, Bram Stoker, called the psychopath a predator. Perhaps he had personal experience. History itself does not claim to be reality. But the metaphor is good. Dracula comes at night, ready to kill. He sucks the vitality out of you, even when he seduces you with his charm and sexuality.



Dracula does not kill immediately. Rather, it turns you into a weak copy of itself. He is strong and animalistic. He can read your mind even when you try to escape. And the main storyline is a confrontation between a man and a vampire, seasoned with a conflict of two vampires, a kind of cherry on a cake.



Dr. Robert Hare began describing psychopaths as social predators in his famous 1994 article in the journal Psychology Today. Subtitle: "This charming psychopath - how to detect social predators before they attack." In this article and in his work, he focuses on identifying psychopaths. Many of us are familiar with his Psychopathic Checklist.



The main strategy of predators is to confuse their prey. The main strategy of predator people is to deceive their victims. It is the same. Animal models are necessary for understanding and predicting human behavior. It is difficult for us to perceive ourselves without engaging in self-deception. Our introspection is divided into the concepts of ‚Äúfree will‚Äù and ‚Äúconsciousness‚Äù. We can neither improve nor reject these concepts, so we become obsessed with them. Nevertheless, we can easily analyze the behavior of animals without distractions of this kind.



In this book, I used the predator model as the basis on which I can hang everything else. We start with predators who have made their way into the human social universe through deception. Everything else follows from this and makes sense in this context.



My first acquaintance with the predator model occurred when I read Stefan Verstappen 's excellent work , Protection Against a Psychopath . This was the first text of those that I read, which proposed strategies to oppose the psychopath and defeat him, and this is the path I have chosen in this book.

This is not about a couple of spiders. Thousands of different insects somehow break into the colony of ants. One caterpillar mimics the queen of ants voice to trick ordinary ants. The paussus beetle is born, lives and dies in a colony of ants. Not only does it smell right (ants use smells to distinguish friends from enemies), it also mimics the sounds that ants make. More precisely, he imitates the queen of ants, tells the ants and larvae that ‚Äúeverything is in order‚Äù, even at the moment when he eats them.



Ants have evolved to work together to collect food and protect it from thieves. They share work, take care of their children together and live in large colonies. They communicate and think collectively. The ant colony exhibits intelligent behavior.



Ants succeed in obtaining food and providing protection, despite the parasites and predators among them. Indeed, ants are one of the most successful species. Ants have languages, tribal identity, social organization, the ability to work together. This is their adaptation. Perhaps you ask, to solve what problems? The answer seems to be simple: in order to protect yourself from a horde of such deceivers ...



Ants began by reducing the risk of extinction due to seasonal feeding. More ants can span a wider area than one ant. A happy ant can share food with those less fortunate. An unlucky ant will survive a bad period. Thus, ants developed altruism, which is a good solution to this problem. Other solutions are migration, hibernation, and synchronized breeding cycles.



However, altruism has one big flaw - it provokes dishonest behavior. Ant food is open to all who need it. If you get to the colony, you can eat ants, larvae and any food without straining. Thus, altruists had a choice: to develop protection from deceivers or to die out. For an ant colony, this means detecting intruders and then killing them. Altruism genes can only survive if they also provide for mutual exchange.

There are also vampire bats, killer whales and humans. We, too, form an intercontinental supercolony that is aggressive, dominant and often acts as one family.



In his 2012 book, The Social Conquest of the Earth, Edward Wilson described humans as apes . Our division of labor, intersecting generations and joint concern for young people give us a "superpower" that only a few other types can achieve.



Humanity did not develop in the Garden of Eden. Serious climate change has hit us again and again. We survived many obstacles, were almost wiped off the face of the Earth, only a few thousand people remained and this was repeated from time to time. These events did not kill us. Like the Argentinean ants, we came from one small population of genetically similar people. This allows us to recognize each other as members of the same tribe.



We survived disaster after disaster, working together. We have developed the ability to transmit knowledge from generation to generation. We developed altruism, reducing the risk of extinction of our species, and passed them through tribes and generations.



Among the early altruistic people there were many deceivers: scavengers, parasites and, above all, other people. Besides every social instinct we have developed, we have also developed the ability to deceive others. And as cheaters became smarter, social people began to better identify and punish them.



People form networks of relationships. Sometimes these relationships are hierarchical. More often than not, we form relationships with other people and groups. These relationships are not arbitrary. They are based on careful calculation. We count on the trade in genes, food, shelter, sex, affection, information, time. Basically, this process takes place at a subconscious level, but it happens all the time and it dominates us.



We have sophisticated tools in our heads to track these relationships. We can remember faces for life. We remember the good and the bad in detail. We can guess the relative value of any service or item in a given place or time. That fried chicken that you shared with me for lunch costs three bottles of beer tomorrow, or one bottle every two weeks. We remember the deceivers forever and do not forgive them.



We have imagination, so we can plan how we will work together. We have a language for sharing knowledge. We express our emotions with the help of faces, voices, body language and blush on our face, redness of the ears and body.



All these are devices to protect against deceivers. The ant colony is a product of confrontation, as is human society. What we are is formed due to this endless war at the junction of joint work and the promise: ‚Äúmoney in an envelope!‚Äù

As we speak of evolution and a long confrontation, one question arises. When did human psychopathy begin to develop? What time period are we considering? This is a question that no one has ever asked, according to Google. I am going to try to answer this question.



First, we can rule out a recent origin. Psychopathy is an integral feature of humanity throughout the world. It is universal. Thus, this precedes our expansion from Africa, which occurred about 150,000 years ago.



The origins of humanity make us continue to unwind time. The ritual burial cave Homo Naledi in South Africa appeared about 3 million years ago. The oldest stone tools date back 3.3 million years .



Ritual burials speak of sympathy for the dead and social emotions that go beyond the tribe and family. Stone tools indicate a structured society, the division of labor, a certain level of trade and long-term planning.



Let me explain. To turn a stone into useful tools requires additional skills and knowledge. It is necessary to adapt to the methods that arise during slow evolution. This means that knowledge is passed down from generation to generation, which means that there was a certain category of people involved in the manufacture of tools.

Raw materials are not widespread. Tool manufacturers had to travel to places with the right stones. They had to make their tools. They had to carry these tools to the people who needed them. This means that they needed to take food and water, bags or ropes and so on.



It also means the ability to plan ahead and organize collaborative activities with other people. It means a language rich enough to talk about the future and about different possibilities. That sounds great considering we are talking about hominids with a small brain, until you realize that ants do the same. This behavior should not be conscious. It can be instinctive.



The stone tool manufacturing chain includes rods, chips, and lugs. This goes far beyond the mental abilities of one person. This tells us about the existence of a social structure. Some specialize in making tools. Others use tools for hunting, cleaning meat, breaking bones, cutting wood. Such a social structure means altruism, that is, the ability to share with others. And whenever there is altruism, there are deceivers.



The first people were universal. They created their own tools the way they needed them. From this point of view, specialization and trade come much later. However, this is a very simple model to discredit. Hunting requires its own sets of specialized skills. Men will have strong competition to become the best hunter or the best tool maker. Women need tools the same way men do, but they are unlikely to make them. Two specialists who can trade can always work better than two generalists. Thus, the universal model cannot survive either sexual selection, or the economy, or the division of labor between men and women.



Therefore, I think that human psychopathy began to develop at least 3 million years ago.



The mystery of the big brain



The main distinguishing feature of a person is a large brain. According to records about two million years ago, he began to become more and more. What triggered this increase? It turns out other people. As David Geary says :



In the sample of skulls of ancient people, very small changes in the size of the brain were observed, everything changed when we reached a certain size of the population. After a certain population density was reached, the size of the brain increased very rapidly.


Why do more people mean more brain? Giri claims that it is all about ‚Äúsocial competition‚Äù, more people compete in the extraction of the same food. The most intelligent ones win, give birth to children, pass on their genes to them, and as a result, the gene for a small, dull brain dies out.



However, human food supply is not a static resource. Rather, it is a direct result of human activity. More people means more food, not less. Fish do not line up in shallow water, waiting for someone to catch them. Deer do not come in packs of twelve individuals. The food is resisting. Food production is a complex puzzle, solved with the help of technology, knowledge sharing, altruism and trade. Our social model is becoming more, not less effective, with more people. So, the more people, the more food.



This ceases to be true only when we reach the limits of our environment. That is, during a demographic decline, not growth. Only in catastrophic situations do people compete for food.



We might also wonder why food should go smart. Other animals do not. The big brain is expensive and dangerous for both the mother and her child. Why not develop larger teeth or stronger muscles or longer legs? It seems ridiculous to say that intelligence is the key to getting more food, without further explanation.



We can make Geary's model work if we replace ‚Äúsocial competition‚Äù with ‚Äúconfrontation between altruists and deceivers.‚Äù When a population consists of small isolated families, cheating is a bad strategy. It is easy to spot, and cheaters easy to punish. Predators need a certain population density. They should be able to move on after the resources run out in a certain territory.



Thus, economic incentives for deception increased as the population of ancient people grew. At a certain point, the confrontation became serious. People working together have developed social emotions to discover and punish deceivers. Deceivers have developed manipulation and emotional facial expressions to break into the language of emotions. Social emotions became more complex as the mimicry of deception got better. As cooperative people developed better social memories, liars became better liars.



And so on and so forth. Our brains are full of psychopathic talents and psychopath detectors. It's not that smarter people had more children. Tough confrontation left no choice for people. Either we are excellent altruists, or we are excellent deceivers. Both that, and another demands a lot of forces at a brain: the more, the better. A safe middle ground does not exist.

Let me examine some of these psychopath detectors. One of them is our sense of humor. Humor is something universal, something that all people have and what we can observe in children from an early age. Babies giggle with joy as they play with their parents. We instinctively trust people who can make us laugh. We do not trust those who do not like our jokes or those who seem to lack a sense of humor.



We use humor more in stressful situations. We value the original humor and reward for the ‚Äúmanner of storytelling‚Äù more than for the joke itself. In our horror films, monsters laugh in an exceptionally creepy way that scares young children. Monsters have no sense of humor.



A joke is a design, a story with a definite and consistent form. Every joke, even a pun, depends on secrecy. We do not tell a secret. It‚Äôs like ‚Äúexplaining a joke‚Äù. Rather, we tell a joke, and then wait for the other person to "understand." When they do this, they laugh, and we laugh, and the event is completed. Or, depending on the joke, we can expect a groan of disapproval.



It‚Äôs not enough just to laugh. Both sides should laugh at the right moment, not too early and not too late. Laughter should last long enough. It should not be too loud and should not be too soft. A good joke pleases both the person who told her and the listener. A bad joke bothers and annoys us. Humor is strongly associated with our emotions.



Humor is a very subtle concept. Humor is not random and does not happen by chance. Thanks to humor, we developed an empathy detector. A joke is a card with two sides. We show one side and hide the other. If the listener has empathy for the character in the story, he sees the hidden side. This causes a reaction of laughter. If the listener does not have empathy, he is confused.



The psychopath cannot laugh ‚Äúright.‚Äù He either does not laugh at all, or laughs too much or too long. We are more careful with people who laugh too much than those who don‚Äôt laugh at all. We think that this person is hiding something.



How does that make you feel?



Another talent unique to man is art. Why did our ancestors love to paint on the rocks and walls of caves? The traditional explanation is that they did art for the sake of art, from boredom, while they were stoned or performed part of the hunting rites.



Nevertheless, like Venus from ivory, created 40,000 years ago, art serves a single purpose - to evoke emotions in the viewer. The talent for creating something is so widespread that we can see and hear it on every corner and see how people get a penny for it. Nevertheless, we respect this talent, and it seems that our species has respected it for a long time. First of all, we expect art to make us ‚Äúfeel‚Äù something. And we ask others about it: ‚ÄúWhat do you feel?‚Äù And we scan their faces when they respond.



Psychopaths have many interesting traits that I will cover in the book. One of them is their lack of interest in creative activities. They do not draw, sculpt, or carve. They photograph only themselves and their property. They do not cook for pleasure, do not come up with recipes and do not bake bread as a hobby. They do not create music, although they can be excellent performers of someone else's work.



The lack of creative attraction is a rather curious thing when you first encounter it. She matches their complete lack of a sense of humor. Their hobbies are traveling, shopping, eating out, meeting new people. This is consumption, not creation.



Art is a precious thing. This is a universal human language. As is the case with comedy, we reward originality more than the technical component. As in the case of comedy, we get more pleasure from art, being in the company, rather than alone. As with comedians, we praise and respect artists, although talent has no value for survival. Finally, we rate artists based on their track record: success alone is not enough. One success can be fake, stolen or random. While for scientists or athletes, one victory can last a lifetime.



I am sure that creativity is another secret language of empathy. He asks the world: ‚ÄúFriend or enemy? Like a great joke, great creative work should radiate emotions and evoke emotions. Only part of the story that only the social brain can understand and appreciate must be told.



A small child learns to draw at school and takes his work for parents. These gifts are useless in any particular sense. Yet they are important and considered special at that particular moment. We create things for ourselves and others. We create for other people to feel something. This is usually happiness, although sometimes it can be loss, sadness, or other emotions. Creativity is a message about empathy. And we value the quality of our art as well as our humor: we value its originality and, therefore, its authenticity.
