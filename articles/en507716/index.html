Effective search for functional dependencies in databases

The search for functional dependencies in data is used in various areas of data analysis: database management, data cleansing, database reverse engineering and data exploitation. About the addictions themselves, we have already published an article by Anastasia Birillo and Nikita Bobrov. This time, Anastasia, a graduate of the Computer Science Center this year, shares the development of this work as part of the research work she defended at the center.











Task selection



During training at the CS center, I began to study in-depth databases, namely, the search for functional and difference dependencies. This topic was related to the topic of my coursework at the university, so while working on the coursework I started reading articles about various dependencies in the databases. I wrote a review of this area - one of my first articles in English and submitted it to the SEIM-2017 conference. I was very glad when I found out that they still accepted her, and decided to delve deeper into the topic. The concept itself is not new - it began to be used back in the 90s, but now it is used in many areas.



In the second semester of training at the center, I started a research project to improve functional dependency search algorithms. Worked on it with a graduate student of St. Petersburg State University Nikita Bobrov based on JetBrains Research.



Computational complexity of the search for functional dependencies



The main problem is computational complexity. The number of possible minimal and nontrivial dependencies is bounded above by the valueN2‚ãÖ2Nwhere N- the number of table attributes. The running time of the algorithms depends not only on the number of attributes, but also on the number of rows. In the 90s, FZ search algorithms on a regular desktop PC could process data sets containing up to 20 attributes and tens of thousands of lines, up to several hours. Modern algorithms running on multi-core processors find dependencies for data sets consisting of hundreds of attributes (up to 200) and hundreds of thousands of rows, in about the same time. Nevertheless, this is not enough: such a time is unacceptable for most real applications. Therefore, we developed approaches to speed up existing algorithms.

In the first part of the paper, we developed caching schemes for a class of algorithms using the partitioning method. Algorithms that use partitions, when the dependency is broken, add additional attributes to the left side of the dependency, after which they recalculate it, performing the operation of crossing partitions. Such an operation is called specialization in articles. But we noticed that partitions for dependencies, which will be retained only after several rounds of specialization, can be actively reused, which can significantly reduce the working time of the algorithms, since the intersection operation is expensive.

ADBIS 2019 Conference



Based on the results of the study, in September 2019, I presented the article Smart Caching for Efficient Functional Dependency Discovery at the 23rd European Conference on Advances in Databases and Information Systems (ADBIS-2019). During the presentation, work was noted by Bernhard Thalheim, a significant person in the field of databases. The research results formed the basis of my dissertation in the magistracy of mat-fur of St. Petersburg State University, during which both proposed approaches (caching and compression) were implemented in both algorithms: TANE and PYRO. Moreover, the results showed that the proposed approaches are universal, since both algorithms with both approaches showed a significant reduction in memory consumption, as well as a significant reduction in the operating time of the algorithms.
