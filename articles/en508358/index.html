How to become a "Superstar" (superager)





Think about whether there are people older than 65 among your friends. Most likely, some of them suffer from senile mental disorders, for example, forgetfulness or decreased attention span. Nevertheless, for some reason, some people manage to maintain a sharp mind. My father-in-law is 83 years old, he is a retired doctor, but he still edits books and maintains several medical sites.



Why, then, do some older people manage to maintain flexibility of thinking, while others do not? ‚ÄúSuper old people‚Äù (the term was coined and introduced by the neurologist Marcel Mesulam) are people whose memory and attention are not only better than the average indicators of their age group, but are at the level of healthy and active 25-year-olds. My colleagues from the Massachusetts hospital recently conducted a study to understand what is associated with the phenomenon of super-old.



In our laboratory, using functional magnetic resonance imaging, we scanned and compared the brain of 17 super-old people and their peers. We were able to identify a number of differences in some areas. In ordinary people, certain areas of the brain were thinned due to age-related atrophy. However, in super-old people these areas were no different from the norm for young people, and it would seem that they were not subject to the destructive effects of time.



So what are these key areas of the brain? If you ask scientists to guess, the majority will name areas that are considered ‚Äúcognitive‚Äù or that are responsible for thinking. For example, the lateral prefrontal cortex. However, we did not find this at all. Almost all processes took place in the ‚Äúemotional‚Äù areas, such as the mid-cortex and islet lobe.



For our laboratory, the discovery was not a shock, because we watched modern neurobiology debunk the idea that there is a difference between the ‚Äúcognitive‚Äù and ‚Äúemotional‚Äù regions of the brain.



This difference arose in the 1940s, due to the three-layer brain model developed by Dr. Paul Maclean. It was assumed that the human brain consists of three layers. The ancient layer, inherited from reptiles, contains contours for elementary survival. The middle layer, the ‚Äúlimbic system,‚Äù contains emotional patterns likely inherited from mammals. And the outer layer contains rational thinking, unique to man. Dr. Maclean called this theory the "triune brain."



The triune brain theory has become a popular topic of discussion in the media, the business world, and in certain academia. But scientists involved in brain evolution have discredited this theory several decades ago. The human brain did not evolve like a model of sedimentary rock, with layering of cognitive processes. Rather, (according to professor of neurobiology Georg Stridter), the brain evolves in the same way that companies do - it reorganizes as it grows. Areas of the brain that Dr. Maclean considered emotional, such as areas of the "limbic system," are now known as the main centers of communication. They participate in many important processes and, in addition to emotions, are also responsible for stress, speech, the work of internal organs and even the unification of the five senses into a single whole.



And now our study has shown that these centers play a significant role in ‚Äúsuper aging‚Äù. The thicker these areas of the cerebral cortex, the better a person copes with tests for memory and attention. For example, it better remembers the list of nouns and then easily repeats it after 20 minutes.



Of course, the main question that interests us is how to become a superstar? What types of activities, if any, will increase the chances of maintaining a healthy and sharp mind in old age? We are still studying this issue, but we can only say for sure one thing: work on something. Many laboratories noted that activity in these extremely important areas of the brain increased when people solved complex problems, engaged in physical or mental stress.



Thus, you can avoid thinning areas and keep them healthy with the help of vigorous exercises and intense mental work. My father-in-law, for example, does swimming and takes part in sports bridge tournaments.



However, the path to ‚Äúsuper aging‚Äù promises to be challenging. We noticed one surprising regularity: as you increase activity in areas, as a rule, you begin to feel bad - tired, driven, irritated. Remember the last time you struggled with a math problem, solved a problem, or brought yourself to physical wear and tear. Hard work causes discomfort. The Marines have a motto that best reflects this principle: ‚Äúpain is weakness leaving the body.‚Äù That is, the discomfort from stress means that you are building muscle and increasing discipline. Super-olds are like the Marines: they do an excellent job of temporary trouble from intense effort.
