This year's ARKit development and new feature in ARKit 4: Location Anchors



In recent years, I have been very active in the field of developing native games for the Apple platform and am interested in the possible integration of game processes in AR. Therefore, I try to monitor all updates annually announced at WWDC. Although there have been no updates for SpriteKit, SceneKit, and GameplayKit in recent years, Apple continues to actively update and promote ARKit and RealityKit, which can be integrated with these gaming tools.



Although Apple paid little attention to augmented reality during a presentation at WWDC , the new version of the software development kit (SDK) released this week can really be very useful and interesting for everyone involved in creating AR applications. In ARKit 4 presents new opportunities for developers, which are available on all iOS / iPadOS devices with the processor A12 Bionic above.



As you can see, in recent years, Apple‚Äôs investment in the development of AR technology has proven to be justified, because using computer visualization, machine learning and the function of motion detection, you can now create a more complex world of interactive computer graphics using a stationary camera. The development of this technology turned out to be even more successful and productive than the attempts of competitors to create a virtual reality system based on a smartphone.



The tool also adds important new depth detection capabilities available on devices equipped with Apple LiDAR Scanner (currently only available on the latest iPad Pros). But perhaps the most important thing in ARKit 4 is the location binding, which allows developers to place a virtual object in a specific place in the real world.



LiDAR: Advanced Remote Tracking System



Apple introduced the Scene Geometry API in ARKit 3.5 after the launch of the iPad Pro line with LiDAR scanners. I expect Apple to add LiDAR scanners to its next-generation iPhone, which it plans to release by the end of this year, so this feature is likely to be the most talked about during the next launch.



The new Scene Geometry API allows you to collect information using a LiDAR scanner and create a topological map of the world based on the data obtained. This information can be used to identify certain physical objects, place them on the stage and create a game simulation of the interaction between objects of the real and virtual world.



ARKit 4 will help you place virtual objects in front or behind real people and realistically show events using the Ray Casting function to blur the line between what is really real and digital content.





In iOS 14, Apple extends the capabilities of iOS devices with the support of the LiDAR scanner to better determine the distance between them and objects in a virtual environment. Machine learning allows you to combine a color RGB image obtained from the wide-angle camera of the device with depth indicators from the LiDAR scanner to create a dense, deep picture. This depth data is updated at a frequency of 60hz, which allows iOS not only to display objects on the stage, but to ensure their placement in real time in a virtual environment.



LiDAR also allows you to refine a feature called Ray Casting, which is a rendering method that uses computational geometry to create three-dimensional space on a two-dimensional plane. In addition, Apple provided tracking capabilities in the previous version of ARKit, but their use was only available for devices with a front-facing True-Depth camera. ARKit 4 significantly expands these capabilities by making face tracking available for all devices equipped with an A12 Bionic processor or later, including the recently released next-generation iPhone SE . Face tracking allows developers to create applications that place a person‚Äôs image on top of virtual content and watch their expression in real time.





Location Anchors



Of course, the capabilities provided by the LiDAR scanner are very impressive, amid all the new features announced by Apple. ARKit 4‚Äôs new Location Anchors technology now brings higher-quality AR content to the street, allowing developers to set the longitude and latitude for virtual objects. Then ARKit 4 uses these coordinates and data from Apple Maps to place the AR object in a specific place, at a certain height in the real world.



The next-generation AR interface management process is called Location Anchors. It allows you to accurately lock your device in relation to the environment and, according to Apple, this can be done much more accurately than using GPS. This whole process is controlled based on advanced machine learning techniques locally on your device.



As a result, when a developer places a virtual object in the real world, for example, a virtual sculpture on a busy square, this object will be saved and displayed in this place so that everyone who views it using the Apple AR device can see it in this locations. Location Anchors will first appear in major cities such as Los Angeles, San Francisco, Chicago, Miami and New York, and then, by the end of this summer, several more cities will become available.

How Location Anchors Works



With ARKit 4, you can now easily bind AR content to a specific place in the real world by simply setting the coordinates. AR-based applications that were previously based on image recognition or code scanning to trigger actions at a specific location now have the ability to update in real time and take advantage of location-based capabilities.



As part of the transformation of the Apple Maps database, the company for a long time collected data from cameras and 3D LiDAR on city streets in different parts of the world. To bind to a location, ARKit downloads a virtual map surrounding your device from the cloud and maps it to camera data. Using GPS, ARKit can quickly and accurately determine your location in the real world. All information processing is carried out using machine learning technology directly on your device.



Availability



As I already noted, the Location Anchors function, or in other words Geo-Tracking, is supported on all devices with GPS and A12 chip and newer models. Since the feature requires that this area has already been previously mapped to an Apple card, it is only available in certain cities in the United States. As of June 2020, there were only five cities in its support area, but I really hope that Apple will continue to expand its support area.



Geo-Tracking is based on the new ARConfiguration, dubbed ARGeoTrackingConfiguration , which makes it easy to check for compatible devices and their availability.



First, check if your device supports this feature: 



guard ARGeoTrackingConfiguration.isSupported else { return }


Now you can check if the device is in a supported city. If so, start the geotracking configuration in ARView. If you use RealityKit, you cannot apply automatic configuration, so you have to start it manually. 




ARGeoTrackingConfiguration.checkAvailability { available, error in
    guard available else { return }
    arView.session.run(ARGeoTrackingConfiguration())
}


Important Note. The checkAvailability function requires a network connection to load AR resources. Available will be false if the device is not connected to the Internet.



Building Location Anchors



How does Location Anchors work? After all, it is known that ARKit uses its own coordinate system to determine the relative position of the device, while in the real world the location is described with latitude and longitude. In geotracking, this problem was solved by creating a single coordinate system. The ARKit axes automatically coincide with the compass, where the X axis indicates the direction to the east, and the Z axis to the south.



All you need to know to create ARGeoAnchor is separate GPS coordinates. Here's how to create a snap, for example, to locate the Golden Gate Bridge . For greater reliability, we use a coordinate system accurate to decimal places. 




let coordinate = CLLocationCoordinate2D(latitude: 37.8185, longitude: -122.4738)
let geoAnchor = ARGeoAnchor(name: "Golden Gate Bridge", coordinate: coordinate)


If desired, you can specify the height in meters, which, by default, is accepted by setting parameters above sea level. 

let geoAnchor = ARGeoAnchor(name: "Golden Gate Bridge", coordinate: coordinate, altitude: 67)


Now you can add the anchor to the main scene. In RealityKit, this works based on the AnchorEntity tool from ARGeoAnchor . 


arView.session.add(anchor: geoAnchor)
let geoAnchorEntity = AnchorEntity(anchor: geoAnchor)
arView.scene.addAnchor(geoAnchorEntity)


Now, creating the application, you can add any other objects in relation to the selected location, rotating and placing them, as you like, in real space.

Progress AR technology is inevitable



Many who closely monitor the development of AR-technologies periodically complain about the slow pace of advancement of this technology sector. Of course, many of us would not refuse to have, for example, Apple's AR glasses today and now. In fact, this is a rather complicated technology, and here it is not speed that is more important, but its correct implementation. In addition to the real problems of creating such devices that are related to optics, battery life, wireless connectivity and many other aspects, you also need good AR content with a deep understanding and reflection of our real, ever-changing world. Only a few companies have the resources to handle this problem on their own, among them, apart from Apple, among the more successful, we can single out Microsoft and Niantic, which recently acquired startup 6D.AI.



Another problem continues to be the lack of hardware and software platforms that AR content can run on. With ARKit 4 and iOS 14, Apple can significantly strengthen its position as the world's largest AR platform in the market for modern technologies, providing developers with new tools for creating AR applications that users have been waiting for.
