In order to understand the principle by which it is more correct in this way, and not in another way, we need to discuss on the fingers how Wi-Fi networks are friends with each other - if this coexistence were a serious problem, Wi-Fi would not stick out every coffee maker. As we already found out in my previous article , the main goal of the 802.11 protocol is not to provide the maximum possible bandwidth per megahertz of busy ether, but uncompromising compatibility and protocol performance even in the worst of conditions (such as the title picture, yes). The protocol was thought up correctly, implemented, ahem, in different ways , but on the whole it is not stupid, and yet sooner or later every margin of safety will know its limit.

So, let's imagine that in the world there are only two devices that can work with Wi-Fi, and this is an access point and a client. The first rule of Wi-Fi is not to tell anyone “While one says, the rest are silent.” And they’re not just silent, but they are listening attentively.

When planning to transfer data, the first thing any Wi-Fi device does is listen carefully to see if anyone is transmitting their data. It will be very embarrassing if we start talking at the same time with someone else, right? Unlike 802.3, it’s Ethernet (too generalized, but let it be), in which the moment of simultaneous conversation determines when it happened (remember the Collision light on old hubs? I don’t, but it's about it), in 802.11 they try this moment avoid and prevent. The main reason is that the difference between the transmitted and received signal in wifi can reach BILLIONtimes (I'm not joking!), and what the transmitter is transmitting can clog tightly and burn the receiver if it tries to listen at the same time as the broadcast. All this etiquette of the mutual “After you - no, after you!” among 802.11 devices, it is called the complex acronym CSMA / CA , which is divided into three parts:

CS - Carrier Sense, carrier definition;
MA - Multiple Access, multiple access;
CA - Collision Avoidance, collision avoidance.

I have a spider flair about the fact that you have read all this lyrics more than once, but be patient a little, now we get to the meat of our task of arranging goats, wolves and cabbage. As part of this note, we are interested in the first two letters, namely CS . What is this all about?

So, the definition of the carrier - this, in fact, is the mechanism for determining whether someone else is talking now or not. It all boils down to the fact that there are almost constantly checked the presence of two possible reasons for the use of the ether - Wi-Fi devices and all other devices (yes, this is xenophobic, “ours and everyone else” - twenty-odd years to the protocol, and relevance as see, does not lose!). Before you just think about data transfer, the device needs to evaluate the employment of the ether (naturally, it is called Clear Channel Assesment, or CCA). “Our” and “not ours”, according to each device, are not equal in importance, and there are two threshold values ​​- SD (Signal Detect), which means that we heard something in 802.11, and ED (Energy Detect ), which means any power at the receiver input (any other language).

And now attention: to “our” Wi-Fi devices, service stations are times more attentive than to “everyone else”. That is, the ether is considered busy if we heard some kind of 802.11 frame at only 4 dB better than the noise level - we are sooooo polite to other Wi-Fi devices! And all the rest (all kinds of Bluetooth, for example) will prevent something from being transmitted only when the signal level from them is 24 dB higher than the noise!

Is it a lot or a little? Let's give the most textbook numbers as an example. So, in order for 802.11n devices to develop maximum speeds (with a channel width of 20 MHz and one transceiver is 72.2 Mb / s), they need a signal level of about -64 dBm with a signal-to-noise ratio of at least 25 dB (if anyone is interested where I got these numbers from here , use it if you haven’t looked at the article so farskhomm “All useful Wi-Fi content in one place.” ) That is, data transmission will stop ANY frame on the same channel with a reception level above -85 dBm! In some apartment building, this is a good plus or minus two floors (I hate to evaluate power length, but in this case I’m ready to sin for the sake of clarity), and in a clean field - half a kilometer of distance!



But if our device, ready for transmission, hears some signal, but cannot decrypt it, then it will ignore it up to -65 dBm, that is, until the level of this third-party interference is almost equal to the signal level from the very ideal access point to which it wanted to transfer data. Wow!

“But allow me,” someone will quite correctly object with my own fingers, “we all know that bluetooth interferes with Wi-Fi, like microwave ovens, there are all kinds of cameras!” That's right. At the level of “unreadable” interference in, say, -70 dBm (well, that is, it is not yet considered strong enough to stop the entire transmission and force the medium to be considered occupied), it becomes the noise from which we have the “signal” ratio / noise ”and count. We hear our access point at -65 dBm, we hear any unreadable signal at -70 dBm, so our signal-to-noise ratio suddenly dropped to 5 dB, and with these parameters, the channel speed is 72.2 Mb / s cannot be developed anymore, but the maximum that can be developed is the unfortunate 27 Mb / s. Everyone within the range of this interference sharply dropped their channel speeds,as a result, per second traffic through the access point can be pumped much less - that’s where the “brakes in Wi-Fi” started, ah-ah-ah, everything is bad, the wheel is spinning, YouTube is not loading. So that!

“What’s the relationship,” a new logical question will follow from an attentive reader idealized by me, “does any bluetooth have to our question?” Indeed, in the pictures in the test there is no bluetooth, there is only wifi! ” But what: any 802.11 device can decode a frame only when it is transmitted FULLY on the channel that it listens to ! Take a look at these two networks:



An access point operating on the first channel does not understand what the second access point says because it hears only 75% of what it is transmitting (like a point on the second channel that hears only 75% of what the first says). That is why she does not understand that it is “ours” - she does not believe that she must cede the medium for the transfer! Hence, the signal-to-noise ratio rolls down, the channel speed (and with it the resulting bandwidth) rolls down, and, mind you, it is not even proportional to the channel overlap, but inversely proportional to the difference in power - the better the client who wants to transmit data the first point, he hears the second, the more his channel speed will fall.

But this, unfortunately, is not all the reasons for the destructive action of overlapping channels. Now we turn to the next two letters, namely MA, or Multiple Access. We will not delve into the details of access to the environment in 802.11 protocols - I will mention only one feature that is important in the context of the issue under discussion. So, after each frame, it doesn’t matter if it is official or contains data, any Wi-Fi device should wait a while before trying to access the environment again. Moreover, it doesn’t matter if it sent the frame itself or only heard it - you have to wait a certain time, called InterFrame Space (IFS), and only then start the game “Who's the First to Stake Wednesday”. There are several of these IFSs, and here’s what’s interesting: if our device didn’t hear confirmation after frame transmission,that the recipient received it, thenit will wait longer than if it received. At times longer.

Let's go back to the picture from the paragraph before last. The access point from the first channel receives the frame. At this time, the access point from the second channel also receives a frame. Both of these frames are damaged, and both networks are forced to stand idle for a longer time, losing bandwidth even more (because, as we recall, time = money, and for wifi time = bandwidth). Full ambush.

So, from all this, a simple rule follows: if you can’t avoid crossing the channels, put the access points on one channel! Yes, both networks will lose in bandwidth, but, in any case, they are designed for such work.
