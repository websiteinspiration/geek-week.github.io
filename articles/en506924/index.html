How to choose the best material in the early stages of design?

It would seem that there are areas of application of materials developed over the years. There are recommendations from the manufacturer of materials - take and do. But in the modern world, hundreds of thousands of different materials with their own characteristics are available to consumers. How to understand this chaos? How to understand that the selected material has the best properties for a particular part? And most importantly, how to make the process of material selection algorithmized, repeatable and reasonable? Let's figure it out.



Status in Russia


Actually, it was just the scarcity of information on this topic in Russian literature that made me write this article. There are few sources where a rigorous methodology for selecting material for specific applications is considered. The methodology in domestic literature refers to recommendations and does not have mathematical rigor, which is not as beautiful as it could be. There is some good news. If the methodology is not common in Russia, this does not mean that it is not in the world. This technique offers to determine the most important properties for the material, set the criteria for the selection of these properties, and screen out the material according to these properties. If screening does not work, then you need to turn to the products-analogues and materials from which they are made. After determining the list of materials, restrictions on manufacturability and cost are imposed on it. And it ends with some short list of materials and their characteristics, by which it is already possible to determine the final candidate.



This is a great method. And everything is logical in it. But he does not answer the question "what criteria do you need to ask for material properties?" Of course, you can say that if we are designing an airplane, then the parts should be durable and lightweight, yet, preferably, cheap. And use such a parameter as specific strength - the ratio of strength to density. And you will be right. Partially. More precisely, only if your plane is a tensile rod. Why? And because in this approach you do not take into account the shape of the object and the type of load.

Cambridge Methodology


Michael Ashby, a materials scientist and professor from Cambridge, just started research in this area. Based on the results of his research, he published a number of books that describe this methodology. Further this section is written according to the book Materials Selection in Mechanical Design [3], which is publicly available.

This book provides a simpler decision-making scheme for the choice of material (figure below).



The scheme suggests first to weed out the materials according to the most ‚Äústriking‚Äù properties. For example, if the designed part has transparency requirements or, if possible, to produce the part only by stamping, then this immediately greatly narrows the list of potential candidates for materials. In this approach, the entire list of existing materials is considered at the beginning of the search. Oddly enough, this can lead to unexpected results. If aluminum has been used in similar parts for years, then the use of glass-filled polyamide may be unexpected.

Further, a narrower list is proposed to be ranked depending on the required properties. To do this, you need to ask some optimization criterion, such as those presented in the book about ultralight aircraft. We will focus on this a little lower.

Using this scheme, it is assumed that at the entrance to the material selection process, information on potential materials is presented in a general way. That is, we can even use some evaluation properties or property ranges for the material grade. For example, polyamide-6 produces a huge number of enterprises. All of them have material that is slightly different, with a unique set of properties. To determine the list of potential candidates, it is not important for us to know the exact properties for each manufacturer. It is enough for us to understand that the material polyamide-6 may have some variation in properties and know this variation. It turns out that we should have a stage at which we specify the properties of materials. This can be information from GOSTs, DINs, information from scientific journals, manufacturers data sheets, and so on.

Given this information, we have an even narrower list. But it would be strange not to take into account such indicators as the availability of material in the local market, existing contracts with suppliers, potential risks due to sanctions and other local features.

And finally, the decision to use a particular material or group of materials.

Now back to the main stage - ranking. This stage can be used not only in Ashby‚Äôs methodology, but used, for example, when choosing from 20 materials available at the enterprise.

We need to get some criteria by which it would be convenient to rank the materials. To do this, we ask ourselves several conditions: function, purpose, and limitations.

Any part is created to perform some function or several functions: the ability to hold pressure, transfer heat, withstand bending loads, transfer electric charge and so on. When designing, the engineer sets the goal - to make the part lightweight, durable, cheap, efficient or something else. Maybe their combination. But there are always limitations: the part must have fixed dimensions, work in a certain temperature range. This list defines the conditions for the selection of material.



Next, we can raise the question "how to achieve the goal?". If we are talking about a rigid beam, then you can choose a material with a high modulus of elasticity, you can increase the cross-sectional area, you can make a beam with a small length. If the beam should be light, then you can choose the material with the lowest density, reduce the length, reduce the cross-sectional area. Somehow too many options for an early stage, right?

This leads to the idea that it is possible to derive some relationships that will take into account the type of loading and the goals set. Such relations will be called the efficiency index .

In a general sense, an efficiency index is a combination of material properties that characterizes the use of a material in a particular field.

We formalize this thought. Note that the effectiveness of each particular application will depend on the functional requirements (withstand the load, transfer heat), geometric parameters (dimensions, shape) and material properties (relevant to the particular application):

p=f(F,G,M)

Where p - efficiency f() - function (in the mathematical sense), F - functional requirements, G - geometric parameters, M- material properties.

With this formulation, the goal is to minimize or maximizep.

Functionp=f(F,G,M) can be expressed in such a way that it consists of a product of three functions, each of which would be determined by a set of parameters F, G and M (why it will be clear in the example below):

p=f1(F)f2(G)f3(M)

With this separation, the function p proportional f3(M)determined by the properties of materials and independent of functional requirements F and geometric parameters G. Obviously, with increasing functionf3(M) will increase and p. In this case, the functionf3(M)can be considered separately and it will determine the index of efficiency of materials. That is, you can optimize the functionf3(M) not knowing all the functional requirements F and geometric parameters Gand consider them separately, already at the very design. That is, we first select the optimal material, and then we are already engaged in the design and optimization of the structure.
