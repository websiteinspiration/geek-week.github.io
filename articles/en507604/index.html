10 myths about calcium batteries





We will talk about the very common today lead-acid batteries with the addition of calcium in the plate material. Rechargeable batteries (batteries) with calcium in negative grids and antimony in positive ones are called hybrid (Ca +, Sb / Ca), with calcium in all lattices - calcium-calcium (Ca / Ca), both of which are simply calcium. Also, silver (Silver, Ag) can be added to technical alloys for calcium batteries, which is why they sometimes talk about ‚Äúsilver‚Äù batteries.



Most often, calcium starters are automobile starter batteries with liquid (free-spatter) electrolyte, which for short will be called bulk, even if the filler caps are protected from opening or are completely absent. However, calcium is increasingly found in traction (cyclized, deep cycle), as well as backup (for uninterruptible power supply systems) batteries. These are often made using AGM (absorbent fiberglass separator mats) and GEL (silica gel thickened electrolyte) technologies, and both can be combined, so the name AGM-GEL is not always a mistake.



Calcium, silver, silicic acid gel in such batteries are not active substances of the current-forming reaction, but auxiliary to improve technical characteristics, because calcium, silver and gel are types of lead-acid batteries, unlike other chemical current sources, in which the electrode maybe silver and so on.



When mentioning voltages, we assume that we are talking about the most common batteries - 12-volt, i.e. consisting of six cells connected in series (cans). Charging current is expressed as a percentage of capacity. For example, a 10-hour current, it is 0.1C, it is 10%, it is 6 amperes for 60 A * h.

Myth 1: in order to fully charge a calcium battery, it must be ‚Äúboiled‚Äù with a voltage of 16 volts and a current of 10% capacity



Reality: the stages of recharging with overvoltage up to 16V and higher are provided only after the main charge is completed until a certain voltage is reached (most often in the range of 14-15V) and the charging current decreases when the voltage is stabilized at this level to a certain value. The current strength at the stages of the "high voltage" (hereinafter without quotation marks) charge must not exceed 5% of the nominal capacity. Exceptions are smart memories that charge with pulses or modulated current of complex shape, including asymmetric (reverse). Thanks to electronic control, the amplitude and average (integral) values ‚Äã‚Äãof currents and voltages can be higher without harm and danger than when charged simply by a power source with stabilization (limitation) of current and voltage.

Reality: it‚Äôs true, you can‚Äôt charge the battery with a half-stage (current stabilization, then voltage) profile with the parameters from myth 1 (if the goal is not to intentionally harm the battery and what surrounds it). To fully charge a calcium battery, it is necessary to observe a multi-stage charge profile, either manually observing its progress and controlling a stabilized power source with voltage and current adjustment, or using an automatic charger (charger) that implements the desired profile. But the first stage alone, up to 15 volts, is not enough to fully charge a calcium battery.



Myth 3: ordinary users are not told the intricacies and secrets that professionals use



Reality: Simple users are told what they can safely use with the tools and knowledge they have.



Myth 4: mixing electrolyte is useless and harmful. All acid should be in the depths of the spread, there it is most useful



Reality: when the battery is discharged, spongy lead of negative active masses (AM) and positive lead oxide are converted to lead sulfate, with the consumption of sulfuric acid from the electrolyte and the release of water. When charging, on the contrary: energy and water are expended, acid is released, sulfate of discharged active masses is converted into metal and charged oxide. This Gladstone-Tribe double sulfation is the main current-forming reaction. Its well-known equation describes far from all processes in the battery, but gives the keys to understanding them.



Sulfuric acid is heavier than water, therefore, in relation to a lead battery, the concentration and density of the electrolyte are synonyms.



EMF - the electromotive force of the electrochemical cell of a lead battery - is proportional to the acid concentration, temperature and, of course, the degree of charge, that is, the fraction of charged active masses in their total volume. EMF without load is called NRC - open circuit voltage.



If the charged active masses are surrounded by an electrolyte with excess water and a lack of acid, they will not be able to adequately give current (amperes) and useful capacity (pendants, amperes * hours) during the discharge, since there is not enough acid to convert lead and its oxide to sulfate. Also, the EMF under load will decrease and, accordingly, useful power and energy (watts, watts * hours).



If discharged AMs are surrounded by an electrolyte with a lack of water, then they will not be able to charge, because without water, there is nowhere to take hydrogen to convert the sulfate ion to sulfuric acid and oxygen to form lead oxide. For the implementation of electrochemical transformations, a charging current must go, and for its flow to the source (charger) it is necessary to overcome the EMF of the electrochemical cell. A local excess of acid during stratification creates an increased emf, which prevents the charge.



The height of the battery can can be uneven and acid concentration, and the charge of the active masses, and the latter have a porous volumetric structure. Therefore, there is both a vertical stratification of the electrolyte, caused by gravity (sulfuric acid is heavier than water and tends to down, pushing water up), and horizontal, in the pores of active masses and separators - dielectric partitions and envelopes that prevent short circuit and destruction of plates.



As a result, a real lead battery has in its banks sections of high and low electrolyte concentration, as well as charged and discharged active masses. Electrically in each bank, all sections of the active masses of each half-block of plates are connected in parallel, therefore a voltmeter connected to the jumpers will show the total voltage, which can differ greatly from the actual EMF in different places of the bank.



An increased concentration of acid at the bottom of the can and in the depth of the active masses, as well as gas bubbles in the pores and the distribution of ions, the diffusion of which is prevented by the structure of AM and separators, lead to overstated NRC cans and batteries. In this case, a significant part of the AM can be discharged and sulfated, the useful capacity is reduced. This phenomenon is called "imaginary charge."



Where there is a lack of acid, charged AMs will not be adequately discharged to the benefit of the consumer, and where there is a lack of water, discharged AM will not be charged when a charging voltage is applied. At the same time, gas evolution may be observed in other areas, from which we can draw the erroneous conclusion that the battery is fully charged.



The freezing temperature of the electrolyte depends on the concentration of acid. If a low-density electrolyte layer appears in the bank at low temperature, it will freeze and expand, since the ice density is less than the density of water and the volume is correspondingly larger, which leads to the destruction of the battery.



But the elimination of electrolyte separation is also necessary in warm time, otherwise self-discharge, sulfation and a decrease in battery performance will progress to failure.

Reality: Calcium in a calcium battery is actually not enough. This is an alkaline earth metal that is expensive and aggressively interacting with other substances, especially acids, and it is used as an alloying additive, and in the material are not active masses undergoing chemical transformations, but lattices that perform a non-conductive function. No gypsum is formed on the working surface of the active masses during the discharge.



But why, then, in practice, a deep discharge really greatly reduces the performance of a calcium battery, or even makes it impossible to use it: the battery refuses to be charged from both the car‚Äôs generator and the charger ?!



The fact is that when the battery is deeply discharged, strong separation of the electrolyte occurs, the dense separators of modern calcium batteries, especially EFB and the like, interfere with its mixing and ion diffusion, and calcium prevents the evolution of hydrogen, especially in the lower part of the plates, where mixing is especially necessary. As a result, with insufficient charging voltage supplied by the car or charger generator for classic antimony batteries, a significant part of the AM does not charge, because the Gladstone-Tribe reaction conditions are not met and the electrolyte does not mix. The battery is not functioning properly, its degradation is progressing.



Charging such a battery with a profile adequate to its material and design will allow it to return to service, but after the next deep discharge, or some time with partial undercharging, such maintenance will be required again, otherwise the battery will cease to perform regular functions again.



Myth 6: the electrolyte mixes well when the car is moving, so mixing with a stationary charge is not necessary



Reality: for mixing the electrolyte in a modern bulk starter battery, a fairly significant mileage of the vehicle with the corresponding fuel consumption is required, therefore it is advisable to completely mix the electrolyte during stationary maintenance. In addition, as already mentioned, the stratification interferes with the charge, i.e. where local anomalies of acid concentration remain, the active masses will remain undercharged and sulfated.



Myth 7: the equalizing charge gives a very insignificant increase in the delivered useful capacity, therefore it is better to neglect it so as not to waste energy, time and battery life



Reality: A battery with unredeemed undercharging, sulfation and stratification of the electrolyte will be worse to give and replenish the charge, the mentioned problems will progress, plus self-discharge.



Myth 8: it is desirable to produce a high-voltage charge with direct current or pulses as often as possible



Reality: everything is good in moderation. It is better to produce a leveling overcharge with overvoltage not often, but completely, than often and not fully.



Myth 9: pests came up with calcium to reduce battery life and cash in on new battery sales



Reality: invented calcium alloys and improved separators to increase the strength and durability of the plates, reduce water consumption. But a modern battery created using such (Ca / Ca, EFB, etc.) technologies requires appropriate maintenance parameters and equipment to provide them (chargers), different from those intended for low antimony batteries of previous generations.



Myth 10: high-voltage recharge is only for bulk starter batteries



Reality: at least two manufacturers of traction batteries recommend that they carry out a charge stage with a voltage of up to 16.02V, but with a current of 1% capacity, not more than 2 hours, after the main charge and two stages of recharging are completed, and provided that the main charge lasted more than 3 hours, t .e. the battery had a significant depth of discharge. Interestingly, these are manufacturers' recommended profiles for gel batteries - Chilwee EVF and Tianneng TNE.
