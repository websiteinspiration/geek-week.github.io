"Without leaving the laundry." How CEOs of Google, Amazon, Nasdaq, and Slack Work From Home





While the coronavirus is walking the planet, even the leaders of the world's largest IT companies - who are used to flying from city to city, from a meeting to a conference, on private jets - are forced to sit in isolation. In the USA, the rules differ from state to state, but basically the CEOs take the situation quite seriously and are not going to put their lives at undue risk. In many companies - Google, Microsoft, Adobe, Facebook - they even allowed employees (if possible) not to come to the office until the end of 2020.



But how do CEOs cope with the unusual rhythm of new life?



Their home offices temporarily became the headquarters of the companies. From there, CEOs conduct their business, monitor the development of the market, try to establish supply chains again and make decisions about the work of their company, which has now suddenly become remote.



The New York Times spoke with the leaders of several of the largest and most characteristic companies about their experiences during this time. And we added a few other recent interviews - from Zuckerberg and Bezos.

‚ÄúThe atmosphere of great stress is now‚Äù: Adena Friedman, Nasdaq







Nasdaq CEO, member of the board of directors of the Federal Reserve Bank and the first woman to head the global stock exchange, Adena Friedman is known for making Nasdaq a real technology company. She leads her as an IT firm, focusing on data processing. Adena is one of the proponents of the ideas of modernization of capitalism, and lectures on this topic. Nevertheless, she is not enthusiastic about the latest (forced) innovations, and she liked to do business in the old way more.



At the very beginning of the pandemic, Adena divided the key personnel on her stock exchange into two teams. Every week, one team works from home, while the second supports everything you need in the office. Every weekend, the office is cleaned and disinfected. So even if one team becomes infected, the second will be able to keep the company afloat, and the exchange will not have to close.



Such a decision in early March seemed too harsh and even reckless. But later it turned out to be very robust - given that New York, in the center of which is located Nasdaq, subsequently became the center of distribution of coronavirus in the United States.



When it is her turn to spend a week outside the office, Friedman works from his residence in Maryland, near Washington. Together with her husband and two sons, who also work from home. However, she follows the same working routine as on ordinary days. He gets up at 5:30 in the morning, rides on a simulator, eats breakfast and starts to work around eight. She monitors the state of the markets. It is trying to make sure that banks, which also have the majority of employees working remotely, carry out multi-billion dollar transactions without errors. And holds meetings with the leaders of large companies - who also sit at home.
