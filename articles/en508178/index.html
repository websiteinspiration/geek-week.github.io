Tesla Model 3 was released three years ago. And yet, thanks to software updates and hardware replacement (from HW 2.5 to HW 3.0), Tesla promises to keep Tesla 3 up to date and prepare it for the future with unmanned driving.



At least that's what they promise.

Model 3 is a more compact and affordable electric car, first released in mid-2017. Thanks to its proprietary on-chip system introduced last year, Tesla promises Model 3 customers that if they purchase a full suite of unmanned control software, they will receive an upgrade from HW 2.5 to HW 3.0 simply by visiting a service center.



To make things clear: the software package for fully unmanned driving today still does not allow Tesla cars to drive without human intervention. In this issue of ‚ÄúUnder the Hood‚Äù with System Plus Consulting (Nantes, France), we will delve deeper into the Tesla Model 3, and focus on automotive sensors and the Tesla Autopilot ECU installed in Model 3.







Availability First



The computing power of cars is becoming an increasingly important feature. Significant computing power is needed to provide optimal driver assistance and automated driving, as well as to activate safety features.



To optimize automatic driving, many OEMs and leading automakers use a variety of sensors, such as cameras, radars, lidars, and ultrasonic sensors that allow cars to detect their surroundings. All data received from the sensors must be collected together, and it is here that the control unit comes into play.



Given the image of advanced technology that Tesla supports, the general public will forgive a lot if she believes that all the hardware inside the Model 3 is technically the most advanced and affordable on the market.



Looking under the hood, you will see that the main goal of Tesla when designing Model 3 was to reduce the cost of advanced driver assistance systems, which made the model ‚Äúaffordable,‚Äù explained System Plus CEO Romain Fraux.



Model 3 uses eight cameras, one radar, and 12 ultrasonic sensors as sensors. Model 3 does not use lidars, which corresponds to the well-known statement of Mask that lidar is a ‚Äústupid idea‚Äù.



Next, System Plus will share information about the sensors and the computing unit Model 3.



Sensors for ADAS







(Source: System Plus)



The Tesla Model 3's sensor suite includes eight cameras that provide 360-degree visibility around the car within a radius of 250 meters and 12 ultrasonic sensors that complement this vision system. Together they make it possible to detect hard and soft objects at a distance with almost twice as much accuracy as the previous system. This set of sensors also includes a direct-view radar system with advanced image processing functions. This system provides additional environmental data at an excess wavelength that can see through heavy rain, fog, dust, and even outside of previous cars.



On the front panel there are four cameras that support the radar and have different characteristics. The main camera covers 250 meters, but has a small viewing angle, while others cover shorter distances (150, 80 and 60 meters), but have a wide viewing angle of the environment around the car and they are used to read traffic signs. The remaining four cameras look at the side and back of the car and can see at a distance of up to 100 meters.



Sonar, in turn, uses ultrasound to detect obstacles within a radius of 8 meters around the car. It works at any speed, and also controls the blind spot. The data collected by the sonar is also used by the autopilot to control the automatic lane change system during overtaking. Finally, GPS is used to determine the position of the car relative to the road.



Front cameras







(Source: System Plus)



For direct viewing, Tesla has developed a three-chamber module with three image sensors from On Semiconductor. Model 3 also uses two side cameras for direct viewing, two side cameras and a rear camera.



The eight cameras designed for Model 3 are based on the same 1.2-megapixel image sensors released by On Semiconductor in 2015. ‚ÄúThey are inexpensive. They are not new and do not differ in high resolution, ‚Äùsaid Fro.



Purchasing all eight image sensors from a single supplier means that ‚ÄúTesla must be committed to a better purchase price,‚Äù said Fro.



Triple Camera Comparison







(Source: System Plus)



The Tesla Model 3 triple camera is equipped with three ON Semi AR0136A CMOS image sensors with a 3.75 Œºm pixel size and a resolution of 1280 √ó 960 1.2 MP. This camera provides the operation of the front image capture system at a distance of up to 250 meters, used by the autopilot control module and driver assistance.



To add context to the Tesla three-chamber module, System Plus compared it to the three-chamber module developed by ZF, one of the largest suppliers of first-level cars. ZF S-Cam4 comes with two solutions, one with a monocamera and the other with a triple camera.



S-Cam4, a triple-camera version of the ZF module, is equipped with Omnivision CMOS image sensors and Mobileye'a EyeQ4 computer vision processors.



The PCB mounting technology used by Tesla is different from BMW technology, as shown in the image above. BMW prefers an isolated combination of sensors on three different printed circuit boards. In turn, all three CMOS sensors on the printed circuit board are built into the Tesla three-position camera module without using a chip system.



ZF's S-Cam4 includes Mobileye's vision processing capabilities.



Choosing mature image sensors from On Semiconductor and not adding post-processing, Tesla made its camera module ‚Äúnot about the latest image sensors, but about the cost,‚Äù said System Plus. The company estimates the cost of the three-chamber ZF module at $ 165, and the three-chamber Tesla module - at $ 65.

Radar







(Source: System Plus)



Tesla has chosen Continental's proven radar module. The Continental ARS4-B contains a 77 GHz radar chipset and a 32-bit microcontroller supplied by NXP Semiconductors. System Plus Fro noted that although several chip companies, including MediaTek and Texas Instruments, have announced their entry into the automotive radar market, NXP and Infineon are undeniably a pair of leaders. Continental is a key player among the suppliers of radar modules. Their ARS4-B ‚Äúcan be found in at least 15 other vehicles, including the Audio Q3, VW Tiguan, Nissan Rogue and others,‚Äù Fro explained.



The Continental ARS4-A radar system is used for collision warning, emergency braking assistance, collision mitigation, or adaptive cruise control (ACC). An important element of this system is the simultaneous measurement of large distances (up to 250 m with an accuracy of ¬± 0.2 m) and small distances (up to 70 m), relative speed and angle between two objects.



The system consists of two electronic boards, including an NXP semiconductor microcontroller and a Broadcom Ethernet transceiver. The radio frequency (RF) board is made with an asymmetric structure with a PTFE / FR4 hybrid substrate equipped with flat antennas.



As a high-frequency radar transceiver, the NXP Semiconductor 77 GHz chipset is used, consisting of four receivers, two transmitters and an associated voltage-regulated oscillator (VCO).

Tesla integrated full-fledged modules from several manufacturers: graphic chips from Nvidia, processors from Intel, microcontrollers from NXP and Infineon, memory from Micron Technology, Samsung and SK Hynix, and audio amplifiers from STMicroelectronics.

Switching to HW 3.0, Tesla integrated two new Tesla SoCs, two graphics chips, two neural network processors, and one additional processor.

HW 2.5 vs HW 3.0

Return on investment for your own SoCs?







(Source: System Plus)



The automotive industry has rarely seen automakers designing their own special-purpose integrated circuits for their cars. This is a ‚Äúbig risk,‚Äù Fro said, ‚Äúunless you have a talented team of hardware developers within the company.‚Äù Given the modern automotive market, this will be a difficult decision, he added.



And yet, Tesla is not alone. There are a number of automakers who are eager to do what Tesla has done with developing its own processor for the autopilot system.



But is it really worth spending a lot of money researching and developing your own special purpose integrated circuit for your own cars?



‚ÄúIf you want to maintain a good margin and switch to mass production, that might make sense,‚Äù said Fro. Over the past few years, with more and more electronic components being installed in automobiles, it has been a real shock for many car manufacturers to know that leading chip suppliers, such as Nvidia and Intel, are used to having large margins from their chip systems, processors, and graphics chips. If OEMs are reluctant to continue price negotiations with chip suppliers over the next five years, it may be easier for them to develop their own systems on a chip to control their fate.



According to System Plus estimates, Tesla's HW 2.5, consisting of three Nvdia chips and an Infineon microcontroller, costs $ 280. In turn, Tesla's HW 3.0, based on two systems on a Tesla chip, costs $ 190.



Assuming that the automaker spends $ 150 million on the design of its own processor, without any changes in component prices and annual production of 400,000 units, Fro said: ‚ÄúOur quick assessment shows that you can recoup your investment in four years ".
