Security Week 26: Vulnerabilities in D-Link and Netgear Routers

Two studies of the last week were devoted to vulnerabilities in routers and affected at least one D-Link model, as well as 79 Netgear home devices.



Let's start with D-Link: the problem was found in the DIR-865L model ( news , research , manufacturer's bulletin ). In total, six vulnerabilities were discovered that, individually or in a specific combination, allow you to take control of a device.



All holes require presence on the local network. For example, in the web interface for exchanging files, credentials are transmitted in clear text - they can be intercepted and, after modifying the user id, used to access password-protected documents. In a similar way, you can get into the main settings of the router by exploiting the vulnerability in a web server or by intercepting the ‚Äúunique‚Äù cookies of the current owner generated by a weak algorithm. An attack will require one more step, but rather simple: convince the user to click on a special link in the browser.



Vulnerabilities in Netgear routers cover a large number of devices - 79 models ( news , research , another study ). Researcher Adam Nichols from Grimm discovered a problem by analyzing firmware for R7000 series routers.









The code above refers to the firmware update scheme of the router. Inadequate verification of user input leads to buffer overflows and in theory entails the execution of arbitrary code. Result in Nichols' PoC report: router settings are available via telnet without a password. Stack cookies would help to eliminate the possibility of exploiting such a vulnerability, but there is no such design in the firmware of the studied router. Moreover, in the Netgear D8500 router, according to Nichols, there was a mechanism complicating the exploitation of vulnerabilities like ‚Äúbuffer overflow‚Äù, but it was excluded from the code.



In these examples, model release dates and manufacturers' reactions are interesting. The D-Link developers consider the DIR-865L model to be outdated and do not provide updates, although they still released a ‚Äúbeta version‚Äù of the firmware, which partially covers the vulnerabilities discovered. As for the Netgear routers ( list ), vulnerability information was transmitted to the vendor in January, but there was no solution at the time of publication.



The D-Link router was released in 2015, and its support continued until 2018. Among the affected Netgear devices, there is the AC1450 model of 2016, although an analysis of the firmware indicates the presence of a vulnerability back in 2007. In any case, we are talking about completely modern devices, and not about a ten-year-old modem, which is high time to change.



The three-year D-Link support cycle is longer than the typical two-year delivery period for updates for smartphones, but for such long-lived pieces of hardware, too little comes out anyway. It turns out an interesting situation: tens of thousands of devices are completely satisfied with their owners, but they have security holes, with which the vendor is having trouble fixing.
