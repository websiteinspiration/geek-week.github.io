Part 7. WAR MANAGEMENT AND MILITARY ALGORITHMS


DECLARATIVE AND PROCEDURAL KNOWLEDGE IN MILITARY SCIENCE

The theory of war, military affairs, and military-strategic management throughout history (as, indeed, all science since the days of Aristotle) ‚Äã‚Äãdeveloped mainly or even almost exclusively in the form of declarative knowledge (descriptive knowledge).

Procedural knowledge in military science is found only occasionally in the form of isolated "inclusions" surrounded on all sides by declarative knowledge, such as definitions, logical reasoning, explanations, justifications, arguments, references to lessons from past wars, etc.

Procedural and algorithmic knowledge in the form of precise and detailed step-by-step descriptions of complex ramified and cyclic algorithmic processes is almost never found in military scientific and educational literature.

Procedural and algorithmic ‚Äúinclusions‚Äù in the declarative text are like many tiny islands in the vast ocean of military literature. Between the islands there should be connecting algorithmic bridges, but they, as a rule, are only implied and are not explicitly described.

This is a fundamental flaw in world and domestic scientific military literature.

The vast majority of procedural ‚Äúblotches‚Äù exist in military publications in the form of isolated points. They remain lonely algorithmic "orphans", do not unite into logically finished fragments and complexes, and do not turn into an integral system of military algorithms.

Thus, military scientific and educational literature contains, as a rule, only declarative, but not procedural and non-algorithmic knowledge. There are two reasons for this:

- underestimation of the importance of procedural and algorithmic knowledge;
- lack of convenient notation and expressive means for visual recording of military purpose and control algorithms (manuals).

There is an urgent need to create an expressive and powerful algorithmic notation. Without such a notation, it is very difficult and almost impossible to present procedural and algorithmic knowledge in a visual and suitable form for training and printing.

The lack of easily perceived ergonomic high-precision graphical algorithms in military textbooks and manuals is in the way. It delays the training period and contributes to the fact that the training of future officers becomes unreasonably expensive. Personnel and cadets do not receive the necessary knowledge, which they replenish only in the process of service.

The foregoing means that the problem of the algorithmization of military scientific and educational literature is not only not solved, but not even realized.
The adoption of the DRAGON language standard as the standard of the Russian Armed Forces can help to eliminate this shortcoming and improve the quality of training of military specialists.
