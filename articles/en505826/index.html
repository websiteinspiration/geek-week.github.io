Super Vision - Myths and Reality


Now about the chicken, which sees more and better than us.

At the beginning of the blog, I wrote about such a thing as the density of cones in the center of the retina. So it just determines its maximum resolution. As the matrix quality of the display, plus all sorts of filters. It is a fact that photoreceptors cannot be grown - their density is genetically determined and changes over the course of life only to a smaller side.

First, photoreceptor density is important in determining the maximum achievable visual acuity. Humans have 150-200 thousand receptors per mm 2 , chickens have four hundred thousand, and hawks have a million!

Secondly, there are other factors that significantly increase the possibilities of vision. In the human retina there are three types of cones, the maximum sensitivity of which falls on red, green and blue.

All other shades are formed by mixing these colors. And the chicken also perceives ultraviolet, so the picture it receives is much fuller and richer. We see a million shades, and chickens due to UV cones - about 100 million.


I note that a lot of factors influence visual acuity, for example, the level of illumination of the environment, the level of color and brightness contrast between the object and the background, the diameter of the pupil, the age of the person, especially the refraction of the eye.

Two eyes are not the same - this is another fact!


Two eyes are always not the same for us, always, for everyone. There is a dominant for the distance and for the near, and, like any paired organ, for example the hands, they are not absolutely symmetrical. The quality of vision may vary and is usually different. Take your photo and cut in half with one of the mirrors mirrored. You will not be in the resulting photograph - because there is no perfect symmetry in nature, just as there is no perfect zero.

Normal binocular disparity, that is, the difference between what the right and left eye sees, is more pronounced in the perception of close objects.

With the same shape of the eyeball and lens, and even the same refractive power of the visual system, the limiting visual acuity may be due to the difference in the distance between the retinal receptors (rods and cones).

Before correction, this asymmetry is often not obvious, therefore, after correction, we strongly recommend looking with two eyes, the brain needs to adapt in the new optical conditions.

As a rule, when detecting the movement of an object, the patient's eyes involuntarily begin to move. The minimum size of an object is taken as a basis for determining the quality of visual acuity.
