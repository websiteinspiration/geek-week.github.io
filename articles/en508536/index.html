NDC Conference London. Microservice disaster prevention. Part 1

You spent months converting your monolith into microservices, and finally, everyone gathered to flip a switch. You go to the first web page ... and nothing happens. Restart it - and again nothing good, the site is so slow that it does not respond for several minutes. What happened?



In his speech, Jimmy Bogard will hold a ‚Äúposthumous autopsy‚Äù of a real microservice disaster. He will show the problems of modeling, development and production that he has discovered and tell how his team slowly transformed the new distributed monolith into the final picture of sanity. Although it is impossible to completely prevent project errors, you can at least identify problems at an early stage in the design so that the final product turns into a reliable distributed system.







Greetings to all, I‚Äôm Jimmy, and today you‚Äôll hear how you can avoid mega-disasters when creating microservices. This is the story of a company where I worked for about a year and a half to help prevent their ship from colliding with an iceberg. To tell this story properly, you will have to go back in time and talk about how this company began and how its IT infrastructure grew over time. To protect the names of the innocent in this disaster, I changed the name of this company to Bell Computers. The next slide shows how the IT infrastructure of such companies looked in the mid-90s. This is the typical architecture of the HP Tandem Mainframe large, universal, fault-tolerant server for a computer hardware store.







They needed to build a management system for all orders, sales, returns, product catalogs, customer base, so they chose the most common mainframe solution at that time. This gigantic system contained every bit of information about the company, everything that was possible, and any transaction was conducted through this mainframe. They kept all their eggs in one basket and thought it was normal. The only things that did not enter here were mail order catalogs and placing orders by phone.



Over time, the system became more and more, a huge amount of garbage accumulated in it. In addition, COBOL is not the most expressive language in the world, so in the end the system turned into a large monolithic lump of garbage. By the year 2000, they saw that many companies had sites with which they conduct absolutely the entire business, and decided to build their first commercial dot-site.



The initial design looked pretty nice and consisted of a top-level site bell.com and a number of subdomains for individual applications: catalog catalog.bell.com, accounts account.bell.com, orders orders.bell.com, product searches search.bell.com. Each subdomain used the ASP.Net 1.0 framework and its own databases, and all of them communicated with the backend of the system. However, all orders continued to be processed and executed within the only huge mainframe in which all the garbage remained, but the frontend was separate websites with individual applications and separate databases.







So, the design of the system looked orderly and logical, but the real system was as shown in the next slide.







All elements addressed calls to each other, accessed APIs, built in third-party dll libraries and the like. It often happened that version control systems grabbed someone else's code, stuffed it inside the project, and then everything broke. MS SQL Server 2005 used the concept of link servers, and although I did not show with arrows on the slide, each of the databases also communicated with each other, because there is nothing wrong with constructing tables based on data obtained from several databases .



Since they now had some separation between the various logical areas of the system, this turned into distributed clods of dirt, and the largest piece of garbage still remained in the mainframe backend.







The funny thing was that this mainframe was built by competitors of Bell omputers and is still serviced by their technical consultants. Having ascertained the unsatisfactory operation of their applications, the company decided to get rid of them and redesign the system.



The existing application has been in production for 15 years, which is a record for applications based on ASP.Net. The service took orders from all over the world, and the annual profit from this single application reached a billion dollars. A significant portion of the profits came from the bell.com website. On "Black Fridays" the number of orders made through the site reached several million. However, the existing architecture did not allow any development, since the rigid interconnections of the elements of the system practically did not allow making any changes to the service.



The most serious problem was the inability to place an order from one country, pay for it in another and send it to a third, although such a trading scheme is very common in global companies. The existing website did not allow anything like this, so they had to accept and place such orders over the phone. This led to the fact that the company was increasingly thinking about changing the architecture, in particular, the transition to microservices.



They acted reasonably, having studied the experience of other companies, to see how they solved a similar problem. One such solution was the Netflix service architecture, which is a microservice connected via an API and an external database.



The leadership of Bell omputers decided to build just such an architecture, adhering to some basic principles. First, they abandoned data duplication using a database sharing approach. No data was sent, on the contrary, everyone who needed it had to go to a centralized source. This was followed by isolation and autonomy - each service was independent of the others. They decided to use the Web API for absolutely everything - if you wanted to get data or make changes to another system, all this was done through the Web API. The last important thing was the new main mainframe called ‚ÄúBell on Bell‚Äù, unlike the ‚Äúmainframe‚Äù Bell, based on the hardware of competitors.



So, for 18 months they created the system, guided by these basic principles, and brought it to the stage of pre-production. Returning to work after the weekend, the developers got together and turned on all the servers to which the new system was connected. 18 months of work, hundreds of developers, the most advanced Bell hardware - and no positive result! This disappointed a lot of people, because they repeatedly ran this system on their laptops, and everything was fine.



They acted reasonably, throwing all the money to solve this problem. They installed the most modern server racks with switches, used gigabit fiber, the most powerful server hardware with crazy RAM, combined all this, set it up - and again nothing! Then they began to suspect that the reason could be in timeouts, so they went into all web settings, all API settings and updated the entire timeout configuration to maximum values, so that they could only sit and wait for something to happen with the site. They waited, waited and waited for 9 and a half minutes until the website finally loaded.



After that, it dawned on them that the current situation needed careful analysis, and they invited us. The first thing we found out - during all 18 months of development, not a single real ‚Äúmicro‚Äù was created - everything was getting even bigger. After that, we started writing a post-mortem, also known as ‚Äúregretrospective‚Äù, or ‚Äúsad retrospective‚Äù, aka ‚Äúblame storm‚Äù - an ‚Äúindictment storm‚Äù similar to a brain storm storm to understand the cause of the disaster.



We had several evidence, one of which was full traffic saturation at the time of the API call. When you use a monolithic service architecture, you can immediately understand what exactly went wrong, because you have a trace to a single stack that reports everything that could cause a crash. In the case when a bunch of services access the same API at the same time, there is no way to track the trace except to use additional network monitoring tools such as WireShark, thanks to which you can consider a separate request and find out what happened during its implementation. Therefore, we took one web page and for almost 2 weeks put together the pieces of the mosaic, making various calls to it and analyzing what each of them leads to.

Look at this picture. It shows that one external request causes the service to make many internal calls that return. It turns out that each internal call makes additional hopes in order to be able to independently service this request, because it can no longer go anywhere to obtain the necessary information. This picture looks like a meaningless cascade of calls, because an external request calls additional services that call other additional services, and so on almost indefinitely.







The semicircle in which the services call each other is shown in green in this diagram - service A calls service B, service B calls service C, and the latter calls service A. As a result, we get a ‚Äúdistributed deadlock‚Äù. A single request made a thousand network API calls, and since the system did not have built-in fault tolerance and loop protection, the request failed if at least one of these API calls failed.



We have done some mathematical calculations. Each API call had a SLA of no more than 150 ms and 99.9% uptime. One request caused 200 different calls, and in the best case, the page could be displayed after 200 x 150 ms = 30 seconds. Naturally, it was no good. Multiplying 99.9% uptime by 200, we got 0% availability. It turns out that this architecture was doomed to failure from the very beginning.



We turned to the developers with the question, how did they fail to discern this problem over the course of 18 months of work? It turned out that they only read SLA for the code they started, but if their service called another service, they did not count this time in their SLA. Everything that started within one process adhered to the value of 150 ms, but accessing other service processes increased the total delay many times. The first lesson learned from this was formulated as follows: ‚ÄúDo you dispose of your SLA or does the SLA dispose of you‚Äù? In our case, the second came out.



The next thing we discovered - they knew about the existence of the concept of misconceptions about distributed computing, formulated by Peter Deutsch and James Gosling, but ignored its first part. It says that the claims ‚Äúnetwork is reliable‚Äù, ‚Äúlatency is zero‚Äù and ‚Äúbandwidth is infinite‚Äù are misconceptions. Misconceptions are the statements ‚Äúthe network is safe‚Äù, ‚Äúthe topology never changes‚Äù, ‚Äúthe administrator is always only one‚Äù, ‚Äúthe data transfer price is zero‚Äù and ‚Äúthe network is homogeneous‚Äù.

They made a mistake because they ran their service on local machines and never ‚Äúhooked‚Äù external services. When developing and using the local cache locally, they never encountered network hopes. For all 18 months of development, they have never wondered what could happen if we touch external services.







If you look at the boundaries of services in the previous picture, you can see that they are all wrong. There are many sources that advise on how to define the boundaries of services, and most do it wrong, such as Microsoft on the next slide.







This picture is from the MS blog on the topic ‚ÄúHow to Build Microservices‚Äù. It shows a simple web application, a business logic block, and a database. The request comes directly, probably there is one server for the web, one server for business and one for the database. If you increase traffic, the picture will change a little.







This is where a load balancer appears to distribute traffic between the two web servers, a cache located between the web service and the business logic, and another cache between the business logic and the database. This is exactly the architecture Bell used for its application - load balancing and blue / green deployment in the mid-2000s. Until some time, everything worked well, since this scheme was intended for a monolithic structure.



The following picture shows how MS recommends switching from a monolith to microservices - simply divide each of the main services into separate microservices. It was during the implementation of this scheme that Bell made a mistake.







They divided all their services into different levels, each of which consisted of many individual services. For example, the web service included microservices for rendering content and authentication, the business logic service consisted of microservices for processing orders and account information, the database was divided into a bunch of microservices with specialized data. And the web, and business logic, and the database were stateless services.



However, this picture was absolutely wrong, because it did not map any business units outside the company's IT cluster. This scheme did not take into account any connection with the outside world, so it was not clear how, for example, to get third-party business analytics. I note that they also had several services invented simply for the development of the career of individual employees who sought to manage as many people as possible in order to get more money for it.



They believed that to switch to microservices, it‚Äôs enough to just take their internal infrastructure of the N-tier physical layer and insert Docker into it. Let's take a look at what the traditional N-tier architecture looks like.







It consists of 4 levels: UI user interface level, business logic level, data access level and database. The more progressive DDD (Domain-Driven Design), or software-oriented architecture, where the two middle levels are domain objects and repository.







I tried to consider various areas of change, various areas of responsibility in this architecture. In a typical N-tier application, various areas of change are classified that permeate the structure vertically from top to bottom. These are the Catalog, the Config settings that run on individual computers, and the Checkout checks that my team worked on.







The peculiarity of this scheme is that the boundaries of these areas of change affect not only the level of business logic, but also apply to the database.



Let's look at what it means to be a service. There are 6 characteristic properties of a service definition - it is software that:



  • created and used by a specific organization;
  • is responsible for the content, processing and / or provision of a certain type of information within the system;
  • It can be created, deployed and launched independently to meet certain operational tasks.
  • communicates with consumers and other services, providing information on the basis of agreements or contractual guarantees;
  • protects itself from unauthorized access, and its information from loss;
  • operates with failures so that they do not lead to damage to information.


All these properties can be expressed in one word "autonomy." Services operate independently of each other, satisfy certain restrictions, define contracts on the basis of which people can receive the information they need. I did not mention specific technologies, the use of which is implied by itself.



Now consider the definition of microservices:



  • microservice has a small size and is designed to solve one specific problem;
  • microservice is autonomous;
  • when creating the architecture of the microservice, the metaphor of the ‚Äúcity planning‚Äù town planning metaphor is used. This definition is from Sam Newman's book, Creating Microservices.


The definition of Bounded Context is from Eric Evans' Domain-Driven Design. This is the main pattern in DDD, an architecture design center that works with volumetric architectural models, dividing them into different Bounded Contexts and explicitly defining the interaction between them.







Simply put, a Bounded Context refers to the area in which a particular module can be applied. Within this context is a logically unified model that can be seen, for example, in your business domain. If you ask ‚Äúwho the customer is‚Äù from the ordering staff, you will receive one definition, if you ask those who are involved in sales, you will get another, and the performers will give you the third definition.



So, Bounded Context says that if we can‚Äôt give an unambiguous definition of what the consumer of our services is, let's define the boundaries within which we can talk about the meaning of this term, and then define the transition points between these various definitions. That is, if we are talking about the client from the point of view of placing orders, this means this and that, and if from the point of view of sales, this and that.



The next definition of microservice is the encapsulation of any kind of internal operations, preventing the "leak" of the components of the workflow into the environment. What follows is the ‚Äúdefinition of explicit contracts for external interactions, or external relationships,‚Äù which is represented by the idea of ‚Äã‚Äãcontracts coming back from the SLA. The final definition is the metaphor of a cell, or cell, which means the complete encapsulation of a set of operations within a microservice and the presence in it of receptors for communication with the outside world.
