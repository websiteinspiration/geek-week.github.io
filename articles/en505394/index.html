The Nokia 9000 communicator, released in 1996, was not at all like that. It was a transformer phone: the exterior is a regular cellphone, perhaps a little larger. Inside is a wearable computer, a follower of the very first compact devices such as the Atari Portfolio . In 1997, you could buy a Psion handheld computer of the fifth series , and connect it to your mobile phone via infrared. In Nokia communicators, cellular communication was built-in.


This particular series of Nokia smartphones was born before the advent of smartphones. And it developed in its own way, first working on a highly modified clone of MS-DOS, then on Symbian, but its own special series, known as Series 80. Based on this combination of OS and interface, two generations of communicators were released: Nokia 9210 in 2000, Nokia 9500 and 9300 in 2004. The main innovation in the Nokia E90 model was the rejection of this unique interface, which goes back to Psion's 32-bit PDA. Instead, they used the popular UI Series 60, also installed in many of the company's regular smartphones. What did it give? First of all, compatibility with software. Secondly, we finally managed to make friends the external part of the phone (small screen, numeric keypad) and the internal (large screen, full keyboard layout).Before the E90, the ‚Äúcomputer‚Äù and ‚Äúmobile‚Äù parts of communicators each lived their own lives a bit. It was impossible to run the application on the big screen, and then switch with it to a small external one. In 2007, it became possible. Almost.


We can say that modern smartphones have their pedigree from the PDA. Nokia communicators are closer to wearable computers, as well as laptops. Dead End Branch of Evolution? Maybe yes. This did not stop Nokia communicators from being quite popular among a small number of loyal fans.

Flashing and jailbreak


In general, I wanted to implement the scenario "in the yard in 2007, I bought a new smartphone, and am exploring its capabilities." Read the instructions, deal with the menu, set up mail. To do without a smartphone hardcore with ‚Äúroot‚Äù, ‚Äúfirmware‚Äù, ‚Äújailbreak‚Äù, ‚Äúcodecs‚Äù and the like. With the old device, it is difficult to completely avoid shamanism. At a minimum, two things had to be done. I would have had to reflash the phone 13 years ago, since I have a copy from Europe. Accordingly, there are no Russian letters on the keyboard, and the Russian language in the firmware.


The language problem in mobile devices has four difficulty levels. First: the language is installed in the OS, which is wired in ROM and not updated. An example is Psion 3a, a keyboard PDA from the early nineties. The second option: the Russian language (or another language you need) is not supported by the manufacturer, but can be implemented using additional software - crack. Example: early versions of Windows Mobile, Nokia 9210 smartphones, Psion 5MX, the first Apple iPhone. Third level: the language is supported by the manufacturer, but is not always present in the regional firmware. This is our case: my copy of the E90 supports English, Dutch, Romanian and Bulgarian. But not Russian. The fourth level, modern, and the simplest: buy a phone in any country in the world, and put any language you want.


I will not try to give instructions for flashing here. Even now, this is a special voodoo magic, secret shamanism available to the elect. With such rituals as obtaining utility utilities from the manufacturer or self-made, searching for the firmware itself, often - converting the firmware to the desired form. Search for a working cable, struggle with old software that does not want to take off on a 64-bit OS. Instructions for the firmware (in three different ways) are on the Allnokia website, and this resource is generally very useful for researching the recent computer past. One of three methods worked. The peculiarity of Nokia firmware is the need to change the product ID, which just sets the phone‚Äôs affiliation to a specific country. There are many opportunities to run into inexplicable glitches in this process. In my case, for some reason, the Bluetooth headset stopped working and the punctuation buttons worked according to an obscure algorithm.


The next mandatory step is to wean the smartphone on Symbian from checking digital signatures of the software, otherwise almost nothing will be installed on the smartphone. This procedure, introduced in 2005, pursued a good goal, the same as that provided by modern appors (with varying degrees of success): to provide the user with software only from trusted sources. Malicious programs for Symbian existed in numbers. There was a reason for security. Another thing is that at that time there was no centralized application store (Nokia made this attempt a bit later). The signature procedure was bureaucratic, and some developers simply could not or did not want to go through it. In 2010, I applied a partial solution to the problem: signing applications with my own certificate attached to the device‚Äôs IMEI. It seems he got itfor little money on some dubious Chinese resource.


The w3bsit3-dns.com forum describes a method of radically getting rid of a certificate problem, a process that is now called a "jailbreak". The system files are overwritten, which the user should not have access to. Interestingly, in 2020 there is a problem even with legitimate applications signed with a developer certificate. The certificate has expired (for example, in one of the cases, in 2018), and, naturally, no one has renewed it, Symbian is no longer supported. It is solved in the old-fashioned way - by translating the date several years ago.

Certificates, web and mail


Another thread on the w3bsit3-dns.com forum is dedicated to solving senile problems with access to any network resources. The set of certificates sewn into the latest firmware of Nokia E90 (2009) is hopelessly outdated. More or less modern cryptographic algorithms, such as SHA-2, are not supported. Lifehack adds SHA-2 support to the OS and installs current certificates. This allows you to browse websites, but not all (Habr, for example, does not open), and not always (there is not enough RAM to display monstrous web pages).



But it was possible with the help of a regular client to access email.

A moment between past and future


Nokia E90 in 2020 may well become that very reliable ‚Äúold Nokia‚Äù, although it is too complicated for such an honorable task. It can, with great reservations, be put to business. Such a retroexperience shows well which protocols and standards were the most durable. These are, of course, open standards. SSH for remote access, RSS for receiving updates from the web (it works, although the websites themselves do not always open), XMPP for instant messages, IMAP for mail. All this works on the E90 without problems, there are no unicode problems typical of earlier devices, and a small dopilivanie even gives support for modern encrypted data transfer methods. You can still install Nokia PC Suite proprietary software on a modern PC, and it will even work. How good was he in 2007, in the context of that digital reality? Here I have some doubts. This is the most compact Nokia communicator, but the dimensions have been reduced, including due to the size of the keys and the depth of travel. The keyboard is full, even in the Russian layout only a couple of letters are out of place. But tough, which makes typing difficult when the phone is on the table. When you hold the device in your hands, another problem arises - it is too wide, it is not easy to reach with the thumbs to the buttons in the center.


You can get used to everything, and the Nokia E90's captivating is not the perfect keyboard (it's not perfect), and not the power (not the maximum possible even by the standards of 2007). The reason for the love of the E90 is the numerous small interface amenities. For example, you can answer a call in a closed form. Then it is enough to open the device, and it will turn on the speakerphone on its own. The interface of the main applications for real affairs (calendar, messages, mail) is thought out and convenient. Switching between tasks is easy. This is a wonderful phone, in which there is always the opportunity to answer a message in detail, to see the attachment sent by mail on a large and moderately convenient screen.


This phone is a bit similar in design to a modern attempt to make a smartphone of a ‚Äústrange‚Äù design.
