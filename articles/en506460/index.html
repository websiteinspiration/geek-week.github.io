Differences of the male brain from the female: women code better?


In the wake of total tolerance, it has become indecent to say that the male and female brains function and work differently. In the same way as judging the ability to program, based on gender.

However, brain research has proven two things: the male and female brains actually function with a significant difference, and judging by a study by the California State Polytechnic University, women on average code better than men.

Let's figure out what the difference is and whether the researchers tried to tighten the data.

How is the male brain different from the female


Research that is indecent


In 1998, a fresh Ph.D. from California Institute of Technology Niaro Shah decided to study gender-based brain differences.

At that time, this was not a very popular idea - the neurobiological community was inclined to believe that the whole difference in human behavior is due to cultural characteristics. Scientists who study animals rarely took the brain of female rodents for studies of the brain, fearing that cyclical changes in their reproductive hormones would interfere with the cards and influence the findings.

Researchers who tried to prove the opposite were accused of ‚Äúneurosexism‚Äù: they say they simply fell victim to stereotypes or draw hasty conclusions that men differ from women more biologically than culturally.

Researchers contrasted that data from animal studies, cross-cultural studies, natural experiments and brain research showed real, if not enormous differences in brain structure and that these differences could contribute to differences in behavior and consciousness.

But in the next 15 years, the situation changed dramatically: new technologies appeared that brought a ton of irrefutable evidence of physiologically determined differences in how the male and female brains are arranged and work.

It is worth emphasizing that the question is not how well the brain works: it does not follow from the existing difference that someone is smarter or more worthy due to their gender. The thing is rather how .

In 1991, just a few years before the Shah began her research, Diana Halpern, PhD and former president of the American Psychological Association, began writing the first edition of her famous academic work, Sex Differences in Cognitive Abilities.

She noted that animal research papers have steadily generated reports of differences in gender-related neuroanatomical and behavioral differences, but most of these books mostly collected dust in university libraries. Social and ordinary psychologists have smashed the principle of any fundamental cognitive differences between men and women.

In the preface to the first edition of his book, Halpern writes: ‚ÄúAt that time, it seemed obvious to me that any differences in thought processes between a man and a woman were the result of socialization, cultural values ‚Äã‚Äãand errors in research, prejudice and prejudice. ... After reading kilometers of articles in scientific journals and many books ... my opinion has changed. ‚Äù

Findings from animal studies resonate with the fact that the difference based on gender is very similar to that demonstrated by Homo Sapiens.

For example, in a study in which 34 macaques took part, males preferred cars with wheels, while females liked teddy bears. It‚Äôs hard to argue that parents of macaques bought the wrong toys and monkey society encourages boys to play with trucks.

A very recent study found that boys and girls from 9 to 17 months - the age when children show little sign of awareness of their gender or the gender of other children - nevertheless showed significant differences in preferences in stereotyped boy and girl toys.

Halper and Shah began to catalog many behavioral differences in behavior.

Their studies have shown that in women:

  • Verbal abilities are several times stronger, almost everything except verbal analogies
  • Female ability to perceive and write text on average exceeds male
  • Women overtake men in tests for fine coordination and speed of perception
  • They are more adept at extracting information from long-term memory.

At the same time, men:

  • On average, they can remember faster
  • They have a much stronger ability for visual-spatial orientation: they better visualize what will happen if a complex two or three-dimensional object rotates in space
  • More precisely determine how many degrees the angles
  • Better follow a moving object and throw a projectile at a target

This echoes the behavior that rats demonstrate: comparative studies of humans and rats showed that females tend to rely on pointers, while males usually rely more on the ‚Äúnavigation method‚Äù: they calculate the position of an object, evaluating the direction and distance traveled than look at the signs.

Amazing how different our brains are.


Neurobiology has proven that the human brain is an organ different in different sexes with clear anatomical differences in neural structures and accompanied by psychological differences in functions, says Dr. Larry Cahill, professor of neurobiology and behavior at the California Institute. Cahill published his 70th article in January-February 2017 in the journal Journal of Neuroscience Research  , the first topic in a neuroscience journal devoted entirely to the effect of sex on the nervous system.

Studies with brain imaging have determined that these differences go far beyond the reproductive sphere, Cahill writes. By making adjustments for brain size (the male is larger), the female hippocampus, a critical department for learning and remembering, is larger than the male and works differently. On the other hand, the tonsil associated with experiencing emotions and remembering emotional experience is more common in men. And it also works differently, as Cahill's research proved.

In 2000, Cahill scanned the brains of men and women who watched different videos: both very repulsive and emotionally neutral. Unpleasant videos were expected to trigger strong negative emotions and therefore are imprinted in the amygdala, a structure reminiscent of an almond seed in the cerebral hemisphere. Tonsil activity during viewing, as expected, predicted the subject‚Äôs ability to recall watched clips. But for women, this connection was observed only in the left tonsil , for men - only in the right .

Such discoveries should be an alarming bell for researchers.

If, as it is likely, the amygdala is related to depression and anxiety, any attempt in depression studies to analyze only the male or female brain in order to understand their vulnerability to these syndromes is doomed to failure due to a simple misunderstanding of where left and right .

Female brain: more powerful coordination between the hemispheres


Two hemispheres of a woman‚Äôs brain speak to each other much more than a man‚Äôs. In a 2014 study, researchers at the University of Pennsylvania observed the brains of 428 young men and 521 young women ‚Äî an unusually large sample ‚Äî and found that women‚Äôs brains regularly showed more powerful coordination between the hemispheres, while the male brain was more closely coordinated with local regions of the brain. This conclusion is a confirmation of the observation that the corpus callosum - crosses and binds the hemisphere more in women and the female brain, as a rule, is more symmetrical than the male one.

Many of these cognitive differences appear rather early. You can observe the difference based on gender in the ability to spatial visualization already in 2-3 month old children.
To a significant extent, this difference in the brain should draw a behavioral difference, says Cahill. Many studies show that it is, sometimes with significant medical consequences.

Boys are more prone to autism


In a 2017 study, JAMA Psychiatry magazine examined the brains of 98 people with autism spectrum disorders from 8 to 22 years old. Both groups consisted of the same number of female and male subjects. The result confirmed that the pattern of differences in the thickness of the cerebral cortex differed between men and women. But most women with autism had a cerebral cortex thickness close to that of a healthy boy. 

In other words, a typically male brain structure, whether you are a boy or a girl, is a significant risk of autism. By definition, this means that more boys' brains have a similar structure and this helps explain the four to five-fold predominance of boys with this disorder than girls.

The effect of hormones on brain development


But why are the male and female brains different? There is another reason: throughout life, fuel with very different additives flows through the brain of men and women: sex steroid hormones. In female mammals, the main additives are a few representatives of a number of molecules called estrogens, along with another molecule called progesterone; males have testosterone and some similar androgens.

It is important that the male fetus that normally develops in the uterus receives large strokes from the burst of testosterone, which constantly forms not only parts of its body and proportion, but also the brain. Genetic defects that interfere with the influence of testosterone on the development of the boy‚Äôs cells also make his body more feminine - femininity is our ‚Äúbasic human equipment‚Äù.

In general, parts of the brain that differ in size in men and women (tonsils and hypothalamus) tend to have particularly high concentrations of sex hormone receptors.

Another key variable stems from sex chromosomes, which are just one pair of 23 pairs of human chromosomes in each cell. Women have 2 X chromosomes in pairs, while men have one X and one Y. A gene with a Y chromosome is responsible for a cascade of events leading to the development of the brain and body to male characteristics. Some other genes on the Y chromosome can also affect psychology and consciousness.

We regularly see how the presence or absence of a single DNA base pair makes a huge difference.

The difference in brain structure and field-based psychology reflects the alchemy of these hormone-receptor interactions, their effect within cells, and the mediating effect of genetic variables ‚Äî especially the possession of genotype XX against XY, says Cahill.

Now let's start the fight: women program better than men


At least on Github there is evidence of discrimination against them in the open source community.

Researchers from the California State Polytechnic University drove out about 3 million request pools sent to Github and found out that code written by women was accepted more often (78.6%) than code written by men (74.6%).

Trying to find an explanation for such a gap, scientists investigated several facts, including whether women make tiny, minor changes in the code (it turned out that they don‚Äôt) or maybe women surpass men in mastery only in certain programming languages ‚Äã‚Äã(it turned out that there isn‚Äôt either )

The conclusion was unequivocal - the acceptance rate for code written by women was higher in each of the 10 popular programming languages.

Researchers then speculated that women leave for reverse discrimination - the desire of developers to encourage women to make contributions to areas where they are an absolute minority. To test this, the authors divided accounts that were clearly women-owned and gender-neutral.

And they made an ugly discovery: the code written by women was adopted more often in cases where the gender of the programmer could not be determined. When the code was explicitly written by a girl, the acceptance level was on average lower than that of a code written by a man.

Interviews with several women developers who use Github showed a complex picture of how girls pave the way in the open source world, taking into account gender discrimination.

Laura Jane Mitchell, a developer whose work goes almost entirely on Github, says it‚Äôs impossible to tell if her request pool is ignored because she is a girl or simply because the project owner is busy or knows another IRL developer. Her Github account clearly shows that she is a woman and she will not change it because of the results of the study.
I understand that sometimes this can interfere, but it is important for me that it is clear from my account that I am a woman. I want people to understand that minorities exist. It is important for other development girls to see that they are not the only ones.

Another developer, Isabelle Drost-Fromm, who has a female cartoon character on GitHub's avatar, claims to have never encountered discrimination while working on Github, but she uses it to work on projects with a team that knows her personally and the way she programs.

Jenny Brian, a professor of statistics at the University of British Columbia, uses Github in teaching and as an R-developer.
Men who do not know me sometimes begin to explain things to me that I understand much better than they do. But the men with whom I communicate in the R-community on Github know me and if my gender somehow influences our communication, then I rather feel support in my work and training.

Conclusions: more questions than answers


Is it true that women code better? Could it be that due to the attitudes of society into the profession, only those who really have talent, perseverance and ability make their way?

Even if the brains of men and women are significantly different, does this affect the ability to learn technical sciences?

Be that as it may, support your close girls in their quest to become good programmers - and see how the situation will change in 30-40 years and time will put everything in its place.
