A bit of history


It makes sense to count the history of Wi-Fi since 1971, when Professor Norman Abramson, with a group of colleagues at the University of Hawaii, developed, built and launched the ALOHAnet wireless packet data network.

In 1980, a group of IEEE 802 standards and protocols was approved that describe the organization of the lower two layers of the OSI seven-layer network model. Prior to the release of the first version of 802.11, one had to wait a long 17 years.

With the adoption of the 802.11 standard in 1997, two years before the advent of the Wi-Fi Alliance, the first generation of today's most popular wireless data technology has stepped into the big world.



IEEE 802 standard. Generations of Wi-Fi


The first standard truly massively supported by equipment manufacturers was 802.11b. As you can see, the frequency of innovations since the end of the 20th century has been quite stable: time-consuming changes are necessary. In recent years, the main work has been done to improve the physical environment of signal transmission. In order to better understand the current issues of Wi-Fi, we turn to its physical foundations.



Recall the basics!


Radio waves are a special case of electromagnetic waves - propagating from a source of perturbations of the electric and magnetic fields. They are characterized by three main parameters: the wave vector, as well as the electric and magnetic field intensity vectors. All three are mutually perpendicular to each other. The wave frequency in this case is called the number of repetitive vibrations that fit into a unit of time.

All these are well-known facts. However, to reach the end, we are forced to start from the very beginning.



On the conditional scale of the frequency ranges of electromagnetic radiation, the radio range occupies the lowest (low-frequency) part. It includes electromagnetic waves with an oscillation frequency from 3 Hz to 3000 GHz. All other ranges, including visible light, have a much higher frequency.



The higher the frequency, the more energy can be reported to the radio wave, however, at the same time, it goes around obstacles worse and damps faster. The converse is also true. Given these features, two main frequency ranges were selected for Wi-Fi operation - 2.4 GHz (frequency band from 2.4000 to 2.4835 GHz) and 5 GHz (frequency bands 5.170-5.330, 5.490-5.730 and 5.735-5.835 GHz).



Radio waves propagate in all directions, and in order to prevent messages from affecting each other due to the interference effect, it is customary to divide the frequency band into separate narrow segments - channels with one or another bandwidth . The diagram above shows that adjacent channels 1 and 2 with a bandwidth of 20 MHz will interfere with each other, and 1 and 6 will not.

The signal inside the channel is transmitted using a radio wave at a specific carrier frequency. To transmit information, the wave parameters can be modulated in frequency, amplitude or phase.



Channel Separation in Wi-Fi Frequency Bands


The frequency range of 2.4 GHz is divided into 14 partially overlapping channels of optimal width - 20 MHz. It was once believed that this was enough to organize a complex wireless network. It soon became clear that the range capacity was rapidly exhausting, so a 5 GHz range was added to it, the spectral capacity of which is much higher. In it, in addition to 20 MHz, it is possible to allocate channels with a width of 40 and 80 MHz.



To further improve the efficiency of the use of the radio frequency spectrum, orthogonal frequency division multiplexing ( OFDM ) is currently widely used .

It implies the use of several subcarrier frequencies along with the carrier frequency in the same channel, which makes it possible to carry out parallel data transmission. OFDM allows you to distribute traffic in a rather convenient "granular" way, but due to its respectable age, it retains a number of significant disadvantages. Among them, the principles of operation according to the CSMA / CA network protocol (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Avoidance), according to which only one user can work on a single carrier and subcarrier at certain points in time.



Spatial streams


An important way to increase the throughput of a wireless network is to use spatial streams.

An access point carries several radio modules (one, two or more) that are connected to a number of antennas. These antennas emit in a certain pattern and modulation, and you and I receive information transmitted over a wireless environment. The spatial stream can be formed between a specific physical antenna (radio module) of the access point and the user device. Due to this, the total amount of information transmitted from the access point increases by a multiple of the number of streams (antennas).

According to current standards, up to four spatial streams can be implemented in the 2.4 GHz band, up to eight in the 5 GHz band.



Previously, when working in the 2.4 and 5 GHz bands, we focused only on the number of radio modules. The presence of a second radio module provided additional flexibility, as it allowed old subscriber devices to operate at a frequency of 2.4 GHz, and new ones at a frequency of 5 GHz. With the advent of the third and subsequent radio modules, some problems arose. Emitting elements tend to create tips on each other, which increases the cost of the device due to the need for better design and equipping the access point with compensation filters. So only recently it became possible to simultaneously maintain 16 spatial streams per access point.



Practical and theoretical speed


Due to the OFDM working mechanisms, we could not get the maximum network bandwidth. Theoretical calculations for the practical implementation of OFDM have been carried out for a very long time and only in relation to ideal environments where a fairly high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and bit error probability (BER) were predictably expected. Under the current conditions of high noisiness of all the radio frequency spectra we are interested in, the throughput indicators of OFDM-based networks are depressingly small. And until recently, the protocol continued to carry these shortcomings, until OFDMA (orthogonal frequency-division multiple access) technology came to the rescue. About her - a little further.

Let's talk about antennas





As you know, each antenna has a gain, depending on the value of which a spatial pattern of signal propagation (beamforming) with a certain coverage area is formed (we do not take into account the re-reflection of signals, etc.). This is exactly what the designers ‚Äôreasoning has always been based on exactly where the access points should be located. For a long time, the shape of the pattern remained unchanged and only increased or decreased in proportion to the characteristics of the antenna.



Modern antenna elements are becoming more manageable and allow you to dynamically change the spatial pattern of signal propagation in real time.

At the top left, the principle of propagation of radio waves using a standard omnidirectional antenna is shown. By increasing the signal power, we could only change the coverage radius in the absence of the ability to significantly affect the quality of channel use - KQI (Key Quality Indicators). And this indicator is extremely important when organizing communication in the conditions of frequent movement of a subscriber device in a wireless environment.

The solution to the problem was the use of a large number of small antennas, the load on which can be adjusted in real time, forming propagation patterns depending on the spatial position of the user.



Thus, we managed to come close to the application of MU-MIMO technology (Multi-User Multiple Input, Multiple Output). With its help, the access point at any time generates radiation flows directed specifically towards the subscriber devices.

From physics to 802.11 standards





With the development of Wi-Fi standards, the principles of working with the physical layer of the network have changed. Using other modulation mechanisms made it possible - starting with versions of 802.11g / n - to contain much more information in a time slot and, accordingly, to work with a large number of users. Among other things, this was achieved through the use of spatial streams. And the newfound flexibility in terms of channel width has enabled the formation of more resources for MIMO.

Wi-Fi 7 is slated for adoption next year. What will change with its arrival? In addition to the usual increase in speed and the addition of the 6 GHz range, it will be possible to work with wide aggregated channels, such as 320 MHz. This is especially interesting in the context of industrial applications.



Theoretical Wi-Fi Bandwidth 6


The theoretical formula for calculating the Wi-Fi 6 nominal speed is quite complicated and depends on many parameters, starting with the number of spatial streams and ending with the information that we can put in the subcarrier (or subcarriers, if there are several of them) per unit time.

As you can see, a lot depends on spatial flows. But before, an increase in their number in combination with the use of STC (Space-Time Coding) and MRC (Maximum Ratio Combining) worsened the work of the wireless solution as a whole.



New key physical layer technologies


Let's move on to the key technologies of the physical layer - and start from the first level of the OSI network model.



Recall that OFDM uses a certain number of subcarriers that, without affecting each other, are capable of transmitting a certain amount of information.



In the example, we use the 5.220 GHz band, which contains 48 subchannels. By aggregating this channel, we get a larger number of subcarriers, each of which uses its own modulation scheme.



Wi-Fi 5 uses 256 QAM (Quadrature Amplitude Modulation) quadrature modulation, which allows you to form a field of 16 x 16 dots in the same frequency slot in the same time slot, which differ in amplitude and phase. The inconvenience is that only one station can transmit on a carrier frequency at any one time.



Orthogonal frequency division multiplexing (OFDMA) came from the world of mobile operators, gained distribution simultaneously with LTE and is used to organize downlink (communication channel to the subscriber). It allows you to work with the channel at the level of the so-called resource units. These units help to break the block into a certain number of components. Within the block, we can at every moment not work strictly with one radiating element (user or access point), but combine dozens of elements. This allows you to achieve remarkable results.





Easy connection of channels in Wi-Fi 6


Channel Bonding in Wi-Fi 6 allows you to receive combined channels with a width of 20 to 160 MHz. Moreover, the connection does not have to be done in nearby ranges. For example, one block can be taken from the 5.17 GHz band, and the second from the 5.135 GHz band. This allows you to flexibly build a radio environment even in the presence of strong interference factors or in the vicinity of other constantly emitting stations.



From SIMO to MIMO


The MIMO method has not always been with us. Once mobile communications had to be limited to the SIMO mode, which implied that the subscriber station had several antennas that simultaneously received information.



MU-MIMO is designed to transmit information to users using the entire current antenna pool. This removes the limitations previously imposed by the CSMA / CA protocol related to sending tokens to transmission devices. Now users are united in a group and each member of the group receives its part of the resource of the antenna fund of the access point, rather than waiting in line.



Beam Formation


An important rule of MU-MIMO is to maintain a mode of operation of the antenna fund, which would not lead to mutual overlap of radio waves and loss of information due to phase addition.

This requires complex mathematical calculations on the access point side. If the terminal supports this function, MU-MIMO allows it to inform the access point with what delay it receives a signal for each specific antenna. And the access point, in turn, adjusts its antennas to form an optimally directed beam.



What does this give us as a whole?

The main advantages that new OFDMA-enabled solutions bring are related to resource units implemented at a level similar to TDM (Time Division Multiplexing). Previously, this was not in Wi-Fi. This allows you to clearly control the allocated band, providing the minimum signal transmission time through the medium and the required level of reliability. Fortunately, no one doubts that Wi-Fi reliability indicators need to be improved.

History moves in a spiral, and the current situation is similar to the one that developed around Ethernet in its time. Already then, the view was established that the CSMA / CD (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Detection) transmission medium did not provide any guaranteed bandwidth. And so it continued until the transition to IEEE 802.3z.

As for the general application models, as you can see, with each generation of Wi-Fi, the scenarios of its use are multiplying, more and more sensitive to delays, general jitter and reliability.



And again about the physical environment


Well, now about how the new physical environment is being formed. When using CSMA / CA and OFDM, an increase in the number of active points (Active STA) led to a serious drop in the bandwidth of the 20 MHz channel. This was due to what was already mentioned: with not the latest technologies STC (Space-Time Coding) and MRC (Maximum Ratio Combining).



OFDMA through the use of resource units can effectively interact with distant and low-power stations. We get the opportunity to work in the same carrier range with users consuming different amounts of resources. One user can occupy one unit, and another - all the rest.



Why there was no OFDMA before?


And finally, the main question: why there was no OFDMA before? Oddly enough, everything rested on money.

For a long time it was believed that the price of a Wi-Fi module should be minimal. When the protocol was launched into commercial operation in 1997, it was decided that the cost of production of such a module could not exceed $ 1. As a result, the development of technology has gone the non-optimal way. Here we do not take into account carrier LTE, where OFDMA has been used for quite some time.

In the end, the Wi-Fi working group decided to take these developments from the world of telecom operators and transfer them to the world of corporate networks. The main objective was the transition to the use of better elements, such as filters and oscillators.



Why was it so difficult for us to work in old MRC encodings with or without interference? Because the MVDR (Minimum Variance Distortionless Response) beamforming mechanism dramatically increased the number of errors as soon as we tried to combine a large number of transmission points. OFDMA has proven that the problem is solvable.



The fight against interference is now based on mathematics. If the information transfer window is long enough, the resulting dynamic interference leads to problems. New work algorithms allow you to get away from them, excluding the influence of not only interference associated with Wi-Fi transmission, but also any other that occurs in this range.



Thanks to the adaptive fight against interference, we can get a gain of up to 11 dB even in a complex heterogeneous environment. Using Huawei's own algorithmic solutions allowed us to achieve serious optimization exactly where needed - in indoor solutions. What is good in 5G is not necessarily good in Wi-Fi 6. The approaches of Massive MIMO and MU-MIMO differ in the case of indoor and outdoor solutions. Where required, it is appropriate to use expensive solutions, like in 5G. But other options are needed, such as Wi-Fi 6, which can provide delays and other indicators that we are used to expect from telecom operators.

We borrow from them those tools that will be useful for us, as for corporate consumers, and all this in order to provide a physical environment that you can rely on.
