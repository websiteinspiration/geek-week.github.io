Construction is dying out. Covid-19 crisis and growing challenges for the construction industry

The construction industry is huge, “sustainable”, as old as the world, and consumes up to 10% of all money used in the world. On average, it can be considered that one in ten able-bodied people in the world is somehow connected with the construction industry. The construction industry is partly the driver of the economy; many internal processes are tied to it. And although the construction sector is more adaptable than it affects the development of our lives, the contribution of this industry to the development of social processes cannot be underestimated.



Since 2000, there has been a steady tendency in the construction industry around the world to reduce the number of people employed in construction, and the 2008 crisis hit the construction sector especially hard and increased the outflow of specialists from the industry.



Where is the construction sector moving now and how many people after the crisis of 2020 will leave the construction industry and change their profession?



The crisis of the construction industry in Europe



The construction industry remains one of the most important sectors of the economy in Europe, representing about 10% of GDP and 50.5% of gross fixed capital formation, providing 20 million direct jobs in the EU alone.



The 2008 crisis hit Europe's construction industry hard. Greece suffered the most and lost 80% of its construction industry between 2010 and 2013. In other EU countries, a sudden decline in production occurred - from -54.4% in Lithuania in 2009 to almost stable activity levels in Germany and Austria.

Crises smooth out the surge in activity in the pre-crisis years.





Until 2007, construction volumes in the EU grew every year. However, the market showed a sharp decline after 2008. The euro crisis and the subsequent global financial and economic crisis had a great impact on the construction industry in many European countries and in the post-crisis period, the number of people employed in construction fell by 15-30%:



  • In Europe alone , about 4,000,000 people “left” construction (from the peak in 2007 to the “bottom” in 2013)
  • from 2014 to 2018, 70% of these people returned to the construction industry


The population of Europe over the past 20 years has grown by 15 million people and at the same time, about 5 million people have left the construction industry.







In the absence of productivity growth in the construction sector, and population growth, the outflow of workers from construction is more likely to indicate not problems but an increase in the number of offers in related industries.

Where do builders go? Does this mean that new, more attractive value-added industries have emerged?



US Construction Sector Crisis



A similar downward trend is observed in North America, where employment peaked in 2000. But unlike Europe, the long-term trend here until 2018 had a continuous tendency to a slight increase.



In 2006, at the peak of the last housing boom, 7.7 million people worked in the industry. As of July 2018, construction employment in the United States reached nearly 7.2 million people (a total of 480,000 less (6%) less than in the previous peak period of 2007).







And now, the long-term growth trend in the US construction sector is clearly on the plateau.


As in Europe, after the crisis of 2020, in the United States alone, about 2-3 million people working in construction will have to “change" jobs.



Home buyers in developed countries (where the loan rate is less than 3%) are hesitant to invest in real estate (new home), due to expectations that the market will slide into recession and interest rates on mortgage debt will increase too much. And since since 2008, mortgage rates in many developed countries have come close to zero, and the governments of these countries have chosen the entire margin for easing monetary policy, it can be expected that the upcoming decline in construction will be no less than after the 2008 crisis. A similar trigger (an increase in the interest rate in floating rate agreements) provoked the collapse of the bubble of mortgage loans in 2008 in the United States, triggering the onset of the global financial crisis.

The crisis may not significantly affect the construction market in developing countries, where many medium and small companies will leave the market in a short time or be absorbed by the heavyweights of the market. Mortgage rates, unlike developed countries, have much to reduce here - consumer demand is likely to be stimulated, and the bottom will be reached faster than what happens in developed countries.

The crisis will affect all countries equally, but in the United States, such mortgage crises are faster because the floating rate forces consumers who are at risk of bankruptcy to sell their property in the short term. European countries, being hedged by a fixed rate, stretch the consequences of a possible mortgage crisis for 10 years.



On average, in developed countries, construction currently accounts for 5-10% of the country's total GDP and about 5% of all European workers are employed in the construction sector. In developing countries, the share of the construction sector in the country's GDP is about two times higher than in developed countries, and is at the level of 10-20% of GDP. On average, it can be considered that one in ten able-bodied people in the world is somehow connected with the construction industry (the construction industry uses low-paid and less skilled workers, and thus is the main employer worldwide for labor migrants).







If we project the situation on the construction market, which will probably unfold in Europe after 2021, to the rest of the world, we can assume that approximately 20% of all people working in construction today, starting in 2021, will lose their jobs and move to other industries (possibly temporarily).

The construction industry will suffer more from COVID-19 than after the 2008 crisis



Since the beginning of autumn 2020, we can expect a recession in the construction industry, which will probably hit harder in the economy than the 2008 crisis. With the preservation of permanent quarantines and the development of infrastructure for remote work, the demand for office premises, the supply of apartments near these offices, commercial space and offline shopping centers in cities will decrease.



Already now you can work from any convenient place and sell / buy any goods from anywhere in the world where the Internet catches. In this crisis, new companies and start-ups will begin to grow, which will lead us to a new level of consumption and the possibility of making money before the next big crisis.



We will need fast Internet access and a warm bungalow for the night. And in the cities there will remain families with children and elderly people who need the infrastructure of relatives, education and leisure centers.







The crisis exposes the problems of the construction industry



Even before COVID-19, construction industry performance was low compared to other sectors of the economy. In terms of digitalization, only comrades working in the agricultural industry are behind the builders, and therefore the construction industry has nowhere to retreat.



1. Stagnant productivity, low data digitization and low profitability (2-10% in developed countries, and 15-30% in developing countries) have plagued the industry for many years. A highly individual approach to construction and a fragmented ecosystem only adds problems to the treasury of factors that impede the development of the entire sector.

Over the past decades, the amount of data used in planning has grown like an avalanche, while the incoming data has not only not been processed, but subsequently rarely systematized, lost forever on local drives. Unfortunately, the construction project cannot be compared with an average IT startup. The construction industry is much more complicated and confusing than the banking sector (100x) or autonomous cars (10x), so the use of big data and machine learning in project planning, which could give a new impetus to the industry, is still a long way off.

The construction industry lags behind other sectors - because it is large and heterogeneous and it will take many more years to catch up with the level of automation that has already been achieved, for example, by mechanical engineering.



3. One of the main problems compared to other industries that the construction industry is facing is the low level of productivity. At the same time, 78% of engineering and construction companies believe that the risks in the design of new projects have increased several times.







During the crisis - the construction industry is waiting for the long-awaited (difficult) update



The use of big data to systematize the incoming information and machine learning to predict the cost and time parameters in construction has not yet reached the development departments, which are completely immersed in the implementation of BIM tools and solving current problems between the growing number of contractors involved in construction.



Large construction companies that in the near future will not use BIM tools and automation tools (BIM in construction has demonstrated the ability to save up to 20% of project costs) in their work will be forced to leave the market in this crisis.



And companies that after the crisis will not think about using big data, data analysis and machine learning in their work,risk leaving the market during the next crisis.



Big Data and machine learning must reach every construction company before we wait for a new big crisis.
