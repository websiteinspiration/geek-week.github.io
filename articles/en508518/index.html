Migrating to IPv6 May Take Another Ten Years

This view was expressed by the representative of the RIPE NCC Internet registrar, Marco Hogewoning. However, some members of the IT community found this forecast overly optimistic. We discuss the opinions of experts and tell how the implementation of IPv6 is going.

Address Situation



At the end of November last year, the RIPE Internet registrar in charge of Europe and Asia announced the depletion of the last block of IPv4 addresses. Five years ago, a similar situation occurred with the American ARIN . Other registrars also share their latest resources.



Many experts - for example, Darien Graham-Smith, an IT journalist and editor of PC Pro - have urged to make the transition to IPv6 as soon as possible in order to forget about the problem of a lack of addresses for at least a million years.


How long can migration take



The new generation protocol is still not widely distributed, despite the fact that more than eight years have passed since its global launch . According to W3Techs, a year ago , 14.3% of sites used IPv6 , today this figure has increased by only 2%.

But a number of experts are convinced that soon the situation will begin to change. For example, according to Marco Hohevoning of RIPE NCC, this procedure will take about 5-10 more years. He is convinced that this time is enough to get rid of the need for dual-stack.



According to him, government organizations must migrate to IPv6 first - this is one of the factors hindering the development of the protocol and forcing backward compatibility with IPv4. But some consider the timelines set by Marco to be rather optimistic.



Another point of view



Representative of the international organization Internet Society Mat Ford (Mat Ford) and some residents of Hacker News note that one should not expect large-scale migration in the near future. Internet providers work successfully even in the context of IPv4 shortage - for example, they buy vacated addresses at specialized sites .





/ Unsplash / Markus Spiske



Migration is also hindered by technical difficulties, especially in large networks. For example, SIE Worldwide Studios, a game development company, has been implementing IPv6 for seven years. But in the end, abandoned the project, as they were not able to completely get rid of the legacy of IPv4.



Another reason slowing down the transition to the new protocol is that engineers still find bugs related to IPv6. For example, at the end of 2019, one of the Australian network providers had to stop testing IPv6. The reason is a bug in the firmware of the Cisco ASR routers, which caused the DHCP process to fail. To fix the problem, I had to reboot the router, which affected not only IPv6 subscribers, but everyone else.



It turns out that simply expanding the address pool is not enough to quickly switch to a new generation protocol - at least you should not expect this in the next decade.
