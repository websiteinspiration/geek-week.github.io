Usability Testing A to Z: A Detailed Guide

The continuous process of receiving and processing user feedback, as well as the timely response to it, is the key to the success of the project. Such a process is necessary in any scenario: whether it is developing software from scratch or improving an existing one.



I would like to talk about how to effectively plan the usability testing process and get high-quality feedback. This material affects the activities of UX designers and will be useful for product owners, product and project managers, as well as anyone closely associated with software product development.



Usability testing is a method of evaluating an interface in terms of convenience and effectiveness of its use. To get it, you need to attract representatives of the target audience of the software product.



Usability testing is often carried out in two stages: the user's passing the Nth number of tasks (quantitative or qualitative tests), as well as talking, filling out questionnaires or in-depth interviews with the user (qualitative research).



As a rule, it should be carried out when the interface is already formed in the form of a paper or digital prototype or a finished software product, and you want to understand what problems arise in the work and how much the product meets the expectations of users.



The testing process takes on average from a month to three. It depends on many factors: the availability of different types of product, the complexity of the scenarios and the availability of their alternatives, the number of user roles.



In carrying out usability testing, we distinguish the following stages:



  • goal definition;
  • creating a test plan;
  • determination of the number of researchers;
  • determination of the target audience;
  • obtaining user consent;
  • organization of access;
  • observation;
  • analysis.

Every study should have a goal. You must understand exactly what information you want to receive. It is important for yourself to answer the following questions:



  • What will be important for you in the study? What user roles or their specific tasks do you want to test?

If there are several products, such as a mobile web and desktop application, and you want to test each of them, then it is better to do this at different times. So get more accurate reviews and avoid mixed feelings from testing all products at once.



For observation, I usually prepare a document for each user - a road map that tells what to do and how to answer what questions. The document is called UX testing plan & script and consists of the following elements:



  • introduction;
  • immersion of the user in the context of tasks;
  • description of tasks and a list of steps with the ability to enter comments on each of them;
  • a list of questions for each task;
  • final round of questions.


Do not try to identify all usability problems in a large product at once. It is much more efficient to break up the scenarios into separate small studies by roles with a specific purpose in each of them.



Usually during the formation of tasks I spend their decomposition. What it is? I replace the solution of one problem with a series of small simple and interconnected subtasks with a specific purpose in each.



Let's look at an example. The scenario of the task ‚ÄúAs a user I can book a hotel I like‚Äù actually consists of a number of small subtasks:



  • As a user, I can find a hotel. We find out how the user searches for hotels and check the appropriateness of the selected search criteria and its algorithm.
  • As a user I can get information about hotels. We will find out if there is enough information in our data.

Scenarios of tasks should be specific and reflect the real needs of the target audience. Do not ask the consumer to buy meat if he is a vegetarian.

If you conduct quantitative research and measure, for example, the time taken to run the script, the number of errors in one script, the script passed / failed, as well as the level of user satisfaction - all measurements are made exclusively on the production server, with real user load, with real data and working services, with their validation and queries to real databases.



It is worthwhile to pre-run script execution on a test audience to make sure the timing of the session is up to date and the user understands all the instructions. Usually I put in the budget "run-in" scenarios with three trial users.

Often, interface designers are interviewers and unknowingly push their solutions during testing. This is because it is difficult to remain impartial if you are very close to the software product. What to do in such situations?



First of all, you should love the task you are working on, not its solution. Only in this way can you evolve in the design of an interface for a specific task. Next, attract three independent opinions in the person of an interviewer, observer, and moderator, who will help clarify the situation in disputed situations in a totally correct way.



However, it is not always possible to involve three researchers for each testing session. Often the functions listed above are assigned to one interviewer, which makes the research process a difficult test for him.



If you are testing alone, it is best to carry out no more than three tests per day with a maximum session duration of 1.5 hours. If testing goes beyond the specified time, break it into two parts.



Target audience definition



When working on software from a narrow subject area, demographic indicators are usually not enough. It is important to understand the roles and their functions.



For example, you are working on a financial product and plan to study the activities of a broker as part of creating a CLO (collateralized loan obligation) business in the US market. In this case, you need to understand the interaction algorithms of an investment banker with other financial roles.



Preliminary questionnaires will help determine the target audience. They are created both in free services, for example Google Forms, and in paid ones - SurveyMonkey, Typeform and others.



Ask the candidates about their occupation, territorial affiliation, what features of the application they are subscribed to, if there is a subscription at all. Ask how often and why they use the product. If their goals for using the service coincide with the goals of the study, these are your people.



By comparing the test plan and the preliminary questionnaire, you can correctly select potential candidates for the study.



The rules for creating such questionnaires are very simple:



  • Accurately formulate questions. As a rule, people understand the same phrases differently. Using insufficiently accurate formulations, we cannot be sure that the user correctly understood the question.
  • Do not ask respondents what you know in advance. For example, his name, age or position.

You can use dichotomous questions (yes / no answers) to determine which next question the respondent will answer. This is easily done, for example, using Google Forms.







Ask questions with gradation if you want to measure the degree of judgment. Basically, the Likert scale or a sliding scale is used for this. I recommend using the Likert scale, as it is more convenient for the respondent to perceive. Since the answer options are written in numbers on a sliding scale, and the respondent has to determine the value of the ratings independently.







In the case of a sliding scale, options 6‚Äì9 belong to the ‚Äúpartially agree‚Äù category, but there is no way to understand how they differ. It is worth avoiding a large number of answers unnecessarily.



If you want to find out if the user agrees or disagrees with something, use an even number of ratings. If you are also interested in neutrality - use an odd number of ratings.







The selection of users depends on many factors:



  • the probability of an error in a certain percentage of respondents;
  • uniformity in the user sample of each role;
  • the availability of alternative script paths;
  • the complexity of the scripts.


Let's talk about the likelihood of a user having an error. If all users have the same error, then for testing you will need only one user who is 100% likely to find this problem.



Another story is obtained if only half of the participants are faced with a specific problem. Then you need at least three people to identify it. However, it is likely that all three will not have a problem. You will have to continue testing until the user finds a problem.



Today there are calculators for calculating the number of users. They are based on the average probability of error detection in one study. However, the problem is that different researchers get different indicators of this probability. For example, Jacob Nielsen at one timereceived a value equal to p = 0.31, at the same time, studies of Spool and Schroeder lead to a result p = 0.16.



The choice of probability is always a big risk. You can rely on your research and calculate your probability, or use p = 0.31 for simple scenarios and p = 0.16 for complex and diverse scenarios, relying on the research of others.



When analyzing various studies, one indisputable fact follows - the more users you test, the more accurate you will get the results. But what to do when you are limited by the number of end users?



There is still a debate in the world about the minimum number of participants suitable for testing. According to Jacob Nielsen, this is 5 people, and according to Laura Faulkner - 10.



I recommend starting with 5-10 tests of one prototype to test hypotheses in qualitative studies and 10-20 tests for quantitative ones. If errors begin to repeat, this indicates that the sample of users is uniform. It is also allowed to use the RITE iterative change method when edits are made to the interface as problems are discovered. One user is being tested.



If a group of researchers is sure that they understand the essence of the problems discovered, then it makes a decision to correct them or not. If not, she continues to test the current interface until the point is clear. After making changes, testing begins anew. Typically, the RITE approach requires 5-6 iterations.



If we are talking about finding new ideas for developing a product or finding patterns of behavior, then a large sample is extremely necessary.

It is important to write down absolutely all the conditions under which it will be conducted (examples of conditions below). This will be a good insurance for the use of all the data that you will receive in the research process.



I try to make video and audio recordings in my observations. This allows you to accurately capture all the answers and user response to the tasks. As a test organizer, you should always notify the person of the recording in writing or electronically.

Organization of access



Organize access to the necessary software, install equipment for recording the session. Observation should take place in a natural environment for the user. Analysis of the workspace is important, and in specific cases it is extremely necessary.



For example, you can monitor the work of an electrician at an electrical distribution substation, since it will be impractical and difficult to repeat the working conditions in the ‚Äúlaboratory‚Äù. During the study, you will understand what factors and how affect the work of an engineer: types of devices, software and its individual settings, seasonality, temperature, light, organization of the workspace, and so on.



So in one of the studies, we found that the electrician could not perform his work on the tablet in the summer, because the room temperature reached 50 degrees and the tablet was periodically turned off. When working in the open air, a sun ray fell on the device and gave glare, which also interfered with the tasks. The use of specialized industrial tablets with a matte display and a protective cover, designed to work at high temperatures, helped to solve the situation.



As a result of the analysis of the real workspace, you can check whether the selected type and configuration of equipment is suitable in this context or not.



There are cases when it is not possible to conduct observations in the natural environment, for example, due to the difficult access to the perimeter or its absence at all. Then make sure that the hardware configuration, software settings, as well as the environment, are as similar as possible to a person‚Äôs workspace.



Observation



An important point during the entire testing process is the observation of the user. There are many reasons why this may be necessary, but there are two main ones:



  • Some actions are difficult to describe. If I ask how you ride a bicycle, you probably won‚Äôt remember which side you approach the bicycle with, what foot you push off the surface to pile up on it, and so on. It‚Äôs easier for you to take it and demonstrate skills.

Observation of a person allows you to identify his real behavior and get the maximum understanding of the situation in which he finds himself.



Before starting the observation, it is important to explain to the user what the purpose of the study is, how it will take place and what its role is. It is important to explain that during the testing process we will not test his abilities, but only test the prototype of the system in order to understand what is worth improving.



During tasks, ask participants to comment on their actions and share their thoughts out loud. This will allow a more accurate understanding of their actions. Do not nod if the respondent gives the correct answer and especially do not show despair if he is not coping with the task.



Do not use professional terms in the conversation, as the user may not understand what, for example, ‚Äúcustom controls‚Äù, ‚Äúnative alert‚Äù or ‚Äúmegamenu‚Äù are. Listen to what he says and use clear words and explanations.



Avoid leading questions. They contain tips and you can unknowingly influence the results. Specify all the details if the person did not understand the task immediately.







Do not rush to immediately draw conclusions or write down for the user. It is important to clarify what he means and verify his thoughts with him in order to more accurately record the answer.



Video, audio recording for recording the response does not exclude personal recording of the session in case the recordings are lost or erased.



Take notes using a pre-prepared structure. In UX testing plan & script, I usually use a column to write custom comments for each step of the script. During the execution of tasks, I make small notes in the form of ‚Äúpassed / failed script‚Äù, ‚Äúthere were / did not arise difficulties‚Äù. These notes can be linked to the corresponding moments of the video, and you can easily return to them later.



View notes immediately after observation. Make sure that you can make out your handwriting, supplement observations if you do not have time to do it.



If during testing a person is distracted by a researcher with a notebook, then there may be difficulties in the conversation. Based on my experience, I will say that most users are confused by the fact that the researcher immediately records his answers during the interview. If you have an agreement to record, use the recorder on the phone, for example in a personal conversation. After a couple of minutes, the person stops paying attention to the phone, begins to sink into questions, and at the end of the session completely forgets about it. After the interview, make a short sketch of the answers on paper.



What is still very important. Do not try to conduct testing if the user is selected incorrectly and is not part of the target audience. For example, once in a preliminary questionnaire, the respondent noted that he had a subscription to the module I was researching. But at the beginning of testing it turned out that he had never used it and had no plans.



The best solution in this situation was to roll back to the stage of determining the target audience and choosing another person. It is also worth preliminary to refine the inaccuracies in the questionnaire.

Affinity charting is a process of grouping according to significant signs of the observations and conclusions of the study, which interface designers rely on during product development.

During the analysis, also be prepared to get interesting facts that were not related to testing scenarios. For example, when testing the ‚Äúchat creation‚Äù scenario in the Microsoft Teams web version, you will understand that the user has several different work accounts. He has to be authorized in the system under different accounts in different browser tabs. When analyzing feedback, it is worth considering these questions:



  • What or how much business will gain if he listens to the user and implements the changes? For example, the possibility of expanding the horizons of a business or leadership in a competitive environment.
  • What percentage of dissatisfied users is ready to leave the customer if it is not heard? How much money does the business lose?


When everything is thought out, you need to present the results of the study to the customer. You offer him improvement options and explain the consequences if everything is left as it is. Further, he already decides whether there are resources for revision and whether it is advisable.



Conclusion



With the clear goal of the study, the right tasks, carefully formulated questions, you can fully collect interesting tips and criticism about the product, and also prioritize the plan of tasks for its improvement.



Error analysis and detection is always a subjective process. The results you will come to are your understanding of the situation. Therefore, the more diverse you select a team of researchers, the more diverse you will interpret the essence of the problem.



However, do not forget that when solving problems, you will again face the need to test them. This is essentially an endless process. But such an approach will make it possible to verify in practice whether your solution worked or not.
