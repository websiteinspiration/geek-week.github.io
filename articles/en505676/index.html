How to detect and eliminate places with overheating of equipment in the data center

Summary


Data center engineers often face the problem of overheating servers and take many different actions to eliminate it. Some of their actions make it possible to solve the problem, but only for a short time, and at the same time they can create even more overheating points. The simplest and most cost-effective way to solve the problem, which saves energy, which in turn avoids the capital cost of cooling equipment, is to control air flow. This article describes the main causes of server overheating, gives recommendations for identifying and eliminating them, and also discusses the typical actions of engineers in such a situation.


Introduction


According to a study conducted by the Uptime Institute, 10% of racks generate more heat than prescribed in the instruction manual. As the rack power density in the data center increases on average to 5 kW or more, it is expected that the percentage of hot spots will increase.

If the hot spots are active for a long period, this can be a serious threat not only to the reliability and performance of the equipment, but also to warranties or service agreements. Therefore, data center employees need to take measures as soon as possible to avoid these risks.

To begin with, we need to find out what the ‚Äúhot spots‚Äù are, the reasons for their appearance and how to identify them. As a rule, after detecting points with high temperature, the data center engineers take various measures to eliminate them. Some actions include: setting an extremely low cooling temperature, placing perforated tiles in a hot corridor, and placing fans on a stand in front of problem areas. These simple steps can temporarily fix the problem, but can also lead to unnecessary energy loss or even create even more places with excess heat.

Understanding the root causes of hot spots is crucial in the fight against equipment overheating. It also provides additional benefits in the form of energy savings and helps to avoid additional economic costs for the purchase of additional cooling equipment. The cited document describes the root cause of the ‚Äúhot spots‚Äù, provides recommendations for their determination, discusses typical actions and recommendations for their elimination.

What are hot spots?


In most cases, specialists measure the temperature in a hot corridor or in the wrong place in a cold corridor and think that they have found a hot spot. Further, measures are taken to resolve the problem, but sometimes it turns out that the chosen method to eliminate the hot spot not only does not help, but on the contrary creates even more places with excess heat. Understanding what a hot spot really is, the main reasons for the appearance and the correct identification methods, is crucial in solving the problem of overheating.

Definition


A hot spot is not a random place with a fever inside a data center. The hot spot is defined as the area where the IT equipment is located, with a temperature exceeding the permissible value established in the recommendations of ASHRAE TC 9.9. Hot spots occur most often at the top of the rack. Recommended and acceptable temperature ranges can be found in the ASHRAE thermal guidelines.

Main reasons


Often in Data Centers an excess amount of cooling equipment is installed, especially when the cooling capacity is determined by the nominal "factory" capacity of IT equipment. If there is enough cooling equipment, why do hot spots appear?

The real reason lies not in excessive heat load or lack of cooling devices, but in their improper use. In other words, cooling is enough, but due to the lack of airflow control, cold air does not get to the places where it is needed at a certain point in time, resulting in zones with excess heat.

Fig 1. An example of the use of cooling in a conventional and real data center.

Figure 1 shows an example of an improper use of cooling, based on a real study by Schneider Electric. This is a traditional perimeter data center. Raised floor and suspended ceiling are used as a distribution chamber for supply and exhaust air. First, cold air is supplied to the floor chamber with a certain pressure and speed using the CRAH units. The cold air then moves into the rack area through the perforated tile (54% of the CRAH airflow) and open cutouts in the raised floor slabs (46% of the CRAH airflow). Leakage of air from the cutouts leads to a loss of cooling capacity, since air does not enter the front of the rack, but bypasses it instead.

Most of the airflow from the perforated tiles, namely 96.29% of the perforated airflow, passes through the equipment in the racks. A small part of the cold air, 3.71% of the perforated air flow, bypasses the servers and returns back to the cooling units. Lack of airflow control results in loss of cooling capacity. Meanwhile, some servers cannot get enough cold air and therefore have to compensate for the difference by drawing in hot airflow (7.15% of the airflow) from the back of the rack, which often causes a hot spot in the front upper corner. Consequently, actions that reduce air leakage, bypass and air recirculation help to eliminate areas with excess heat.

The laws of energy conservation and conservation of air mass allow you to calculate the percentage of various types of air flows described above in this data center:

  • 52% of all CRAH airflow enters the front of the racks;
  • 46% of the available cooling power is lost due to leakage through cable cutouts, openings under the racks or inappropriate perforated plates, and 2% of the CRAH cooling is lost due to bypass air. These problems are due to the lack of airflow control;
  • 7.2% of the total air flow is recycled (that is, the hot air stream is recycled back to the front of the racks, which is one of the main causes of hot zones);
  • 92.9% of the total air flow, which is 52% of the total CRAH air flow, is returned to the CRAH units (CRAH - Computer Room Air Handler) through the ceiling.

How to identify a hot spot?


If you find a hot spot as early as possible, this makes it possible to prevent overheating and improper operation of the equipment. There are three main ways to detect a hot spot:

  • Feel walking around the data center.
  • Measure the temperature manually.
  • Automatic monitoring.

The cheapest and easiest way to detect hot spots is to feel the elevated temperature by applying your hands to the front of the equipment rack. This is not a very accurate method, but it is quite effective for extremely hot zones.

Measuring temperatures in manual mode is a good method, because the meters show the exact temperature. Apply various equipment for measuring temperature:

  • Plastic temperature strips ‚Äî a temperature thermometer or a thermometer with a plastic strip ‚Äî is a type of thermometer that contains thermosensitive liquid crystals in a plastic strip that change color depending on the temperature.
  • Temperature pistols.
  • Thermometers.
  • Infrared Sensor (FLIR) in cameras.

Manual temperature measurement is an inexpensive but effective way to identify places with excess heat. Even infrared cameras that allow you to create thermal diagrams with accurate temperature data in real time are available today for around $ 300.

Data center engineers can use these tools to collect temperature data at the server inlet at the front of the rack and at the back of the rack, which allows you to obtain data on the temperature difference and determine the cooling efficiency.

Automatic monitoring is the best method, since it can display the temperature inside the data center in real time. Automatic monitoring is carried out using software that allows you to manage the data center infrastructure (DCIM), which in real time sends an e-mail or text message to a specific person when a certain temperature threshold is reached. Using the DCIM functionality, you can get the actual temperature at the input and output of the device, depending on your requirements. Schneider Electric's StruxureWareTM can serve as an example of this type of DCIM software, allowing you to get detailed 3D heatmaps using data from installed sensors in real time. This is the most expensive and accurate method.In addition, the equipment itself (servers) often has temperature sensors that monitor the temperature of the components and notify of high heat load through monitoring available through IPMI.

It seems logical that a lower temperature of the incoming air will help solve the problem of hot spots. However, you need to use this in extreme need, as this can reduce the overall efficiency and performance of the cooling system. The effect of this action depends on the loading of CRAC / CRAH blocks. If the cooling system has additional power (i.e., it operates at a load of less than 100% and does not have its own thermodynamic limit), then a decrease in the set value can have a positive effect. When the CRAC / CRAH is in close proximity to the hot zone, setting an extremely low cooling temperature can lower the temperature.

If the cooling unit is already operating at its maximum power (100% load), then it is impossible to lower the set temperature, since the system is already at its maximum operating temperature and can not do anything to solve the problem of overheating. Each cooling system has its own maximum power. This ‚Äúmaximum‚Äù power decreases as the setpoint decreases.

Placing perforated plates in a hot corridor (inefficient practice)


Some people think that placing perforated tiles in a hot corridor is a good method, since there is no understanding of the benefits of a hot / cold corridor scheme and any hot zone is perceived as a problem. This method does not eliminate hot spots in cold corridors, and sometimes it can even create more problems. In addition, placing perforated plates in the hot aisle reduces the availability of cooling equipment. The layout of the hot / cold aisle rows is best practice. It is important that the cold aisle remains cold, because it is the so-called ‚Äúcold air reservoir‚Äù for equipment.

In the early days of air-cooled mainframes, cooling was realized using a raised floor, and cooling equipment controlled the return temperature. This approach worked thanks to the uniform, well-mixed air in the room. Today, the layout of hot and cold corridors specifically creates areas with hot and cold temperatures, which, by design, lead to an uneven temperature of return air. People who are accustomed to a uniform temperature in the room can place perforated tiles with cooling in a hot corridor, believing that they solve the problem of overheating equipment.

Placing racks and perforated tiles next to cooling equipment (inefficient practice)


Some people think that a good way to get maximum cooling power is to place racks and perforated plates a few feet from the cooling equipment. In fact, this can give the opposite effect. The above actions can lead to insufficient supply of equipment with cold air, which in consequence will not solve the problem with hot zones. Although this method can help pick up most of the recycled hot air. The reason this method can lead to insufficient replenishment of the equipment is because the air leaving the fan of the cooling unit usually moves at a very high speed, which creates low static pressure in this area. It means,that perforated plates produce very little cold air in this area and can even suck air into the underground plenum. Correct - if the racks are placed at a distance of at least 3 tiles from the cooling units.

An easy way to identify problems with air velocity under the raised floor is to place a small sheet of paper on the perforated tile. If the paper is drawn in by a perforated tile, it should be replaced with a solid one to balance the pressure under the raised floor.

Installing fans on a stand in front of the problem rack (temporary use only)


It is sometimes believed that installing fans is a good way, as the air flow is directed directly to the place with excess heat. However, this action should only be used temporarily and in emergency situations, for example, when the equipment may turn off due to overheating. This method (shown in Figure 2) can eliminate the hot spot by lowering the current temperature of the equipment. Pedestal fans, in fact, work as mixers, mixing the exhaust air of the equipment and the cold incoming. This creates a temperature that is higher than the supply temperature, but colder than the exhaust air from the equipment. Which will also allow to increase the air flow passing through the equipment. Mixing hot and cold air will reduce the efficiency of the cooling system,as well as inadequate use of resources. Among other things, fans are an additional source of heat in the data center.

Best ways to fix hot spots


Most of the generally accepted methods for solving the problem of hot spots listed above are not recommended, because they do not eliminate the two main causes of the appearance of zones with excess heat: bypass (bypassing the air of the desired zone) and recirculation. The complete elimination of bypass and recirculation means that the flows of hot and cold air are completely separated, this means that hot spots simply cannot exist. The first four methods listed below are effective because they solve the problem of bypass air and recirculation. The last recommendation is the best, but should only be implemented after the implementation of practical methods of airflow control.

  1. Airflow control in the rack.
  2. Air flow control in the room.
  3. Redistribution of load.
  4. Change the location of the air temperature sensor.
  5. DCIM .


Many hot spots result from the recirculation of hot exhaust air in or around the rack. Therefore, improving rack airflow control plays an important role in resolving the problem. Open U-shaped shelves and cable management are a common cause of recirculation, resulting in hot spots. Using plugs to fill unused space and brushes for cable entries is one of the easiest and most cost-effective ways to improve laminar flow in a rack.

For more information about airflow control using plugs, see Technical Paper 44, ‚ÄúImproving Rack Cooling Performance with Airflow Plugs‚Äù.

Some switches and routers require two-way cold airflow. If we talk about data centers with a traditional air flow between the racks, the hot air leaving the switch / router can return, and as a result create overheating points. The rack-mounted air distribution systems shown in Figure 4a can be used to supply cold air from both sides, eliminating the possibility of the formation of hot zones.

For more information on this topic, see Technical Documentation 50, Cooling Options for Rack Equipment with Side Airflow.

In places with sufficient cooling and high density equipment, hot spots may appear. In this situation, the modernization of fan devices can help to improve airflow and increase cooling capacity.

The devices shown in Figure 4b can efficiently ‚Äúborrow‚Äù air from neighboring racks in order to allow the removal of additional heat generated by the equipment with a power of 3 kW per rack. This can minimize the temperature difference between the upper and lower parts of the rack and also prevent the recirculation of hot exhaust air at the inlet. As with all air-absorbing devices, care must be taken when placing it so that air drawn from an adjacent space does not cause it to overheat. These devices must be powered by the UPS in order to avoid equipment shutdown during a power outage. In areas of high equipment density, overheating may occur during the time required to start the backup generator.

Some people think that if you place as many perforated plates in each row as possible, this will help to provide the right amount of cooling. However, too many tiles violate the laminarity of the air flow along the route. Adding perforated plates does not always solve the problem of areas with excess heat, but if you remove a few, it can help air get to the problem areas. The following guide shows how.

First take a flow meter and measure the total CFM in a row. Then divide by 125 CFM / kW, and this value should be approximately equal to the rack power in the row. If not, you need to remove several tiles and repeat the above procedure until they become approximately the same.


Indoor airflow control


After improving rack airflow control, the next important step is to improve indoor airflow control. First you need to close all the holes in the raised floor. The sealant should be used to seal cable cutouts behind the racks and under the PDU. These openings cause unwanted air leaks. It is necessary to fill the gaps around the cooling devices and other holes in the floor with foam, find the perforated tiles that are the source of the bypass, and replace them with solid ones. For example, if a tile is placed in front of an empty rack, then it must be replaced by a solid one. Also balance the airflow under the floor. Proper placement of the slabs in combination with sealing the openings on the raised floor will restore lost cooling power.

Another reason for the occurrence of hot spots is the mixing of hot and cold air, which occurs at the top and at the ends of the rack. One of the main methods is the separation of hot and cold corders using racks around the perimeter. Figure 5 shows examples of insulating hot and cold corridors. Isolation not only helps fix hot spots, but also provides the added benefit of energy savings over traditional data center designs without using isolated modules. Distribution of problem load (power)


As discussed above, moving equipment from problematic hot spots to a rack with lower energy consumption can solve the problem. But it is also important to provide the room with sufficient power and cooling, which allows you to remove heat from the power consumption, as well as evenly distribute the load across all cabinets. In places where the load exceeds the permissible value, it is necessary to divide the equipment into several cabinets.

We want to note that the distribution of equipment between several racks leaves a significant amount of unused space in the cabinet in their upper parts. This space must be filled with plugs to avoid a decrease in cooling performance. If it is possible to turn off the server or other important equipment, then this action can fix the overheating zone at the lowest cost. For more information on this topic, see White Paper 46, ‚ÄúCooling Strategies for Ultra High Density Racks and Blade Servers‚Äù.

Changing the location of the temperature sensor


In most older data centers, thermostats are installed in a CRAC return air stream, which can be unpredictable. It can also lead to uneven loading of CRAC, which in turn leads to temperature fluctuations at the server inlet. Moving the thermostats into the supply air stream, on which the exhaust temperature depends, will ensure a uniform temperature of the equipment at the inlet. In combination with other methods, moving the thermostat also allows you to increase the supply air temperature, thereby saving energy of the cooling system, without worrying about changes in the supply air temperature.

DCIM resolution for airflow control of cooling devices


There are systems that control individual room cooling devices depending on the temperature in front of the rack. These systems use comprehensive algorithms for dynamic prediction and adjustment of the fan speed, and also show which cooling units can be disabled. By establishing control over the amount of air in the data center, you can reduce possible bypasses (inefficient air bypass flows). An example of such a system is the Vigilent cooling system.

Conclusion


Zones with local overheating can lead to reduced reliability, performance, or even server damage. Hot spots usually appear at the equipment inlet due to inefficient airflow control, such as cold air leakage due to bypass flows (bypasses) and hot exhaust air recirculation.

Feeling the elevated temperature while walking around the data center, measuring it manually or setting up automatic temperature monitoring are the three main ways to detect places with excess heat.

Most of the actions that data center employees take to solve problems with hot zones are either temporary and are used in extreme situations, or they are inefficient and can only aggravate the situation. Rack and room airflow control, localization, movement of problem equipment, relocation of air temperature sensors, and DCIM's ability to control airflow from cooling equipment are effective, simple, and economical methods for resolving hot spot problems.
