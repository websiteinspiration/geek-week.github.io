How can a tech team build their startup, or the path from functional monitoring to the AIOps platform

Three months ago, I published a story about how it was not possible to make a product from a project, how it turned back into a project and never entered the market (you can read about it here ).


The second approach to the shell began a few years ago, and so far the flight is normal. Already have customers, revenue, prizes at international competitions, interest from investors. I would like to tell the story of product development in this article. And also to share the lessons that were learned during the race to the product. This article will be interesting to both those who build the product, and those who are engaged in monitoring in a large organization. Since we are building exactly a system for automation, umbrella monitoring, functional monitoring and predictive analytics.


How the idea was born


Once upon a time, there was a team of techies in one integrator in 2014 who wanted to make their product and enter the market with it. No, not even that. The team did not dream of making their product; at first, they saw the opportunity to help their colleagues support information systems more efficiently.


This integrator was then mainly engaged in supporting highly loaded systems with a huge number of users and a large number of processor cores for each system. Support was in the best traditions of the 24/7 genre. In one shift, 20-30 people could work. Someone looked at the screens of infrastructure monitoring, someone monitored user requests, someone continuously tested the functionality with their hands, someone supported autotests in selenium, etc.


There were two problems that could be tackled:


  1. It is very difficult and expensive to verify the functioning of systems with your hands; you need to automate this process;
  2. An insane number of alerts from monitoring systems is flying, we need to find a way to reduce the number of alerts and identify only “important” ones from them.

Both problems were resolved.


The first was decided by the deployment of Jenkins, it launched the assembly of tests on Selenium, then the data was parsed and displayed in the form of metrics in Zabbix, on which the triggers were built. Alerts to the support team came about triggered triggers.

As they say, appetite comes with eating. At first, the problem with the reliability and control of contractors was solved, but now the load on situation centers, which received another monitoring system, has increased many times.

The “service monitor” at the customer was not then perceived as a means of combining data from different monitoring systems. He performed this function inside the integrator, where he really helped reduce the number of “garbage” notifications and increase the efficiency of the duty shift.


When you have 10 engineers and 200 alerts per day within several integrator projects, this is not the same when you have 100 systems, 350 engineers and 10 thousand alerts from monitoring systems per day at a large organization with millions of customers and tens of thousands of employees.
