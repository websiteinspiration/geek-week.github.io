Over the years, there have been many approaches - and most of them, to put it mildly, are far from ideal. Hopes and prayers are common among managers who believe that the fate of their project depends on God's will. And those who do not believe in this often rely on motivational methods: strict deadlines with lashes, chains, hot oil, photographs of people conquering rocks, and seagulls soaring over the sea.

Such approaches almost everywhere lead to the characteristic signs of disgusting project management - development teams constantly delay the project, despite the fact that they work a lot overtime. Teams that write programs of obviously low quality that do not meet the needs of customers.

40 hour work week


On the seventh day, God decided to take rest. And later, God told everyone to rest on that day. Apparently, even God needs the ‚Äú40-hour work week‚Äù method, or even work.

In the early 1970s, when I was eighteen, I and my school friends were hired to work as junior programmers to work on an extremely important project. Managers set deadlines. The deadlines were tight. It was required to give all the best. We were indispensable cogs in the complex mechanism of the company. We were important!

Well, when you're eighteen, isn't it?

We, young and hot, having just graduated from school, worked like oxen. We worked long hours month after month. On average, we worked 60 hours a week. There were weeks when we worked even for 80 hours. Dozens of times we worked at night.

And we were proud to work overtime. Well, we were real programmers. We are dedicated to the project. We were appreciated. Because we were the only force that could save such an important project. We. There were. Programmers.

And then we burned out, and hard. So tough that they left all in a crowd. We flew out of there, leaving the company a barely working time-sharing system, while the company did not have sensible programmers who could accompany it. That's it for them!

Well, when you are eighteen and you are furious, right?

Don‚Äôt worry, the company has climbed out. It turned out that there were still smart programmers besides us. The guys who worked quietly for themselves 40 hours a week. The guys whom we thought were indifferent to work and lazy, whom we laughed contemptuously during the crazy night marathons until they saw them. These guys, without much fuss, took the system into their own hands, providing quite suitable support. I‚Äôm not afraid to say, they were glad to get rid of a bunch of noisy and annoying jerks.

Overtime work


Do you think I learned a lesson from what I just told you? Of course not. Over the next 20 years, I burned at work in the same way for the sake of my employers. I continued to be seduced by the tales that the project was extremely important. Of course, I did not go crazy working for days, as in 18 years. On average, I already worked somewhere around 50 hours a week. Night gatherings began to occur less frequently, but did not disappear anywhere.

When I grew up, I realized that the worst technical mistakes I made at night, when manic energy was the key to me. I realized that these errors were huge obstacles that I constantly had to correct in the hours when I was really working.

Then something happened that made me think. My future business partner, Jim Newkirk, and I did night watch. Somewhere around two in the morning we tried to figure out how to move a unit of data from the low-level part of our program to another part, which was much higher in the execution chain. The decision to push this data onto the stack did not fit.

We have created a transportation system inside our product according to the type of mail. We used it to send information between processes. In our veins flowed caffeine, we were at the limit of possibilities. Suddenly, there was a realization that it was possible to make the low-level part of the process send a unit of data to itself, where the high-level part could read it.

Even now, after more than three decades, every time Jim and I want to describe someone‚Äôs unsuccessful decision, we say: ‚ÄúYeah. They just sent it to themselves. ‚Äù
I will not overload you with boring details why this decision was stupid. It is enough to say that it took a lot more effort than we seemed to have saved. And, of course, this decision led to too deep and difficult to reverse changes, so we lost a lot of time.

Marathon


And at that time, I realized that a software development project is a marathon, not a sprint or a series of sprints. To win, you need to count strength. If you jump out of the starting blocks and fly at full speed, you will run out of strength before you cross the finish line.

Therefore, you must work at a pace that can be maintained for a long time. There should be a 40-hour work week. If you try to run at a faster pace than you can maintain, you still have to slow down and rest until the finish line crosses. The average speed will be lower than at a moderate pace. When the finish line is close, there is still little energy left to make a breakthrough. But there is no need to do it ahead of time.

Managers may ask you to hurry. In no case do not succumb. Your job is to properly manage your resources in order to survive to the end.
