How to make generative images look natural with mathematical algorithms



From time to time they write me comments that my works do not look like computer-generated. As for me, this is the best praise: my deep interest in generative art began when I realized his ability to create just such works. Here I will describe some of the methods that I use to improve the naturalness of my work.

This post is not about the language and not about the framework. We will only talk about technology.

And let's immediately agree, please: I am not saying that works that look natural are to some extent better than those that look digital. No need to set naturalness as a goal. I'm chasing her just because I like it, that‚Äôs my taste.

Leading principle: model your work based on reality


If possible, consider how you would depict your creation on paper or how it might look in the real world. Modeling a real physical process is likely to lead to realistic looking work.

For example, evenly distributed data is usually not found in nature. Most of the data is something like a bell-shaped curve. The use of normally distributed random variables (those with an average value and standard deviation) often creates a more natural effect than random selection from a gap or list.

Random tilt


Suppose we draw several horizontal lines, and we want to make them more natural. First you can move the end points of the lines a little. We can do this by moving them using a normal distribution with a small standard deviation in the x and y directions.



Wave wobble


Now we have lines that are slightly offset from the original position. And vary slightly in length. However, they are completely straightforward. This is not like the lines we meet in the real world. Let's make them less straight.

For example, this can be done like this:

  1. Select N points between the end points of the line to create a new line.
  2. Shift every point.
  3. Soften the line and draw it.

There are many variations of this pattern. Simple - take N interpolated points between the start and end points, correct each of them using the static standard deviation, as they did before. I will use the algorithm for changing the Chaikin curve to get a smooth line. Here is the result:



Influence of neighboring points


Well, something started to work out for us. Changing the standard deviation parameters allows us to control the "fluctuations" of our line. However, you probably noticed that the oscillations are independent of each other. The line can fluctuate, then approaching the neighboring upper, then to the lower lines. To protect myself from this, I propose this option.

Take the average of each pair of adjacent points in your resulting line after adjustment (this is a very simple example of nuclear smoothing ). You can do it several times to get a smoother line. Now draw a curve through the dots. Now each point affects the neighboring one more naturally.



I would say that now the form looks pretty decent. If I tried to draw a straight line on paper, it probably would have looked just like that. We can change the standard deviation in our function with manual correction in order to simulate a line made as if by a more or less accurate hand.

Note: in this case, you can still use the one-dimensional noise function to move the point in a straight line. The result will also be a smooth curve, but it will be more predictable, depending on the noise function you use.

Texturing lines


Now our line looks natural enough. But we are still drawing with the perfectly accurate ‚Äúpen‚Äù when we call the stroke () function. In the real world, when we use various painting tools, they have differences in texture. One way to make these differences is to use the sand splines technique popularized by Anders Hoff (aka inconvergent). The idea is simple:

  1. Adjust each point in the line a little bit using the normal distribution.
  2. Generate a few more dots, between each adjacent pair of dots, and draw them as tiny dots.
  3. Repeat several times.

If your dots are super small, you get this texture:



This is only one of many ways to add texture to lines, but it is also my favorite topic to start with. Experiment with your textures and see what works best for you.

Forms


Now that we can draw unusual lines, let's move on to the second dimension - shapes. For the sake of simplicity, we will focus on the quadrangles, but these techniques can be applied to many other shapes.

We can just draw a square.



We can apply the already mentioned principle of random tilt, gradually changing each angle.



You can select and smooth the points between each adjacent coordinate to get a ‚Äúcurve‚Äù square.



We can also smooth them by averaging neighboring points. Since the segment is now closed, you need to be careful so as not to catch the points in the first coordinate.



None of this is new. We are here, in fact, talking only about closed segments, because we just smooth the corners. What about filling the space? We can use the fill () function, but it's a little boring.



To make the texture more interesting, you can fill the quadrilateral with dashed elements, generating many points inside it *. We will simplify the figure again to make the borders more understandable (you can also hatch complex shapes, but let's just for now).



* Note: you can do this by dividing the square into 2 triangles and generating a point in each using a uniform distribution.

Although it looks a bit messy, because even distribution is not as intuitive as it seems. For a more natural distribution of points, we can take values ‚Äã‚Äãfrom one of my favorite sequences - Halton Sequences (2.3) [you can read about it in a cool article ].

Halton sequence (2.3) generate points in the interval (0, 1) √ó (0, 1) in R2. To fill the square with the points generated in this sequence, we can:

  1. Find the minimum bounding box containing the "Rectangle".
  2. Generate N points in (2.3) Halton sequence.
  3. Graduate each point with the width (or height) of the minimum bounding box.
  4. Move the point along the upper left coordinate of the square.
  5. Filter out all points that do not lie in the "Square".

This can all work on any shape that supports squaring and filtering outside points. The easiest way to try it out is to fill the square with dashed elements, in it the operations of squaring and filtering are simple.

In general, (2.3) the Halton sequence is generated simply; Read about pseudo wikipedia ( eng ). Here is a 10,000-point stroke fill.



Since (2.3) the Halton sequence is not randomly generated, it creates the same looking texture. A couple of ways around this:

  1. Graduating and (optionally) easily swaying the bounding box to shift the center of the sequence.
  2. Drop N points from the beginning of the sequence.
  3. Randomly dropping a small fraction of the points from the sequence (but carefully, this creates more noise).

Of course, these approaches can be combined. Experiment!

Perceptibility


Returning to our guiding principle, we recall that in order to add naturalness to your work, you need to simulate real physics.

This idea has been touched upon in detail in the Nature of the Code, which I simply recommend to you without quoting Daniel Schiffman. Daniel divides complex physical problems into easily digestible slices, simplifying the implementation of each idea. Making my creations, I often run a physical simulation for a while and take a picture at a certain moment, which becomes the final picture.

For example, in the picture ‚ÄúSweetening Serum‚Äù, gravity affects the ball, which jumps over each square in the grid. The direction of gravity is determined by an additional noise field, and the ball leaves a mark.



At the heart of the "Dust Bowl" is a simulation of tearing gum.



As for me, these pictures are alive. And no wonder, really. They are closely related to our guiding principle:

Model your work based on reality.

When we focus on simulating natural processes in code, we get more natural work.

Summing up, I will say that following the naturalness in my work is what I like to do. It‚Äôs not a burden for me to make my computer spend a little more time on generating textures and simulating physics, so that in the end I get a more lively creation. I hope this article will be your starting point in experimenting with naturalness in your work.
