About software development timelines

Throughout the history of software development, we have been looking for reliable ways to assess the time taken to complete tasks and projects. But even after more than 60 years of the industry's existence, our forecasts still leave much to be desired. Maybe the point is not exactly how we are trying to evaluate, but that we generally rely on estimates?

For example, take the Scrum methodology, which many companies use today. The central idea of ‚Äã‚ÄãScrum is to take no more tasks into the sprint than your team is able to complete during this time. At first glance, it sounds reasonable. Unfortunately, too often in practice this approach slows down the work of the team in exchange for the illusion of planning. Let me explain why.

Evaluations hinder team work


Doing your job well is an absolutely natural desire for most people. At first, almost all developers are optimistic and underestimate the time required to complete the tasks. What inevitably happens next: they agree to implement the task for the period that they themselves have called, and do not have time. Even if no one reproaches them for this (which, incidentally, is not always the case), this creates a certain discomfort. As this situation repeats over and over, people learn to give estimates ‚Äúwith a margin‚Äù, because being late with the deadlines that you yourself called is not a very pleasant story.

Contrary to intuition, naming the terms ‚Äúwith a margin‚Äù does not necessarily help to fit into them. This effect is known as Parkinson‚Äôs First Law., here psychology plays against us. Imagine that you assume that the task will take a couple of days. However, taking into account all the unknowns and taking into account the past sad experience, you call the term ‚Äúweek‚Äù. Let's say your manager says ‚Äúokay‚Äù and you get to work. The first feeling you have is enough time! You have just agreed on a term with a good supply. Therefore, the first half of the week passes either in a relaxed mode, or, more often, you switch your priorities to other tasks for which there was no time before. As the appointed time draws nearer, you take up the job thoroughly, and it is here that the very surprises begin to come up on which you, in fact, were laying a reserve. You work hard maybestaying up late at work and, as a result, hardly fit (or do not fit) into the period that you yourself called ‚Äúwith a margin‚Äù. The next time the same story repeats itself again.

Problems can arise no matter how you approach grades. If people underestimate, they often have to cut corners somewhere to meet the deadline. Surprisingly, if estimates are issued ‚Äúwith a margin‚Äù, then exactly the same thing happens, only later. As Maxim Dorofeev aptly put it in one of his reports, ‚Äúthe deadline is the deadline before which the task will not be completed under any circumstances.‚Äù

Best performance is shown by teams that do not use grades. The more often you ask your team about ratings, the more you slow down its work. If your team has to evaluate all their work in general, then this slowdown reaches its maximum. First, people spend time on the initial estimates, then they discuss why the estimates did not come true and what to do next (the usual solution is to require new estimates), and then the requirements change, and voila - you need to evaluate how the change in requirements affected the estimates. And the team has to deal with the technical debt accumulated in numerous attempts to meet its own estimates, which slows down the project in the long run.

Finger to the sky


Complexity and uncertainty are constant companions in software development. Almost everything we do as developers is something new. Even if we did something similar in the past, now we do it in new conditions. This may be a new project, or new requirements, or our knowledge gained over the past time from past experience. If the task is typical and regularly repeated, then someone must have automated it and suggested it in the form of a finished product, library, or framework. Thus, in the vast majority of cases, developers have to give estimates for tasks with a high degree of uncertainty.

It would be better if the developers had the opportunity to work on the task for some time before issuing the grades. In this case, they could better understand the problem and would be much more confident in their assessments. Unfortunately, this is rare in practice. As a rule, developers have very little time to study the task before they are required to provide an assessment. As a result, the scores are essentially poking a finger at the sky.

Of course, all experienced managers know that ratings are just estimates, and they don‚Äôt try to interpret them as hard deadlines. Nevertheless, evaluations are still perceived as some form of promise. If estimates didn‚Äôt mean anything at all - why would you ask about them in principle? Issuing an assessment means a certain degree of readiness to complete a task in a specified time. It is not surprising that many developers do not like this - in fact, they are asked to subscribe to terms for what they themselves do not fully understand.

Initial development is the least cost




People constantly ask for estimates for initial development, but few people are interested in estimates for subsequent support of a solution. And this is a big problem, support is very serious. Support can take up to 60-80% of all project costs. In addition, each new feature that you add to the product adds complexity and slows down subsequent development.

In theory, people should be very careful with the choice of tasks for development and work only on what is of the highest value for the product. Unfortunately, in project-driven projects, people tend to promote features that do not require large initial implementation costs and put off what seems complicated. As a result, the most important work risks being delayed, and the product is cluttered with minor features and crutches.

How to manage a project without ratings


Well, let the estimates require sacrifice. They may be inaccurate, they slow down the team, and perhaps the developers sometimes do not like them. But we still need estimates for project planning, right? In fact, optional. Yes, sometimes evaluations are really needed, but in many cases they are not required to make project decisions.

First, evaluations should not be the main factor influencing priorities for the development team. Work on what is important, not what can be done quickly. If you do not have absolute confidence that this feature is important, it should not be considered at all for full development. Use MVP and product discovery to identify the real needs of your users.

Secondly, if you have a certain deadline or expectations from the business that you need to meet, let them know to your team, instead of asking for their ratings. There is a wonderful approach Fix Time and Budget, Flex Scope . Each problem can be solved in many different ways. Carefully prioritize requirements and work on them from the most important to the less important, and be prepared to start the project at any time. In such circumstances, working with deadlines is much easier. Even if you fail to fully complete the project by the deadline, you will have time to fulfill the most important requirements and will be ready to launch - and often this is exactly what you need.

Thirdly, if you really need an assessment, let the developers work on the task for several days before asking it. If you are lucky, the task will simply be completed in these few days, and, accordingly, you no longer need an assessment. If not, by then the developer will have a much better understanding of the difficulties in this task, and you will be able to have a more productive discussion. Take into account not only the costs of the initial development, but also the subsequent support of the solution, which can be a much more significant factor. And again - if you are not sure that the task is important, it should not reach the developers at all, even to get an estimate.
