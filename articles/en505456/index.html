Why printers put invisible dots on documents

On June 3, 2017, FBI agents raided the home of 25-year-old Reality Leah Winner, who worked for Pluribus International Corporation, a contractor for the NSA in Georgia. The girl (pictured) was arrested on charges of divulging classified information allegedly leaked to the press. Indeed, by that time the document was already at the disposal of the online edition of The intercept , and on June 5, 2017 it was published in the public domain .

There is an interesting point in this story. FBI agents claimed that they carefully examined copies of the document provided by The Intercept to calculate the source of the leak., and allegedly "noticed folds indicating that the pages were printed and manually removed from the protected space."

But an analysis of independent experts from Errata Security showed the true picture. The fact is that the PDF document from the leak contained scanned copies of paper documents of very good quality. Including marker dots that secretly affix printers to each printed page. Apparently, these points were calculated by Winner.

From the FBI’s court testimony , Winner admitted after the detention that she had actually printed the secret documents and sent them by e-mail to The Intercept . The girl was not such an experienced user of cryptographic tools as Edward Snowden, and sent documents directly from her workplace.

Shortly after the publication of the leak, charges against Winner were made public.

And then the experts paid close attention to the PDF files, which are now publicly available on the Internet. They found something interesting: yellow dots in a roughly rectangular pattern that repeats throughout the page. Barely visible to the naked eye, they created a coded pattern.

First quick analysisshowed that these points can be used to determine the date and time of printing documents: 06:20 May 9, 2017, at least this is probably the time on the internal clock of the printer at that moment. Dots also encode the serial number of the printer.


These "microdots" are well known to security researchers and human rights defenders who are fighting against surveillance of citizens. Many color printers add them to documents. At the same time, some users do not even know what they are there.


HP Laserjet printer dots highlighted in blue (photo: Florian Heise / Wikipedia)

In this case, the FBI did not publicly admit that it used microdots to identify the suspect and refused to answer questions. The US Department of Justice, which published information on the charges against Winner, also refused to provide further clarification.

In a statement by The Interceptstates: “Winner has been charged that are not proven. The same goes for the FBI’s statements about how they came to arrest Winner. ”

Ironically, it was the presence of microdots that attracted particular interest in a secret document that intelligence services (probably) would like to avoid.

One of the first to be noticed was Ted Khan of Document Cloud. He says that when you enlarge the document, these points are clearly visible. Errata Security's security researcher Rob Graham then posted a blog post explaining in detail how to identify and decrypt microdots. Based on their position on the grid, they indicate a specific hour, minute, date and date. It turned out 07/09/2017, 6:20 and the number 29535218.

The site of the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) once even had a tool that helps to decrypt a picture of microdots on a grid. EFF maintains a list of color printers that participate in this program and label each printed sheet of paper. And a list of printers that do not set yellow microdots (but maybe they mark documents in some other, unknown way).

According to EFF experts, with a high degree of probability, all modern commercial color laser printers use one or another steganography method to identify printouts. These are not necessarily yellow dots. Most likely, some types of secret markings are not known to the public.

Formally, this technology was introduced to protect against forgery of documents, but in practice it is used in reconnaissance, investigation of the origin and dating of documents. In fact, steganography through microdots was still used by German intelligence in World War II (see the certificate from the NSA archive ).


Microdots inside the label of an envelope sent by German spies from Mexico City to Lisbon during World War II (source: Wikipedia) The

technology can also be used for peaceful, civilian purposes. Companies like the British Alpha Dot sell small bottles with permanent glue, which is filled with miniature balls with a diameter of 1 mm with a PIN number. If the police find some stolen item, then this PIN theoretically proves your ownership.

There has been much debate about the secret labeling of documents with printers. The problem is that manufacturers keep this information secret and do not notify their users to whom these printers are sold. Some of the steganography technologies they use are not known until now and are being implemented with the knowledge of the special services.

Some forms of text steganography do not use special characters at all. For example, the system of “snow” is known, that is, Steganographic Nature Of Whitespace, when information is encoded through invisible changes in the width of spaces and indents between characters and lines of text.

Human rights organizations consider this a violation of human rights.

However, intelligence and intelligence today rely on these technologies. The example of the arrest of Reality Winner demonstrates how the FBI tries to hide information about the methods that allowed the informant to be calculated and arrested.
