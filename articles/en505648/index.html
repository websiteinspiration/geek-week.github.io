Scientific research is based on the relationship between the reality of nature, as it is observed, and the representation of this reality formulated by a theory in mathematical language. If all the consequences of the theory are experimentally proven, then it is considered reasonable. This approach, which has been used for almost four centuries, has created a consistent body of knowledge. This can affect the progress of science, even the greatest minds.

First mistake


In his main work on the general theory of relativity, Einstein wrote an equation describing the evolution of the universe in time. The solution of this equation shows that the Universe is unstable, and does not represent a huge sphere with a constant volume around which stars glide around, as was thought at that time.

At the beginning of the 20th century, people lived with the established idea of ‚Äã‚Äãa static Universe in which the movement of stars never changes. This is probably due to the teachings of Aristotle, who claims that the sky is unchanging, unlike the Earth, which is perishable. This idea caused a historical anomaly: in 1054, the Chinese noticed the appearance of a new light in the sky, but not a single European document mentions this. Nevertheless, it could be seen even in the afternoon, and it lasted several weeks. It was a supernova, that is, a dying star, the remains of which can still be considered as a Crab nebula. The prevailing thought in Europe did not allow people to accept a phenomenon that so strongly contradicted the idea of ‚Äã‚Äãan unchanging sky. Supernova is a very rare phenomenon that can be observed with the naked eye only once a century.The most recent of them dates back to 1987. To stay in agreement with the idea of ‚Äã‚Äãa static Universe, Einstein introduced a cosmological constant into his equations, which froze the state of the Universe. His intuition led him astray: in 1929, when Hubble demonstrated that the universe was expanding, Einstein admitted that he had made "his biggest mistake."

Quantum randomness


Quantum mechanics developed at about the same time as the theory of relativity. It describes physics on an infinitely small scale. Einstein made a great contribution to this field in 1905, interpreting the photoelectric effect as a collision of electrons and photons - that is, infinitesimal particles carrying pure energy. In other words, light, which is traditionally described as a wave, behaves like a stream of particles. It was this step forward, and not the theory of relativity, that brought Einstein the Nobel Prize in 1921.

But, despite this vital contribution, he stubbornly rejected the key lesson of quantum mechanics - that the world of particles is not bound by the strict determinism of classical physics. The quantum world is probabilistic. We only know how to predict the probability of an event occurring among a number of possibilities.

In Einstein's blindness, we again see the influence of Greek philosophy. Plato taught that thought should remain ideal, free from the coincidences of reality - a noble idea, but not subject to the requirements of science. Knowledge requires perfect coordination with all predicted facts, while faith is based on the probability generated by partial observations. Einstein himself was convinced that pure thought was able to completely capture reality, but quantum randomness contradicts this hypothesis.

Einstein did not accept this fundamental indeterminism, which was formulated by his provocative sentence: "God does not play dice with the Universe." He imagined the existence of hidden variables, that is, not yet discovered numbers outside the limits of mass, charge and spin, which physicists use to describe particles. But the experiment did not support this idea. It cannot be denied that there is a reality that surpasses our understanding - we cannot know everything about the world of infinitesimal.

This is what leads to the conceptualization of the theory, and Einstein with his thought experiments sets a famous example of this. He stated that "imagination is more important than knowledge." Indeed, considering disparate observations, the physicist must imagine the law underlying them. Sometimes several theoretical models compete in explaining a particular phenomenon, and only at that moment logic takes over again.
