A bit about the algorithms and protocols of safe and reliable communication of a user group



Secure instant messaging is used in two ways: face-to-face chat and group chat. While the first option has received much attention recently, little is known about cryptographic mechanisms and security guarantees for secure group communication in real time.

Group Communication Protocol


A secure group communication protocol should provide a level of security comparable to when a group of people communicate in a private room: everyone in the room hears a connection (monitored delivery), everyone knows who spoke (authenticity) and how often these words were spoken (no duplication) , no one outside the room can either speak into the room (lack of creation), or hear internal communication (confidentiality), and the room can only be open to invitees (closed).

Group members


A group in the general scheme consists of many members of the group and many administrators of the group, where the last set is a subset of the first. Each group has its own unique ID and the necessary information, such as name.

Each member of the group keeps long-term secrets for initial contact with other members and the state of the session for each group of which he is a member. Session state contains utility variables and secrets exclusively for use in a group. Messages delivered in a group are not saved in session state.

Threat Model


Here we identify adversaries seeking to break one of their subsequently defined security goals into one specific group called the target group .

Malicious user . Since all protocols are open to new users, an attacker can act as a malicious user that can arbitrarily deviate from the protocol. To eliminate trivial attacks on instant message delivery, we assume that members of the target group behave correctly, always following the protocol.

Network attacker . This attacker has full control over the communication channel, can gain access and modify all unprotected traffic.

Malicious Server. In addition to directly impersonating the central server, this attacker models attacks on transport-level security between users and the central server.

To analyze the stability of the protocols against the compromise of user secrets, to determine the perfect direct secrecy and future secrecy, the following two possibilities are added to the opponents previously described.

Compromise of long-term secrets . This allows an attacker to compromise a specific user during or after the protocol is executed in order to obtain his long-term secrets. As described for a malicious user, impersonations are considered trivial attacks, and therefore, sessions that run after a compromise are not considered safe.

Session Compromise. This allows the attacker to compromise the user to obtain the full state of the session at some intermediate stage of the protocol execution. In contrast to the compromise of long-term use, this additional opportunity is not limited in relation to the personification of the members of the target group in explicit form, since the corresponding definitions of perfect direct secrecy and direct secrecy take this into account accordingly.

Group Cryptography Algorithms


Compound key generation methods today are various extensions of the fundamental algorithm proposed by Diffie and Hellman in 1976. The original protocol offers a method for establishing a common key by two interacting parties, while its group extensions offer a generalization of this scheme to the case of N interacting parties. As for the original protocol, the complexity of the disclosure of the installed group key is based on the difficulty of finding the discrete logarithm, so if the two-way protocol is considered secure, the same can be said for all its extensions.

The GDH.3 protocol was introduced along with the GDH.1 and GDH.2 protocols. This is especially suitable for environments that require minimal processing power from each member of the group.
