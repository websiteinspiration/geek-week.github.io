My name is Alexey, I am the technical director of Okko. There are a lot of questions, I will try to answer everything.



When will premieres be going on in online cinemas in parallel with regular cinemas? Is the industry still not ready?



Let's talk about how the movie licensing industry works in principle.



The main income from films is obtained during rental in cinemas. All studios, especially Hollywood players, want to attract as many people as possible to theaters.



Previously, the next step was the opening of rights to sell DVD / Blu-ray, and only after that did the rights to digital display open. A few years ago, the picture began to change under the pressure of services that provide digital display services - Netflix, in the first place. Of course, we also contribute. Each large studio conducts an audit of online services for compliance with security standards, and I try to add the topic of shifting the release window to the negotiations. Now the situation has changed a lot: for example, films of Russian studios often go to online cinemas before the end of the rental in physical cinemas.



By the way, our market was influenced by a pandemic: the same Trolls went on physical rent a week before the closure of all cinemas, and after the closure they immediately appeared on online platforms. The industry is moving forward, but large companies - Sony, Fox and other Paramount - are quite conservative in themselves, and their point of view is changing slowly.



An online window for sale (with prices of several hundred rubles for each film) opens now a few months after the start of rental in cinemas, and only a few months later (sometimes a year later) do the rights to show the movie by subscription open. Many people ask why certain content is available only with direct payment, and not by subscription - in fact, this is the way cinema licensing works. Music catalogs are licensed differently. Often, large studios still do not allow to lay out the entire catalog at the same time in a subscription and require rotation of the content - that is, instead of opening access to all available films at the same time, the subscription opens up access to successive sets of films.



When will the user have the opportunity to return money for an uninteresting film? For example, with reference to the size of the returned funds to the percentage of viewing the movie.

This is an interesting feature, and many online movie theaters think about it. True, you need to understand: for such an interrupted viewing of the film (usually starting at 20%), the copyright holder should still receive the entire funds, and in this case the movie theater will have to pay them. Maybe such costs can be attributed to marketing. The total price of the film consists of the minimum window price (it can also be set by the copyright holder), deductions for subscription / sale and deductions to platform vendors (for example, Samsung SmartTV) - that is, a movie theater is obliged to pay a movie company and a platform vendor for every movie viewed.



Why is everything so complicated with movie rights? Why not like with music?

Now, in order to watch all new films and favorite TV shows, one has to subscribe to all streaming services. Any chance this will change in the future?



Moreover - in addition to subscriptions, you often have to pay extra. Netflix, which is held exclusively in a subscription format, solves this problem through its own production of content, available only through a Netflix subscription.



Users come to them precisely for this content, and not for the one that will be available a year after the release. Perhaps something will change in the future, but so far there are no prospects for ending content fragmentation (for example, in the US market it is divided between Amazon, Hulu and Netflix).



Is it possible to get a copy of a movie in Okko without DRM, without dubbing, with the original track and quality?



The source can take a terabyte, for example - therefore, the true source quality cannot be exactly. We prepare content for delivery on the Internet, cut it into several different bitrates, encode the 264/265 codec and put it on different platforms in different types of streams with support for adaptive bitrate. That is, while you look at the mobile application via WiFi, the bitrate adjusts to the connection speed of about 6 Mbps, and if you take the phone out of it, the quality drops to SD. Without DRM - it is impossible, this is prohibited by agreements with copyright holders. Although some films do not need to be protected - mainly those that are considered part of the world heritage. We have films with the original track, but I can‚Äôt say for sure how many there are.
