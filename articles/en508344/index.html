Tribal behavior: are we driven by stereotypes?

Throughout my life, I have noticed oddities in the behavior of my colleagues and friends. It seems that adequate people, getting into an unusual situation for themselves, began to behave completely different from what I expected from them.



  • An employee of my unit , with whom I worked for a couple of years, moves to another department and dramatically changes his view on the subject of discussion.
  • A silent friend , who always behaved quieter in the company of water, lower than the grass, begins to light in the stadium as the burnt fans do.
  • A colleague who has been promoted is abruptly stopped calling for lunch or coffee between meetings.


Later, after analyzing various situations after the fact, I found a general pattern in all the oddities that surprised me - strange behavior was always accompanied by the fact that the person at that moment associated himself not with the usual (for me), but with some other group of people, friends, colleagues.



I found the explanation in the book by Raymond Immelman, "BOSS: Peerless or Useless." This book, in the form of a business novel, describes how context-driven people behave. And knowing this context, it is highly likely to predict how a person will behave in a particular situation.



What I mean by "context", I will tell you under the cut.



We all realize that man is a social being. We need to have around us the same two-legged erect legs that can appreciate us, with whom we can talk, who we can love or even hate. Loners do not take root in society, they are rejected, they do not contribute to evolution.



And the oldest social education that exists is the Tribe .



If we look around, we will see that people here and there, get together in interest groups and build their relations in the same way as they built relations tens of thousands of years ago, at the dawn of civilization, members of the same tribe.

Now imagine - you are at a meeting of classmates in the company of your school friends. How will you behave? Just as it was customary to behave in this "tribe" when you were in school.



And if you came to a concert of your favorite band? You are already a member of another tribe with different rules. You will raise lanterns to a slow time or swing to the beat of the music.



Based on the analysis of a person's belonging to the Tribe, Immelman makes interesting statements:



  • Each person has an inborn need to belong to any social group - a tribe.
  • Confidence in a people‚Äôs tribe.
  • Belonging to a tribe enhances self-esteem of an individual
  • Belonging to a tribe increases individual security
  • The social , emotional and psychological life of a person is determined by the tribe to which he belongs.


Separately, I will highlight the statements that control the behavior:



  • Belonging to the tribe sets the stereotype of behavior
  • In the structure, when a person simultaneously belongs to many different tribes, his behavior is determined by a dominant (stronger) tribe
  • In different situations , stereotypes of behavior of different tribes may dominate.


Here it is. The context is changing, the association of man with the tribe is changing, the point of view is changing. Remember, I described examples above when people's behavior changed?



An employee of my unit, with whom I worked for a couple of years, moves to another department and dramatically changes his view on the subject of discussion.



What are we observing here - the employee changed the tribe, and his point of view began to shift to the one that prevails in his new tribe. The new context created a situation that either he accepts new views, or the new tribe does not accept him. Instinct forced him to behave in a new context.



A silent friend, who always behaved quieter in the company of water, lower than the grass, starts to light in the stadium as the scorched fans do.



Stadium. Match. Favorite team. Rose in hand, team colors on a t-shirt. That's all it takes for a friend to take control of the rules of a completely different tribe, and she begins to behave in accordance with them. Tomorrow, having come to the office, sitting down at her usual workplace, she will again become the very quiet that our team knows.



A colleague who has been promoted is abruptly stopped calling for lunch or coffee between meetings.



What happened here? It seems to be one company, why is behavior changing here? Here a colleague left the Performers tribe and moved to the Tribe of Leaders. And in the Tribe of Performers it is not customary to go to the dining room with members of another, not the fact that a friendly, tribe.



By analyzing the context , observing other people, you can predict the behavior of people. For example, in negotiations, realizing that the opponent associates himself with a tribe hostile to mine, it would be wrong to immediately begin to state your position - it will be accepted with hostility. I am the Enemy! You must first enter a position where we can both be associated with the same tribe - professionals, lovers of TV shows, fans of the same team, residents of the same city ... And then, you see, the conversation will go very differently.
