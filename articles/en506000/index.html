DIY ECG backpack


The campuses of modern colleges have one thing in common: intense students!

Today's students are often torn between several areas at once, and all because of pressure from both the social and the scientific side. Because of this, many students feel that they can not cope, and as a result show poor results both in science and in sports, and in communication.

This semester, our class studied various biometric instruments that can measure all kinds of biological signals emitted by the patient‚Äôs body, such as muscle contraction, brain waves, and heart waves. We wanted to develop a medical tool that could be used to track students' stress levels based on the pulse. Our ‚ÄúECG backpack‚Äù is equipped with an ECG sensor that monitors the patient‚Äôs heart rate and provides instant feedback through the LEDs and the phone app with which it communicates via Bluetooth. An ECG backpack will warn the user with the help of LEDs when his stress level is much higher than normal, based on a resting pulse. Data from a mobile application can be exported to Excel and used for further analysis.

Step 2: preparation



One heart cycle

To understand all the possibilities of an ECG backpack, you must first have a basic understanding of what it actually records an ECG. An ECG records electrical signals coming from the heart. In each heart cycle, the heart goes through several different systoles (contractions) and diastole (relaxations), and each of them corresponds to a certain wave. Studying the ECG, we see three main sections: the P-wave, the QRS complex, and the T-wave. They are shown in fig. higher. The time between the two QRS complexes allows us to determine the heart rate. All this will be taken into account in the code.

In addition to a basic understanding of the ECG method, you must be able to program the Arduino . You will need to add Flora board support.. And finally, you will need to download the library from GitHub for Adafruit Bluetooth.

Step 3: Security




When working with electronic circuits, some precautions must be observed:

  • By changing the circuit, make sure that the power is off.
  • Do not place liquids or food near the circuit.
  • Do not carry the ECG backpack during exercise so that the wires do not come off the board or sweat penetrates the electronics.
  • Work with insulated wires and tools.

Attention! This is not a medical device - it is just a tool to track your heart rate. See your doctor for more accurate measurements and tips.

Step 7: start assembling the backpack




Having familiarized yourself with the preparatory phase, safety and tips, you can start collecting an ECG backpack!

Step 8: solder the buttons to Adafruit Flora




Buttons help a lot to attach boards to the fabric. Adafruit has helpful tutorial material on buttons . It is important to solder very carefully, since an incorrect connection can ruin the entire circuit.

The microprocessor will need the following contacts:

  • Both pins are 3.3 V.
  • RX # 0.
  • TX # 1.
  • 2 earths (GND) (in our picture it is indicated 3, but we found that we can use only 2 - the lower left and upper left contacts).
  • Digital Pin # 9
  • Digital Pin # 10

Some contacts in the picture (3rd GND and SCL # 3 are not needed).

Step 9: Solder the buttons on the Bluefruit Bluetooth Module




Buttons are needed on the following contacts of the module:

  • Power Supply 3.3 V
  • TX
  • Rx
  • GND

Step 10: Sew Buttons and Connect Bluefruit Bluetooth to Flora




At first, it is very important to arrange the buttons so that they coincide with the location of the buttons on the microprocessor and the Bluetooth module. This can be done using chalk - rub each button with it, and then press the boards to the right places.

After that, sew the button with a conductive thread, starting from the back of the backpack, and then threading the thread through each of the four holes.

To connect the Bluetooth module and the Flora board to each other, a conductive thread must connect the corresponding buttons.

At the end of the process, tie a thread with a knot on the back of the backpack.

To connect the two boards, you need to run the threads between the following contacts:

  • Flora GND - Bluetooth GND
  • Flora TX # 1 - Bluetooth RX
  • Flora RX # 0 - Bluetooth TX
  • Flora 3.3V - Bluetooth 3.3V

In the photo above, the Bluetooth module is on the left, and the Flora board is on the right.

Step 11: add power to the circuit




Make a small hole under the Flora to stretch the wires from the battery. Pass the wires through the hole and connect them to the board. When the Flora board is turned on, the green LED should light up, and on Bluetooth, the red LED will flash.

Step 12: sew buttons to power the potentiometer and LED




Now we need to sew on the buttons located on top of the Flora board. Among them:

  • GND, which we will connect to the LED and potentiometer.
  • 3.3 V, which we will connect to the potentiometer.
  • pin # 10, which we will connect to the potentiometer.
  • pin # 9, which we will connect to the LED.

After sewing buttons and pulling wires to the back of the backpack, I recommend leaving a supply of thread so that you can continue sewing later. Some of these components will connect to the buttons on the other hand.

Step 13: Sew the internal buttons to which several components will be connected




Since several components will need to be connected to the ground and 3.3 V, you must add one more button inside the backpack so that several crocodiles can connect to it.

In the previous step, we left a supply of thread. Sew another button with it, just make sure that the wires do not cross each other.

Step 14: assemble the ECG cable




The cable consists of an ECG chip, an electrode cable with three contacts, and a UC-E6 cable. To connect the cable to the circuit, I connected the crocodiles to various ECG components:

  • The red ECG wire is to the red crocodile.
  • The black wire of the ECG is to the green crocodile.
  • The purple ECG wire is to the blue crocodile.

Step 15: continue to assemble the ECG cable




We continue to supplement the ECG cable by adding short wires to it with crocodiles. How I connected the wires:

  • Red ECG cable - red crocodile - orange wire.
  • Black cable ECG - green crocodile - yellow wire.
  • Purple ECG cable - blue crocodile - green wire.

For reliability, I wrapped the connections with electrical tape. These short wiring is needed to connect the circuit to the Flora board.

Step 16: cut a hole to route wires from the ECG to the top of the backpack




In the far corner of the front pocket, where all the wires are located, cut a hole connecting the pocket to the main compartment of the backpack. This will allow you to arrange in the main compartment the wires from the ECG electrodes and Bitalino. The photo above shows how the wires pass through the hole.

Step 17: route the wires for the ECG through the backpack so that the wires go through the straps




As shown above, after you pass the wires through the hole (circled in a red square), they must be routed through the main compartment so that they hang from above.

Step 18: pass the wires through the straps of the backpack




Cut a hole in the top of each of the straps where the lining is located. Route the wires down the straps to the indicated places.

The black electrode should be on the right side of the user and should be located above the red one. The red electrode should be on the left side and below. The line between them should form a diagonal running through the heart. The white electrode must be placed on the bone. It can be positioned at the top of the strap to fit on the shoulder bone.

Step 19: connect the ECG to Flora




As I described above, the ECG was connected to crocodiles, to which I then attached the wires:

  • Red ECG cable - red crocodile - orange wire.
  • Black cable ECG - green crocodile - yellow wire.
  • Purple ECG cable - blue crocodile - green wire.

After that, you need to bring the wires to pin No. 6 (analogue 7), ground (GND) and 3.3 V.

I made several small holes in the backpack so that the wires went from inside the pocket to the outside.

Here's how to connect wires to pins:

  • Green wire (purple ECG) - pin number 6.
  • Orange (red ECG) - 3.3 V.
  • Yellow (black ECG) - ground (GND).

Connected to the ground and 3.3 V wires, I wrapped around the corresponding buttons. The green wire is wrapped around pin No. 6.

Step 20: connect the potentiometer to Flora




The potentiometer must be connected to pin No. 10, ground and 3.3 V. These places are indicated by circles in the photo. Pin 10 is just a thread connected to this button and the Flora board. Download the potentiometer connection diagram for easier reference.

Step 21: make a small hole so that the user sees the LED




As shown in the photo above, make a small hole through which the LED light can penetrate. On the left is a photo after I made a hole. On the right - already with an inserted LED. To make it glow, I connected it and downloaded the code.

Step 22: Connect the LED to Flora




To connect the LED:

  • Pass the LED through the hole and press its legs so that they go parallel to the backpack.
  • Connect the short leg (cathode) to the crocodile, and then to the 220 Ohm resistor. Using a second crocodile, connect the resistor to the ground.
  • Connect the anode (long leg) of the LED to pin No. 9.

Step 23: upload the code to Flora!



In order to use Flora, you need to load the code into it. It can be downloaded from GitHub . The video provides detailed instructions for downloading and using the code.

Before downloading, you may need to install the necessary software. Instructions are available on the Adafruit website .

The backpack is almost ready!

Step 24: correct the code so that the LED turns on when the heart rate exceeds the normal value




The plate shows normal heart rate values ‚Äã‚Äãdepending on gender and age. Find your value and paste it into the code.

The upper limit of the pulse is set in the code, equal to 90. If you want to leave this border, then do not change anything. Otherwise, change the number in the following section of code:

if (HR > 90) { 
  digitalWrite(LEDpin, HIGH);
} // If the resting HR is above 90, or another preset HR, turn the LED on. HR number can be changed based off of chart in Instructable

Step 25: place the sticky electrodes on the body




Place the electrodes on the human body at the points indicated in the picture. The electrode marked with a green dot is located on the bone of the shoulder. The red dot indicates the black wire of our ECG, and the yellow dot indicates the red wire of our ECG.

Step 26: connect the cables from the backpack to the electrodes on the body




Electrodes are connected next to those places where they look out of the straps. Adjust the straps so that they suit the user and allow you to connect the electrodes located on the body.

Step 27: adjust the potentiometer




Connect Flora to the computer and download the code by clicking the arrow in the upper left corner. After loading, select ‚Äúserial plotter‚Äù (serial plotter). When turning the potentiometer, you should see a line on the graph. Make sure that the peaks of the QRS complex pass through the potentiometer line, as shown in the picture above, otherwise the heart rate calculation will not work accurately.

Step 28: Download Bluefruit LE Connect




Download the Bluefruit LE connect app . With it, you can download data to your phone.

Step 29: connect the phone to Bluetooth




Launch the app and connect to Adafruit Bluefruit LE with the settings shown above. Press Uart, and after that the data will appear. The heart rate will be the first number on the line.

Step 30: put on your backpack




Turn on Flora first. Then put on your backpack and carry it, trying to avoid jumping and running - such movements can lead to artifacts.

Step 31: export data




After collecting the data, click on the gear in the upper right corner of the application, and click Export. You can send a text file by email to yourself or to someone who wants to analyze them.

Step 32: examine the data in Excel




Copy data to Excel. Select all data and plot a line graph. The yellow line indicates the threshold of the potentiometer, and the gray line indicates the heartbeat. Pulse is defined as the number of contractions per minute, that is, how many times the gray line crossed the yellow line per minute.

Step 33: what can be improved




This scheme can be improved:

  • By placing the potentiometer in a more convenient place for tuning. Now every time for this I need to open a pocket with electronics.
  • Add a pressure gauge to track the number of breaths. This will allow you to track the increased respiratory rate and draw conclusions about the patient's stress and relaxation. Backpacks usually have fasteners connecting the straps on the chest, and it will be convenient to place such a sensor there.

Step 34: final product






The first photo shows the data received by the application via Bluetooth. At this time, the pulse was 96 beats per minute. I compared the data with the Apple Watch, and they are pretty good - in the same time period, the clock showed from 95 to 98 beats / min. The LED in the photo is on because the pulse exceeds 90 bpm. 90 in my case is a threshold value, and its excess indicates a high level of voltage and excitation.
