Physics of light: can there be waves if there is no sea?

Is the statement true that light is a wave? - Yes.



Does it follow from this that the entire space of the universe is filled with a certain sea - the ether - inside of which these waves go? - Not.

So, I think you already understood: in this article we will talk about playing music on the organ and wind instruments.



If a musician plays music using a string or bow instrument - for example, using a guitar, or using an indirectly string instrument such as a piano or piano, then what does the sound vibrate? As surely as with percussion instruments - in a sinusoid: the wave crest is followed by a wave failure. The upper point of the sinusoid is followed by the lower.



Can a guitar sound in a vacuum? - Not.

Can you catch a note played by a guitar (as an atom of matter does with a photon) and then emit it? - Not.

So how does light have these properties? I'll explain now.



Light waves are not such vibrations of the environment as air when playing the guitar.

Light waves are vibrations of the environment, as when playing the organ and wind instruments.

The organ, clarinet, pipe, flute and trombone do not sway the air like a string does.

They add air. Similar to how a person does it when he whistles.



We mistakenly imagine that a wave is always fluctuations in the height of the sea (or air density) down and up, one way and the other. However, if we pour a bucket of water into a puddle, then we will visually see a wave going only higher from the level it was. There is no part of the wave that would be below the level that was originally - after all, we added (emitted) a portion of water, and did not fluctuate the available water.



So is it possible to start a wave in a puddle that has already dried up - is it possible to start a wave if there is no environment? Well - if you just start jumping in the place of a dried puddle, then there will be no waves in it.



But if we pour the same bucket of water onto a flat hydrophobic surface, then there will be a wave. Thus, in the environment of objects of usual dimensions, it is possible to emit a wave from a particle (from a bucket), and the wave can propagate in the absence of any ether. Its absorption is also possible.



It is quite possible that a photon has bonds within its volume that allow it to be absorbed in its entirety - either by means of surface tension (analogy is a drop of mercury), or of a different nature (analogy is a rope, a chain, a fishing net).



Finally, the most difficult question is whether the interference of a wave with a wave is possible if they pass at different times? Yes, weak interference is possible if the medium is not empty, and the first wave leaves a mark on the sand.
