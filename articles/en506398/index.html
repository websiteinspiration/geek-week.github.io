In short


My current goal is not to whine that usability is not important ... Usability is very important if we consider it as half measures, and there are many examples of how poor usability makes airplanes fall, brings hunger, pestilence, etc. .d.

My goal today is to talk about the next level of software development problems that need to be solved after you have made the right interface: designing a social interface.

I guess I should explain what I mean.

In the 1980s, when usability was "invented", the whole point of software was the interaction of a computer with a person. A lot of software is still this. But the Internet has brought us a new kind of software: software based on human-human interaction.

Forums Social network. Internet ads. Oh yes, and email too. This is all software that mediates between people, not between a person and a computer.

If you write software that serves as an interaction between people, then after you bring usability to mind, you will also have to take care of the social interface. And the social interface is much more important. The best user interface in the world will not save software with a poor social interface.

The best way to illustrate this is to give some examples of unsuccessful and successful interfaces.

Social Software Design


Let me give you an example of designing a social interface.

Suppose your user does what he shouldn‚Äôt do.

According to the correct design of usability, you must explain to the user what he did wrong, and tell how to fix it. Usability consultants promote this under the brand name ‚ÄúProtective Design‚Äù.

And that would be too naive if you are working on social software.

Suppose a user is mistaken by posting Viagra ads on a forum.

You tell them: ‚ÄúSorry, Viagra is not a good topic. Your post has been declined. ‚Äù

Do you know what they will do? They will advertise V1agra. (Either that or they will start a long boring discussion about censorship and the First Amendment.)

When designing a social interface, you must consider sociology and anthropology. In society, there are freeloaders, scammers and other bad people. If we are talking about social software, then there will be people who try to use the software for their own benefit to the detriment of the rest of the forum participants. If this is not kept under control, this will lead to what economists call the ‚Äútragedy of the communities.‚Äù

While the goal of user interface design is to help the user succeed, the goal of the social interface is to help the community succeed, even if it means that one user must fail.

Therefore, a good designer of social interfaces will say: let's not display an error message. Let's just pretend that the post on Viagra has been adopted. We will show it to the author so that he is happy and proceeds to the next forum, which is not suitable for such posts. But leave this post invisible to everyone else.

Indeed, one of the best ways to repel attacks is to make them look successful. This is the software equivalent of pretending to be dead.

No, I will not give guarantees that this will work in 100% of cases. But in 95% of cases, this will definitely work and reduce the number of problems that you encounter by twenty times. Like everything else in sociology, this is a fuzzy heuristic algorithm. He works most of the time, so it's worth it even if he has no protection from the fool. New subject area


Designing a social interface is still in its infancy. I have not seen a single book on this subject; only a few people work in this field of research, and there is no organized science of designing social interfaces. At the dawn of usability design, development companies hired ergonomic experts and human factor experts to develop useful products. Ergonomics experts knew a lot about the correct height of a computer table, but they did not know how to develop graphical interfaces for file systems, which created a new area. In the end, a new discipline of user interface design was formed and brought out concepts such as correctness, accessibility, feedback, etc.,which have become a fundamental element of the science of user interface design.

I expect that over the next decade, software companies will hire people trained as anthropologists and ethnographers to design social interfaces. Instead of building usability laboratories, they will test everything in practice and write ethnography. I hope that we will be able to deal with the new principles of designing social interfaces. It will be fun ... as much fun as designing a user interface in the 80s ... so stay tuned.
