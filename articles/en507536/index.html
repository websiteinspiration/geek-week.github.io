Idea ‚â† Hypothesis



Before embarking on the main process, you need to introduce hypothesis-based thinking into the team. It is important to understand that the idea, in its original form, is not yet a hypothesis.



The main plus of hypotheses is that they are measurable. You know exactly what metrics you will improve if you start testing the hypothesis.



Hypothesis formula



‚ÄúThe action [X] will increase the metric [Y] by [Z], because [N].‚Äù



Let's look at an example of the differences.



Idea:

‚ÄúWe need to shorten the online application form from 5 to 4 steps, I think we will increase the conversion to registration.‚Äù



Hypothesis:

‚ÄúIf we reduce the online application form from 5 to 4 points, then we will increase the conversion to registration by 1 percentage point from 2% to 3%, because in the analytics system we see that some of the users leave at step 4, and without completing registration. ‚Äù



If the idea cannot be brought into the form of a hypothesis, then it is worth abandoning it. A correctly formulated hypothesis kills meaningless actions that do not affect metrics in any way.



Move on.



We form a backlog of hypotheses







We decided what a hypothesis is, and how it differs from an idea.

Now it's time to fill the backlog with hypotheses.



The backlog of hypotheses is a single base where our hypotheses are in an ordered form.



Remember that a hypothesis test costs money (time of a developer, analyst, marketer, designer, etc.), and if you understand that a hypothesis does not affect metrics in any way, do not add it.



All hypotheses, if we are talking about IT products, can be entered in the AAARRR funnel (in the illustration above, I schematically depicted it).

By analyzing each step of the funnel, we can find a larger number of growth points for each of the steps.



For example, we take the acqusition step and analyze all the channels of attraction by channels / campaigns / groups / creatives, etc., during the analysis we write down hypotheses that can significantly improve the attraction of new users.



What other tools will help us in finding growth points:



  • CJM (customer path in your product).
  • JTBD.
  • Quantitative analytics from the product.
  • UX analyzes and CustDev.


I will not describe in more detail about each of the ways, since the topics are very extensive and are worth separate articles.



Backlog recommendation:



  • The team must have a single backlog of hypotheses for transparent work and avoidance of takes.
  • Indicate to which part of the AAARRR funnel the hypothesis applies.
  • So, at first glance you will understand which of the levels of the funnel it affects.


Hypothesis prioritization and scoring for ICEs and PXL



We filled the backlog and distributed the hypotheses along the steps of the AAARRR funnel, now it is important to understand which hypotheses we are testing in the first place.



So, we smoothly come to prioritization and scoring.



Imagine that all employees of the department add hypotheses to the backlog and everyone wants his hypothesis to be the first in the queue for testing. To avoid misunderstandings and conflicts, the process of prioritizing and scoring hypotheses should be as transparent as possible. This will help the ICE Score framework.



ICE Score - this:



Impact - demonstrates how the idea will positively affect the key indicator that you are trying to improve.



Confidence - Demonstrates how confident you are in evaluating the impact and ease of implementation.



Ease(Ease of implementation) is an assessment of how much effort and resources are required to implement this idea.



Formula ICEs:



ICE Score = Impact x Confidence x Ease



How it works.



The team gets together, and each of its members evaluates Impact, Confidence and Ease on a scale of 1 to 10. Further, the scores of each employee are multiplied among themselves and added to the values ‚Äã‚Äãof other employees, so we get an ICE Score for each hypothesis.

Impact:



Everything is simple, the greater the impact on key metrics, the higher the score in the table.



Confidence:



There is only one way to calculate confidence - this is the search for supporting evidence. To do this, you can create your own card for the distribution of grades on a scale of 1 to 10, this will help the team put grades more transparently and eliminate the scatter of grades when one employee puts 1 and another employee - 10.



The card lists common types of tests and evidence, which you may have and the level of confidence that they provide: test results, launch date, personal confidence, market data, opinions of other people, etc. The

Ease:



Ease of implementation you evaluate in accordance with speed the work of your team.



Note that the time and scores for each team are indicated individually, if your hypothesis implementation is calculated in days, not hours - indicate the days.



Ease calculation example:

There are many types of scoring and prioritization, you can fine-tune the existing one or make your own framework that suits your team exactly. The main thing here is that scoring should help you in your work; you do not need to use the framework for the framework itself.



Experiment design



We prioritized the hypotheses, and now we need to design the experiment.



The design of the experiment is a detailed technical task for testing our hypothesis, and we need it in order to understand what we are testing, on which sample and which metrics we consider.



The design of the experiment is based on such a template:



  • Hypothesis.
  • What are we doing?
  • What users are testing on.
  • Key metrics to evaluate the experiment.
  • The expected effect.
  • Action plan depending on the results of the experiment.


Let us analyze in more detail an example:



Hypothesis:



We take the hypothesis from backlog.



In our case, it looks like this:



If we reduce the online application form from 5 to 4 points, then we will increase the conversion to registration by 1 percentage point from 2% to 3%, because in the analytics system we see that some of the users leave at the step 4, and without completing registration.



What we do:



Control version: we leave everything as it is.



Test version: reduce the online application form from 5 to 4 points.



On which users we test:



Only on new users.



Metrics:



Conversion to registration.



Action plan:



If our experiment is positive, and we see an improvement in metrics (CR in registration will be> 3%) - we scale to all users.



If the metrics fall, then roll back.



If the metrics do not change, then leave as is.



Before starting the experiment, be sure to discuss and agree on a plan of further actions that you intend to take upon receipt of the results. So you get rid of situations where the test passed, and Product Owner in the end is not going to implement the experiment.



Test preparation



Before you start an experiment, you need to take some preparatory steps.



  1. Decide on the type of testing.
  2. Calculate sample size, confidence interval, and power.
  3. Calculate the duration of the experiment.
  4. Prepare test tools.
  5. Make sure the experiment is clean.
  6. Set up analytics systems.
  7. Set up segments for testing.


Let's take all the steps in order.



1. Decide on the type of testing



Choose which test option is right for you.



Here are a few options:



  • A / B testing.
  • MVT (multivariate testing).
  • Deteriorating A / B testing.
  • A / A / B testing.


In our example, we will consider the most frequently used of the options - A / B testing.



2. Calculate sample size, confidence interval, and power



Before starting, determine how many users you need to attract to each of the A / B test options so that the data in the end is statistically significant.



We will use the Evan Miller calculator to calculate the sample (see the screenshot below).



In ‚ÄúBaseline conversion rate‚Äù we indicate the conversion of the control version, in our case 2%.



In ‚ÄúMinimum Detectable Effect‚Äù we indicate how many percent the conversion of the control version will increase, in our case by + 1%.



Significance level Œ± (confidence level) is the level of risk that you take with errors of the first kind (rejection of the control version of the test, if it is correct), usually Œ± = 0.05.



This means that in 5% of cases you will find the difference between A and B, which is actually due to chance.

The lower your significance level, the lower the risk that you will discover the difference caused by chance.



Statistical power 1 ‚àí Œ≤ (statistical power) are errors of the second kind, the probability that we will accept the test version on the sample if it is actually true (a chance to detect the effect, if it really is). When planning an experiment, it must be remembered that the power must be reasonably high in order to detect deviations from the control version. If you do not know what percentage of the indicator should be indicated, leave the default values ‚Äã‚Äã(80%).



As a result, we understand that for the correct test we need 3,292 users for each of the A / B test variants.







Next, we can calculate how many conversions we need to get, and what the difference between them should be, to obtain a statistically significant result.



We will use the Sequential Sampling calculator.







For the correct analysis, we should get 170 conversions, and the difference between the versions of the A / B test should be more than 26 conversions.



4. Calculate the duration of the experiment



Take the sample size obtained in the calculation for testing each version, and divide it by your daily traffic, so you get the number of days required for the test.



Test at least one full week, as weekend data may differ from weekdays.



5. Prepare test tools



We calculated how much traffic and conversions are needed for a statistically significant experiment, now we need to choose the tools for testing.



The toolkit consists of two main blocks:



1. The source of traffic.



Prepare the source of traffic, set up advertising campaigns.



2. Traffic distribution systems between versions A and B.



Here you can go in two ways: make your own tool for distributing traffic between versions using the help of developers, or use third-party services, such as:



  • Google Optimize (a free service located in GA)
  • Kameleoon.
  • Optimizely






6. Make sure the experiment is clean.



Before starting the test, make sure that there is no difference between the two test versions.

There should be no technical differences in versions or traffic, since any differences affect the purity of the experiment.



For maximum cleanliness, A / A testing is performed before starting. Unlike A / B testing, in A / A we test two identical pages (elements). The purpose of the experiment is not to see differences in their performance.



7. Set up analytics systems



Recheck analytics systems, make sure that all the metrics needed for the experiment work correctly.



A common mistake is to run the test without checking the analytics and metrics systems, as a result, experts spend time, budgets, and everything is done in vain.



8. Configure segments for testing



Returning to the design of the experiment, let us recall on which users we conduct the experiment.



We indicated: "Only on new users."



In a traffic distribution system or ad system, we need to clear out returning users.



And to make absolutely identical advertising campaigns (if you conduct the test not on organics).



Experiment



Before starting, check if everything really works correctly. Check again and run the experiment.



As the experiment progresses, monitor traffic problems and testing systems, and fix them as they arise.



Recommendations for the experiment.



1. Do not draw premature conclusions.



Bring the experiment to the end, even if on the first day you see a statistically significant improvement in the test version.



The premature decision problem is called the ‚ÄúPeeking problem‚Äù or the ‚ÄúPeeping problem‚Äù.



The more often you look at the intermediate results of the A / B test with the willingness to make a decision based on them, the higher the probability that the criterion will show a significant difference when it is not there.



Look at the picture below, it perfectly illustrates the whole essence of the "Peeking problem".







If we did not know about the problem of snooping, we completed the experiment on the fourth day, deciding that the test version is much better, and there is no point in conducting the experiment further.

But having completed the experiment to the end, we will understand that there is no stable measurable difference between the groups.



You need to understand that the difference in conversions may periodically go beyond the boundaries of the indistinguishability zone as observations accumulate, even if we test identical versions.

This is completely normal, since the boundaries are formed in such a way that when testing identical versions, only in 95% of cases the difference appears within them.



In order not to fall for the ‚ÄúPeeking problem‚Äù hook, before starting we calculate the sample size and duration of the experiment. Follow your plan.



2. Have a test emergency shutdown system



If you are testing on a self-service service, then it is advisable to have an emergency shutdown system for testing and analyzing anomalies, since the experiment can break the product.



Analysis and statistical significance



We conducted an experiment and received data.



Now we need to understand that the change we received in the metrics between versions A and B is related to our hypothesis or to chance.



In other words, whether the change is statistically significant or not.



In our example, we received the following data:



Sample A: 3,292 sessions, 67 conversions.

Sample B: 3,200 sessions, 99 conversions.



We will use the calculator to calculate statistical significance.







P-value = 0.000691, that is, the probability of seeing the observed difference with identical test groups is very small. This means that it is possible to relate the growth of the metric with our changes with a high degree of certainty.



A / B test confirmed our hypothesis, the test version is statistically significantly better than the control.



Having a statistically significant result, do not rush to scale it to all users. Do not take any chances and double-check the data received.



Make sure there are no intersections of samples and anomalies.



It should not be such that the same user falls into two samples, otherwise you risk the reliability of the data received.



But what if there is no difference between the versions, or if the test version is significantly worse?





Get ready for the fact that our version of the experiment may lose, which is quite normal with frequent testing.



At the end of the experiment, retrospectively analyze why your hypothesis has lost.



Go deep into the data, do a user survey, try to understand why the test version does not work as expected. Maintain a common knowledge base (Confluence) with the team, the place where you outline the initial expectations of the experiment and the subsequent result. All this data, in turn, will help you in the following experiments.



We are all mistaken, and this is normal.



People generally tend to overestimate their ability to rational and correct decisions.



There is an excellent aphorism about this: ‚ÄúMistakes are a development tax.‚Äù



Experiment implementation



After a positive experiment, its implementation should go (replacement of the control version with the test one).



Conducting an experiment without further action is pointless.



It often happens that when we obtain a statistically significant result (our hypothesis was confirmed), we cannot implement it, because initially all stages of coordination did not go through. Consider in advance all possible implementation problems.



Instead of a conclusion



Regardless of whether your test was successful or not, treat each experiment as an opportunity for learning.



Use what you have learned in working with the following hypotheses.



Good luck with further experiments!
