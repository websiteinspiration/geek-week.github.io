Who are we


Hello everybody! We are Ekaterina Galitskaya and Daria Egorushkina from Kaspersky Lab (documentation and localization department). A little more specific: the team in which we work is responsible for writing and localizing interface texts and help for mobile applications.

Pain


The main trigger for change was development needs. Development switched to frequent releases once every two weeks. Scope decreased, but they began to translate more often, and we had to do it faster. In fact, localization has become a narrow neck of development. And if earlier the project managers didn‚Äôt even know the names of the localizers - and why at all, because the translations magically appeared themselves - now almost everyone was aware of the problems and even knew what linguistic testing is :)

The timing


The localization cycle took 3 weeks:

  • 3-5 days - transfer;
  • 2 weeks - linguistic testing.

With the translation, everything is clear, but why linguistic testing, and what is it all about?

The main goal of linguistic testing is to check the translation in context, that is, to really make localization. The translators knew our terminology, but still they simply translated the text, not seeing that it was a button or a heading, which text was next.

In addition, linguistic testing allows you to catch inconsistencies, under-translation, text that is not put in lines (hard-coded text), reduce legal risks (when payment texts, for example, are not placed in the right field). Linguistic testing is usually done using screenshots.

Volumes


There is a myth that if the application is mobile, then it is small, and what is there to translate?
Haha Some statistics:

  • texts in the interface - an average of 25 thousand words in the project;
  • 10 applications;
  • on average 19 localizations in each project;
  • updating texts in the interface, translation of documentation every week.

Why could not accelerate?


Let's see what each of the stages of localization consisted of. Translation step (9 steps):

  1. pick from VCS manually from different brunches;
  2. manually create a translation delta;
  3. create translation packages;
  4. upload to FTP;
  5. write a bunch of letters to agencies, freelancers and local offices;
  6. after transfer pick up from FTP, upload to CAT, check;
  7. put in VCS - do not get confused in brunches;
  8. start assembly, fix errors, rebuild assembly;
  9. start additional translations and bug fixes in those cases when the translation process had to be restarted.

Translation stage problems: in short, it is a limitation of old processes and a lot of routine work when using old CAT:

  • The collection of lines from several brunches is not supported - the delta for translation from all brunches was formed manually, and the translation was manually laid out in brunches. It was hard to maintain, easy to get confused, and impossible to forget this horror.
  • Maintaining uniformity within the project and in languages ‚Äã‚Äãin manual mode was not possible.
  • You cannot run parallel translation in parallel - update source resources during the translation process. It was necessary first to receive the first bundle of translation and only after that start additional translation.
  • Cases of assembly failures due to errors in variables, apostrophes, and due to other localization errors have become more frequent.

Problems of the linguistic testing stage: manual screenshots took the lion's share of the time. If the feature has about 40 screens, and 20 languages, then it could reach up to 70 hours of manual screenshots ...

In addition, there was a human factor.

It is one thing to go through these steps once every three months. Another thing is to repeat all this every two weeks. With each new iteration, the localizers plunged into the swamp of routine - send-accept-remove-repeat.

We had to look for a solution, and at the same time pretty quickly. What were the solution options? It could be:

  • hire more students;
  • reduce the number of localization works (and, therefore, squander quality);
  • Automate routine tasks.

We settled on the latter.

What did you want


We did not have a hundred years to sit down, pour a cup of coffee, roll up our sleeves and begin to analyze the entire cloud solutions market within a year. We were looking for a ready-made solution to start working tomorrow. Our goal was to solve the problem.

What other requirements did we have:

  • Less approvals : in order not to wait until the purchase is agreed, they will write out the keys and that‚Äôs all.
  • Ready basic function l: to sit down and start doing. Which does not need to be written from scratch. Stable. The rest can be twisted along the way.
  • It does not require huge server capacities : again, so as not to get bogged down in lengthy approvals.
  • Inexpensive (preferably free) starting entry to the service.
  • There was no need for an inhouse developer : that is, adequate server-side support and the ability to deploy it yourself.
  • Compliance of the service with the requirements of internal security : we connect to the service, and not it to us.
  • Support for simultaneous work with multiple brunches : translation of several features in parallel.
  • Parallel launch of additional transfers .

Of the various options, we looked most closely at Zing (a translation service from Evernote developers).

From the pros :
  • customizability for yourself;
  • free installation pack - only server capacities were needed;
  • no monthly fee;
  • connecting your translators;
  • private access (can be hosted internally).

Cons : in order to connect translators and open them access, it was necessary to connect at least two units. Which sharply raised the cost of the service in terms of time and resources. ‚Ä®

What have you chosen


Since we cannot directly connect the CAT system to the internal version control system, we needed a different connector. You can write yourself or take an existing one. So we tested a bunch of Git - Serge - Smartcat.

From the pros :

  • Support for working with multiple brunches.
  • Update resources on the fly.
  • Independence from CAT parsers (writing configuration files on our side). Smartcat leaves PO files.
  • Correspondence with freelancers is practically ‚Äúin one window‚Äù.
  • There is a search and selection of freelancers (direct communication, selection for the needs of the project - in our case, the speed and quality of the translation is important).
  • You can pay for work in all languages ‚Äã‚Äãand projects in one account.
  • At our request, they raised the priority in the development of new features: introduced new features (search for text in all project files, etc.), fixed some problems.
  • Quick TechSupport - help in setting up.
  • Actually free access to the service (subscription is optional).
  • Checks.

Cons :

  • There was no text search within the entire project (and there may be more than 1000 files in the project). But Smartcat developers introduced this feature at the end of last year.
  • You cannot open multiple documents in one browser tab.
  • Resource files (documents in Smartcat) in one language can be up to 200. The user needs to make corrections to the translations after checking the text in the screenshots. The user does not know in which document the segment is. Therefore, the user needs to open all 200 documents and look for this line.
  • There remains a problem with notifications for freelancers: they turn them off and do not receive notification of a document update. In this case, we still write in the chat.

What localization looks like now (9 steps for everything):

  1. The writer commits new lines in Git. Strings are automatically processed and fly away in Smartcat.
  2. The localizer appoints translators (this step will be gone soon, is it true guys from Smartcat?))))
  3. Translators translate not just like that, but with screenshots - that is, in context.
  4. Localizers check the translation (make a complete file). The robot takes back the translation not by line, but when the work on the entire file is finished. The translation automatically flies back and submits to Git.
  5. Localizers run autoscreenshots.
  6. Localizers upload screenshots to FTP.
  7. Localizers answer the questions of linguists.
  8. Localizers, if necessary, make changes to Smartcat. Edits are automatically committed to Git.
  9. Localizers close pull request.

Of course, there is still a field for automation and improvements. But you can already feel the difference with what was first.

What is Serge


This is an open source solution, a connector between a version control system (SVN, Git, Gerrit (Git-based code review system), Mercurial) and TMS, in our case Smartcat.

Why we are ‚Äúlogged in‚Äù: all cloud TMS have a connector out of the box. But such boxed connectors connect directly to the repository. Which is impossible in our case. What are the options:

  • disclose part of the version control system;
  • Clone folders with resource files for public access;
  • receive and process resource files before sending to TMS, then export to TMS.

Revealing part of the system is risky.

It‚Äôs possible to make a clone, only this requires temporary and human resources.

Serge is just able to receive resource files and process them before sending to TMS. As a result, the architecture is as follows: Git - Serge - TMS.

Serge takes files from Git and processes them according to certain rules. Then it converts them to PO format and sends them to Smartcat. Serge gets the translated PO files from Smartcat, converts them and commits to Git.

Also, Serge‚Äôs big plus for us is that it is deployed within our company. Thus, the entire "kitchen" remains behind a stone wall. Nothing secret comes out :)

Main features:

  • Match sors target by file and resource string ID.
  • The ability to select files by mask in the path or by content.
  • Processing the contents of resource files before / after parsing.
  • Configuring parsers.

You can get acquainted with other features of Serge on the website or watch the video .
