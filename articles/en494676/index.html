The most powerful Xbox ever


Just starting work on the Xbox Series X, the team was determined to create the most powerful Xbox in history, and this led to a discussion on how to determine power in the new generation of consoles. In previous generations, power was determined primarily by graphic innovations: the transition from 8-bit to 16-bit graphics, then from 2D to 3D, from SD to HD, and finally to 4K.

Today, gamers want as many games as possible to run at 60 fps (frames per second), with high visual accuracy and a clear, effective response. The developers proposed creative solutions, such as dynamic scaling of resolution, which allows to maintain high image quality at a high frame rate, but very often this is done bypassing the limitations and obstacles in existing systems. All this will change with the release of the Xbox Series X. Not only the appearance of the games will be improved, but the entire gameplay.

The team considers such parameters as resolution and frame rate to be creative solutions that are better left to the game developers, but it was necessary to ensure that the system could provide the proper quality for the most powerful blockbusters, e-sports disciplines and innovative adventure games.

Improved immersive effect and breathtaking speed


The next important aspect of the Xbox Series X is speed, which can be measured in a variety of ways. Modern devices have changed our expectations: most of us now want to be able to instantly switch between applications and continue to play from where we left off. Therefore, the developers designed the system architecture in such a way that you could spend more time in the game and less in anticipation.

In many ways, this has become possible thanks to the use of a solid state drive (SSD). Traditional hard drives have already exhausted almost all available performance limits, so investing in data transfer at the SSD level was simply necessary to ensure the proper quality, given the plans for the Xbox Series X. The developers put special emphasis on these changes, since it was clear that the key to a new level of performance lies here. But they did not want the upload and download speeds to affect only how fast the games started.

Thus, the revolutionary new Xbox Velocity architecture was created for gaming  . with close integration of hardware and software solutions. This will open a new, previously inaccessible opportunity in the development of consoles - instant access to 100 GB of virtual memory for developers. The components of the Xbox Velocity architecture are involved in such a way that it was possible to run multiplayer games entirely based on physical memory, and this literally changes everything.



‚ÄúThe central processing unit (CPU) is the brain of the new console, the graphic processor (GPU) is its heart, and the architecture of the Xbox Velocity is the soul,‚Äù said Andrew Goossen, technical partner at Xbox Series X at Microsoft. - The architecture of the Xbox Velocity does not just provide speed. This is one of the main innovations of the new console. It completely changes the way games are created. Giant and most realistic worlds await us with her. ‚Äù

First of all, these technological improvements will affect the quality of games with large-scale worlds that offer players freedom of movement and discovery. Games such as Final Fantasy XV, Assassin's Creed Odyssey and Red Dead Redemption 2 have determined what dynamic, life-full worlds should be like on the new generation of consoles.

To make these worlds even more dynamic, large-scale and detailed, you need a significant increase in computing power and the ability to reproduce as quickly as possible, without violating the effect of presence (without long trips on the elevator or long empty corridors, which developers usually mask loading processes). Developers will also be able to effectively reduce loading times between levels and create truly fast moving systems.

Speaking of speed, one cannot but mention the delay time.

‚ÄúDemanding eSports fans need clear, responsive management,‚Äù Goossen said. ‚ÄúThe Xbox development team has analyzed all the elements involved in the gameplay - from the controller to the console and from the console to the display - we set ourselves the goal of using every opportunity to reduce the delay time to ensure the best gaming experience on the Xbox.‚Äù



To do this, the team created a new Dynamic Latency Input (DLI ) technology  , and also made some changes to HDMI 2.1, adding new game-oriented features such as 120 Hz support,  Variable Refresh Rate (VRR)  and automatically switch to a low latency mode (the Auto-Mode the Low Latency, ALLM) . In addition, over the past two years, developers have collaborated with leading TV manufacturers to prepare modern screens for the new features of the Xbox Series X.

When using each of the above technologies individually, it would be difficult to notice some effect, but when they work together, the result becomes much more responsive management.

And finally, thanks to the speed of the Xbox Series X, a number of opportunities for players have been significantly improved. The most noticeable thing is  the  boot time , significantly reduced due to the processing power of the Xbox Series X.

About the new Quick Resume technology   already told earlier. On today's consoles, you can continue the game from the moment you left off. However, most players on average run three to four games a month, so the developers decided to provide the ability to easily and quickly switch between them. With the Quick Resume feature, you can resume several games at once with the click of a button and instantly switch between them right from where you left off.

Since the progress of each game will be stored directly on the system SSD, it will remain with you even after turning off the console, completely disconnecting it from the network and after updating the system. One of the testers turned off his console for a week, and then updated the system, after which he was able to continue the game from the place where he stopped - no download was required.
