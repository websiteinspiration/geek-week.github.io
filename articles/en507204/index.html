Industrial design interior kitchen: from sketch to product in box



It happens that at the first meeting, customers say ‚Äúwe only need a picture‚Äù, ‚Äúwe will save on design‚Äù, ‚Äúwe just throw an idea‚Äù, ‚Äúoh, what to do there.‚Äù Few people imagine the full picture of what is happening in the interior kitchen of the designer, especially industrial. In this article, we will reveal the whole process - from the first sketches (and even earlier) to the start of mass production - and explain why industrial design is not just a picture.

The industrial designer is responsible for future production and partly for the successful sale of a new product, its work can be compared with a detective investigation. Where does it all start?

Stage 1. Questions for the first meeting and project launch


The customer comes up with an idea for a new product or a problem that arose during the project implementation phase. For example: "we tried to draw a design ourselves, but it didn‚Äôt work out very well, we decided that we still need a designer."

The designer listens carefully to his client, asks key questions:

  1. For whom and for what is this product? What user problem does it solve, what functions does it perform? Who are these users?
  2. How will the product be used, in what conditions?
  3. Where and how will the production be organized: serialization, materials, budget restrictions?
  4. Do you have your own corporate identity? The history of the product line and, possibly, the brand philosophy?
  5. Who are your main competitors - direct and indirect (they may have a completely different product, but they work with your audience)?

As a result of the conversation, the designer has a list of additional questions to which, as a private detective, he will look for answers in the course of his analysis-investigation.

The client leaves with the phrase ‚Äúmake us beautiful,‚Äù and the designer delves into each of these five areas: user, product, production, style and identity, competitors. Let's follow the course of his thoughts:



1.1. Users Product Target Audience


Who will use the new product? Children? Technologists? Older people who need a very clear interface? What country are they from, with what mentality? Or is it a product for the global market?

The culture and conditions in which users live set their own framework for products, it is important to consider them. Example: a few years ago, we took a new VR helmet with us to electronics exhibitions. Inside was a virtual tour of our office and a portfolio of developed devices. In Europe and the USA, people with great interest studied the jobs of engineers and our devices in virtual space, and at an exhibition in Dubai, almost all local visitors refused to wear a common helmet (long before the COVID-19 pandemic).

The customer himself does not always fully understand the needs of users of his new product, because focuses mainly on themselves, their acquaintances or a specific target audience with which they are used to working (but these are not all people who may be interested in a new product).

And here the designer is connected again, in the process of market research, reviews and conducting problematic interviews, he can clarify and expand the audience of potential users of the product.



1.2. Product features and usage scenarios: where, how, why


At this stage of the investigation, the designer answers the following questions:

  • What problem does the new product solve? How?
  • Which product features are primary and which are secondary? How to make them intuitive so that the product can be used without instructions?

During the design process, a good designer takes into account the requirements of ergonomics, i.e. provides convenient user interaction with the product in specific conditions: sitting or standing in the office, walking along the street or even making tea.


Figure: main points for taking into account ergonomics of the workplace.
Angus McIntyre & Mattthew, CC BY-SA 3.0

Ergonomics combines the research of many sciences: psychology, medicine, biomechanics, anthropometry and sociology. The designer adapts the appearance of his product and its design to the size of the person and his ability to perceive certain physical and psychological stresses. The goal of ergonomics is to provide a safe and comfortable environment for work and leisure.

Example: the angle of the touch screen in the information panels is calculated based on the average height of people in a particular country or region. Mass products cannot be equally well adapted for all users, because designers are guided by percentile curves with indicators of growth, weight and other parameters. The result is a product that is suitable for about 80% of people (10% at the beginning and 10% at the end of the curve are excluded from the calculations, so most of the mass-produced goods are not suitable for the ‚Äúsmallest‚Äù and ‚Äúlargest‚Äù people).

1.3. Production


Even at the stage of preliminary research, the designer takes into account seriality, materials and limitations on the project budget. This is the standard practice of the DFM approach (design for manufacturing), which we already talked about in the article ‚ÄúHow to Develop an Electronic Device Using DFM Principles‚Äù .



1.4. Corporate identity and brand philosophy


If the customer company has its own corporate identity, the existing product line and brand philosophy, then the designer has a ready-made identity and work style.

If not, it‚Äôs also good, more freedom for experimentation.

In industrial design, the form, the sensation of the weight of the item, the texture and color work. Not only the psychology of color, i.e. influence on the emotions of the user, but also scenarios for using the device (color as a hint to the user).

You probably saw the designers of this colorful fan, if you ordered furniture or materials for the home. Why is the physical palette still in use? The fact is that the screens of computers and smartphones will display the same color in different ways due to different calibrations and color profiles. And the colors on the paper palette will be the same anywhere in the world: both the designer and the client will be able to refer to a specific color in a personal or online meeting.

In addition to the general principles of the company - its corporate identity and identity - the customer himself and his personal preferences influence the project. At the first meeting, we allowed him to freely express any, even the most daring, ideas and wishes. At the stage of analyzing sketches, we will work with these expectations: acquaint with references, i.e. similar products of other companies or prototypes from other areas, which we take as the basis of our design. For example, when designing the body of a router for a yacht, a designer can use the body shape of marine animals as a prototype.

Beginning designers often face internal resistance when their taste does not match the taste of the customer. There is a sense of competition. But with experience comes self-confidence: a good designer does not go into confrontation with the client, but becomes his ally in solving problems on the project. When you and the client are on one side of the barricades, everything changes: you do not argue about colors and shapes, about personal preferences, but focus on business goals and discuss how certain decisions can affect the results of the project.

For example: ‚ÄúOf course, we can make this button pink and this green, but analysis of available technologies and product usage scenarios shows that these buttons can be implemented not physically, but programmatically - on the touch screen.‚Äù

1.5. Competitors


We analyze the market:

  • What product does it have analogues - serial devices for sale or design concepts?
  • What are their successes and failures, how do users respond to them, and why?

The designer studies the materials of seller companies and user reviews, analyzes the price range and technical parameters. This information will be useful for the next meeting with the customer and will help determine the most important limitations for the development of a new product: will we compete in quality, price or other parameters? The most interesting task for a designer is to find problems in competitor's products and solve them.

Stage 2. Terms of Reference


Based on the results of studies, it is possible to prepare a statement of work (TOR), which will indicate the operational, technical and aesthetic requirements for the product. To create a competitive product, they must be at or above market peers. As a rule, technical specifications are drawn up together with the customer, taking into account his wishes.



Stage 3. Search for ideas and concept development


So, at the first stage, we conducted a study, at the second - we made a technical specification, and only now the designer will make the first sketches.

Contrary to the expectations of some clients, the designer is not a printer; he cannot draw two A4 color sheets in 10 minutes after the first meeting.

Imagine: before you is a blank sheet on which you need to depict ideas for a new product. Hundreds of analogues and prototypes are collected in your head, you are well versed in the topic, there is an understanding of market trends. But still. It will take more than one hour and not one day before those that you present at the meeting to the customer, having previously worked out the color and shadows, appear in the pile of sketches made.


Figure: examples of sketches for a new product - a fish finder

During the sketching, the designer is armed not only with his market research and the wishes of the customer, but also with interesting ideas from other areas, even the most distant ones. This can be technology, nature, architecture, decorative products, art.

In the process of sketching, much more ideas are born than in the process of observing or collecting information. Quick sketches help to understand the direction, dimensions of the product, it is better to present it in work. Already at this stage, oddly enough, we choose materials for the case. So we can sketch based on the physical properties of the material, counting on the further production of the prototype and the launch of the series.



As a result, the customer will see two or three options - clear, colorful, but not printed in 10 minutes.

The purpose of the analysis of the first sketches is to select together with the client the best solutions for further development.

Stage 4. Create a three-dimensional model of the device



In the photo: three-dimensional modeling based on the approved design concept The

3D model helps both the designer and the customer visualize the idea in more detail, transfer it from a flat sheet to three-dimensional space.

It happens that an idea that seemed perfectly worked out in pencil at the modeling stage is not so successful and needs to be improved.

3D modeling and subsequent visualization of the project are a kind of bridge between the designer and the audience.

Stage 5. We design


The designer sends the finished 3D model to the designer. Now at least three people work in the team: designer, constructor and engineer. The engineer‚Äôs task is to provide all the information about the hardware of the device: the exact dimensions of the printed circuit board, ventilation requirements, etc.


Explosion diagram of a walkie-talkie body - this is how the finished body design looks like, taking into account the inset parts and other components

At this stage, it is important to implement field supervision, i.e. control the design development process so as not to distort the appearance of the product. The fact is that when preparing the device for production, the designer takes into account the limitations of technological processes: lays certain angles for plastic molding in the mold, thinks over the joints of the parts and the mold connector lines, can add stiffeners inside the housing to provide the necessary strength. All these manipulations are described in detail in two articles:

  • Injection molding: how it works
  • Modeling of physical processes in the development of electronics: why and for what?

Without supervision, these changes may not have the best effect on the aesthetics of the product; therefore, it is important to coordinate them with the designer. And this is another reason why the ‚Äúwe only need a picture‚Äù approach does not work. Industrial design is not only a picture, but a product suitable for production. It is the designer who should be responsible for the fact that the device released from the conveyor will be as close as possible to the original plan.

Not every customer likes to delve into the filling of their product. The task of the designer and constructor is to present a ready-made solution and explain how everything works inside the case.



Stage 6. Visualization, create a photorealistic render of the product.


Visualization (rendering) allows you to get a photorealistic image of the future product and in all details study its color and materials even before the creation of the prototype and the start of production.

Another task of the render is to demonstrate the product to consumers or investors. A presentable picture forms the interest and trust of future buyers, increases the likelihood of successful sales or investments.

Stage 7. Production of the prototype device



In the photo: in the foreground - a prototype of the echo sounder case, grown in a 3D printer, in the background - a finished product made by three-component casting

So, we go to the finish line - we create a prototype of the device and prepare the product for production. We have already talked about the advantages and disadvantages of various technical processes in the article ‚ÄúPrototype of the housing for electronics: grow or mill‚Äù , so this time we‚Äôll only highlight the key issues that the project team discusses when studying the received sample:

  • Does the product work? Is the case going?
  • What needs to be adjusted? What can be removed or added?
  • How can a product be improved?

Even if the prototype seems complete and ready to enter the market, a good designer will find an opportunity to make it even better.

Stage 8. We start production


The circle is closed, we went on to the start-up stage of the conveyor, which was described in detail in the last article, ‚ÄúThe housing for an electronic device: how to design and manufacture it in China . ‚Äù

The successful launch of mass production is the result of the work of an industrial designer, which, of course, is impossible without the support of his colleagues, designers, engineers, production technologists and a project manager who monitored the timing and coordinated work of this entire team.

So, you have a finished serial product in a box. The case is solved! As Ralph Kaplan writes in his popular books on design: ‚ÄúIt's hard to think about design, but not to think about it even worse: the consequences can be disastrous.‚Äù
