Bonuses, benefits and bonuses in IT: the results of Habr Career research



Employers compete with each other for the best employees, offering in exchange for their work not only a salary, but also interesting tasks and modern technologies, professional and career development opportunities, comfortable working conditions and a cool team, various benefits and bonuses. 

Today we’ll talk about benefits and bonuses. We conducted a survey among IT specialists, in which we found out to whom what bonuses are paid and for what, how much IT people are satisfied with these bonuses. 

From the received answers, we made visual diagrams and want to share the insights received.

How common are bonuses, perks and bonuses


How many companies that employ IT professionals have bonuses, benefits and bonuses? By privileges we mean compensation for medicine, education, nutrition and other services. Under bonuses are cash payments that occur when certain events occur, for example, a birthday or a new year.

It turns out that benefits are the most common practice, three out of four companies have them, outsourcing companies with this are a little worse, two out of three have benefits. 

Every second product and outsourcing company has bonuses, non-IT companies with this are much better, four out of five companies have bonuses. 

Paying cash bonuses is the rarest practice; it is found in less than half of companies.


Note that more than half of domestic specialists work in food companies. The other half is divided approximately equally between outsourcing and non-IT companies.

It is important to understand this so that you can figure out what experience of prizes IT specialists most often encounter in their companies.


Bonuses, benefits and bonuses are aimed at increasing the loyalty of employees to the company in which they work. Whether this works, we can find out by asking employees how satisfied they are with the benefits they receive.

As you can see, the average ratings of satisfaction with bonuses are the highest and do not differ in companies of different types. But satisfaction with bonuses and benefits in food companies is the highest, and in non-technical companies the lowest. Although the difference is not very large.



What about the benefits


What benefits do companies offer their employees? What are they willing to compensate in full or in part? And what can affect satisfaction with benefits? 

In the survey, we asked to indicate what benefits your company has and then looked at the number of answers, not the companies. That is, they looked at what kind of experience employees most often face, and not how many companies offer such experience.

Most often, employees receive compensation from medical companies from companies, but in outsourcing companies this benefit is much less common. 

Next comes the compensation of conferences and training, here the leaders are food companies in which this benefit is found more often than in other companies.

This is followed by compensation for fitness services, but in non-technical companies this benefit is much less common than in others.


If you put all the companies together, we get the next top benefits that IT employees receive from their employers.



Some companies have the opportunity to choose their own benefits, this is called the "cafeteria benefits." This practice is not very common, as it requires additional financial and organizational costs from the employer. As you can see, no more than 20% of companies practice such a system.


The ability to choose benefits, pretty much affects satisfaction with them.

Let us examine further what size of premiums in IT, how they are arranged in different companies and what affects the satisfaction from premiums.

As you can see, the premiums on average make up about 20% of the total annual income of an IT specialist. In food companies this share is higher, in non-technical lower.


Most often, bonuses make up 10% of the employee’s annual income, then bonuses amounting to 5, 15, 20 and 30% go with the same frequency.


The share of managers and administrators is noticeably higher than the average, and significantly lower than the average among designers. But still, the difference with the average is not very big.


The share of premiums in their annual income for managers is slightly higher than for specialists.



On average, for all IT people, they are most often paid annual, one-time and quarterly bonuses.


Annual awards more often occur with managers, quarterly with eychars and administrators, one-time awards with developers, testers, designers and administrators.



Prizes can be personal and collective. In IT, both those and others are common, personal - much more often.


Personal prizes are much more often than collective awards among eychars, managers and developers. With equal frequency, personal and collective awards are found among designers, analysts, administrators and testers.



The most common reason why an IT premium is paid is to achieve a result. Next comes the payment of a predefined amount that is not tied to any indicator. Next comes the award for personal achievements.


And the most often awarded personal achievements are work in difficult conditions and advanced training. Mentoring and gaining new skills is rewarded much less often, but also quite often.



The result is awarded less often than others by managers and designers. Moreover, the latter are more often rewarded with a predefined amount than by the result. 

Personal achievements more often than others are awarded by administrators and testers. 

Overfulfillment of KPI more often than others is rewarded by HR, managers and analysts.



If you look at the reasons for premiums in different companies, you will see that in food and non-technical companies they award more often for results than in outsourcing ones. But in the latter they award more often for personal achievements.

Even in non-technical companies, they are much more often rewarded with a predefined amount than is customary in grocery and outsourcing companies.


Of personal achievements in all types of companies, work in difficult conditions is most often awarded, but in outsourcing companies this is much more common. There, much more often than in other types of companies, mentoring is encouraged.

In non-technical companies, mentoring is rewarded much less often than in other companies, and bonuses for long service are more often practiced.



Consider further what affects premium satisfaction.

In most companies, the size of the premium is determined by the head.


However, satisfaction from premiums is higher where it happens automatically. Absolutely beyond satisfaction arises in systems where premiums are determined by voting, but there are very few such companies.



In most companies, bonuses are formally fixed in an employment contract or in a special bonus order. Especially often this practice is present in non-technical companies.


Employee satisfaction with bonuses in such companies is much higher. This is due to the fact that such awards are more transparent and predictable.



The grading system in the company is cool. This means that everyone sees the possibilities of their career paths and there is an understanding of what needs to be done for this. More than half of the companies have such a system, and in all types of companies it is approximately equally common.


Prize satisfaction in companies with a grading system is also significantly higher, which is also associated with a transparent and predictable bonus system, which logically integrates into the grading system.



In most companies, the size of the bonus depends on the salary of the employee. This is especially common in food and non-technical companies.


If there is such a link, then it also has a beneficial effect on greater satisfaction from the received bonus. Although not as strong as the other factors listed above.



From the open question about what is not pleasant in the current bonus system in the company, we learned that specialists are most often dissatisfied with the opacity and ill-conceived system when it is not clear what needs to be done to receive the award, or when the award depends on the subjective feeling of the leader. There is also dissatisfaction with the rarity and irregularity of prizes, their size.
