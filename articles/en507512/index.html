DNA tests - do you feel bitterness from phenylthiocarbamide?

Personal DNA tests open up new possibilities for researching your body.

Let's look at something not terrible like Alzheimer's disease but harmless like "the ability to feel the bitter taste of certain substances."



The first thing to understand is how we can generally taste and how in theory genetics can influence it (and what we mean by ‚Äúgenetics‚Äù in this case).



We taste thanks to molecules - receptors . The term receptor was coined by German scientist Nobel Prize winner Paul Erlich. You can study the history of the concept of receptors in more detail here .

Let's take a closer look at how membrane receptors work .





Diagram of the membrane receptor



Membrane receptors are proteins located in the membranes of cells. It is convenient to think of receptors as cellular sensors that respond to specific environmental changes outside the cell. Changes can relate, for example, to the concentration of some substances, there are also receptors that respond to physical changes in the environment - for example, receptors for photons.



An important property of the receptor is its selectivity. Receptors will respond to strictly defined molecules (or molecules containing certain constituents in their structure) surrounding the cell. Such molecules are called ligands . From interaction with ligands, the receptor sends a signal into the cell. There are many different mechanisms of action of these signals ‚Äî for example, the release of certain chemicals into the cell from the receptor.



How do we taste?



As you may have guessed, the taste of food is determined by the presence of a ligand in it, which interact with taste buds. The conventional wisdom is that taste perception arose during evolution to help animals separate toxic ‚Äútasteless‚Äù substances that are dangerous for them from nutritious ‚Äútasty‚Äù ones.



It is believed that people are able to distinguish five tastes: bitter, sour, salty, sweet and umami (meat). A bitter taste indicates the presence of harmful substances in food, sour and bitter can indicate that the food has deteriorated. The main source of salty taste is sodium salts, although some minerals also seem salty to us. A sweet taste signals the presence of high-energy carbohydrates, while a taste with the minds signals the presence of proteins in food.



Not all ‚Äútastes‚Äù we feel due to receptors. For example, salts can directly pass through cell membranes and create a cascade of chemical reactions inside the cells that we perceive as taste.



In the course of taste research, quite a few different proteins have been proposed - candidates for taste buds. A table with such candidates can be found, for example, in a 2007 article (tables 1 and 2 of the appendix). However, this list is most likely not final and new candidates for receptors will be regularly reviewed.



Receptors are complex molecules consisting of thousands of atoms and performing very specific functions. If the structure of the receptor is slightly changed, its properties can change greatly and, for example, cease to interact with certain ligands. In this case, a person or other living organism with this receptor will lose sensitivity to certain substances.



Receptors and DNA



What is the relationship between large protein receptors and DNA? Most likely, for most people who studied biology at school this is elementary, but I will repeat it all the same. This connection is expressed in the " Central Dogma of Molecular Biology ." Cells ‚Äúread‚Äù the DNA code to synthesize proteins - which are large molecules made up of amino acids. In general, the whole process goes through an intermediate RNA molecule, but omit the details in this article. So the DNA code consists of four letters - A, T, G and C. To synthesize the proteins of the cell, read these letters three at a time. Combinations of three letters in a row are called triplets . Reading each triplet, the cell adds some of the 20 amino acids to the protein. Which triplet encodes which amino acid is shown in the table.





A table of how amino acids are encoded



For this table, its authors received the Nobel Prize in 1968 and spent several years on its creation. The truth is that triplets are indicated in the RNA code, not DNA, in which the letter T is replaced by the letter U, but this does not change the essence. Thus, receptors are proteins that the body has built on the basis of some part of the DNA called the gene, and the sequence of amino acids, as well as what amino acids are in this protein, is determined by the nucleotide sequence (letters A, T, G or C) in DNA based rules from the table above.



Imagine that we have an RNA sequence: AUG-CCA-CGU- ... After reading its cell, it synthesizes the protein Methionine-Proline-Arginine- ... The amount of amino acids in proteins is usually in the hundreds or thousands of units. For example, the TAS2R38 protein, which is just a bitter taste receptor, has 333 amino acids in its composition.



Now let's imagine that a mutation occurred in DNA and in the second triplet the first C changed to G and the final combination became AUG- G CA-CGU- ... or in amino acids: Methionine- Alanine- Arginine ... Now, reading our sequence, the cell will start to synthesize a little another protein that has different properties.



It is such a mutation that can occur in the TAS2R38 gene, which encodes a bitter taste receptor protein, oddly enough, also called TAS2R38. The abbreviation P49A means that in the amino acid number 49 of the TAS2R38 protein, proline ( P ) was replaced by alanine ( A ) - each amino acid has short letters of three and one letter .



Now some information about the TAS2R38 protein. In 1931, DuPont employee Arthur Fox accidentally spilled dust consisting of phenylthiocarbamide(also PTC or PTU). His colleague began to complain about the bitter taste of the PTC dust cloud, although Fox himself did not feel anything. For some reason, Fox began testing other people for the taste perception of PTC and discovered that this property was inherited. According to Wikipedia, the perception of the taste of PTC used to be used for paternity tests . Later it was also found that the gene responsible for the perception of taste of PTC is located on the 7th chromosome, and in 2003 it was found that this gene is TAS2R38. In 2005, individual mutations were found in the gene that led to a lack of sensitivity. Read more about the history of TAS2R38 in a 2011 article .



What amino acid substitutions can occur in TAS2R38 and lead to an inability to feel a bitter taste? The answer is P49A, A262V and V296I - you already know how to interpret them, right? More details are available in the same article of 2011.



How does this apply to DNA tests?



So, you passed the DNA test, and would like to know if you feel bitterness from phenylthiocarbamide or not. This can be done in several ways. Firstly, many default tests will give you information. Suppose you want to see for yourself whether you have the replacements mentioned in the article. To do this, open the 2011 article and carefully look at chart 4:





Images numbered 4 from a 2011 article A is the Manhattan plot, C is the relationship between the sensitivity threshold of the PROP substance and the genotype in rs10246939.



Figure A shows the Manhattan plot, on which various markers are grouped on the horizontal axis, grouped by chromosomes. Remember, in an article I wrote that replacing C with G changes the amino acid in a protein? So the marker refers to the designation of the place where C can change to G, and in the case considered above, the marker is denoted by rs713598. The logarithm of the p-value is plotted along the vertical axis of Figure A and all this is multiplied by -1. Genetics look for markers for which the p-value (maybe from 0 to 1) is very small. Therefore, if we take the logarithm of a very small number close to 0 and multiply it by -1, we get a large number, which is immediately visible on the graph. And here you can see that the smallest p-values ‚Äã‚Äãwere found for markers designated rs713598 and rs10246939 . The article also shows that these markers are responsible for replacing P49A and V296I, respectively.



A small p-value for the marker says that there is indeed a relationship between what is in this marker and the perception of bitter taste. Now we know at least two markers that are associated, according to studies with the perception of bitterness from propylthiouracil - also PROP (has the same properties in terms of bitterness as phenylthiocarbamide, but safer). The question remains which letter in the marker is associated with the perception of bitterness, and which is not. This can also be found in the 2011 article.. In addition, there is also Figure C under the Manhattan chart. Here, the possible letters in the marker rs10246939 are plotted on the horizontal axis, and the sensitivity threshold is PROP on the vertical axis. That is, the minimum concentration at which people could feel the bitterness of PROP is plotted on the vertical axis. We examined the curious connection between genetics and the real properties of the body. Perhaps APOE protein is synthesized by cells not only in the head but also somewhere in the respiratory system.
