Do you want to be given the correct design task? Help the product put it

Once in the village, my uncle Slava asked what I was doing. Big, they say, already 25 years old. I have to do something. I replied that I work in Moscow as a mobile application designer. He nodded and was silent for half a minute. Then he asked again: “So that means ... everything is on the phone ... are you drawing?” “Yes,” I say, so as not to spread. He takes a Nokia button from his pocket and hands it to me - say, come on, show what you have drawn from this. Is this the “message” icon or the one with the telephone directory?



This story happened to me just a couple of years ago, in 2018, and was supposed to be a curious exception to the rule, but somehow quietly turned into a rule. Even product managers often mistake product designers for guys who draw telephone directory icons and colorize action buttons in a selling color. Therefore, they set tasks in the form of “let's make this button bigger, otherwise they don’t press the button, but here the title is fatter.”



How to make tasks from product managers take the form “I want to increase the Retention of our section, let's think about how to do it”, without “move this button to the right” and “make green greener”?



By trial and error, I came up with a list of questions that help clarify the task with the product manager and prevent all possible misunderstandings.



This list will help the designer to build communication with the product manager, and the product - to better understand what to expect from the designer.



First step. User



The question "what kind of user problem do we want to solve" will immediately help to look at the future feature with different eyes. That is, to move away from ready-made solutions back to goals.



Ask the product:



  1. How does the user solve this problem now?
  2. What result do we want for the user?


Answers to these questions will help to build objective criteria for evaluating the final decision. So, you will have a strong position when discussing it.



But the most important task of this stage is to move away from ready-made solutions and start thinking about hypotheses and their testing.



Second phase. Business



This stage is the most understandable for the product, whose main task in the company is to earn money and develop their direction. Start speaking his language to understand his real motives.



Having understood what drives the product, you can demonstrate alternatives to it, and your arguments will become more convincing and understandable for it.



Ask the product:



  1. Let's pretend that everything worked perfectly. What have we achieved then? How did this affect the business?
  2. How much is this in numbers?


The goal of the stage is to collect the business component of the case, so that when deciding to rely on the interests of not only our beloved user, but also our employer.



Having answered these questions, we will continue to formulate objective criteria through which it will be possible to evaluate the effectiveness of the decision.



The third stage. What is happening with the product?



The foundation of a good solution is understanding the product and its users. To collect this data, sometimes you have to study giant volumes of information and talk with a large number of users.



But most often the answers are closer than they seem - with the product. Ask him, and he will tell you everything:



  1. Who are our competitors?
  2. Where can I see product analytics? Where can I get metric events?
  3. Have any qualitative or quantitative studies been carried out? Where can I see them?


The purpose of this stage is to obtain data on what is happening with the product and how it developed.



The fourth stage. The timing



Check how much time you have to find a solution to plan your work.



Ask the product:



  1. When do we want in production?
  2. When should the design be ready?
  3. How tight are these deadlines? Can they be moved?


Of course, it happens that there is no time to research, generate new solutions and select the best ones at all - you just need to take and do it. Such tasks can be immediately recognized by the fire in the eyes of the product. If you see a flame, this questionnaire will not help you.



The goal of the stage is to collect the information for the development of a further work plan, in order to avoid a situation where developers are ready to take the feature to work, and there is no design yet.



The fifth stage. Technical features



At this stage, we collect information about the capabilities of the system so as not to spend time on a spaceship that will never take off.



Ask the product:



  1. What fronts do you want to roll out the feature on? Web, mobile, other fronts?
  2. Are there any technical limitations that I should be aware of?
  3. If the process is being finalized, then how to look at the existing process? Is there any test data?
  4. If there is already a backing, is there any documentation of the answers?
  5. Can we change the backing?


The goal of the stage is to collect technical limitations so that our solution can be implemented in a reasonable amount of time.



Contacts



When developing a product, communication is very important in order to quickly get answers to all your questions or discuss outline solutions. To do this, collect all the necessary contacts.



Ask the product:



  1. Who can learn the specifics of the product? Its product features? System features?
  2. How to reach these people? Mail / telegram / mobile?
  3. With whom within the team to coordinate design drafts?
  4. What is the best way to agree on a solution? A meeting? Letter? Other?


And now just a questionnaire, save it for yourself and use it as a conversation script when you are tasked.
