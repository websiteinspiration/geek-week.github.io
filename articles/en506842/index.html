RPA: robotic invoice registration process. The most comprehensive guide

Hello! The theme with RPA is becoming more hype. The principle of operation of software robots and the difference between RPA and other automation methods are covered on thematic portals. I decided to focus on approaches to modeling RPA processes. For example, I chose the process of registering an account with a buyer. I stopped on it, since this process is universal and relevant for companies of any industry and scale.

Summarized account registration can be represented as follows:
  1. The sales manager sends the accountant a request to the account.
  2. The accountant creates an invoice in the accounting system, forms a printed invoice form and sends it back to the manager.
  3. If the buyer exchanges electronic documents, the accountant creates and sends him an invoice in the EDI system.


In each case, this case will be organized in different ways. The manager can work in a CRM system. An application for an account can be sent through the BPM system. And as an accounting, various configurations of 1C, SAP or Oracle can be used.

As a result, the invoice processing process is only partially automated - at each of the stages where this or that system works. But people work at the junctions between the systems, and their actions are boring and monotonous: they simply copy data from one system and paste it into another. The question arises about the integration of systems among themselves.

Some systems already in the standard configuration may contain ready-made functionality for integration. The difficulty is that it will not work if the system with which the integration is performed has an unsupported version or is very heavily written ‚Äúfor you‚Äù.

In this case, the solution may be the implementation of their own integration between systems. This solution has a drawback - it is an expensive and lengthy project. Sometimes this integration may not be possible due to the lack of an API or closed source code. Sometimes impractical, for example, in the case of an outdated system, which is not planned to be maintained in the future.

RPA technology is best shown in routine tasks where simple data transfer is required by filling out forms and pressing keys. In such problems there should be no logic that cannot be represented as a system of very clear rules. The simpler the logic, the cheaper it is to implement such a process. The more often it is repeated in the company's business processes, the more tangible will be the benefits of its implementation.

The robot performs the process at a speed slightly higher than the speed of a person. On one robot, only one task can be performed at a time. Therefore, if with the help of automation we plan to switch to the robot operations that are now performed manually, then RPA is an appropriate option, but if the data must be processed at a high speed, it is better to consider other methods.

In my example, where simple transfer of data between systems is required and superhuman speed is not necessary, integration via RPA is the optimal solution.

Process Modeling in RPA


The specific account registration case I simulated is as follows:

  1. The sales manager starts the process of creating an account in the BPM system.
  2. The accountant, having received the application for the account, creates it in 1C, forms the printed form of the account and applies it to the task.
  3. If the application contained information that there was an agreement with the buyer and work through EDI, then the accountant creates an account in the Circuit Diadoc, attaches a printed form to it and sends it to the buyer.



The robot takes care of creating an account in 1C and sending it to Diadoc, so the process will be fully automated.

Of course, there is no point in describing step-by-step instructions here, because basically in the simulation the repeating steps are performed. And in your case, the implementation will probably be somewhat different. I want to talk more about the general approaches to implementing RPA processes and show how the most frequently used steps are performed in the RPA designer.

An RPA process is primarily about interacting with a user interface. The robot must repeat the user's actions. And the user most often uses mouse clicks on the corresponding visual interface elements to navigate inside the application window. If you want to close the window, the user clicks the "cross", to confirm the action - the "OK" button, if you need to enter a date - he prefers to find it in the drop-down calendar. To repeat this action, a pointer is written in the process to search for the desired item. In general, this is the title of the window where the element and its name should be. Applications can be organized in different ways; it is not always possible to select such a text pointer. In this case, the robot can be guided by the image, then a picture will be recorded as a pointer in the process.

Searching for an item by a picture pointer will work slower than searching by a text pointer. In addition, such a search is less reliable: changing the resolution of the screen or background under the element - and the robot may not find the element. There is another option - use the keys to navigate. With their help, you can open applications, navigate between items, click buttons. Search is not needed. This is the fastest and most reliable option, it is he who is the most preferred for RPA. After it - text pointers and in extreme cases - search by image.

For simulation, I took a free RPA system from ELMA.

Call 1C


I create a new process in the designer.



The first thing a robot needs to do is launch the 1C application. The operations performed by pressing keys work most reliably. Therefore, I launch 1C using the "Run" command.

To do this, I move the ‚ÄúKeyboard Shortcut‚Äù action to the graphical model of the process and indicate which keys should be pressed - Win + R. Then I move the ‚ÄúText Input‚Äù action and specify the address of the 1C executable, for example, ‚ÄúC: \ Program Files \ 1cv8 \ 8.3.16.1063 \ bin \ 1cv8s.exe ". And finally, the "Keyboard Shortcut" action, which should press the Enter key.



Configuration selection


Next, a 1C configuration window will appear on the screen. In the simplest case, you can also use a sequence of keystrokes to select a configuration. But if you don‚Äôt know what order the required configuration will be displayed in the list, you will have to provide a search using the pointer.

To search for a pointer, a special designer tool is used - the recorder. It allows you to record the sequence of user actions and automatically identifies pointers. When the recorder is running, it recognizes the interface elements that the mouse pointer is over and highlights them in green. In this way, you can navigate to which element the recorded action will be applied. The recorder can record a variety of user actions: dragging and dropping items, scrolling, mouse clicks, keystrokes, etc.

I start the recorder, I bring to the name of the desired configuration. After it is highlighted in green, click on it. Then Enter. I stop recording and confirm the addition of actions to the process.



Enter login password


The next step is to enter the login and password to enter the application. Let these parameters be passed to the process as call arguments. Add them to the process context.



Now you need to enter the values ‚Äã‚Äãof these variables in the corresponding fields 1C. I will use the recorder for this. In the recording mode, after hovering over the desired item, you can call up the special menu of the recorder, allowing you to apply additional actions to the item. For example, inserting a value from a context variable.



I record a click on the "User" field, then apply the "Paste" action to it, specifying the login variable, then Tab, the "Paste" function for the "Password" field and finally entering the Enter key.

Transition to the form ‚ÄúDocuments of the supplier‚Äù


If you start playing the process now, the designer will ask you to specify the values ‚Äã‚Äãof the context variables login and password, then start 1C, select the configuration and log in to the application. Now we have the main application menu.



The next step we need to open the form ‚ÄúDocuments of customers‚Äù. Most likely, you have already guessed what needs to be done for this: we launch the recorder, click on the ‚ÄúSales‚Äù menu, and then on ‚ÄúDocuments of suppliers‚Äù.

new document


Next you need to create a new document. In 1C, I press the Insert key. I add this action to the process using the recorder or manually by dragging the "Keyboard Shortcut" action from the palette.

Select a document type


1C will ask us to choose the type of document. I need a ‚ÄúBuyer Pay Invoice‚Äù.

Working with tables and lists is often difficult. Pointers are not always defined. Or it is difficult, using the simple actions available to the robot, to build a process that requires analysis of a table, where there are many fields, many rows. There is no universal way, for each task its approach is selected.

But in this case, everything is simple. You can either record a click on the desired type of document or use the search bar at the top of the form or start typing the name of the document we need and 1C will highlight it. Then Enter, and we are ready to enter the account data.



Account header


In the header of the account we enter the date of the account, counterparty, contract, warehouse. These values ‚Äã‚Äãmust be passed to the process as input arguments, so I add the necessary variables to the context.

1C allows you to simply enter these values ‚Äã‚Äãin the appropriate fields, open the forms of directories and select them is not necessary. I start the recorder and use the quick menu to record the insertion of context variable values ‚Äã‚Äãinto the desired fields and move between the fields using the Tab key



Account line


Now add the account lines. Obviously, how to simulate work with a separate line. But in each particular account there can be as many lines as you like. It is necessary to provide for the possibility of transferring to the process an arbitrary number of lines, which in turn consist of several fields, and then adding each received line to the form in 1C.

To transfer a collection of strings, I create a context variable with the type "Array". A separate line of such a collection will be a text line listing the field values.

That the process could work with separate fields of lines, I will use the action "Script". This action allows you to include arbitrary C # program code in the process logic. In our case, the program will be called in a loop and at each subsequent execution take the next line of the collection, parse it and assign the field values ‚Äã‚Äãto the corresponding variables. To exit the loop, I will use a special variable of type ‚ÄúYes / No‚Äù, the value of which will change in the script code when parsing the last line.

To organize the cycle, select the action "Repeat until ...". It is a container where you need to put actions that should be repeated.



Print, save file


The process is almost ready. Using the same techniques that I showed above, I add the formation of the printed form of the invoice and save it to disk, save the invoice in the system and close the application.

Sending Invoice to Circuit Diadoc


All the basic modeling techniques for example 1C are also suitable for modeling work in any other applications.

The next stage of invoice processing is sending it to the client through Diadoc. Creating an account in 1C and sending it is better to simulate as two different processes. This will allow you to independently debug and run them on different machines.

The robot will interact with Diadoc via the web interface. Not all browsers can recognize pointers well. They are best defined in IE and Firefox.

To open the Diadoc web page, use the Run command. As a parameter I indicate "iexplore diadoc.kontur.ru". Next, create the necessary input parameters. Further, using a recorder, I model logging in the system, loading an account file, entering with a parameter and sending. Where possible, I perform actions by pressing keys, where not, I use pointers.



To summarize


Two new areas of responsibility appeared in the process: Robot and Robot Administrator. During the process, the robot may encounter something that we did not foresee when modeling. For example, 1C will produce an unexpected error, or the interface will change in the Circuit Diadoc after the update, and the robot will not find the necessary controls. In this case, it is required that the specialist responsible for supporting the robots check what went wrong and restore the robot. The process I described provides for such a situation. Having received the status of the robot job ‚ÄúError‚Äù, the system will create an appropriate task for the administrator, after checking it, the robot starts again.
