Pondering Storey Points





I like to say that I may have invented story points, and if I really did, then I'm sorry today. Let's take a closer look at what I think of story points now. At least one of us is definitely interested in my thoughts.



The story idea, of course, came from XP, not Scrum. Somehow, the scrum practitioners adapted this idea into their work. Although the official scram guide only talks about backlog items, using user stories as backlog items is a very common practice in scrum.



Common - at least to the extent that scrum practitioners understand stories. Earlier, I wrote about how to use user stories correctly. Here we discuss story points.



In extreme programming (XP), stories were initially evaluated over time: the time it would take to complete a story. We quickly started using what is now called ‚Äúideal days,‚Äù which unofficially meant ‚Äúhow many days a couple will need to complete if they are finally left alone‚Äù. We multiplied the ideal days and the ‚Äúload factor‚Äù to get real time until the completion of development. The load factor was usually about three: three real days were spent to complete the work of one ideal day.



We called our grades in days, and usually did not pronounce the word ‚Äúideal‚Äù out loud. For this reason, our stakeholders often could not understand how we needed three days to complete the work of one day, or, on the other hand, why we could not complete 50 ‚Äúdays‚Äù of work in three weeks.



So, as far as I remember, we started calling our ‚Äúideal days‚Äù ordinary ‚Äúpoints‚Äù. Thus, a valuation of three story points meant that the story would be completed in about 9 days. One way or another, we used points only to understand how much work we can take in iteration, so when we said that these were about 20 points, no one really objected.



Maybe I suggested changing the name. If I really did this, now I'm sorry. Here are some of my current thoughts on this topic. I wrote them to my colleague Simon, who asked:



Do you really regret that story points were invented, or do you just condemn their misuse when people do not fully understand the relative sizes?



I replied that:



  • I definitely condemn their misuse
  • I think using them to predict ‚Äúwhen the work is done‚Äù is a bad idea at best.
  • I think comparing how real labor costs overlap with estimates is a waste of time at best.
  • I think that comparing commands based on the quality of ratings or velocity is bad
  • Let's take a closer look


Some Agile approaches really recommend normalizing story points between teams in order to simplify the planning process. At first glance, this seems reasonable enough; in reality, it is too easy to fall into the trap of comparing teams. And organizations often fall into this trap.



Comparison



First of all, even if the teams are ‚Äúalmost the same‚Äù, each of them has its own set of competencies and its own working environment. So if they look at two stories that look the same and one team will say that it is a deuce and the other will say that it is a six, this is simply useless information. And this is definitely not the most useful way to compare commands.



Now let's try to understand the situation. First, let's see if both situations of comparison were identical, and then we clarify, maybe the teams with a higher rating needed the help that we could provide. Such an approach would be a great example. To implicitly or implicitly render a verdict that a ‚Äúslower‚Äù team is somehow worse or less professional ‚Äî that would be a very bad example, which in reality, unfortunately, is the norm.



I think that when there are two teams that produce value, for managers to compare them is an irresistible temptation. I think it is so irresistible that I am ready to abandon the idea of ‚Äã‚Äãstory points and even the whole concept of evaluating stories, where possible. We will return to the question of how to work with fewer ratings. In addition, there are other articles on this topic.



Tracking



For many managers, the existence of an assessment implies the existence of ‚Äúreal work‚Äù, which means that you need to compare the assessment and the real work and make sure that they match. If they do not match, then you need to learn to evaluate better.



For me, an important feature of Real Agile is to select several things to work, and then quickly do them. The key question is how to find the most valuable things and how to do them quickly. Making fast usually translates into supplying small pieces of value and dynamic iteration. Estimating the value of the stories does not help much, if at all.



So if the existence of evaluation forces management to shift its focus from delivering value to improving the evaluation process, then it takes away the attention from the real goal - to quickly deliver real value.



Because of this, I come to the conclusion that evaluations, whether in points or time, should be avoided.



Pressure



Close to the relationship of estimates and real labor costs there is a natural pressure due to the desire of management to get ‚Äúmore." No matter how much the team puts value, this is not enough. Need more, more, more.



The best way to deliver value is no more, more, more. The best way is to deliver value often in small batches. If, instead of evaluating the stories, we divide them into ‚Äúfairly small‚Äù pieces, then we can come to an even flow of value delivery, delivering without interruption.



Focusing on ‚Äúmore‚Äù prevents value from increasing. Delaying increased deliveries with virtually no options leads to a bad result: the team tries to speed up and does so at the expense of code quality or testing. Soon they begin to supply more defects, slow down due to the increased number of alterations to fix defects, slow down even more due to the fact that the quality of the code is rapidly deteriorating. The situation is getting worse and worse, pressure is increasing and everything is turning into a pursuit of failed situations.



Since estimates are associated with excessive pressure, I prefer to avoid them.



I will say more: I prefer to completely abandon the planning of sprints or iterations. We are not working to get the budget for the coming weeks: we are working to make a short list of important things for the next delivery.



Predicting readiness



A common practice is to make a list of required features, think them over and decide that they will make up the next release of our product. Of course, the next question is ‚Äúwhen will all this be ready?‚Äù.



The answer is no one knows. We can do a great job on our ignorance. Sometimes it‚Äôs really worth it, for example, when a big contract is awaiting for evaluation. However, when we work in the development of solutions for internal or external customers, the best option is to deliver a small amount of value very often, rather than wait for monstrous releases that are endlessly postponed for later.



It is much better to choose a recent date for the next release and include the maximum amount of value in this release. Evaluation interferes with this task, whether it be in story points, Gummy Bears, or even time. If estimates can be avoided, in my opinion, this should be done.



Decomposition



The question is, if I do not like the assessment of stories, then what do I like? The answer is - I like the decomposition of stories. It is the practice of cutting large stories into small pieces. The main thing is that these small parts carry as much value as possible, but require a minimum of implementation time - ideally, less than a day or just a couple of hours.



I will not bicker with readers on the subject of evaluation in the process of decomposition. If your team secretly conducts an assessment and does not tell anyone about it - then there will most likely be no problems with this assessment, in story points or in time. And of course, to know the difference between ‚Äúprobably small enough‚Äù and ‚Äúprobably not small enough‚Äù and to know how ‚Äúthree days‚Äù and ‚Äúone day‚Äù differ are completely different things.



In addition, there is one trick. I mentioned her inGetting Small Stories and in Slicing, Estimating, Trimming . I learned it from Neil Killick: decompose stories until they are the size of a single acceptance test. After a little training, your stories will be the most suitable size.



Of course, there are many other articles on the subject of evaluation. Just follow the link that I left above - there is more information than you can imagine.



Predicting the future



But ... doesn‚Äôt there be a completely logical need to know how long a product release will take, and doesn‚Äôt it need to be evaluated for this? Perhaps an assessment is needed, but not an assessment of the stories. You probably won't even have requirements worked out to the level of stories. And if they do, then they will surely turn out to be bulky and mostly useless.



Of course, if assessments need to be done, then they need to be done. What I do or how I imagine what you need to do is all my theories. In the end, it‚Äôs up to you to decide how to act in order to succeed in your specific situation. However, I think there is a better way.



Firstly, think about some of the most important qualities of the next release. Discuss the problems they solve and how your software can help solve these problems. Discuss the simplest solutions that can at least help a little. We do not need to decide everything at once: often, even a little help can set everything in motion.



Secondly , think about the nearest deadline to which you could put some of the good decisions. Install this deadline and get started.



Thirdlydecompose important decisions into small parts and implement them. Surely you can easily make these pieces the size of one day, or less. Work only on the most important parts: do not try to implement the whole solution all the way. Your goal is to start thinking in the key ‚ÄúIf we do this little function, then our User Jack can already use it.‚Äù Then complete this function and give User Jack a try. Our goal is to get as close as possible to a continuous supply of value. And you need to do this quickly.



It is important for us to make the delivered value so tangible that our Product Owner or other stakeholders are in a hurry to release it as soon as possible. Then ... then we will be on the right track, with or without an assessment of the stories.



To summarize



Well, if I invented story points, I guess I'm a little sorry. But not too bad. I really think that they are often used incorrectly and we will avoid many problems, if at all we refuse to evaluate stories. If the story points in your company do not carry value, I would suggest abandoning them, because this is a waste of effort. On the other hand, if you really like them, then, well, go ahead!
