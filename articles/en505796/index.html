CO2, Sp02 and COVID-19: why you need to ventilate more often (few measurements)

For several years I have been holding a CO 2 sensor and have already written about what quality of air is in different rooms, vehicles, situations. And now I wondered what is the relationship between the increase in CO 2 in the surrounding space and the level of oxygen in the blood. For this, I used the Huawei Band 4 Pro bracelet. An experimental rabbit was himself, having made a good hundred measurements in different situations (in the stuffiness, in the fresh air). Under the cut - a bunch of all kinds of digits.



And here is COVID-19


Just a thought out loud: the main indicator of hospitalization with COVID-19 is the appearance of shortness of breath caused by a drop in blood oxygen level (Sp02). However, dyspnea may occur without any SARS CoV-2. The main reason is the deaf plastic windows of apartments, where many now, by the way, work in the home office mode. They interfere with normal air exchange. And if it suddenly happened that you or your friends suspected pneumonia with the accompanying symptoms, simple ventilation can play a role. But more about that below.

What exactly is the problem


In houses and apartments with closed plastic windows, even with working ventilation, the air very quickly becomes practically unsuitable for breathing. No, there is enough oxygen in it, but the level of CO2 is growing, which very noticeably affects well-being and performance.

How much CO2 should be


I will cite numbers familiar to many (for those who have previously passed this topic). Briefly about the norm: in a city on the street ~ 400 ppm (parts per million), which for CO2 corresponds to 0.04% vol. (volume fractions). In a well-ventilated area ~ 650 ppm. During the construction and design of ventilation systems, GOST 30494-2011 is taken as the basis . This document considers health to be optimal for CO2 levels between 800 and 1000 ppm. And air with CO2 at 1000 ppm or more is officially called low quality.

How much should be SpO2


Summing up information from various sources, I conclude that if the level of oxygen in the blood falls below 85-90%, this is an occasion to seek help.



What is the relationship between CO2 and SpO2


Approximately 80% of CO2 is transported in the body by red blood cells, another 7-10% of hemoglobin of red blood cells (which carries oxygen) and the remaining - by blood plasma.


Gas exchange occurs due to the difference in partial pressure of each gas.



The deterioration in the return of CO2 in the alveoli of the lungs acidifies the blood, which in turn slows down the process of oxygen addition to hemoglobin.

This is a brief theory, and now a small experiment.

What happens to the SpO2 level


Here everything is more interesting. Huawei Band 4 Pro measures the level of oxygen in the blood for a long time. The first values ‚Äã‚Äãappear after 15 seconds, and then another 30 seconds it shows them in dynamics, after which it gives the final result, calculated according to some of its algorithms.



I expected that in a stuffy room and in the fresh air the results would be about the same. This partly turned out to be so. On the street or in a room with fresh air, dozens of measurements showed that the SpO2 level stably kept in the range of 95-98%. In the dynamics, jumps are observed within another 1-2 units, but this is rare.

If the CO2 sensor is about 2000 ppm, the picture with SpO2 does not change. But with 3000 ppm SpO2 readings begin to "swim". I made the first measurement in the morning after waking up in a ‚Äúclogged‚Äù room with CO2 at around 3400 ppm. The bracelet in the dynamics jumped from 85 to 90%, fixing a total of 89%.



After I breathed a couple of minutes in the next room, the data no longer fell below 92%, regardless of where I took the measurements (in the stuffy or in the fresh air). In addition, the suspicion crept in that after awakening, the oxygen level may be reduced for other reasons. Therefore, I did a second cleaner experiment, sitting in the bathroom with a book (pre-damped ventilation). An hour later, with a small sensor, it showed 3800 ppm, and the bracelet stably recorded SpO2 values ‚Äã‚Äãat the level of 93% and increased heart rate (93 instead of typical 85).



Exit to fresh air after some time gave stable SpO2 values ‚Äã‚Äãof 95-96%. Those. there is a formal difference in terms of SpO2 between a stuffy room and aired room.
