Let's get acquainted. What kind of ML team are we? We solve ML-problems for three services: Taxi, Food and Shops. There are four main areas of optimization of our activities: optimization of the budget, business processes, the system as a whole and the product.

Now more about these areas.



Budget optimization, as the name implies, is related to the budget. We have a certain amount of cash or other resources and there are targeted actions that we want to get with these resources. For example, we want to give users discounts to encourage them to take more taxis or order at the Food or Shop. Or we want to give some kind of subsidy to the driver to stimulate demand. Or we want to predict what the demand will be in the region in order to plan courier changes and bring them out in such a way as to deliver all orders on time.

In these tasks, using ML, we either predict the business or solve the targeting problem in order to achieve the greatest impact effect.



The next direction is the optimization of business processes. There are a lot of large systems in Taxi, Food and Shop. For example, user support. In addition, there is a photocontrol of the car and its quality: whether the car is broken, dirty, the interior clean, etc. Or, for example, checking that the driver is not tired and everything is fine with him.

In all these tasks, one can increase the share of automation, automate some parts of the processes precisely due to ML-models. For example, to predict what the user‚Äôs appeal consists of in the appeal text. Or classify the picture for satisfaction of service requirements.



The next direction is the optimization of the system as a whole. There is a large area in Taxi that is responsible for the platform‚Äôs efficiency, that is, how to balance supply and demand with the help of prices, how to assign a car to order to be as efficient as possible, and other algorithms that affect the efficiency of systems. Here, using ML methods, we help colleagues make these algorithms even more cool.

For example, you can predict where the car will be in five or ten seconds and assign the car not taking into account the current position, but taking into account where it will be in a few seconds when the driver physically accepts this order. Thus, it is possible to avoid the situation when the car is assigned, but the driver, taking the order, drove the turn to the user, he has to turn around and the delivery time rises sharply compared to the promised one.



The next direction is product optimization. We want our application to be as convenient, functional and useful as possible for the user.

For example, we suggest points B so that he can select a destination in one click. Or we recommend convenient landing points so that it is as easy as possible for the user and driver to meet each other. Either we rank the restaurants according to the user's order history so that he can order where he liked best, or where he will be interested to try something new.

In addition to recommendations, there are tasks of predicting various statistics for the future, so that the user can find out how long the car will arrive or when the food will be delivered.

I'll start with a project to predict demand at Yandex.Lavka. Briefly, what is the essence of the project? In order to bring couriers to different areas and do it most efficiently, I want to understand what will be the demand in a particular area. This model has a set of requirements. For example, to make it as interpretable as possible, because if something goes wrong and the model is wrong, then we need to understand why it happened.

In addition, we would like the model to be as stable as possible, since demand is still a rather sensitive quantity that changes actively over time. And to make the model more stable, we, firstly, decided to use a linear model, such stability and interpretability.

Secondly, to make it more stable, we decided to use the RANSAC method, and this had a very positive effect on quality. Even reduced the error by a few percent. In general, everything was perfect.

But under the conditions of the coronavirus, something unexpected happened. You can express your hypotheses about what went wrong in this whole scheme, and after that I will tell and it will be possible to verify.

The following story, namely UpSale, also concerns Yandex.Lavka. To improve the product, we made a recommendation block on the basket, in which, based on the contents of the basket, we recommend the user various additional products that might be of interest to him.

To evaluate the effect of this, we launched an A / B test, with which we decided to measure the average bill - how it changes when there is a recommendation block and if it is absent.

What could have happened here? Since demand was growing very actively and, moreover, people began to order more and more, during the A / B test, a constant linear growth trend was clearly distinguished, within which the average check was growing. And this created an extra variance in the data, as a result, standard methods of calculating the statistically significant could detect that the change is statistically, precisely because of this additional variance associated with growth.

To deal with this problem, I had to invest in additional analytics. Specifically, the method for assessing the significance of significance is called CUPED, I highly recommend reading more about it, a very cool and useful method.

With its help, we were able to take this linear trend into account, compete with it and get a more accurate estimate of the statistical significance, making our metrics more sensitive by reducing variance.

Conclusion: sometimes the usual methods may stop working, and you need to be prepared for this at least mentally.



The following story concerns support automation in Yandex.Taxi. As I said, support is a fairly large area in our business. How to optimize support costs due to its maximum automation?

To do this, we process a lot of messages using the ML-model. We made a fairly large system and began to transfer it from customer support to driver support. But under the conditions of coronavirus, firstly, the message flow increased very much, because the situation was changing, drivers began to write more often.

Secondly, the meaning of these messages has also changed, because at least new situations have been added, for example, in connection with the appearance of omissions with QR codes.

Due to these two factors, the share of automation, that is, the share of requests to which we respond with the ML-model, has fallen by about half. To deal with this situation, we re-compiled a new tree of topics on which drivers turn. Retrained a new model for them, thereby won back the drop in this share of automation not to the initial level, but it turned out to return most of it.

Conclusion: the priority of the task can grow very actively, because volumes have grown. But at the same time, the main KPI can fall dramatically.

It happens and vice versa. Here, for example, is the task associated with the forecast of price changes in Yandex.Taxi. Let‚Äôs briefly tell you what the point is.



From the point of view of the system, it is disadvantageous when demand begins to sharply prevail over supply, since this creates an imbalance that hinders the efficient appointment of drivers for orders, and it is necessary to raise the price in order to attract drivers to the imbalance zone.

You might encounter this effect when you have ‚Äúpurple‚Äù prices in the app.

To make this happen a little less often, we notify the user that in the near future the price may change. We hope that due to this, some of the users will leave a little earlier than they originally planned, and the demand will seem to spread out over time. There will not be such a sharp effect of the prevalence of demand over supply.

But, under the conditions of coronavirus, the demand for taxis has greatly changed its character. Now ‚Äúviolet‚Äù prices practically do not exist in the system. That is, supply and demand more or less always coincide. Therefore, notifications in this current situation have become redundant, the functionality is not in demand, and we decided to turn it off.

Thus, the priority of a task can drop very sharply, although its main KPI has grown very much. It‚Äôs like in this case, when we really achieved the effect that supply and demand became more or less the same.

Boomerang Projects




Once upon a time, people went to work in the morning, and returned home in the evening. And they did it by taxi, on the basis of travel histories one could predict where the users have a house and where they work. Moreover, so many people added travel points to their favorites and signed that it was home, and it was work. Based on such data, a model could be trained.

This was originally planned in order to prompt the user to add new addresses to his favorites. However, in the end, this was not useful for the original purpose. But it turned out that knowing where a person lives and where he works can be useful, so the model was brought to a production state and saved for future use.

And this future, of course, has arrived. Namely, it was very actively used in pushing the opening of a new restaurant in the vicinity. Or that the Shop‚Äôs area has expanded, and now it also delivers to this point.

I would not want, of course, to spam everyone in the district. Therefore, such pushes were sent only to those who, for example, live in this area or who potentially had work in it, until they introduced self-isolation.

This decision allowed to receive tens of percent of additional orders in the A / B test, which was very nice.

Conclusion: initially, your ML predictions may not have a place to use. But if there is no easy way to use these predictions, then such a place may never appear. It turns out the problem of chicken and eggs. In general, sometimes just a cool model is done, then a consumer is on it and everyone from this becomes a little happier.

The following story is about proactive support. As I said, we are automating support using the ML model.



But an interesting thought arises: in order to optimize support costs, it is not necessary to optimize support itself. Let's reveal this idea in more detail.

We have telemetry data about the trip. This is data from the accelerometer, that is, if there are a lot of sharp turns, then maybe something happened during the trip. And it is possible to determine the probability of contacting support on the basis of telemetric data. Suppose it seems to us that this probability is large. Then you can communicate with the user in advance, thereby preventing the case when he writes to support himself. This is the first.

Secondly, we can interact with the driver if he often has such moments and on the basis of these data it seems to us that we could often complain about him. We kill two birds with one stone - we improve the service with a feedback from the driver and get an additional source of optimization: we reduce the number of calls for support.

Conclusion: do not limit ourselves to the initial framework that we set for ourselves in the task. It is worth going out of them to find something cool and new.
