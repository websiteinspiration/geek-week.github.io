7 Ways Data Scientists Fool You



Sometimes, people selling you “artificial intelligence” or “machine learning” can be misleading. In this article, I will share 7 simple principles that will avoid this.

It doesn’t matter who you are: the head of the company, a large entrepreneur, a business angel, a middle manager in a company, a judge on a hackathon, or a person who is somehow connected with “technology”. Sooner or later, someone will surely try to “sell” to you “machine learning software”, “an AI product” or some other mixture of buzzwords. When you find yourself in such a situation, it may seem that you do not have enough knowledge and experience to make the right decision. Do not lose your head! Below are 7 criteria that will distinguish a standing product from a dummy. They will help you not to pay attention to the noodles that they will hang on your ears and understand the true value of the product.

“We use artificial intelligence to ...”


Be vigilant when you hear the phrase “artificial intelligence”. Most likely this is intricate marketing, which may also conceal an attempt to conceal important and complex details from you. Trust, BUT go into the details. Find out which specific machine learning model was used. Ask to explain it using analogies.
If you cannot explain it in simple words, you yourself do not fully understand this.
- Albert Einstein
Important questions to ask:

  • What other methods (models / algorithms / techniques) have you tried and how different were the results compared to the selected model? (if possible, ask for a graphic proof)
  • Why did you choose this particular method?
  • Why do you think this method works better than others with this data?
  • Has anyone else ever solved this problem? If so, what method did they use?

At first, you don’t have to understand all the details, but you should ask, clarify and try to understand as much as possible.

Based on my experience, I can say that I have not yet met a single concept of machine learning that could not be explained using analogies. If a large number of technical details complicate the understanding, ask to explain everything on the fingers. This approach will not only allow you to better understand what is at stake, but also show how well the solution was thought out. (You can also make it clear that you are not one of those easy to fool)

Adaptive survivors


In the nineties and early zero, the spam filter in the mailbox looked for spelling errors and other simple indicators to automatically put spam in the appropriate folder. Spammers are becoming smarter and spam is becoming harder to detect. In order to correctly identify spam, machine learning models had to adapt and improve.
“Any failure is a failed attempt to adapt to new conditions, any success is a successful adaptation”
- Max McKeone
It is important to understand how easy it is to retrain the machine learning model with new data and whether it can be replaced by a more productive model. Do not forget to ask about it. This is important because you need to know if the solution you are selling has a “shelf life”.

Garbage inlet, garbage inlet


How good a machine learning model is is data-driven. Therefore, you must ensure the quality of the data used to train the model. Of course, quality is difficult to determine and may vary depending on context. It’s best to ask how indicative and similar the learning data is to the “real world” data that the model will encounter.
“We believe in God, and everyone else uses the data (of good quality).”
- William Edwards Deming
The model can be very fashionable and modern, but if it is trained on low quality data, disastrous results are inevitable.

More, more, more!


In general, the more data was used to train the model, the better it works (ceteris paribus). This is especially true for deep learning models. The machine learning model is similar to a high school student preparing for exams. The more different questions he addresses, the higher the likelihood that he will pass the exam.
“The worst mistake is to build a theory without collecting (enough) data.”
- Sherlock Holmes
You must ensure that a sufficient amount of data has been used to train the machine learning model. How much is that enough? It's hard to say, but the more the merrier! Ideally, the data should be obtained from reliable sources, and these sources should be fully utilized.

Interpretability


In machine learning, there is often a trade-off between how well a model works and how easy it can be to explain its performance, especially low. As a rule, more complex and intricate models work better for complex data. However, the more complex the model, the more difficult it is to explain the effect of the input data on the final result. Let's imagine that you are using a very sophisticated machine learning model to predict product sales. The initial data for this model are the amounts of money spent on advertising on television, in newspapers and on radio. A complex model can give you very accurate sales forecasts, but it may not be able to tell you which of the 3 advertising sites, TV, radio or newspaper, affects sales more and costs more. On the other hand, a simpler model could give a less accurate result,but could show which of the sites is more expensive. You must keep in mind the direct correlation of model performance and interpretability.

Measure what is needed as needed


The percentage of correct answers is a very common metric for measuring the performance of a machine learning model. For example, a machine learning model for recognizing images of cats and dogs with an accuracy of about 96% can be considered very good. This means that out of 100 images of cats and dogs, the model can correctly recognize 96 images. Imagine that the bank is trying to apply the same indicator to recognize fraudulent transactions. Since such operations are rare, it is very easy to achieve 96% accuracy. However, the goal is not to detect them correctly in 96% of cases, but to allow fewer errors in their recognition and to prevent as many fraudulent operations as possible. Even if you can’t recognize only 4% of such operations, it will cause a huge damage to the bank.
Dimension is a legendary thing. Unless, of course, you waste time measuring what is easy to measure instead of measuring what you need to measure.
- Seth Godin
In the banking fraud example, the number of false negative results is more likely to indicate the effectiveness of the model, rather than accuracy. To determine the accuracy of the model, you need to take into account the completeness of the model, its specificity, measure F1. Here is a wonderful article by Mohammed Sunasra in which he tells when each of these metrics should be used. It is important to remember that you need to use the correct metric and, if possible, somewhat different.

What are your strengths and weaknesses?


The familiar question that everyone is constantly asking at interviews can be very helpful when trying to evaluate machine learning software. When someone offers you a product, you should definitely find out if they have limitations. Knowing the limitations is necessary in order to answer two key questions:

  • Do the strengths outweigh the constraints associated with the implementation of the solution?
  • Could restrictions reduce performance in the future?

“The key to success is understanding your weaknesses and successfully compensating for them. People who lack this ability constantly fail. ” - Ray Dalio

To implement an effective and sustainable product, knowledge of constraints is a key success factor. Moreover, if you ask the creators of the product to explain its limitations, you will get an idea of ​​how honest you are with you. You can understand how well thought out the software is and whether the people who offer it are trustworthy.

Conclusion


Maybe you do not have enough knowledge or it’s breathtaking from the incoming proposal, in any case you have one secret weapon that can help you - a flashlight that will lead you through the fog. This secret weapon is your ability to ask questions. Ask! Ask, clarify and study questions you are not sure about. The seven principles above will give you strategy and direction. Feel free to use them when asking questions. They will help you better understand the product and objectively evaluate it.
