Pricing psychology


Imagine a world in which the cost of each project that you undertake always leaves you in the black; a world in which you consciously decide to spend less than your budget allows to overcome your competitors; and a world where you choose a value that maximizes profits. It's time to unleash the magic of the price range, which is based on our three variables.

In the field of graphic design, there are certain nuances regarding the psychology of pricing. Each project has its own scope of work, budget, customer expectations, required experience, workload of specialists and competitive environment, and these factors directly affect our pricing policy. Before we delve into the details, let's look at the psychology of real numbers that you need to choose when setting the price.

Left digit effect vs round price


Wandering around the supermarket, you can see on the shelves a lot of products with "odd" prices: a liter of milk for $ 2.99, a loaf of fresh bread for $ 3.99 or a small packet of chips for $ 1.99. For decades, food prices have been chosen precisely on the basis of the ‚Äúleft-figure effect‚Äù. This principle is that if at the end of the price you write numbers ending in nine, and not zero, then the very first digit on the left changes. For example, a product costs $ 2.99, not $ 3.00. In fact, the difference between them is only 1 cent, but the price that we actually perceive is 1 dollar lower, since the figure on the left has changed from three to two.

Perhaps most consumers are careful enough to notice this pricing game and see that $ 2.99 is almost equal to $ 3.00. Nevertheless, I myself find myself using this tactic when I replenish my collection of digital films in my Apple account. I tell my wife that I bought the film for $ 9.99 (the exact price), instead of saying that I paid $ 10 for it (the price rounded off by one cent). Of course, she understands the meaning of this performance, but it is psychologically more comfortable for us to hear / see the price of $ 9.99.

Despite the popularity of this tactic, there is another point of view: in fact, consumers prefer round numbers. If you enter ‚Äúpricing with integers‚Äù in the search, the result will show numerous articles and studies supporting this concept. One of such articles shows that rounded prices, such as $ 300, encourage consumers to make purchases guided by their feelings, while non-rounded prices, such as $ 297.50, require a reasoned purchase decision from the buyer: it will be for him it is a bargain or not.

For better or worse, most companies make marketing decisions based on what ‚Äúseems right‚Äù to them, including deciding which company to hire. When determining the cost of their work, we should strive to ensure that customers say to themselves: "Yes, this price suits me."

Graphic designers do not sell eggs, and I don‚Äôt think that if the cost of creative services is full of nines at the end (for example, get your logo design for only $ 5,999! Only today!), This will make a big difference.

However, experience shows that the client is likely to approve the amount of $ 1,750 than $ 2,000. And the point is not that in his view the price of $ 1,750 is actually equivalent to $ 1,000 (as in the case of changing the first digit on the left), it‚Äôs just that usually the client‚Äôs budget is an integer. If $ 2,000 is allocated for the project, and you ask for $ 1,750, the client receives a tangible advantage at the very beginning of the project, saving part of his budget for the future.

In addition, if you are competing for a project with other design studios or freelancers, the price of $ 1,750 will be much more attractive than the price of $ 2,000, simply because it is below the allocated budget.

Combining the effect of the left digit and the importance of transparent pricing in whole numbers, I suggest you round the prices based on values ‚Äã‚Äãproportional to the price. For projects at a lower price, use a lower multiple. For more expensive - higher, respectively. Here are some examples.

Rule 01: if a project costs more than $ 100 and cheaper than 2000, its price should be a multiple of 25

If the project costs more than $ 100 and cheaper than $ 2,000, tell the customer that price that will be a multiple of 25.

For example, you want to set a price for the design of an advertising banner for one of your customers and decide that it will cost from $ 1,500 to $ 1,700. Guided by my recommendations, you would choose any price that is a multiple of 25, since the price range is above 100 and below 2000 dollars. It can be 1525, 1650, 1675 or any other multiple of 25, within a predetermined price range. (You should not offer non-multiple 25 digits - such as 1599 and 1628.)


Rule 02: if the project costs more than $ 2,000 and cheaper than 10,000, then the price should be a multiple of 50

Let's look at the following example. If the project costs more than 2000 and cheaper than $ 10,000, then the price should be a multiple of 50.

Thus, if you decide to take from 3,000 to 3,500 dollars for the design of the booklet in two folds, you can take any number that is a multiple of 50 as the final price. For example , 3250, 3350, 3400 or any other multiple of 50. (Do not take 3399, 3325, 3407 or any other multiple of 50.)

Rule 03: if a project costs more than $ 10,000, then the price should be a multiple of 250

Finally, if the project costs more than $ 10,000, then the price should be a multiple of 250. In this case, if you decide that the cost of the project is in the range of $ 22,000 to $ 25,000, tell the client the amount that is a multiple of 250. The total price of 22,250, 23,000 or 24,500 is perfect. (You should not call such numbers as 23,225, 24,999, 22,777, or 22,889 dollars, because they are not a multiple of 250.)


Most of the success depends on the degree of trust between you and your client. Make your pricing system simple and transparent - this will be a great step towards the right working relationship.
