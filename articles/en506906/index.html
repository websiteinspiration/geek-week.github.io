For decades, black holes have played a leading role in thought experiments, through which physicists explore the extremes of nature. These invisible spheres are formed when matter becomes so concentrated that everything at a certain distance, even light, falls into the trap of its gravity. Albert Einstein compared gravity with curves in the space-time continuum, but the curvature increases so much when approaching the center of the black hole that the Einstein equations stop working. Thus, generations of physicists have turned to black holes for clues about the true, quantum origin of gravity, which should become a harmonious beautiful theory and generally correspond to Einstein‚Äôs ideas anywhere in the universe.

The concept of vaporizing black holes for the knowledge of quantum gravity was proposed by Stephen Hawking. In 1974, a British physicist calculated that quantum ripples on the surfaces of black holes cause them to evaporate, slowly decreasing in size with the release of heat. Since then, the evaporation of black holes has become the basis for the study of quantum gravity.

More recently, physicists have looked at extremes from extremes - entities called extreme black holes - and have identified promising issues.

A black hole becomes electrically charged if charged matter falls into it. Physicists have calculated that black holes have a so-called ‚Äúextreme limit,‚Äù a saturation point, when they store the maximum amount of electric charge for their size. When a charged black hole contracts under the influence of Hawking radiation, it ultimately reaches this extreme limit. It will be as small as possible, taking into account the magnitude of the charge. Further evaporation is not possible.

But the idea that the extreme black hole stops evaporating and from then on is simply unchanged, implausible, says Grant Remman, a physicist at the University of California at Berkeley. In this case, the universe of the distant future will be littered with tiny indestructible remnants of black holes - any black holes, because even a light touch of a black hole after evaporation will turn it into an extreme one. No fundamental conditions are known under which these black holes would be permanently protected from ultimate destruction, therefore physicists do not think that such a stationary state can last forever.


So. ‚ÄúThere is a question,‚Äù as Sera Cremonini from Lehigh University said: ‚ÄúWhat happens to all these extreme black holes?‚Äù

Physicists reasonably believe that extreme black holes should eventually decay, resolving the paradox, but in some other way than Hawking's evaporation. Studying hypothetical possibilities in recent years has led researchers to basic clues about quantum gravity.

In 2006, four physicists came to the conclusion that if extreme black holes are capable of decaying, this means that gravity should be the weakest interaction in any possible universe, which was a strong statement about the relation of quantum gravity to other quantum forces. This conclusion drew closer attention to the fate of extreme black holes.

Then, two years ago, Remman and his colleagues Clifford Chung and Junyu Liu from the California Institute of Technology discovered that the ability to decay extreme black holes directly depends on another key property of black holes: their entropy - as many different ways an object‚Äôs components can be rearranged. Entropy is one of the most studied features of black holes, but before that it had nothing to do with their extreme limit. ‚ÄúIt's straight wow, great that two very cool things turned out to be interconnected,‚Äù says Chung.

And what finally struck, this relationship, as it turned out, is characteristic of any natural phenomena. In an article published in March in the Physical Review Letters, Gun and Riccardo Penko summarized the findings of their previous work by proving a simple universal formula linking energy and entropy. The new formula has been applied to the gaseous system, as well as to the black hole.

Extreme black holes


For physicists, it is fairly obvious that charged black holes have an extreme limit. Combining the Einstein equations and the Maxwell equations, they calculated that the charge Q of a black hole will never exceed its mass M if they are converted to the same fundamental units of measurement. Together, the mass and charge of a black hole determine its size ‚Äî the radius of the event horizon. Meanwhile, the charge of the black hole also creates a second ‚Äúinner‚Äù horizon, hidden behind the event horizon. As Q increases , the inner horizon of the black hole expands, and the event horizon contracts until both horizons coincide at Q = M.

If Qincreases even more, the radius of the event horizon becomes a complex number (containing the square root of a negative number), and not real. This nonphysical ( This is unphysical ). So, according to a simple combination of the theory of electromagnetism and Einstein's gravity, the state Q = M should be the limit.

When the black hole reaches this point, a simple option for further separation would be to decay into two smaller black holes. However, in order for such a splitting to be possible, the laws of conservation of energy and conservation of charge require that in one of the daughter objects the charge ultimately prevails over the mass. Based on the equations of Einstein and Maxwell, this is impossible.

But in the end, dividing extreme black holes into two is possible, as Nima Arkani-Hamed, Lubos Motl, Alberto Nikolis and Kumrun Wafa pointed out in 2006. They noted that the combined Einstein and Maxwell equations do not work for small, highly curved black holes. On a smaller scale, the additional nuances associated with the quantum-mechanical properties of gravity become more important. These details amend the Einstein-Maxwell equations by adjusting the predicted extreme limit. Four physics showed that the smaller the black hole, the more significant of these amendments, resulting in an extreme limit moves farther and farther away from the Q = M .


The researchers also noted that if adjustments have the desired sign - positive rather than negative - then small black holes may contain more charge than mass. For them, Q > M , and this is exactly what is needed for the decay of larger extreme black holes.

If so, then not only black holes can decay. Arkany-Hamed, Motl, Nikolis and Wafa noted that another fact about nature follows from this: gravity should be the weakest force. The charge of the object Q is its sensitivity to any interaction except gravity. Its mass M is its sensitivity to gravity. Thus, Q > Mmeans that of both forces, gravity is the weakest.

Based on their assumption that black holes are capable of decaying, four physicists generalized the hypothesis, suggesting that gravity should be the weakest force in any viable universe. In other words, objects with Q > M will always exist for any kind of charge Q , regardless of whether these objects are particles, such as electrons (which really have much larger electric charges than their mass), or small black holes.

This ‚Äúweak gravity hypothesis‚Äù has become widespread, supporting a number of other ideas about quantum gravity. But Arkany-Hamed, Motl, Nikolis and Wafa did not prove that Q >M or that extreme black holes can decay. Corrections of quantum gravity to the extreme limit can be negative, in which case small black holes can carry even less charge per unit mass than large ones. Extreme black holes would not break up, and the hypothesis of weak gravity did not materialize.

All this meant that researchers needed to figure out what the sign of quantum gravity corrections really is.

Mess is all over the place


The question of quantum gravitational corrections was raised earlier, in another seemingly unrelated topic on the study of black holes.

Almost 50 years ago, the now deceased physicists Jacob Beckenstein and Stephen Hawking independently discovered that the entropy of a black hole is directly proportional to its surface area. Entropy is usually seen as a measure of disorder; it estimates the number of ways in which the internal parts of an object can be rearranged without any change in general condition. (If the room is dirty, i.e., with high entropy, you can move objects randomly and the room remains as dirty; otherwise, if the room is clean, i.e. with low entropy, moving the objects will make it less neat .) Throwing the bridge between the entropy of the black hole, which is determined by its internal microscopic components, and its geometric surface area,the law of entropy of Beckenstein and Hawking became one of the most powerful supports of physicists for the study of black holes and quantum gravity.

Beckenstein and Hawking derived their law by applying Einstein's gravity equations (along with the laws of thermodynamics) to the surface of a black hole. They regarded this surface as smooth and ignored any structures existing on a microscopic scale.

In 1993, physicist Robert Wold of the University of Chicago showed that more could be done. Wald found tricky loopholes for obtaining small effects obtained from more microscopic levels of reality, not knowing what the full description of this deeper level is. His tactics, first used by physicist Kenneth Wilson in a different context, was to describe all the possible physical effects. In Einstein's equations, Wald showed how to add a number of additional factors - any that have the correct dimensions and units of measurement, constructed from all physically significant variables - that could describe the unknown properties of a black hole at a close distance to its surface. ‚ÄúYou can describe the most common set of elements that you can have in principle,which describe the curvature of a [black hole] of a certain size, ‚ÄùCremonini said.


Fortunately, this series can be interrupted after the first few elements, since the increasingly complex components of many variables contribute little to the final answer. Even many of the leading members of the series can be crossed out because they have irregular symmetry or violate the conditions of consistency. This leaves only a few entities of any value that modify Einstein's equations of gravity. Solving these new, more complex equations gives the more accurate properties of a black hole.

Wald did this in 1993, calculating how quantum gravitational effects at short distances correct the Beckenstein-Hawking entropy law. These corrections shift the entropy of the black hole, so that it becomes not exactly proportional to the area. And although it is impossible to calculate the entropy shift directly - variables with unknown values ‚Äã‚Äãare involved - it is clear that the more significant the corrections, the smaller the black hole, and, therefore, the greater the entropy shift.

Three years ago, Chung, Liu, and Remmen applied Wald's same basic approach to the study of charged black holes and the extreme limit. They modified the Einstein-Maxwell equations with a series of additional terms arising from close-range effects, and solved new equations to calculate a new adjusted extreme limit. This led to an amazing result: corrections to the extreme limit of a charged black hole exactly corresponded to corrections to its entropy calculated by the Wald formula; quantum gravity unexpectedly shifts both quantities equally.

Remmen remembers the date they completed the calculation - November 30, 2017 - ‚Äúbecause it was so exciting,‚Äù he recalls. "It was very exciting when we proved that these [additional] elements give the same shifts and entropies and extremes."

But are these shifts the same sign? Both corrections depend on uncertain variables, therefore, in principle, they can be both positive and negative. In their 2018 article, Chung and colleagues calculated that the entropy shift is positive in a large class of scenarios and models of quantum gravity. They argue that it is also intuitively clear that the shift in entropy must be positive. Recall that entropy measures all possible internal states of a black hole. It seems reasonable that taking into account the more microscopic details of the surface of the black hole will reveal new possible states and, therefore, lead to more entropy, and not to less. ‚ÄúThe more microstates, the more plausible the theory will be,‚Äù Remmen said.

If so, then the shift in the extreme limit is also positive, which allows smaller black holes to store more charge relative to the mass. In this case, ‚Äúblack holes can always break up into lighter ones,‚Äù said Chung, adding that ‚Äúthe weak gravity hypothesis is true.‚Äù

But other researchers point out that these results are not direct evidence of the weak gravity hypothesis. Gary Shiu, a theoretical physicist at the University of Wisconsin at Madison, said that the belief that entropy should always increase when you take quantum gravity into account is ‚Äúan intuitive conclusion that someone agrees with and someone no".

Shiu cited counterexamples: unrealistic models of quantum gravity, in which the entropy of black holes decreases due to the cancellation of effects at short distances. In these models, causality or other fundamental principles are violated, but, according to Shiu, the point is that the newly found connection with entropy alone does not prove that extreme black holes can always decay or that gravity is always the weakest force.

‚ÄúIn general, to prove [the hypothesis of weak gravity] would be fantastic,‚Äù Shiu added. "That is why we are still thinking about this problem."


Forbidden Swamp Kingdom


Gravity is the weakest of the four fundamental interactions in our universe. The weak gravity hypothesis states that it could not be otherwise. Besides our universe, the hypothesis also seems to be true for any theoretically possible universes deduced from string theory. A candidate for the quantum theory of gravity, string theory, claims that particles are not points, but extended objects (so-called strings), and that space-time on a microscopic scale also has additional dimensions. When theorists describe the various sets of strings that can define the universe, they invariably discover that gravity - which arises from a particular type of string - is always the weakest force in these model universes. ‚ÄúSeeing that this ends up happening over and over again is very amazing,‚Äù said Jorge Santos, a physicist at the Institute for Advanced Study at Princeton, New Jersey and Cambridge University.

The hypothesis of weak gravity is one of the most important in the set of ‚Äúhypotheses of the swamp kingdoms‚Äù put forward by physicists over the past two decades. These are speculative statements based on thought experiments and examples about which types of universes are possible and impossible. Eliminating impossible versions of universes (placing them in a useless ‚Äúswamp kingdom‚Äù), ‚Äúswamp‚Äù theorists seek to find out why our universe is what it is.

If researchers could prove that gravity is inevitably the weakest (and, as a result, that black holes can always collapse), the most important conclusion, according to Santos, is that quantum gravity "should become a theory of great unification." That is, if Q and Mmust have a fixed ratio, their related forces should be part of one single mathematical structure. Santos noted that ‚Äúthe only existing theory‚Äù that combines fundamental forces into a single structure is string theory. Competing approaches, such as loop quantum gravity, attempt to quantize gravity by dividing space-time into parts without linking gravity to other forces. ‚ÄúIf the weak gravity hypothesis is true, then things like loop quantum gravity are dead,‚Äù Santos said.

Jorge Pullin, a theorist of loop quantum gravity at Louisiana State University, believes the ‚Äúdead‚Äù is too much said. This approach alone can be part of a broader unified theory, he says: "Loop quantum gravity does not exclude a unifying structure, we just haven't put it on the agenda yet."


The weak gravity hypothesis also mutually reinforces some other hypotheses about the ‚Äúswamp kingdoms,‚Äù including the role of symmetry and distance in quantum gravity. According to Shiu, the logical connection between these hypotheses "gives us some confidence that, although these statements are made in the form of assumptions, a universal truth may be hidden behind them."

Shiu compared our current, rough understanding of quantum gravity with the first period in the development of quantum mechanics. ‚ÄúThere were many guesses, many leaps of faith in what is the correct theory of the subatomic world,‚Äù he said. ‚ÄúIn the end, many of these conjectures turned out to be part of the current large-scale picture.‚Äù

Universal Energy and Disorder


A new study can have far-reaching consequences not only in the study of black holes and quantum gravity.

In their March article, Gun and Penko clarified the calculation of the corrections for the entropy and extremality of a black hole. Instead of using concepts related to gravity and black hole surface geometry, they calculated corrections exclusively in terms of universal thermodynamic quantities, such as energy and temperature. This allowed them to discover the thermodynamic relationship between energy and entropy, which is usually observed in nature.

‚ÄúThis relationship is just perfect,‚Äù says Santos.

In the case of black holes, the formula of the scientist duo (Gong and Penko) says the same thing that Chung, Remmen and Liu have already proved: this quantum gravity shifts the extreme limit of black holes (allowing them to store more charge in relation to mass) and shifts their entropy by a proportional amount. Another way to describe the additional capacity due to quantum gravity is that a black hole with a fixed charge can have a lower mass. Mass is a form of energy, and therefore this decrease in mass can be considered in a more general sense - as a shift in the amount of energy. Which is inversely proportional to the shift in the value of entropy.

While for a black hole, equal and opposite displacements in the values ‚Äã‚Äãof energy and entropy occur under the influence of unknown factors of quantum gravity, an equivalent situation is characteristic of any physical system near its extreme limit.

For example, gas becomes extreme when cooled to absolute zero. The thermodynamic formula of Gun and Penko says that any changes in the physics of gas at the microscopic level (for example, when it comes to the types of atoms that make up it) cause shifts in its energy and entropy, both with opposite signs and with matching ones. Gong suggested that the relationship between energy and entropy may be useful in studies of ultracold gases and other cryogenic experiments, "because sometimes one is easier to calculate than the other."

Regardless of whether this entropy / energy relationship is useful in more mundane areas of physics, researchers still have a lot of work to study in the context of black holes and its significance for the nature of gravity.
