A Brief History of Iron and Steel



Iron has been known to mankind for a long time, it is one of the seven ‚Äúmetals of antiquity‚Äù, along with gold, silver, copper, lead, tin and mercury. At first, people dealt only with native iron, the source of which was the nucleus of iron-nickel meteorites. Of course, they were not found very often, so meteorite iron was quite rare and expensive material and was used mainly for ritual purposes. People learned to extract iron from ore much later, but also quite a long time ago. It is believed that ferrous metallurgy arose with the beginning of the Iron Age in Central Asia and the appearance of the first iron tools about 3,000 years ago. Moreover, in other regions, the Iron Age came later, sometimes for millennia. Technology roamed the planet, it was forgotten and ‚Äúdiscovered‚Äù again.



The first method of iron smelting is consideredraw blast . A mixture was loaded into a clay cheese-producing furnace - calcined in a fire and ground iron ore mixed with charcoal. The mixture was set on fire and, since the air entered the furnace by gravity, like a regular fire, the combustion went slowly, about a day. In the process, slag flowed out of the furnace, removing up to 80% of iron from the charge. In the end, it turned out kriza (or cheese-iron) - a large piece of hard iron with inclusions from solidified slag and unburned coal. The die turned out to be larger than the hole for loading the charge and blowing, so it was impossible to extract it without destroying the furnace wall. But why not make a bigger hole? Because then in the furnace there will be no draft and the mixture will not burn. So each time the furnace was partially destroyed to extract the cricket, which was then forged to mechanically knock out pieces of slag and coal from it. Giving the cricket the desired shape was only a byproduct of forging. The furnace was then restored, the benefit is relatively simple - it is clay.



Primitive implements were made of cry iron, but they were superior to wooden ones. Then, of course, they began to make weapons that were stronger and cheaper than the bronze - there was a transition from the Bronze Age to the Iron.



Over the past few thousand years, iron has become one of the foundations of human civilization. Ferrous metallurgy has come a long way from meteorite iron and the first raw-furnace furnaces to modern steel production. The main milestones of this path I collected in the timeline.







At some stages I want to dwell in more detail.



For example, the history of Damascus steel and damask steel is noteworthy, this is an example of technology that was somewhat ahead of its time, but turned out to be a dead end. In pursuit of the high quality of the metal, the ancient metallurgists, acting by trial and error, found several ways to improve the quality of critical iron. Damask steel is obtained by combining high-carbon cast iron and iron so that the carbon content in the volume is heterogeneous. It was a difficult and unreliable way and as technology developed, it was forgotten. In the XIX century, technology was restored, but recognized as low-tech and irrelevant. With Damascus steel, the story is similar, the difference is that the heterogeneity of the composition was achieved by multiple reforging and layer-by-layer welding of cast iron with iron. By the time of the opening, both damask and damascus were significantly ahead in quality of ordinary screaming iron,but getting them was difficult and expensive, the secret of technology was carefully guarded and gradually lost. But the myths remained, at first quite justified, but as the development of the basic technology, they became less and less believable.



Until about the XVII century, the development of ferrous metallurgy was quantitative: the volume of furnaces and the height of pipes increased, the temperature inside increased; The operations of loading the charge and unloading the crits were optimized. In fact, the turning point was the achievement in the furnace of the melting point of cast iron (1300 ¬∞ C), and then steel (~ 1500 ¬∞ C).

Ferrous metallurgy today



The established technological chain of steel production at the upper level consists of the following stages:



  1. Ore mining and processing
  2. Reduction of iron from ore by coke in a blast furnace to produce pig iron
  3. Conversion
  4. Steel processing (hot or cold rolling)






Towards the end of the 19th century, three main technologies applied for redistribution took shape: converter, open-hearth, and electrothermal processes.



Converter process







He is also Besmerovsky , the direct heir to the raw blast. Cast iron obtained in a blast furnace is poured into a converter, where it is blown with air, excess carbon burns out with heat and liquid steel is formed, which does not need to be mechanically cleaned of slag and coal, but can only be cast into the desired shape. Over the past 150 years, the converter process has not changed much, the main difference is that instead of air, pure oxygen is blown through cast iron under pressure.



Open-hearth process







In addition to cast iron, iron ore and scrap metal were placed in an open-hearth furnace. Having some advantages over converter (the possibility of processing scrap metal and the simplicity of controlling the chemical composition of steel), the open-hearth process has a critical drawback - a long duration. In a modern oxygen converter, cast iron is converted to steel in 30 minutes, and in an open-hearth furnace, it takes several hours. So now in the world there are practically no active open-hearth furnaces sung in Soviet art.



Electrothermal process







In the converter process, the charge is heated due to exothermic chemical reactions. But thanks to one of the most important discoveries of the Enlightenment - electricity - a new way appeared: electric arc heating. Moreover, electricity equally effectively heats both cast iron and scrap metal, so the possibility of its processing became an additional advantage of this approach.



At first, there simply wasn‚Äôt enough available sources of electricity in the world and until about the middle of the 20th century converters and open-hearths ruled the ball, but as soon as the era of cheap electricity came, the electrothermal method began to develop rapidly.



The domain process as a whole has been preserved since the 19th century without significant qualitative changes. Of course, quantitatively, he has made great progress, but many plants still operate blast furnaces built a hundred or more years ago.



For the redistribution of cast iron into steel, two of the three smelting technologies described in the previous section are used: converter and electrothermal processes. Accordingly, the basis of the modern steel shop is either an oxygen converter (CC) or an arc steel furnace ( chipboard , not to be confused with chipboard ).



These processes coexist peacefully, without crowding out each other, since they have complementary features.







In some places, open-hearth furnaces still work, but in general, this method of steel production is a thing of the past. In 2018, the last large open-hearth furnace at the Vyksa Metallurgical Plant was stopped in Russia. By the way, one of the recently opened open-hearth shops is located on the territory of the Ashinsky Metallurgical Plant (AMZ), where the Datana team worked in 2018 - 2019 and about which several articles have already been published on Habr√© . The first of the three open-hearth furnaces worked there for exactly 100 years, from 1910 to November 2010.



Next to the open-hearth shop in AMZ, a new electric steel-smelting shop No. 2 (ESPTs 2) was built, where a modern chipboard-120 operates. By the way, it is at the AMZ that steel is obtained exclusively from scrap metal, the blast furnace shop was stopped there back in 1986.



But at the Magnitogorsk Metallurgical Combine (MMK), the conversion of cast iron to steel takes place in the oxygen-converter shop (CCC) , where we have been working since the end of 2019 and are implementing a number of cases at different sites and assemblies.

Features and problems of industrial data



Sources and Transformations



Warm analogue signal



Data is taken from somewhere, so let's start with the data sources and their path to the repository. For example, in web analytics, data is collected through standard APIs and neatly stored, say, in ClickHouse or Cassandra and are always available in a convenient form ... Well, of course, it almost never happens. But nevertheless, in production, a significant part of the data is generated in analog sensors that record certain physical or chemical parameters of the process, therefore, first they take the form of an electrical impulse. The variety of sensors used is very large: simple mechanical contacts, electrical meters, flow meters (for gases, liquids and powders), thermocouples, strain gauges, vibration sensors and many others. On one unit there can be several hundred sensors of varying complexity, the signals from them are collected by controllers,and many - in real time (with discreteness of tens and units of milliseconds), and sent to the industrial control system (automated process control system), and from there they already enter the database. Or do not fall, but more on that later.



In addition to sensors, data sources can be more complex devices, starting with more or less familiar video cameras (often temperature is measured using IR cameras) and ending with spectrometers and X-ray stream chemical analyzers. Almost all manufacturers of complex equipment use their own data formats, so in industrial systems we are dealing with a whole zoo: xml, json or, for example, iba; Industrial protocols like PROFINET or PROFIBUS more familiar dumps of Oracle and MS SQL. Sometimes a database dump is provided in unpacked form, as a set of text files with lists of CREATEs and INSERTs, which we parse and run. It happens that the data exists exclusively in the form of files that the proprietary system saves to the server,in this case, the combat integration with the data source can be a bash script from the production side, which according to the schedule puts a new file in the shared folder, and a script from our side that reads this file, parses it and converts the data to a more convenient form for work .



Manual input



But, unfortunately, it is still very difficult to meet production, where all technological processes are fully digitized. So in all industries we worked with, there is manual data entry. Either the laboratory assistant manually enters the results of the chemical analysis into Excel, or the senior shift manually enters the heat number. Or, for example, the technologist manually compiles a plate in Excel every day, according to which the raw materials are prepared for loading into the furnace (this is called the ‚Äúcharge passport‚Äù). In order not to drive everything every day again, he copies the plate and no, no, and yes, he accidentally skips a couple of cells, leaving yesterday's data there. Or last year‚Äôs.







Often, engineers can edit data in the database, even if they get there automatically.



In most cases, manual input leads to unintentional errors, but sometimes the ‚Äúhuman factor‚Äù becomes completely deliberate, especially when it comes to indicators on which the size of the premium depends ...



Data quality



Volume and depth



In most cases, production storages cannot be compared with the volumes of data storage systems of data centers of traditional IT sectors or, for example, the banking sector. Therefore, a look at a year ago is usually possible, but the set of indicators is very thin out compared to the available in real time. And data deeper than one year usually cannot be called large even with a stretch, if any.



This also includes the very case when the data from the sensors go, but never reach the storage. This occurs when the amount of available information for the plant is excessive and is not collected. For example, the plant was modernized, a new unit was installed, with its proprietary information system, which collects a lot of information about the process. We see a list of signals, we rejoice in abundance, but it wasn‚Äôt there: almost all of this is not needed by the plant, so most of it is not integrated with the automatic process control system, and often even production workers are not aware of what is there and what is not.



Misses, Errors and Delays



It is difficult to meet a set of real data without gaps; the question is how many there are. Gaps arise for various reasons, the simplest of which is the lack of data at a particular point in time or a random error when writing to the database. But under production conditions, additional sources of data loss appear. For example, a detached contact at one of hundreds of sensors, the signal of which is not critical for conducting the process, but is vital for modeling, can lead to unsuitability of the data array over a considerable interval.



Often gaps are associated with manual data entry, which we discussed above.

Data errors, in general, arise for the same reasons as omissions. For example, we constantly encounter zero or even negative values ‚Äã‚Äãof the ‚Äúweight‚Äù parameter. A single failure of the weight sensor - and we can no longer find out exactly how much ferrochrome was ‚Äúdelivered‚Äù to the furnace during a specific melting. It is not difficult to track and discard zero weight, but if the scales are simply incorrectly calibrated, we can find out about it purely by accident or not at all.



And there are mistakes that are simply inexplicable. From the recent: I loaded factory data into our database and the request fell at some point. I started to sort it out and found that there was a certain number of records for November 31, and the smart Postgres did not want to insert such a date in a field of type Date. How it happened and what date it should be there I do not know. Probably, in the factory database, the date is stored in a field with a common text type and at some point the date floated.

Usually new data is available in real time and we fight the maximum in a matter of seconds of delay. But in production, it happens that some desired indicator gets into the plant‚Äôs information systems in a few hours or even days. Melting is underway, a slag sample is taken for chemical analysis, and its result will be entered into the system only in the evening. And if the heat is night, then in general in a day. This is not critical when we examine historical data and train models, but when we need to formulate recommendations for conducting the process in real time, this is important.



The meaning of data



If you are an industry expert and perfectly understand the specifics of production, you perfectly understand what this or that parameter in the database means. But if not, indicators like these can be confusing:



The system of raising the vessel. The control valve of the mobile platform 1. Top

We work in close contact with technologists and we have documentation, so that all the indicators have become more or less clear to us, but at first these examples made us laugh .



Someone may say: ‚ÄúWell, we are data analysts, put everything into the model and it will put the weights on the indicators and discard unnecessary ones, why should we understand at all ?!‚Äù - and it will be wrong.







Understanding the meaning of indicators will allow us to create a primary limited space of features presented in the most appropriate form for the most objective modeling of production processes. If necessary, the space can be expanded, but with an understanding of the meaning of the data, you can immediately discard those indicators that will not have a big impact on the conditional (or actual) target label. In addition, some indicators may be deliberately dependent. And it often happens that to identify such hidden dependencies it is more profitable to apply expert knowledge rather than statistical analysis.
