One thing is certain for sure: it has always changed. We are well aware that from the fourteenth to the eighteenth centuries it was much colder in Europe than now. It was the time of the so-called Small Ice Age. On the other hand, a thousand years ago it was a little warmer here than in the 20th century. So what is wrong now? Should we pay so much attention to the current changes? Or is it better to calm down and not worry in vain?



First of all, let's define what the term ‚Äúmodern global warming‚Äù means. Throughout the world, scientists conduct constant instrumental observations of the climate. Well, then what is the reason for such a warming? Can we compare it with those that have happened before? Instrumental observations began only in the middle of the 19th century; therefore, climatologists are forced to turn to a field of science called paleogeography. Scientists do have many techniques for studying climate change in the past, the most famous of which is dendrochronology.

Scientists cut down trees or drill small holes to study the chemical composition and width of each layer. This provides an understanding of the conditions in which the plant developed, including temperature and humidity. For the same purpose, sediments are studied at the bottom of lakes and oceans, calcite deposits such as stalagmites or corals, and also snow deposits on glaciers.



Geomorphology is another method of studying the climate of past eras.

Now it's time to take a look at the possible causes of this warming. When it comes to the anthropogenic impact on our climate, we immediately recall the greenhouse effect.



The idea that the atmosphere somehow warms our planet arose many centuries ago. At least already in the XVIII century, this was known, but a comprehensive scientific description of how such an effect physically works was given by the Swedish scientist Svante Arrhenius much later. In 1896, he wrote the work "On the effect of carbon dioxide in the air on the temperature of the earth's surface."







The principle of the greenhouse effect is as follows:



If a planet does not have an atmosphere, it receives short-wave radiation from a star and heats up, after which it begins to emit a long-wave radiation back into space. The warmer the planet, the higher the level of long-wave radiation emanating from it. At a certain temperature, thermal equilibrium will come - the planet will release as much energy as it receives.







But what happens if an atmosphere with greenhouse gases is added to this picture?







The planet receives shortwave radiation because the atmosphere is transparent. It is this fact that allows us, for example, to admire the starry sky at night. Then the planet begins to emit infrared long-wave radiation, for which the atmosphere is transparent to a much lesser extent. The atmosphere captures part of this radiation and begins to warm the planet, returning it back to the surface. Only a small amount of this long-wave radiation goes into space. And in this system, the surface of the planet needs much more intense heating in order to achieve thermal equilibrium.



Of course, as always, the real situation is a little more complicated. After all, there are also clouds that reflect part of the incoming solar radiation, part of the radiation reflects the surface itself. Other processes take place (such as convection, which also transfers heat from the surface to the atmosphere), phase transitions, etc. But the effect of the greenhouse effect on our planet is obvious.

People always ask climatologists if the greenhouse effect is good or bad. But the fact is that in nature there are neither good nor bad phenomena, everything goes on as usual. However, for mankind, the greenhouse effect is really very good, because without it the average temperature on our planet would be -25‚Å∞ C. So the greenhouse effect warms our planet by about 40‚Å∞ C and makes life possible here.



Several major gases are responsible for the greenhouse effect. First of all, water vapor, whose concentration in the atmosphere is very high. A reasonable question arises about the greenhouse effect caused by water vapor. In fact, climatologists talk a lot about this, but the concentration of water vapor in the atmosphere has certain physical boundaries, since it easily condenses - unlike carbon dioxide, which is also one of the main greenhouse gases. The volume of CO‚ÇÇ is relatively small, but it is not limited and is now constantly growing. Therefore, the effect of this gas is huge.



Another gas - methane - is much more powerful than carbon dioxide, but, fortunately for us, the amount of this gas is even less. The last gas to be mentioned is ozone. There are others, but it is these four that play a crucial role in the emergence of the greenhouse effect.



The role of the greenhouse effect on the climate of our planet has been known for many decades. Any climate book published in the last 50 years has a section on the greenhouse effect with many examples. For example, during the Carboniferous (Carboniferous) period 350 million years ago, there was much more CO‚ÇÇ in the atmosphere, and the average temperature on Earth was much higher than now. Then the plants began to develop so quickly that their complete decomposition under the influence of bacteria after death was not possible. This led to the accumulation of organic residues. As a result of this process, plants contributed to the removal of a significant amount of CO‚ÇÇ from the atmosphere, the planet began to cool, which ended in large-scale glaciation and the mass extinction of flora and fauna.



Human influence on climate is also not a new idea. Scientists wrote a lot about this back in the 1960s and 1970s. In 1970, Soviet scientist Mikhail Budyko from Leningrad published an article on the climate of the future. He made the following statement: ‚ÄúThanks to human economic activity, the concentration of CO‚ÇÇ will increase to 0.038% by the end of the century ... models show that due to the increase in CO‚ÇÇ the temperature will increase by 0.5‚Å∞ at the same time."



In fact, when he conducted his research, there were still no signs of warming, at that moment there was even a slight cooling. And to many people his idea might have seemed strange. But after he published his work, the situation changed: rapid warming began. Surprisingly, Budyko's calculations turned out to be very accurate, despite the fact that he used extremely simple models designed for relatively primitive computers. However, the actual figures by the end of the century turned out to be very close to its prediction: CO‚ÇÇ increased by 0.037%, and the temperature increased by 0.5‚Å∞ C.

Humanity affects the climate in various ways. The most obvious of these is an increase in the greenhouse effect, but there are others that lead to both warming and cooling. For example, various types of aerosols either heat the planet due to the greenhouse effect, or reflect sunlight, lowering the temperature. They also affect the occurrence of clouds, which, in principle, became larger due to the increased concentration of aerosols.



Another cooling factor is an increase in the albedo of the earth's surface. This is a measure of the reflective properties of our planet - the higher the albedo coefficient, the more energy is reflected from the surface. In general, many processes go in parallel, but nevertheless their total amount leads to global warming.

What is the relationship between the natural and anthropogenic factors that are affecting climate today? We need to present the temperature anomaly in several graphs that show various processes that affect the average temperature on Earth.







If we look at solar activity, we will see that over the past few decades, the Earth receives a little less energy from the Sun than before, so this factor cannot explain the warming.



Then comes the volcanic component: the second graph shows that after each large eruption of the volcano, more aerosols appear in the atmosphere. They begin to reflect sunlight, then the planet cools for several years. In recent decades, volcanic activity has increased slightly, which really lowered the temperature a little.



Some internal variability is also observed (for example, the El Nino phenomenon), but it also cannot explain the warming.



Finally, the anthropogenic factor accounts for about 90% of the climatic trends of the 20th century. Almost certainly (with a probability of> 99.9%), modern global warming is due to anthropogenic impact.







The black line in the second graph is the observed temperature, and the colored lines are model calculations explaining this temperature variability. Climatologists use the term climate change, because on our planet at the same time something more is happening than just warming. And these other climate processes also need to be analyzed. In addition to warming itself, it should be noted that there is a greater spread of extreme natural phenomena. This means that periods of extreme heat in summer and extreme cold in winter can occur more often. And severe frost does not at all contradict the general trend towards warming.



The process that should follow the warming is the melting of glaciers and sea ice. In fact, this is already happening. Similar processes are observed in the continental Arctic - permafrost is melting there. Another process ongoing on the planet is the mass extinction of species. Not all extinctions are directly caused by climate change: people simply kill forests and use the freed up space to grow more agricultural plants, while plants and animals also die from diseases, etc.



There is also the problem of shifting vegetation zones to the north. This is because the climate is getting warmer, which means that new plants begin to grow where they could never survive before. For example, in some parts of the Arctic, taiga has already replaced the tundra. This also leads to some diseases becoming more frequent, and we can already see this in cases with malaria or anthrax.



Currently, the ocean dissolves more CO‚ÇÇ than before, and a subsequent increase in acidity is dangerous for marine species such as corals or fish. The problem of hypoxia (a decrease in the amount of dissolved oxygen) is also already observed in the ocean.
