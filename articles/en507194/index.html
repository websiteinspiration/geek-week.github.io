The mask is user!


Here, in fact, everything is simple. We introduce ourselves as a user and write tests based on which we run the program. In fact, this is the most important type of testing and is most often called Dymov, when the basic, basic functions of the product are checked for compliance with the actual result expected. If you delve into this issue, you will learn many other types and levels of testing, but today we are talking more about the approach to testing.

Mask - support manager


Let's continue - the next role-mask, which a testing specialist should be able to try on, is the Tester-support manager. Why exactly the role of this specialist? Yes, because the first person to encounter the user after the product is launched on the market will be the support manager. Therefore, the tester should always ask myself if I did everything I could to ensure that Support did not go down after the release (in the understanding that the load on the support would increase so much that it would cease to cope with it). Here you can miss a simple detail in the product documentation (guide). Not even that something will not be described, but that even the guide itself may be poorly thought out and executed. Tooltips for fields and buttons in the application may be absent or not true (obsolete).
The support manager is a very difficult and psychologically exhausting job, and the tester has every chance of maintaining the pacified state of a colleague for a slightly longer period.

Mask - Business Intelligence


The next, as for me, the most Important is the role of Business Intelligence. It is most important because the business analyst assumes, and the tester has and already sees much more during testing than the analyst who described the feature in the documentation. You can compare it with poker: A business analyst is a player who knows the rules, knows the playing style of other players, knows what needs to be done, but doesn‚Äôt see the cards, unlike a tester who, though he doesn‚Äôt know everything, sees all the cards. Well, it‚Äôs obvious that the pocket cards are the same for both of them, but they sit side by side at the poker table and play as one - they are a team. All of the above boils down to the fact that the tester should be directly involved in the process of inventing new features and improving the old functionality.

Mask - project manager


Project Manager. Testing is the last step in product development before release. This means that the tester (such a good, scorched one) knows and communicates with all team members, knows the project workflow, knows how all the scrum artifacts in the team are arranged, and in which case they can be picked up and applied, for example, a demo rally or a retrospective. A good tester sees bottlenecks in the team‚Äôs processes because all of them ultimately turn testing into a weak link in the project. For example: a poor assessment of tasks was always a blunder at the end of the sprint and why the release is not ready - they did not have time to test, the lack of a code freeze means test failures and missed bugs due to constant stuffing in the test environment, who missed the bugs - testers, etc. Tester , the most responsible position in the team, as he approves the product for release, which meanshe is responsible for everything that happens next (I mean pop-up bugs), regardless of what caused this. Therefore, taking care of itself, the tester takes care of the team.

Mask - Psychoanalyst


The psychoanalyst tester (returning to the above point) communicates closely with all team members and those who work with the team indirectly. He knows the mood in the team, knows the pain and suffering of its members and, of course, how a sponge pumps it all through himself. It turns out that not only is the tester himself always in the zone of high stress, but he also becomes the pillow for the bitter tears of his colleagues, which, as a result, becomes a tool for bringing employees together to further increase efficiency.
The conclusion is quite simple: you need to be able to put yourself in the place of others and if all members of the development team could and could do so, such a team would send people to Mars a long time ago. But the conclusion is simple, but in practice it is much harder to apply. We are all different, the teams are different, the customers are different, and so on. But do not give up, you can start with the usual morning question to a colleague - how are you? how is your mood?
