Joel Spolsky: Gamification Role in Stack Overflow Success

In 2010, the success of Stack Overflow made venture capitalists want to invest in us. 



The firm that eventually became our sponsor was called Union Square Ventures . Its owners admitted that they were so attracted to the concept of gamification that they invested only in those companies that incorporate a gameplay element into their product. 

For example, Foursquare. Remember such a network? It allowed you to turn a trip into a cheap cafe or bar into an exciting game, which along the way generated data for marketers. Or Duolingo is a fun flashcard app for learning foreign languages. At that time, USV invested in similar companies.

That's when I realized that Stack Overflow also has a gamification element. Not so obvious, of course, onlya little bit related to reputation. 

The Stack Overflow reputation element began with a rating system. The initial idea was that participants get 10 points when their answers like. Such a system of upgrades performs two functions at once: the most useful answers go to the top, letting other forum participants understand that this advice turned out to be useful; she also shows the person who wrote this answer that his labors did not go in vain. And it motivates how.

For downouts on your answer, you lose 2 reputation points. We did not so much want to ‚Äúpunish‚Äù for a bad answer, but wanted to show others that the answer was wrong. And so that people do not begin to lower the rating of answers just so that to put downown you will also have to give 1 point, so it‚Äôs better to think about this decision twice. That, in principle, is what our system consisted of. 

We did not come up with such a concept. We were inspired by the Reddit karma system, which was displayed in the header of your account. If you wrote a post or comment that you like many times, as a ‚Äúreward‚Äù your karma increased. That's all. This karma did not give anything else , but it worked well as a system of rewards and punishments. 

The idea behind karma and reputation is to make it clear that these communities have norms, and you just don‚Äôt have to scatter words there (there is 4chan for this). We did not create a forum so that you could practice your ‚Äúsharp tongue‚Äù and eloquence. You can express freedom of speech elsewhere. Our goal is to provide the best answers to your questions. The system of votes shows that we have certain standards, that some posts are better than others, and that the community itself has a certain idea of ‚Äã‚Äãwhat can be considered a good answer and what is not. And this is what forum members voice. 

The system is not perfect (I‚Äôll talk about the problems a bit further), but it works quite well. 
Incidentally, Alexis Ohanyan and Steve Huffman, the creators of Reddit, were themselves inspired to create their karma system by the more primitive Slashdot scheme. On this site, karma existed for a very limited purpose: you earned it not to show off before others, but to prevent the system from blocking you as a spammer. If your posts were flagged for violation of community rules, then karma decreased, because of which you could lose the right to moderate and publish posts. But this karma was not something cool to brag about. ‚ÄúDon‚Äôt worry too much, it's just a number in the database,‚Äù Slashdot representatives told us.

Honestly, at first I was surprised that people are happy with some numbers in the profile header, like candy.Hey! Look, what a cool four-digit number I have! But it really motivates people to behave well. Even those who are not trying to earn reputation points are still led to this system, respecting users with a high reputation as mentors and masters of their craft.

I suppose the point is this: it‚Äôs nice to know that you have done something useful for others. After spending all day in the kitchen cooking, I want to hear a couple of thanks at the table. Undoubtedly, if someone praises the radish that you cut beautifully, you will be very happy. 

In the hierarchy of human needs, the need to influence the world, to contribute to its development and receive recognition for this is among the highest. The Stack Overflow reputation system demonstrates our respect for the individual and gratitude for the contribution to the common cause. 



However, gamification has its own dark side. This system is not 100% perfect.

The first problem: we noticed that getting upgrades is very nice, but downouts are perceived as a knife in the back. Especially when, in principle, it is not clear why people did not like the answer, or if you do not agree with this. Over the years of Stack Overflow's existence, people have repeatedly expressed their dissatisfaction with the reputation system. It is likely that many perceived downouts as an indicator that they were not welcome here, and therefore ceased to participate in discussions. Another problem with the impact of gamification on Stack Overflow is that for many the site does not seem inclusive and welcoming, it pushes programmers from among the underrepresented groups. Despite the fact that there are women and representatives of minorities among the members of the community with a high reputation, we also heard that many are afraid to take part in the discussions. 

And these are serious problems. Over the next months we will return to these points more than once. There is a lot of work ahead, because our goal is to make Stack Overflow more inclusive, which will help to improve the conditions for providing useful information to developers around the world. 

Gamification shapes behavior. She suggests what can be done and what cannot, thereby contributing to the development of a specific behavioral pattern. But her strength is not great.

Gamification cannot solve all the problems. If a person does not want to do something, the system cannot force him. I have heard of many crazy business projects that rely entirely on gamification as a way to get people to do what they don't want. No one will learn French for points at Duolingo. But if you learn the language and use this application, then saving the shock mode will force you to open it at least once a day.
