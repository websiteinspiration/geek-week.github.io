Problems With Paywalls



Part I



I hate paid subscriptions. I categorically do not digest them.



A typical argument in favor of business: companies can either make your life better (by providing you with your favorite products) or not affect it in any way (by offering products that you don’t like and don’t use). But they certainly can’t harm you. Although there are a couple of exceptions. For example, if they supplant or destroy their favorite company, or their activities nevertheless cause any side effects, or they lobby the government for their own benefit. But in general, if a certain business annoys you, you must understand how one of these unusual conditions works. Otherwise, “companies simply do not bring the benefits that you would like,” without causing any harm.



So, here is a standard explanation for introducing a paid subscription. Journalists offer you the following scenario: you read their articles, and pay in return. You cannot claim their product without paying money. Journalists need to earn a living, like everyone else. Therefore, you can either agree to their proposal by giving money for the article, or reject the deal, and from that your life will not get worse.



But my inner voice says that everything is wrong. I am sure that I would have been much happier living in a world in which there are no major publications than living in a world where there are newspapers that require a fee for their material. Think for a second whether you feel the same way. If so, then what happens?



First, paid newspapers sometimes use clickbait. They lure you with a curious headline, and then require a pay-per-view.



Here are some advertised articles that I’ve recently come across (not all of them come from paid sources): “Why Trump’s fight with Obama can go sideways,” “Technology Guru voiced a shocking prediction for 2020”, “That's why men send loaded pistols to their members ".



Listen, but I didn’t wake up this morning with the thought: “I wonder if the men are pointing loaded pistols at their members, and if so, why. I hope some enterprising journalist has studied this issue, and I will gladly pay her for satisfying my irrepressible curiosity. ” No, on the contrary, I was absolutely, naively happy exactly until the moment I saw this headline. I was literally overwhelmed by curiosity, or even rather the feeling that I would not be happy until I found out what was happening there. In this particular case, nothing bad happened, as access to the scandalous news source (VICE website) is open. I go to the page and after reading the nine paragraphs devoted to the attacks on the "silly Trump supporters", in the tenth, finally, I see one single sentence that reveals the topic: this is a meme,common in the arms community. Every time someone publishes a photo with his gun, amateurs fill up who vyingly give out tips on how to hold a weapon and how to handle it. Therefore, in order to annoy such advisers, some people upload photos in which they hold a loaded pistol at the level of their pants. Now, knowing this information, I did not become wiser, my day was not lit up with new colors. Many criticize capitalism and argue that it creates new needs, thereby motivating people to spend more money to satisfy them. In truth, I have not observed this at all - I buy shoes only when I need shoes, and I buy Coca-Cola only when I want Coca-Cola. But as regards journalism, it seems to me that this just works. The VICE portal came up with a need for me (the need to find out why some people aim at their members), and then satisfied it. In general, I did not get anything valuable, but I did not lose it either. But, it seems, this is not very similar to the provision of services.



They have an excuse that this is the only way they can make money. What's the excuse for the Marginal Revolution blog? I saw a link in their summary. She was obsolete like this: “5. Why do men point loaded pistols at their members. ” Of course I clicked on it, and here we are. But what is MR’s interest in clicking on the VICE article and reading nine paragraphs about the “foolish Trump supporters”?



In fact, I do not blame them, because for years I have been doing the same thing. I posted posts with links, intentionally made links provocative, and was in a good mood when statistics showed that thousands of people clicked on them. Sometimes I made a detailed headline - “Here’s an article about men who send loaded pistols to their members, and they do it because they want to condemn too much attention and overcome insulting comments about the safe storage of weapons” - but then no one followed the link, and I saw it as a sign that I failed. I was an idiot, I apologize to all of you, I stopped doing this. And I urge other bloggers to do the same - we do not receive any additional money, nor the government, nor the readership. We are like dogs rushing for a wild trick VICE,which trick people into reading silly articles. But until bloggers, Facebook, twitter, etc., monitor the hygiene of the Internet, the mere existence of these paid portals will cause a frank negativity of the average user.



It’s not just a frank clickbait, like “men aiming at their members”. The headline “Why Trump's fight with Obama can go sideways” seems to me the same. I do not want to know any ephemeral garbage about Trump and how his selective rating changes by 0.5% per week there, before returning to the initial level. I don’t want my indignation to grow to the point that I’ll build communication with people based on whether they are “foolish Trump supporters” or not. Nevertheless, I am curious what is written there!



(Trump’s fight with Obama may sideways with Trump, because large independent figures are more inclined towards Obama. And the forecast of the technology guru for 2020 was that Trump will lose. Do not thank.)



Secondly, paid articles are among the issues discussed.



Last week, the Wall Street Journal published a review article, “Insulated Vs. Vulnerable ”, which states that, according to statistics, the isolation regime does not really stop the coronavirus, but it especially affects the most vulnerable groups of the population. This article has already been retweeted several dozen times, including by some bloggers with tens of thousands of subscribers.



Are you interested in what specific statistics are used and want to check whether they really show that isolation does not stop the coronavirus? It’s extremely sad - the article is paid, and you can’t read it without subscribing to The Wall Street Journal for $ 19.50 per month. I personally suspect that this article is fundamentally mistaken, perhaps even completely idiotic. But I can neither convince others of this, nor correct my own potentially false first impression without paying the Wall Street Journal $ 19.50 per month for the subscription. I don’t want to do this. Partly because the utility is minimal, and partly because I do not want to reward them for publishing false things.



Newspapers publish articles - factual and with private opinion - with the intention of receiving their public discussion. But if a paid contribution is required to participate in a public discussion, the number of participants in such a discussion sharply decreases.



The situation is more and more like a deaf phone. Conservatives probably subscribe to the Wall Street Journal, while liberals subscribe to the New York Times. Thus, if conservatives publish articles in the Wall Street Journal, liberals can neither use reliable information and change their views, nor refute false material and influence the views of conservatives. If you cannot even read the arguments of the other side, then how can they convince you of something?



Thirdly, newspapers make it difficult to understand whether you get on a paid subscription or not. Some people only subscribe to certain types of articles. Some portals require a subscription if you follow the link from social networks, but do not request a subscription if you came from Google (or vice versa). Some require a subscription if you are already reading the Xth article in the current month from your computer.



In the end, you simply cannot take and learn to avoid newspapers with a paid subscription. If you clearly knew which links were paid and which weren’t, you wouldn’t click on anything and do not waste time. Since any newspaper with a probability of 25-50% receives money every time you read it, you have no choice but to come to terms with this strategy and continue to strengthen the frustrating dependence. And since at any moment you desperately want to click on the link and find out why some men send loaded pistols to their members, you, like a blockhead, press it, but only to howl with rage when the subscription window appears.



This is usually not done deliberately; newspapers, for obvious reasons, provide limited access so that readers can decide whether they want to subscribe or not. But, it seems to me, some strategies are really applied purposefully. For example, when you are allowed to read the first two paragraphs, imbued with history, and then, on the approach to the third, they are dumbfounded by a paid subscription (I think that’s why you are slipping nine paragraphs before you show one single guess). Or when, for the same reason, they wait five seconds before sending a message requesting a subscription.



Fourth, and most importantly, paid newspapers make it difficult to find on Google. When I tried to compile statistics on coronavirus to find out how fast it spreads, I found that the first ten or twenty relevant search results turned out to be paid articles. Since the paid subscription does not appear immediately, and you have a couple of paragraphs / seconds to familiarize yourself, I could not just click on the link, quickly check if there is paid access, and then proceed to the next. Instead, the search, which would take me only a few seconds (if all the sources of paid subscriptions saved us from our existence), dragged on for several disappointing minutes.



Part II





There are a couple of simple solutions to help fix this.



First, search engines should provide users with the ability to hide results from paid articles. I imagine what a storm of shit it will cause, and plan to enjoy every second of this scandal. If for some reason this cannot be done, then at least draw a huge red $$$ sign in front of articles with a paid subscription, so that before they spend the treasured click, users know by clicking on which link they really get the information. If Google refuses to do this, then let Bing take care and then overtake Google. If both refuse, DuckDuckGo is in. If all three refuse, then apparently they are preparing a vacancy for some happy entrepreneur.



Secondly, developers of browsers or browser extensions should come up with some way to automatically display the contents of links, whether they are paid or not. Perhaps something similar already exists, but I have not found it yet.



Thirdly, bloggers (and social media users) must stop intentionally spoiling the mood of their readers. Stop posting teasing links like “Why do men point charged pistols at their members” without further explanation! If you find the cannon-guns to be an interesting topic, post a short link at least in one sentence. If someone needs more details, they’ll follow link, but I did A / B testing - this hasn’t happened.



Fourthly, bloggers (and users of social networks) should first of all post links to free sites. I understand that this is not always possible, but most high-profile topics and stories are so important that at least one free resource will definitely cover them.



Fifth, until a browser extension appears, bloggers (and social media users) who really need to attach a link to a paid site should inform readers that they will need a subscription. For example, Isolated Vs. Vulnerable [SUBSCRIBE] or [$$$] Isolated Vs. Vulnerable. This will save readers a click and, hopefully, make bloggers think about what they are doing and whether it is really necessary.



From now on, I undertake to comply with paragraphs 3, 4 and 5. If I ever change my mind and abandon this obligation, I will let you know. If you notice that I made a mistake somewhere, please let me know (politely) and I will try to correct myself.
