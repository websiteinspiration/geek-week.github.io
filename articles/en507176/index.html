MMS system in the data center: how we automated maintenance management

Imagine that you have a complete server-side engineering equipment: several dozen air conditioners, a bunch of diesel generator sets and uninterruptible power supplies. In order for the hardware to work as it should, you regularly check its performance and do not forget about prevention: conduct test runs, check the oil level, and change parts. Even for one server, you need to store a lot of information: the equipment registry, a list of consumables in the warehouse, a schedule of maintenance work, as well as warranty documents, contracts with suppliers and contractors. 

Now multiply the number of rooms by ten. There were questions of logistics. In what warehouse what to store, so as not to run after each spare part? How to replenish supplies on time so that unscheduled repairs are not taken by surprise? If there is a lot of equipment, it is impossible to keep all the technical work in your head, but hard to write on paper. This is where MMS, or maintenance management system, comes to the rescue — a hardware maintenance management system. 


In MMS, we draw up schedules for maintenance and repair work, and store instructions for engineers. Not all data centers have such a system, many consider it to be too expensive a solution. But we have seen from our own experience that it is not the tool that is important, but the approach to working with information. We created the first system in Excel and gradually refined it to a software product. 

Together withalexddroppWe decided to share the experience of developing our own MMS. I will show how the system developed and how it helped to implement the best maintenance practices. Alexey will tell you how he inherited MMS, what has changed during this time and how the system makes life easier for engineers now. 

How we came to our own MMS


First there were folders . 8-10 years ago, information was stored in separate form. After maintenance, we signed acts of completion, stored paper originals in archives, and scanned copies on network folders. In the same way, information about spare parts was collected in folders by equipment: spare parts, tools and accessories. So you can live if you build the structure and access levels for these folders.

If you faithfully keep the document in Excel, the solution is quite suitable for a small server room. But it is also temporary. Even if we use one air conditioner and do maintenance once a month, in five years we will accumulate hundreds of errors, and our ex-apple will swell. If you add another air conditioner, one diesel generator, one UPS, then you need to make several sheets and interlink. The longer the story, the more difficult it is to grab the right information straight away. 

The first "adult" system. In 2014, we passed the first Management & Operations audit according to Operational Sustainability standards from the Uptime Institute. We passed almost with the same excel, but over the course of the year we greatly modified it: added links to instructions and checklists for engineers. Auditors found this format quite working. They were able to track all operations with the equipment and made sure that the information was up-to-date and the processes were built. The audit then passed “with a bang”, gaining 92 points out of 100 possible.

The question arose: how to live on. We decided that we needed a “serious” MMS, looked at several paid programs, but in the end decided to write software ourselves. The same Excel was used as a detailed TK. These are the tasks we set for MMS. 

What we wanted from MMS


In most cases, MMS is a collection of directories and reports. Our hierarchy of directories looks something like this:



The very first top-level directory is a list of buildings : mashrooms, warehouses, where the equipment is located.



Next is a list of engineering equipment . We collected it by the systems:

  • Air conditioning system: air conditioners, chillers, pumps.
  • Power supply system: UPS, diesel generator sets, switchboards.


For each equipment we collect basic data: type, model, serial number, manufacturer data, year of manufacture, date of commissioning, warranty period.

When we filled out the list of equipment, we draw up a maintenance program for it : how and how often to do maintenance. In the maintenance program, we describe a set of operations , for example: replace this battery, configure the operation of a specific part, and so on. The operations are described in a separate directory. If the operation is repeated in different programs, then you do not need to describe it again each time - just take the finished one from the directory: The


operations “Changing the temperature settings” and “Replacing quick-detachable cable connections” will be common for chillers and air conditioning systems of the same manufacturer.

Now for each equipment we can create a maintenance schedule . We tie the maintenance program to specific equipment, and the system itself looks in the program how often it is necessary to do maintenance, and calculates the time from the date of commissioning:
Automation of such a schedule can even be done using Excel formulas.

The story is not entirely obvious: we maintain a separate directory of deferred work . The schedule is a schedule, but we are all living people and we understand that anything can happen. For example, the consumable did not arrive on time and you need to postpone the service for a week. This is a normal situation if you follow it. For pending and outstanding work, we keep statistics and try to ensure that the cancellation of maintenance is aimed at zero.  

Also, statistics are maintained for each equipmentaccidents and unscheduled repairs . We use statistics for procurement planning, searching for weaknesses in the infrastructure. For example, if a compressor burns down in the same place three times in a row, this is a signal to look for the cause of the breakdowns.   


This history of maintenance and repairs has accumulated over 4 years on a specific air conditioner.

The following reference is a ZIP . It takes into account what consumables are needed for equipment, where and in what quantity they are stored. Here we also save information on delivery times in order to better plan receipts to the warehouse. 

The number of spare parts is calculated from the annual statistics of repairs per unit of equipment. For all spare parts we indicate the minimum balance: what minimum parts are needed at each object. If the spare part ends, its number in the directory is highlighted: The

minimum balance of high pressure sensors must be at least two, and only one remains. Urgent time to make an order. 

As soon as the consignment of spare parts arrives, we fill out the directory with data from the consignment note and indicate the storage location. Immediately we see the current balance of such spare parts in stock: We 


separately maintain a directory of contacts. In it we enter the data of suppliers and contractors who conduct maintenance: 



Certificates and access groups for electrical safety are attached to the card of each contractor-engineer. When scheduling, we can see which of the specialists has the required clearance. 


During the existence of MMS, work with site approvals has changed. For example, documents with methodological instructions for maintenance were added. If earlier the set of operations fit in a small checklist, then the detailed instructions provide everything: how to prepare, what conditions are needed, and so on.
