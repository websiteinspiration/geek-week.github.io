How we develop devices


Development and production are inextricably linked. There is a good theoretical plan from Ben Einstein:



Ben very beautifully reflected the complexity of the process of developing a real hardware product, in his article there are many examples. Let's analyze the plan from left to right.

Problem research and proof of concept


First you need to understand what kind of device we are releasing, why we need it, how it matches the strategy of the company or product, at what price we want to sell it, what features it should have. This is the first time we are thinking about whether it is possible to physically make such a device, if we have enough resources for development and so on.

This is the classic analysis step, ‚Äúwhat to do.‚Äù It takes a lot of time, because you need to think through so many things, find stakeholders within the company and agree with everyone.

Between proof of concept and engineering prototype


Here we decide how the device will look and how it will be arranged. The plan shows that there are two parallel processes. The first process is associated with the appearance of the device, the second - with the internal device.

Appearance and UI / UX: buttons, LEDs, sounds, settings, packaging

On the one hand, we are working on issues of appearance: we plan whether the device will be round or square, fabric or plastic, black or white, etc.

On the other hand , here a very serious part of the tasks arises - everything around the UI / UX. Suppose a device flashes a light bulb. Then it is here that it is decided how many these bulbs should be, whether they are white or colored. How does the device respond with sound, how will we provide device configuration? And so on. It is much more than just appearance.

Internal device

In parallel there is a stream of purely engineering tasks. If we talk about smart speakers, then we make the first estimates, how many speakers we need, where we will place the boards, how they will connect together, what software we need, what components will be inside the device and on the board itself. This is early engineering. Here we understand with broad strokes what the device consists of and how its components are interconnected.

Engineering prototype


For the first time, the branches of appearance (orange) and internal device (blue) are connected in an engineering prototype. The reason is obvious: many UX-related things need to be balanced with engineering. For example - how to bring wires to the buttons, where these buttons are located, how the shape of the device affects the size of the board and so on.

The resulting engineering prototype is very vaguely similar to the final device. Usually it is made on a 3D printer and looks ugly. But it already somehow works and (at least in size) is similar to the final device.

How do we take off with all this?


On the one hand, very cool external concepts arise at the appearance stage. Engineers think: we‚Äôll put some awesome processor, super amplifiers, and everything will sound incredibly cool with us. But not so simple.

Between the engineering prototype and what goes on sale, there is at least a very deep abyss, a whole canyon. Why?

  1. A prototype is never suitable for cost containment. Sometimes it costs thousands of dollars, even if we make an elementary device.
  2. Usually it is physically impossible to produce a prototype on a conveyor; this is a piece of work.
  3. The quality of the prototype is poor. It can freeze, overheat, and more.

How to overcome this canyon of differences between the prototype and the final device? This is a very important stage, the coolest projects with gigantic funding, super teams, etc. stopped at it (Remember the announced, but never released Apple AirPower wireless charging.)

All my further discussion will be about how to move from prototype to mass production. Globally, we can say that this path is divided into three stages: EVT, DVT and PVT. These are very important words for us, directly religious.

Engineering validation test, EVT


Here we check that the device is able to work as it should. What does it mean? We begin to test all the major components of the device. For example, a Wi-Fi module should not only give out or receive a signal, but also work reliably and efficiently, without losing packets and so on. The speakers not only play music, but play it really well, so that it meets the requirements for the device. The device not only works, but also fulfills the requirements issued by certification authorities. For example, passes tests for electrostatic voltage or electromagnetic radiation.

This is a very iterative process. We arrange a mountain of tests of various components and the device as a whole, many times we fix something, select particular components, swear, change suppliers of components, optimize the price, and break deadlines.

At the end of this stage, we still get an ugly device, but already working fine in terms of user perception and basic functions. The appearance of the device will be very far from the final, but we also make the first attempts to make the device look like the final at this stage.

Design validation test, DVT


Here we solve the issue of device optimality. What does it mean?

On the one hand, we verify that the device does have the required cost. That is, we are engaged in the selection of components - high-quality, but not sky-high in price.

On the other hand, for the first time we are starting to see the device in a beautiful way: its design is already quite close to the final one. Let me give you an example: Yandex.Station is covered with fabric, and we check that even with long work or in a room with very high temperature or humidity, the color of the fabric will not fade, the plastic will not change its color, and the device itself will work normally and not overheat.

When a person tests samples at this stage, not only should he not be shocked. It should be convenient for him, the buttons should be pressed nicely, the diodes should shine comfortably and so on. We try to produce at least several hundred copies, bring them to Yandex and distribute to employees under the strictest NDA. People can try the future device and throw us a mountain of comments.

For example, Yandex.Station Mini has a gesture sensor. At the DVT stage, we found that the sensor often responds to pets that pass by. We realized that the algorithm needed to be improved.

Production validation test, PVT


The last stage before mass production. 80% of this task is carried out by the factory, which is preparing for the start of production. She makes sure:

  • that the device can be produced in the required quantity in the required time,
  • that the processes that the factory will use for production provide the necessary indicators for quality, pace of production, etc.

Theoretically, PVT smoothly flows into the mass production phase. Because if the PVT stage is successfully completed, it shows that the device can be mass-produced.

EVT, DVT, and PVT are implemented jointly with the factory. Next, I‚Äôll talk about why and how we interact with her, and don‚Äôt do everything alone.

Factories


For this article, I began to look for an illustration that would tell what models of interaction with factories are. (the words OEM, ODM, or Contract Manufacturing may have been heard by someone, but not by someone). And I realized that this is actually a terribly holistic topic.

I found many definitions of OEM, ODM, and other terms on the Internet. Some of them contradict each other. I do not pretend to be true, but I think that everything is written more or less honestly:



We make smart speakers approximately according to the JDM model. The Russian team of Yandex made an engineering prototype of the device. Then we begin to go around a certain list of Chinese factories and say: ‚ÄúWe have a prototype, we want to produce such and such a volume of devices - perhaps in your factory. Let's think about whether you want to do this, and how much it will cost. ‚Äù We choose a factory according to many criteria, this is a topic for a separate post.

Together with the selected factory, we begin to iteratively bring the prototype to the device, which we will mass produce. That is, we go through the stages of EVT, DVT and PVT. The factory helps us and guides us. For example, advises: ‚ÄúPlease note: you use components that are not optimal in price, we recommend that you use other options.‚Äù We validate this, say: "Yes, let's change." Or: "No, they are not suitable for quality."

The factory in this model either produces or purchases components for the device ‚Äî all or part of them. The factory also prepares the technological process of production: it thinks how many people will stand on the conveyor and in what order the assembly operations are located, develops equipment - the mechanisms by which the device is made.

An example of a snap is a fixture that pulls a fabric onto the plastic case of the device so that the tension is uniform, without wrinkles or stretching.

Of course, the factory always acts with an eye on how many devices per shift we must produce and with what quality. The degree of automation, the quality of equipment, the level of personnel and so on depends on this. The factory is also engaged in assembly, packaging and preparation for shipment.

Disputes between the customer and the factory


1. Inconsistency with the expectations of the parties


We have heard the phrase a million times: "And we thought that you want another from us." Or: "JBL does it himself, but Google has different standards." Why is that?

The fact is that Yandex produces devices in factories, where world leaders in the field of acoustics also produce devices. This is a fairly new business for us, we are only learning to do many things, but the factory‚Äôs expectations for us are built on the basis of many years of experience with large acoustic vendors.

It is necessary either to be similar to other factories customers in processes and standards; or, if we have different standards, agree on expectations between the factory and us. And it is imperative to motivate the factory to engage in a dialogue so that employees share their expectations. It is important to find out what they expect from us, what documents are required and so on. Here we gradually proceed to the next point,

2. Unclear roles and responsibilities


I will give an example from the last project. We proudly brought a rather complicated fee to the factory and say: ‚ÄúGuys, look, we did not spend a minute of your time, here is the finished board, it‚Äôs super, you can put it into production.‚Äù But the project manager from the factory was indignant, there was a heated debate.

When we all calmed down and talked, it turned out that we drew a board for them, but the question remained how to control it at the production stage, how to determine its performance. And the plant had a huge risk that they would produce boards, assemble devices, and in the final tests it turns out that the board does not work.

Therefore, one of the key documents that arise in working with the factory is roles and responsibility, RNR. It fixes who does what and what is the role of each of the parties at each stage. Here is an example of a real table of one of our projects:


R - Responsible
A - Approver
S - Supports
C - Consults
I - Informed

As you can see, we divided the project into key components. For each of them, the roles of all participants are registered. Each of the companies can be responsible, coordinating, supporting, advising or simply informing. By the way, I wanted to focus on option ‚ÄúI‚Äù. Often they forget to insert it, but the question of the exchange of information between participants is very important. If someone believes that you should inform him of the changes, do not forget to do this, this is from personal experience.

RNR is not a panacea. Nothing but regular communication and maintaining an atmosphere of openness and trust will help you to resolve conflict situations. This is the daily work of the project manager - to hear his team, to hear the team of the factory and, in particular, to maintain and establish a constant exchange of information between the parties. An RNR document alone is not enough.

3. Lack of competencies


Indeed, we sometimes do something in our devices that no one in the world has done before. And if you do any unusual thing - just lay enough resources for yourself. For example, as far as I know, we were the first in the world to use the ToF sensor to control gestures in Yandex.Station Mini. We spent a lot of time: it took not only to make such a device, but also to establish the quality control process of this sensor. The latter turned out to be much more complicated than we could have imagined.

4. Misunderstanding of the motivation of the parties


Another example from the production of the Mini Station. We launched mass production, measured the quality of the first few batches, were satisfied with the low level of marriage. But, I see, the director of the plant is not too happy with what is happening. I went to talk to him. It turned out that everything was great, but we overlaid the devices with so many levels of tests that the factory could not produce them with the required volume per shift. To fulfill our plan, they had to bring out a night shift - which is paid higher. The device turned out to be more expensive than they planned.

It's good that we talked, figured out, removed the redundant tests and solved the problem. But globally, one must keep in mind that different project participants have different goals. And of course, the reaction of the parties to the course of the project will also be different.
