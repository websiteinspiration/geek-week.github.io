The story of how three technologies: an atomic clock, a satellite navigation system and a touch screen, came together to help drive a car.



Exact time



Speaking of the three technologies that have led to the widespread use of GPS, atomic clocks are one of the most unexpected inventions, and atomic clocks are very important for GPS technology. Each GPS satellite contains several atomic clocks that calculate the time for each GPS signal with an accuracy of 100 billionths of a second. This allows banks to determine the location of the ATM you used to deposit the check, and the time it takes to complete this operation, which, in turn, allows you to accurately record the exact history of all financial transactions. This allows the Federal Aviation Administration to accurately track hazardous weather using a network of Doppler meteorological radars. This allows your mobile operator to make better use of its limited radio frequency spectrum,so you can always make calls. In addition, this ensures digital broadcasting, due to which the songs arrive at the same station at the same time, regardless of where you are.







(And no, despite the name, an atomic clock is not radioactive.)



Like a traditional clock, atomic clocks track time using vibrations. A traditional watch considers tics created by pendulum vibrations. A mechanical wristwatch uses energy from a winding spring passing through a series of gears to a balancing wheel that oscillates back and forth. A digital watch uses quartz crystal oscillations or oscillations from an electrical circuit. Regardless of technology, they all use vibrations as a way to track time, like an atomic clock.



Atomic clocks use an electric oscillator controlled by the natural vibrations of an atom between a positive charge on the nucleus and the surrounding electron cloud. These oscillations never change, therefore, unlike traditional watches, the frequency of these oscillations can be used for extremely accurate time measurements.



The idea of ‚Äã‚Äãatomic clocks was developed by Isidor Rabi, professor of physics at Columbia University in 1945, using a technique he developed in the 1930s and called the magnetic resonance of an atomic beam. This concept made it possible to accurately measure the magnetic properties of atomic nuclei by detecting single states of rotation of atoms and molecules, and the method, in turn, proved its feasibility as a way to accurately determine the time. The first watches using atomic beam magnetic resonance were introduced in 1949 by the National Bureau of Standards (now the National Institute of Standards and Technology, or NIST) using ammonia atoms, but they were not accurate enough. In 1952, cesium was recognized as the most accurate element, which was first used on a watch called NBS-1.Seven years later, NBS-1 was commissioned as the NIST's main timekeeper.



By the time of the 13th General Conference on Weights and Measures in 1967, an international standard had been established: the second of time was defined as 9,192,631,770 cycles of exposure, which the cesium atom required for vibration.



For the first time, world timing has ceased to be based on astronomy.



Cesium atomic clocks are still used for official time by the US government. And this concept of atomic clocks will be indispensable in the development of global positioning systems in the satellite era - all this is due to synchronism, which is very important in the development of GPS.



Exact location









Transit, the world's first global satellite navigation system, which was launched in 1960.



Like many new technologies, the idea of ‚Äã‚ÄãGPS was already used, even when we had no idea about it.



By 1924, just at the time that Rand McNally had printed the first edition of Rand McNelly's Car Assistant, the sailors began using technology based on early radio stations. A technology called radio navigation allowed navigators to accurately determine the location of their ship by contacting stations along the coastline and harbors. As soon as the ship turned for help, several coast stations determined where the ship signal came from and transmitted it to the ship, helping the navigator navigate his location.



This laid the foundation for the creation of modern satellite-based global positioning systems, which began to function after the launch of the satellite by Soviet troops. Scientists at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) realized that they can measure the distance to a satellite using the Doppler effect by measuring the frequency of radio signals and the distance from Earth receivers and the shifts in its radio signal to find out the distance to the satellite. The radio signals of the Russian satellite increased as they approached and decreased as they moved away, in contrast to the sound of the car‚Äôs engine (its height also changes as it approaches, overtakes and moves away).



This led to the fact that in 1959 the US Navy built its first satellite navigation system. A system called Transit was developed by Johns Hopkins Laboratory of Applied Physics using orbiting satellites to transmit radio signals to monitoring stations. This system allowed users to determine their location by measuring the Doppler shift of satellite signals and, in turn, was used to track the location of nuclear submarines. Although communications were not fast, they proved the reliability of space systems.



Around the same time, the Naval Research Laboratory and the United States Air Force Space and Missile Systems Organization had other ideas. They preferred the Timation program, created in 1964 and launched in 1967. Two experimental satellites with a quartz crystal clock were used in this system (although they eventually switched to rubidium and cesium atomic clocks). And, as if to prove the existence of state inefficiency, the Air Force also worked on a similar technology program called System 621B. It continuously provided navigation using 16 satellites in orbits, which formed four oval-shaped clusters, elongated 30 degrees north and south of the equator. (We mentioned that the army was proposing its own system, SECOR,or Sequential Correlation of Range?).



Finally, someone at the Ministry of Defense in 1968 created the NAVSEG, or Navigation Satellite Management Group, which was tasked with exploring various systems already existing or under development. Not surprisingly, the best aspects of the Navy and Air Force systems were used in the solution that appeared. The Department of Defense approved the development of the NAVSTAR global positioning system in December 1973. Testing began the following year, and full-scale development was approved in August 1979.



Unfortunately, NAVSTAR faced some difficulties at the dawn of the new decade. The budget for its development was reduced by 30% by the Minister of Defense in the early 1980s. The importance of a new tank or aircraft is easy to understand, unlike the new high-tech support system. Ultimately, the number of satellites was reduced to 18 from 24, plus three spare ones. Further, just at the moment when everything went on as usual, the accident of the space shuttle Challenger in 1986 delayed the program for another 2 years. The first satellites were finally launched from Cape Canaveral in February 1989 and began to be used in April.



Persian Gulf War 1990-91 proved the importance of GPS in battle. Along with infrared night vision, this technology probably helped win. GPS increased the accuracy of the bombing, and also allowed to improve the positioning of troops and carry out some special operations. The success of GPS in the Persian Gulf War revealed its commercial potential, and by 1993 civilian GPS systems were integrated into vehicles through third-party free software.



GPS systems outside the United States



Of course, the development of atomic clocks and global positioning systems by the state led to the creation of a commercial market, which soon appeared smaller, faster and cheaper devices, which, in turn, helped the military. Today, the US Air Force space command manages the GPS system, which was developed, in particular, with the participation of NATO and Australia. This system is also used by car infotainment systems for navigation and satellite broadcasting. The rest of the world is catching up, starting with GLONASS, or the Global Navigation Satellite System - the space navigation system created by Russia. Planning for its development began in 1968, and by 1976 the country completed the creation of this system. By 1991, there were 12 satellites in operation,and two years later it was put into operation, although in general the system only functioned in December 1995. By 2002, the system barely worked. With the help of the Indian Space Research Organization, the Indian Space Agency, the Russian Space Agency launched GLONASS in full force by May 2007.



The global navigation satellite system of the European space agency Galileo, working in conjunction with GPS and GLONASS, did not stand aside. The first satellites were launched in 2011. The system began to function in 2013, and currently there are 26 satellites in orbit. After the completion of the project, Galileo will have 30 satellites in operation.



Finally, there is a Beidou satellite navigation system, originally developed for the Chinese military. Beidou allows the Chinese military to no longer rely on US-owned GPS for military and civilian operations.

Digitized cards move to the dashboard



The idea that the GPS system built into your car can tell you where to go when replacing you with a partner or navigator was thought up until the first cartographic navigation system was introduced in Japan as a dealer option for the second generation Honda Accord in 1981. The Honda Electro Gyrocator system used sensors and gyroscopes that compared the surface of the road with the surface of the map, thus indicating the direction of movement. Also known as a system for accurate calculation of the trajectory, it did not use satellites, however, the equipment did not always work correctly due to the presence of inaccuracies in the maps, and this is a problem for the system, which adds an additional 25 percent to the cost of the car. It was the first modern navigation system,but leadership in entering the market does not guarantee a long life. The Honda Electro Gyrocator system disappeared in 1982.







Etak Navigator, a secondary market car navigation system that worked on dead reckoning, comparing the vehicle‚Äôs location with points on the map using a digital map, compass and wheel sensors.



Manufacturers of Etak navigators know this firsthand. Developed in California by Stan Haney and Atari founder Nolan Bushnell, this after-sales navigation system also worked on the principle of accurately calculating the trajectory by comparing the location of the car with the points on the map. The device was designed by Pong, Alan Alcorn, and used the Intel 8088 microprocessor and cassette recorder to map. Thanks to the use of a digital map, compass and wheel sensors, drivers could see the results of the system on the screen.

The development of hardware was an easy, more difficult task was to obtain cartographic information. Etak later took advantage of the U.S. Census Bureau, who were professionals in digitizing maps using topographic mathematics. As a medium for information, engineers chose cards on cassettes with polycarbonate shells. On each of them was stored 3.5 MB of data. They resisted shocks and vibrations better than others, and also withstood the extreme temperatures of a parked car. However, the first area they mapped, San Francisco, required drivers to use six cassettes. The screen of the system was a high-resolution vector cathode emitter that displayed a bright and clear line. The small screen was considered too expensive.



In July 1985, the Etak Navigator 700 with a 7-inch screen for $ 1,595 ($ 3,819 adjusted for inflation) appeared, and the Etak Navigator 450 with a 4.5-inch screen for $ 1,395 (or $ 3,340 today). Cassettes cost $ 35 each (or $ 84 today). These models were successful enough for future competitors to buy licenses for patents, map data, and / or Etak hardware. Etak was bought by Rupert Murdoch's news corporation in 1989 for nearly $ 25 million.







The first Toyota CD-ROM navigation system was installed on the Japanese market in 1987. Toyota Crown Royal Saloon G



Two years later, Toyota's Crown Royal Saloon G, sold in the Japanese market, was offered a navigation system with the first color CRT display and a cartographic system on CDs. But since all these systems used accurate trajectory calculation (that is, they compared the location of the car with the points on the map), these solutions were actually not much more perfect than the 1909 Jones Live-Map. In the 1980s, more technology was used.







The world's first modern car with integrated GPS navigation system, like on the Eunos Cosmo, a high-end Mazda brand in the Japanese market.







1990-1995 Eunos Cosmo is the world's first modern car with integrated GPS navigation system. Eunos was a high-end Mazda brand in the Japanese market and was supposed to be part of the planned Mazda Amati brand, although Mazda ultimately decided not to launch it in America. However, the car was produced until 1995.



Mazda introduced the world's first state-of-the-art GPS car navigation system, which the company only offered in Japan at the 1990 Eunos Cosmo. Eunos was an upscale car, and this car was to become part of the planned Mazda brand - Amati. As a result, Mazda decided not to launch it in America, but, nevertheless, the car was produced until 1995.

She displayed the location of the car on a 6-inch color LCD using GPS satellites.



Next will be the names of GPS systems from the past that may be familiar to you. In 1992, General Motors Oldsmobile and Delco introduced the integrated GPS navigation system with a 6-inch Sony CRT full-color screen called TravTek in Avis Rent-A-Car cars in Florida. In the end, the system became a factory option for $ 1995 on the 1995 Oldsmobile 88 sedan, where it was known as GuideStar, a map-based navigation system with accurate trajectory calculation. And do not confuse this system with the later OnStar service that does not use cards. GPS signal and map,recorded on CDs determined the direction of movement.







In 1998, Garmin introduced its first StreetPilot GPS handheld navigation system. The company was founded in 1991 and became the first company to introduce GPS navigation to the public in the same year.



In 1997, the Japanese company Alpine introduced an after-sales service system that also used maps on a CD-ROM and received a GPS signal, which allowed any car buyer to add them to their car. The following year, Garmin unveiled its first StreetPilot GPS handheld navigation system, and Garmin eventually took a prominent place in the market for aftermarket products compatible with different brands of cars.



At your fingertips



Car navigation quickly gained popularity in the automotive industry in the 1990s. But with most of these solutions it was inconvenient to interact, as they lacked another modern technology - the touch screen.



The modest touch screen itself was created in 1965 and was born thanks to the work of the advanced research unit of the British Ministry of Aviation.









E.A. Johnson invented the world's first capacitive touchscreen, which consisted of several layers of glass and plastic and was coated with a conductive material (such as indium oxide and tin or copper). When you touch the screen, the electrical circuit closes, causing the operating system to respond. The capacitive touch screen either reacts or not, and you need to use your finger to close the circuit. Johnson's touchscreen was used by British air traffic controllers until the 1990s. It has also been used in millions (if not billions) of smartphones, laptops, and tablet computers due to its longer life, superior multi-touch performance and clarity compared to resistive touch screens, which first appeared in 1970.



The resistive touchscreen was invented by Dr. G. Samuel Hurst while studying atomic physics at the University of Kentucky. Moving to the Oak Ridge National Laboratory, he continued to work on his discovery. Hurst realized that when a finger touches a screen consisting of numerous resistive layers with small gaps between them, the computer can read the location of the voltage received and call the appropriate command. In addition to the fact that these commands can not only be called with a finger, glove or stylus, the cost of creating such a screen is lower than in the case of a capacitive one. Currently, this screen is widely used in ATMs and cash registers in connection with its affordable price.



Despite the fact that this technology has existed for more than ten years, the first touch screens for consumers only appeared in 1982 in the Hewlett-Packard HP-150 - a personal computer running the MS-DOS operating system on a 9-inch CRT touch screen from Sony. Touchscreen technology was new, and therefore not cheap: $ 2,795 or $ 7,634 adjusted for inflation.







Buick designed the 1986 Buick Riviera as a technology exhibit for General Motors. A key part of this plan was the replacement of many dashboard controls with a graphical Control Center.



Shortly afterwards, the world received a car that pioneered the use of touch screens: the 1986 Buick Riviera. At one time, this car was ignored - mainly because of its small size and unremarkable design. However, the first use of the touch screen as standard equipment in 1986 was a great success, given that it took more than five years to develop the car.







In 1986, the Buick Riviera was the first touchscreen ever delivered to a car as standard equipment. He appeared on the market two years after the appearance of the first consumer product with a Hewlett-Packard PC touchscreen.



According to company documents, in November 1980 (two years before the Hewlett-Packard HP-150 went on sale), Buick managers in Flint, Michigan decided that by 1985 they would release a car with the most advanced electronics in the industry . As the committee assessed which electronic features to offer, the CRT touchscreen was developed by Delco Systems, a subsidiary of General Motors in Santa Barbara, California. For several months, in early 1981, the GM system was demonstrated and approved by the GM Product Policy Group. AC Spark Plug and Delco Electronics developed the hardware while Delco Systems developed the software. By 1983, specifications for the screen were developed,and next year, these screens were installed in a test fleet of 100 cars to measure customer response.



The graphic control center (GCC) was a CRT screen covered with an invisible Mylar panel, which used transparent wires that were encoded in columns and rows to perform certain functions on certain pages. The functions of each switch changed with each page. Since it takes a few seconds to warm up the CRT, the GCC circuit starts to warm up when the driver's door handle is touched. When the driver's door opened and closed, the Riviera logo appeared on the display.



As soon as the car was started, the display went to the home page, which satisfied 90% of the driver‚Äôs needs. If the screen was not touched for 30 seconds, it turned off. GCC controlled automatic climate control, AM / FM radio, graphic equalizer and trip calculations, and also displayed sensor readings and diagnostic information about the car. Its black screen and green display now seems to be antique, but at that time this technology was advanced.



Buick executives were thrilled, including Carey Wilson, who first drew attention to this idea in 1980. ‚ÄúA new generation of automotive electrical systems is coming, and Buick has laid the foundation for them,‚Äù he said in 1986.



However, some were less optimistic - for example, the legendary car journalist Brock Yates.

The 4-inch full-color touch screen was made by Sony, and the system could be equipped with an optional Motorola mobile phone that could be controlled via the screen.

While critics ridiculed these and other early attempts to build touchscreen systems, new cars (such as the Tesla Model 3) come with a touchscreen that contains all the controls. Thus, despite the fact that all these technologies seem new, the science that nourished them has worked on them for decades. The creation of today's Tesla console stands on the shoulders of technology giants.







Atomic clocks and their exceptional chronological accuracy paved the way for the creation of a global positioning system using space satellites. And although these satellite systems were originally intended for military use, they eventually opened up to the commercial market. And when this system combined with such easy-to-use technologies as touch screens, the market for what we now know as GPS blossomed.



Our current ability to route using the touchscreen is ubiquitous - but never forget that it took decades of development and technology to merge.
