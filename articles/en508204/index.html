There are more than 40 categories of accidents, but the main seven. About them and speech. Description of types is here . Briefly:



SCF - failure of a system or component

This is when someone incorrectly produced something, improperly designed, or didn‚Äôt maintain it properly, as a result of which the component could not complete its task. For example, the engine took and turned off on the march or someone forgot to catch a bug in software, because of which the plane crashed. An example is a Boeing 737 with an engine falling apart over Bernville . Successful emergency landing, the conclusion about the cause of engine failure is still waiting.



ARC - emergency runway touch

This is when the plane is whole, the components are whole, and if another pilot were sitting in the cockpit, everything would have gone well. Well, or if there was a different weather.



An example is the crash of the Boeing 737 in Yogyakarta or the An-24 in Navoi (in both cases, there is still a flight). Another biggest disaster of its time, the Boeing 707 in Kano (weather).



RE - rolling out of the runway during take-off or landing.

These are failed take-offs with overload, landing without turning on the reverse, incorrect speed calculation and so on. It sounds dumb, but only now the warning system is being completed, if you sit too fast or take off too slowly. And this system will save the world from RE. Later.



Rolling out example, Boeing 727 crash in Cotonou: overload, heat, pilot error, the plane could not rise, drove along the beach and crashed into the ocean. Crashing into the ocean cost the lives of 141 people. Another example: Boeing 737 in Buenos Aires - incorrect control of wing mechanization, forgotten the flaps, rolled out and crashed exactly into the gas distribution station. Killed 64 people in the cabin and two in cars on the road. Another Yak-42 near Yaroslavl .



USOS - runway shortfall or flight

It's almost like rolling out, it just starts right behind the lane when the plane begins to plow soft asphalt or turf. Includes touchdowns outside the designated runway touchdown zone. Examples: IL-62 in Mashhad , An-24 in Navoi .



LOC-I - loss of control in flight

Loss of control of an airplane during flight, usually not related to SCF. For example, if the pilots disconnect from heart attacks, and the stewardess touches the pedal when trying to pull them out.

An example is the Tu-154 crash near Khabarovsk .



CFIT - collision with the ground in a controlled flight

Collision with a terrain, water or obstacle during flight without loss of control. Conventionally, the map and altimeter were, but the pilots did not look. An example is the SSJ 100, which crashed into Mount Salak (there was even a TAWS there, but it was ignored).



FIRE - fire

For example, someone was driving a new Samsung Galaxy or again decided to smoke in the toilet, but got carried away. An example is the burned-out L-1011 in Riyadh .

RPK (Revenue Passenger Kilometers) is a metric in civil aviation that shows how many kilometers traveled by passengers who bought a ticket (crew and children flying for free are not considered here). It is calculated by multiplying passengers by kilometers, measured in passenger kilometers (PCM); RPK reflects the demand for air travel, and often it is what they mean by "traffic." There is also the ASK metric (Available Seat Kilometers) - here the number of all passenger seats in the airplane is multiplied by kilometers, it does not matter if these seats were occupied or empty.

First generation: the beginning of the jet era in passenger aircraft



The pioneers of the civil aviation jet era are de Havilland Comet 1 (regular flights since 1952), Tu-104 (regular flights since 1956), Boeing 707 (1958) and Douglas DC-8 (1959).





The cockpit of the Boeing 707-123B from the German Museum in Munich from German Wikipedia The



four-engine 707th carried 200 passengers. Despite the mass of the aircraft at 150 tons, the flight control system was equipped only with power steering and spoilers. The elevators and ailerons were aerodynamically compensated to reduce the effort on the control levers. The technique was limited by the capabilities of analog electronics of the time.



Second generation



In the 1960s and early 1970s, second-generation airplanes appeared (Airbus A300, Boeing 727, 737, 747, Lockheed L1011, McDonnell Douglas DC9, DC10) Boeing 727





crew cockpit



Hydraulic actuators were already in full swing here. Unlike the 707th, the Boeing 727 already had hydraulic control on all control planes. When switching to manual mode, aerodynamic compensation still helped, as well as an adjustable electric stabilizer .



Since the rudders now turned hydraulically, it became necessary to artificially load the control levers to create the pilot's sense of control of the aircraft.



Basic stabilization functions, for example, yaw damper, have been added to the flight management system (aka Flight Management System, FMS).



Analog autopilots were installed, the control signal from which was "mixed" into the signal from the helm. These autopilots had an automatic landing mode for all-weather flights.



Hawker Siddeley and British Aircraft Corporation have released the first airliners capable of performing a fully automatic landing (Trident and BAC 1-11).



The Boeing 737 autopilot was the first autopilot with combined control mode (aka self-propelled guns, control wheel steering, CWS). This is such a mode when the pilot uses the helm to determine the position of the aircraft by roll and pitch, then releases the helm, and the CWS continues to maintain the specified roll and pitch.



Passenger aircraft came to supersonic. Tu-144 (1968) and Concord (1969) became the first (and only) supersonic passenger aircraft.



Concord became the first airliner to have an electric remote control system (EDSU, Fly-by-Wire). Unlike modern airliners, the EMDS was analog there. It was duplicated by a redundant mechanical control system. Judging by the preserved terminology, much was later redesigned for the A320.



Such a reduction in accident rate is shown by statistics when switching to the second generation of liners. Of course, this is not only a matter of the planes themselves - the entire industry was changing rapidly, the air legislation was corrected, and the crew training system was changing.











Third generation



The third generation of liners appeared in the 1980s (Airbus A300-600, A310, Boeing 747-400, 757, 767, McDonnell Douglas MD80, MD90, MD11).





The cockpit of the Airbus A310 crew



A distinctive feature of the aircraft of this generation has become the so-called ‚ÄúGlass cockpit‚Äù (cockpit panel including electronic displays). The displays showed flight information, monitoring data, warnings, and much more. This simplified the interaction of pilots with the aircraft so much that in the Airbus A310 the crew consisted not of three, but of two people (the flight engineer was no longer needed).



Analog equipment was replaced by digital systems. The on-board warning system about the proximity of the earth (Terrain Awareness and Warning System, TAWS). This system looks at the current height and coordinates, superimposes them on its terrain database and warns of a possible unintentional approach to the ground or an obstacle (in fact, it can do much more).



And the main achievement in flight automation was the introduction of a flight control system, which made it possible to automatically execute pre-compiled flight plans.



The transition from reversible steering to irreversible has been completed. A reversible drive is when the output force is proportional to the input force (on the handle) and, conversely, the external load is partially transmitted to the handle. This makes it possible to ‚Äúfeel‚Äù control, i.e. to feel various efforts on the aircraft control lever depending on the angle of deviation of the steering surface, as well as on the speed and altitude. For an irreversible drive, the entire load is ‚Äúeaten up‚Äù by the hydraulic circuit and does not reach the handle.



Remote control has been recognized and has been used for some not-so-critical functions, such as controlling external aileron-spoilers, trimmers, slats / flaps. But for all critical (in terms of flight safety) functions, the communications between the pilot and control mechanisms were still mechanical.



Improved navigation performance and the TAWS that appeared reduced the number of collisions with the ground in controlled flight (CFIT) by 86% compared to the second generation.











Fourth generation



The latest fourth generation of passenger airliners appeared in 1988 with the release of the Airbus A320.





Cockpit of the Airbus A380



sidestick and electric control system concepts (aka EDSU, Fly-By-Wire, FBW), which appeared on new aircraft, were tested and proved to be reliable on the Concord and A300.



If on the A310 only a small number of non-critical functions were on the EMDS, in the A320 all control planes already operate on the remote control. More precisely, there are two connections that are needed during the restart of on-board computers when switching power, for example, pedals - hydraulics - rudder, here is a good publication .



In the Boeing 777, commands from the pilot arrive at the traditional helm, and then are processed by the flight control system. The flight control system is duplicated many times. Moreover, not only control channels are duplicated, but also control modes that allow, in case of failure, to switch to the ‚Äúsimplified mode‚Äù of piloting, and from it to an even more simplified one.



It remained possible to switch to manual mode, so that the rudder and stabilizer can be controlled manually.



The Fly-By-Wire concept made it possible to install the protection of the range of flight modes (Flight Envelop Protection) in the fourth generation of airliners. This is a very convenient thing, which takes on the calculation of safe modes and does not allow pilots to take the plane beyond safe operation.



For example, if the collision avoidance system worked and you need to sharply maneuver, the pilots do not need to be careful and afraid that the aircraft will lose controllability - the automation is responsible for the safety of the maneuver. Or, for example, no matter how much the pilot pulls the handle toward himself, automation will not allow him to create an angle of attack close to stall.



Now Fly-By-Wire is the industry standard and is used in all Airbus, Boeing B777 and B787, Embraer E-Jet and Sukhoi Superjet models produced today.



Here is the whole picture. The advent of the protection of the range of flight modes reduced the number of control loss in flight (LOC-I, gray bar graph) by 81%.

Approach and landing are very difficult phases of flight, which impose significant requirements on the crew in terms of navigation, aircraft configuration changes, communication with the air traffic control service. The crew load may be exacerbated by worsened weather conditions or congested airspace. Therefore, it is not surprising that the largest number of accidents occurs during approach and landing. Together, the high load and increased potential for unforeseen circumstances are precisely those factors that can lead to incidents.

Over the past 20 years, most casualties have occurred due to loss of control in flight (LOC-I). The number of these incidents has been significantly reduced thanks to technologies introduced in the fourth generation of liners.



The number of collisions with ground in controlled flight (CFIT) continues to decline thanks to the introduction and continuous improvement of the glass cockpit and navigation technology available on aircraft of the third and fourth generation.



Rollout outside the runway (RE), both lateral and longitudinal, is in third place in terms of the number of accidents with human casualties and is the main cause of accidents with the loss of an aircraft. Future technical improvements in the future should solve the problem of longitudinal rolling.



Changes in the main categories of incidents



Over the past 20 years, the number of incidents with casualties such as CFIT (collision with the ground) has decreased by 89%, and LOC-I (loss of control in flight) - by 66%.



Since 1999, the share of flights with TAWS aircraft to prevent collisions with the ground has grown from 68% to 99%.



The widespread use of this technology is a key reason for reducing the number of collisions with the ground. Here's a reduction:











As for LOC-I, in 2019 the share of flights on fourth-generation aircraft with protection of the range of flight modes was 52%.



Since the number of incidents with loss of control in flight for fourth-generation airliners is 76% less than for the third, we can expect a further decrease in the number of accidents like LOC-I with an increase in the number of fourth-generation airliners used.



If we consider rolling out of the runway (RE), the first systems designed to combat RE began to be installed at the end of the last decade. Now the number of aircraft with these systems remains small - about 8% of the total operating fleet. Therefore, although we can see a trend towards a decrease in accident rate from RE, it is too early to draw conclusions.



Managed Flight Collision (CFIT)



The introduction of a glass cockpit, a flight control system that implements a fly plan, and a TAWS system reduced the number of collisions with the ground by 89%.



First, to reduce the number of incidents like CFIT, a ground proximity warning system (aka GPWS) was introduced. Then she entered the TAWS.

Subsequently, glass cabins were installed on the third generation of liners, which simplified navigation and further reduced the number of CFITs.











Loss of flight control (LOC-I)



The fourth generation of liners has gained thirty years of experience since the commissioning of the A320 (1988). The experience of more than 200 million flights shows a significant advantage of aircraft with protection of the range of flight regimes (Flight Envelop Protection), which reduced the number of accidents like LOC-I by 76% compared to third-generation liners.











Runway Roll Out (RE)



Most of the longitudinal rolling out of the runway is associated with the control of the energy of the aircraft. A significant reduction in accident rate in this regard can be expected from the introduction of warning systems that monitor energy and landing characteristics. Today, the proportion of aircraft equipped with such systems is too small to see a general improvement. But this additional safety system is a promising change in the direction of decreasing the number of longitudinal rolling out of the runway.











Total



Aircraft fly more, but are damaged less. It is about scientific and technological progress, accumulated experience and constantly improving training and control. Where the technique can free up the pilot‚Äôs time for routine operation, he gets time for additional control procedures.
