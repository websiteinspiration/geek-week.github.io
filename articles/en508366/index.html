Where in Russia they kill the most? Data analysis

Hello, Habr.



My name is Alexey Knorre, and I am a criminologist, a graduate student at UPenn and an affiliate researcher at EUSP. I thought that now, after months of quarantine, it's time to talk about crime. Unclear economic situation, rising unemployment, worsening public health - all this causes concern in the future. What will happen to crime in Russia? As a criminologist, I use statistical methods and programming in order to empirically investigate crime, so I constantly work with data that I would like to talk about in an accessible language today. On Habr√© there were only two posts on the tag "criminology", so I hope my story will be interesting.



Some of you could see last year's city ‚Äã‚Äãsafety ratingOf Russia. How was security data collected: there seemed to be a survey of residents, but how many people were interviewed? Were there any systematic biases in the sample, as if only residents of large apartment buildings were being interviewed? How much can people say for sure what is safe in their city? Safe compared to what, and how to measure this safety? What if the questionnaire reflects more public sentiment than real crime - the likelihood of an accidental attack on the street, robbery or theft?



In science, crime is measured in many ways. Two years ago, my colleagues and I, for example, conducted the first representative victimization survey in Russia, phoning 16 thousand people. Datawe opened for general use. The main conclusion is that the level of real crime is about 8 times higher than that registered by law enforcement agencies. Here is a visualization of the process from Maria Bublik and Natalia Toganova, which was included in the short list of the Information is Beautiful - 2019 award .







The survey gives a picture of the whole country, but does not allow to judge the dangers of individual places. With these thoughts, I tried to make a city safety rating that would use more reliable data. What can best reflect the safety of the city? A good indicator of safety is the risk of violent death. On the one hand, murders are one of the ‚Äúbest‚Äù crimes in terms of recordability. You can forget about the theft of the wallet or simply not inform the police. The discovery of a ‚Äúcriminal‚Äù corpse is always followed by the initiation of a criminal case. On the other hand, the usual Russian murder is a domestic conflict that has gone too far, often intoxicated. So the number of killings is a good safety metric for the environment.



United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) annually releasesanalytical report on murders in the countries of the world . The standard metric is the number of homicides per 100,000 people, or homicide rate. As of 2017, the smallest number of killings are in Japan and Singapore (0.2 kills per 100,000), Hong Kong (0.3), Indonesia (0.4) and Norway (0.5). Most of all - in El Salvador (61 killings per 100,000 people), Jamaica (57), Honduras (41.7) and Brazil (30.5). Russia is far below this international ranking, with 9 killings per 100,000 people, in company with Uganda (11), Uruguay (8.2), Peru (7.7) and Afghanistan (7.1).



It is interesting to look at heterogeneity: where in Russia they kill more or less than the average? With these thoughts in mind, I opened RStudio and started writing code.



To get a rating, you need to get a specific number of murders, for which, in turn, you need to know the number of murders and its population for each city in Russia.



From the Rosstat website, I took a file with the population of Russia disaggregated by settlements for 2016. The year was not chosen by chance: this is the only year where for each unit, whether it is a constituent entity of the Russian Federation, urban district or municipal district, there was an identifier TERSON-MO, copying OKTMO. OK, there is a population.



The killings turned out to be more difficult. There are no open statistics on murders at the city level in Russia. In our scientific work, we use data on all initiated criminal cases in Russia in 2013‚Äì2014. This is a huge depersonized array of approximately 5.5 million digitized statistical cards, which are mandatory to fill out by investigators or interrogators at the time of criminal proceedings. Here is the first page of the card for the identified crime:







Any administrative data is a little dirty. In our case, several regions were completely absent from these data, and some had an empty OKTMO. In addition, sometimes the problem was that in some cities the only police department was responsible for both the city and the adjacent municipal district, and its OKTMO identifier was district. In other words, this is the problem ‚Äúmunicipal district-administrative center‚Äù: in the area of ‚Äã‚Äãresponsibility of many departments, for example, the Ministry of Internal Affairs are both the city in which they are located and the municipal district, which is subordinate to this city. A comparison of the 5th OKTMO sign would overestimate the specific number of murders, since for city N, the numerator will be killings in both city N and the N-th municipal district, and in the denominator the population will be only the city N.Therefore, I aggregated all the murder data at the level of the first 6 digits of the OKTMO law enforcement departments.



It turned out two data sets - killings and numbers - with a common key - OKTMO. By comparing them, I left cities with a population of more than 100,000 people, there were 176 of such cities. The lower border of 100,000 was chosen for three reasons: firstly, the sporadic nature of crime makes the assessment in smaller settlements unstable. Secondly, the accuracy of the comparison decreases with the size of the settlement. Thirdly, the very logic of the specific number of killings per 100,000 people tells us the cutoff.



Out of 176 cities, I find murder data for 140 cities. Our data is completely missing information for Chechnya and there is no mapping between the police department and OKTMO for Bashkortostan, Kemerovo region, Khakassia, Sakhalin, Yaroslavl region, Kostroma region, Kamchatka region. In addition, due to the ‚Äúmunicipal district-administrative center‚Äù problem, I also lose most of the cities in the Moscow region. Nevertheless, 140 cities are already something. We get a homicide rate: divide the number of homicides by 2 (because the data is for two years), and then by the population of the city. And all would be well, but only the data on the killings for 2013-2014.



Since the 1990s all over the world there is a "great crime fall", in the English-speaking world known as the great crime drop. Roughly speaking, people stop stealing, hitting and killing. Criminologists identify a number of possible reasons, such as the development of security systems (video cameras, security systems, etc.), an increase in living standards, and the termination of the use of lead inks, which adversely affect the cognitive development of children and reduce their ability to control themselves. There are many theories. This fall is also characteristic of Russia.



Fortunately, the Prosecutor General‚Äôs Office of the Russian Federation has a website with open crime data at the regional level. Through simple manipulations, I uploaded the data on the killings (considering both Article 105 of the Criminal Code of the Russian Federation and Article 111 of Part 4 of the Criminal Code of the Russian Federation - both actually qualify intentional violence that led to death) over the past 10 years by region and calculated the dynamics of specific killing by to regions:







We see an almost twofold drop in the number of murders in 10 years. Using these data, I predicted how the killing rate of each region in Russia will change from 2014 to 2020. For each region, we get a multiplier, which we multiply by the specific killing rate according to 5.5 million cards, and we get the predicted specific killing rate in 2020 d. An important assumption here is that the trend of crime reduction in cities follows the trend at the regional level. In addition, the forecasts are always inaccurate, so we calculated confidence intervals.







The result was a safety rating of Russian cities based on data on the killings. I posted the data on the killings in these cities, population, multipliers and the final estimate of the specific murder rate in 2020 on GitHub .



The next step could be to recalculate the rating on the data fresher (provided that someone gets access to them), as well as test scientific hypotheses about what causes a large number of murders in Russian cities. For example, if we compare, on the one hand, the specific number of murders, and, on the other, how often physical violence leads to death, then it can be seen that the heterogeneity of Russian cities is not limited to specific murder alone:







You can look at the Yandex Self-isolation Index and city killing. At first glance, it seems that in cities where people who are more law-abiding in terms of quarantine live, they are less likely to kill, and the regression line shown in blue can convince that there is such a connection. But this is not so: to prove this assumption, two variables with a couple of tens of data points are not enough. The graph itself is rather similar to the graph of the relationship between the number of pirates and the average surface temperature of the Earth, on which it seems that there is a connection between the two variables, but without a reliable research design that could claim causality, this is just a false correlation. In fact, this is just an illustration of the fact that criminal statistics can be linked to other data sets and see what happens.







Finally, one more interesting thing can be done. I checked the World Bank data on the average specific number of murders per 100 thousand people by country in 2017 (or the closest available) and got the countries that are closest to Russian cities in terms of homicide. This is not a completely correct comparison, because, firstly, different years, and we know that over time there are fewer murders, and secondly, specific numbers in microstates, such as the Bahamas, are not always suitable for comparisons. There may be an environmental error related to data aggregation at the country level. However, this is a fun comparison.
