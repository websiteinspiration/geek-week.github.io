First of all, Wi-Fi 6 is designed to make the overall wireless network more efficient. Before each station, a time slot was sequentially issued, and it occupied the entire 20 MHz channel, in connection with which the rest were forced to wait for it to send traffic. Now, these 20 MHz are cut into smaller sub-carriers combined into resource units, up to 2 MHz, and up to nine stations can broadcast simultaneously in one time slot. Hence the significant increase in network performance.

We have already said that higher modulation schemes were added to the sixth generation standard: 1024-QAM versus the previous 256. Thus, the encoding complexity increased by 25%: if earlier we transmitted up to 8 bits of information per character, then now 10 bits .

The number of spatial streams has also increased. In previous standards, there were a maximum of four, while now it is up to eight, and in older access points of Huawei up to a dozen.

In addition, Wi-Fi 6 again uses the 2.4 GHz frequency range, which allows relatively inexpensive production of chipsets for end terminals with Wi-Fi 6 support and connects a huge number of devices, whether full-fledged IoT modules or some completely cheap ones sensors.

What is especially important, the standard implements many technologies for more efficient use of the radio spectrum, including reuse of channels and frequencies. First of all, it is worth mentioning the Basic Service Set (BSS) Coloring, which allows you to ignore other people's access points that work on the same channel, and at the same time "listen" to your own.

The first is the number of spatial streams (up to four at 2.4 GHz, up to eight at 5 GHz). The second is a unit divided by the sum of the duration of the symbol and the duration of the guard interval in accordance with the standard used. Since in Wi-Fi 6, the symbol duration is quadrupled - up to 12.8 Œºs, and the guard interval is 0.8 Œºs, as a result, 1 / 13.6 Œºs comes out.

Next: recall, with improved 1024-QAM modulation, up to 10 bits can now be encoded per character. Total we have a bit rate of 5/6 (FEC) - the fourth factor. And the fifth is the number of subcarriers (tones).

Finally, adding up the maximum performance for 2.4 and 5 GHz, we get an impressive value of 10.75 Gbit / s.

Another technology that Huawei has integrated into new access points is called Dynamic Turbo. As noted earlier, Huawei is currently the only vendor that in its solutions provides the simultaneous operation of MU-MIMO and OFDMA. Let's have a little more detail on what the difference between the two is.

Both technologies are designed to provide multi-user access. When there are many users in the network, OFDMA allows you to distribute the frequency resource so that many clients receive and receive information at one time. However, MU-MIMO ultimately aims at the same thing: when several customers are at different points in the room, each of them can be sent a unique spatial stream. For clarity, we‚Äôll imagine that the frequency resource is the ‚ÄúMoscow - St. Petersburg‚Äù route. OFDMA as if proposes: "Let's make the road not one lane, but two so that it is used more efficiently." MU-MIMO has a different approach: ‚ÄúLet's pave the second, third road so that the traffic goes along independent routes.‚Äù Theoretically, one does not contradict the other, in fact, the combination of two methods requires a certain algorithmic base.Due to the fact that Huawei managed to create this base, the throughput of our access points increased by almost 40% compared to what competitors are able to provide.
