The evolution of bank cards: from metal to metal

All new technologies are based on knowledge and analysis of where they came from and how they changed over time. When we take up the task of creating an engine for recognizing another object at Smart Engines , we seriously delve into the history of this object, its evolutionary path of development. In today's world, an instant payment using a bank card or even exclusively its electronic double in a mobile phone will not surprise anyone. At the same time, the card itself - a piece of plastic or - for extremal options - a valuable metal, continues to develop as an independent product. The first prototypes of payment cards appeared at the beginning of the 20th century and were made of cardboard. They were not required to be too wear-resistant: they were not inserted into ATMs or other reading devices. They only needed to be presented when making a purchase in a store, cafe or gas station. It was not necessary to talk about some kind of integration of the card with a single information banking system: the task of the first cards was to identify the owner and designate his solvency in a particular chain of stores, gas stations or food chains. Today we would call these cards loyalty cards.

In the early 1920s, payment cards began to spread in the fuel trade. In 1924, the General Petroleum Corporation in California began releasing what they called ‚Äúcourtesy cards,‚Äù and other gas station chains quickly followed suit. Cardboard payment cards have been issued to regular customers throughout the United States. With this card, the client could pay for gasoline, as well as buy related products at any of the network stations, which could be at a considerable distance from each other, which allowed to keep the client traveling around the country.

The first cards, like other documents, were made of cardboard. Since they were used much more often than, say, a passport, they quickly deteriorated and had to be changed, which caused inconvenience to both the organizations that issued the cards and the customers. A large-scale modernization of cards occurred in 1928, when the Boston-based Farrington Manufacturing company, specializing in the manufacture of engraved metal caskets and cases, released a prototype of a metal loyalty card - Charga Plate, which quickly became popular with retail chains.

Embossing as a step towards artificial intelligence


On the issued cards, the owner data was squeezed out (embossed). The buyer, making a purchase, handed the seller a card that, using a special press, the imprinter, imprinted the buyer‚Äôs card on the receipt. That is, the card served as a print confirming the purchase of goods by the card holder. Embossing simplified and accelerated the interaction between the buyer and the seller, as it eliminated the need for the seller to manually fill out the sales receipt and enter the buyer's data into it. If you imagine this operation in the modern world, then, in fact, with the help of such a card a stamp was placed on the check confirming the purchase of goods by a specific client.

Innovation, as advertised, allows you to save time on dictating the name to the seller, allows you to identify the customer in any store, and reduces the likelihood of errors when filling out the delivery address and billing. Chip is not a luxury


Bank cards that appeared in the middle of the 20th century were already a more complex payment tool, the task of which was not only to identify the client, but also to provide the seller with information about the state of his bank account, as well as to ensure as soon as possible interaction with the bank and transaction. Magnetic strips containing the necessary data appeared on the cards, and later the cards began to be equipped with an electronic chip, providing faster interaction with the cash register.

In their form and appearance, until recently, maps did not significantly evolve. Created in the format of a standard business card - approximately 55 * 90 mm in size (today the standard indicates the exact size of 85.6X53.98X0.76 mm), they exist today, conveniently fit in a wallet. On the card, as before, the main details remain the name of the client and the number of the card tied to the current account. The account number encodes the type of payment system used, the bank identification number and the customer‚Äôs personal number.

The format of a credit card number today is determined by the international standard ISO 7812. In most cases, a credit card number consists of 16 digits. These numbers are used by the most common payment systems VISA and MasterCard in the world. In this case, there are card numbers consisting of 19 or 13 digits.

What is in my inscription to you?


The first 6 digits of the number are the unique number of the bank that issued the cards - Issuer Identification Number (IIN) or Bank Identification Number (BIN).

In this case, the first character of the number printed on the payment card identifies the payment system in which this card works. Diners Club credit card ancestor card numbers, as well as the American Express system or the JCB regional Japanese payment system, start with the number 3, the number 4 indicates the card belongs to the VISA system, 5 - belongs to MasterCard (with the same number, Diners Club cards are issued in the first position in the United States and Canada). With the number 6 in the first place, cards are issued by Discover, Laser and InstaPayment payment systems. The Russian payment system MIR uses the number 2 in the first position.

The numbers in positions 7 through 15 form the Account Number - the account holder number that is unique to each client. The last digit of the number is the checksum (Checksum), calculated by a certain algorithm and necessary to verify the correctness of the entered data.

On the front side of most cards, the surname and name of the card holder are printed. The name is usually put in Latin characters in international transcription, sometimes duplicated in the national language. On the front side of the card, as a rule, its validity is also indicated.

The first four or 6 digits of the payment card number, which are displayed on checks and payment receipts, are duplicated under the first four numbers in ‚ÄúOCR 7b‚Äù font. Other information may also be indicated on the front of the card to simplify card identification. On the back of the card there is a verification code: in the VISA system it is called CVV (card verification value), Master Card CVC (card validation code), in the MIR system it is called three-digit card verification parameter). They are an additional step in protecting the card from misuse, but now they are losing their relevance in connection with the development of multi-factor authentication methods.

When Electronics Does Not Help


Automation of the processes of interaction between bank cards and payment terminals allowed banks, when issuing mass-use cards, to move away from the principle of embossing, simplifying the process of personifying a plastic blank while expanding the possibilities of its electronic filling. Today, the magnetic strip on the card is duplicated by a contact and contactless chip, which allows for interaction with the terminals without the need to insert them into the reader. User details are put on the card less expensive than embossing by laser printing, engraving (indent) and flat printing (flat printed).

Custom card: marketing or something else?


A bank card has long become a commonplace that is used by billions of people around the world. The bank card market is developing independently, and therefore banks are trying to attract customers with cards that are significantly different from competitor cards.

Despite the complexity of electronic identification systems in bank cards, visually they do not allow to significantly differentiate the client as a card holder. In the fight for customers and emphasizing their premium status, banks issue non-standard cards using special color and design solutions, creating a texture and mirror surface, using various methods of lamination and foiling.

In the manufacture of cards, new materials began to be used - transparent and textured plastic. And again - like a hundred years ago - metal cards appeared, but not copper, but from precious and simply expensive alloys. In addition, some banks issue cards of a non-standard size, as well as with atypical placement of the main details: in the vertical orientation or on the back.

All of these non-standard moves, of course, have a positive effect on customer acquisition, but are a hassle for systems related to processing such cards.
