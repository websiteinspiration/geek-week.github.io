As buried undecomposed organic matter sank deeper and deeper, the temperature and pressure grew, and finally fell into such a ‚Äúwindow‚Äù of parameters (higher - temperature and pressure are not large enough, deeper - temperature and pressure are too large), where the organic substance became converted into hydrocarbons that make up oil and gas - this rock is called oil source, because it was she who gave birth (and still still gives birth, only sooooo slowly!) to oil. The resulting hydrocarbons are lighter than water, so they migrate upward, towards the surface, seeping through permeable rocks and getting stuck on impermeable.



On the way to the surface, oil encounters many different layers, and by this time they will not necessarily be horizontal. What was the initial reservoir pressure? If you forget about all these oil traps and imagine that the whole earth is permeable sandstone, saturated with water, then the pressure of the liquid at any depth will be determined only by the density of this water and depth: this is the formula known from school ‚Äúro-je-ash‚Äù. Do as we do, forget the elementary school with its tasks of translating from deciacres to cubic feet and count everything only in SI units, and you will be happy. We get about 1000 * 10 * 2000 Pascals or about 200 atmospheres.







If the reservoir pressure in the field is approximately equal to the pressure of the liquid column at the same depth, we speak of normal reservoir pressure; if much more or much less, then they speak of abnormally high or low reservoir pressure. Oil workers, of course, love high, but not abnormally high. If you drill a well to the field and fill it with water, then in the case of normal reservoir pressure, the water level in the well will not change. In the case of reduced reservoir pressure, the water level in the well will begin to fall and will drop to a height corresponding to the current reservoir pressure - a convenient way to assess reservoir pressure, by the way, you only need two chronometers.



In case of increased reservoir pressure, water will be pushed out of the well at an ever-increasing rate, and finally an oil fountain will be clogged from the well! Now imagine that the field has a normal initial reservoir pressure, not elevated, but the well is not filled with water, but with oil. The density of oil is less than that of water, so the ‚Äúro-je-ash‚Äù of such a column of oil will be less than reservoir pressure, and oil will be pushed out of the well! To stop such a well, it is necessary to ‚Äúshut off‚Äù it, replace the oil in the wellbore with water, and with an abnormally high reservoir pressure, also with dissolved salts, so that the density is higher and the ‚Äúje-ash‚Äù just equals the reservoir pressure (and slightly exceeded it). You understand, with an abnormally high reservoir pressure, there will also be problems while drilling - you need to drill a well,creating an open connection of the field under pressure with the surface, but at the same time preventing emissions due to increased pressure.







What happens to the physicochemical properties of the field during production? In a first approximation, we can assume that porosity, permeability and compressibility do not change, because the rock remains in place, but all the fluids saturating it begin to migrate, so the pressure and saturation change. Let the well be filled with oil and at the same time be in equilibrium with reservoir pressure. The pump begins to produce oil from the well, the dynamic level of oil in the well begins to fall, the bottomhole pressure (the pressure of the liquid column at the bottom of the well, that is, the bottom of the well) also drops. The pump can be configured to work with a given constant flow rate, or (if it has its own pressure gauge) to maintain a given constant bottomhole pressure.The pressure drop (called depression) between the bottomhole (in the wellbore) and the reservoir (in the ‚Äúnear-bottom‚Äù part of the field adjacent to the well) pressure causes oil to flow to the well. But at the same time, it also reduces reservoir pressure in the bottomhole zone of the field! If the permeability is sufficient, then the pressure drop wave propagates further, causing an influx of oil from increasingly distant parts of the field. In reality, after a while, in order to maintain reservoir pressure, injection wells will begin to operate, which, on the contrary, pump water into the wellbore, creating increased bottomhole pressure. The reverse pressure drop (repression) between the wellbore and the bottom-hole zone leads to the fact that injected water begins to propagate from the well into the field along with the pressure wave. On the one hand, this does not allow reservoir pressure to fall, but on the other hand, it‚Äôs as a ‚Äúpiston‚Äù displacing oil from injection wells to producing ones. Although if instead of piston displacement the injected water breaks through the direct channels from the injection wells to the producing ones, you can put an end to the remaining oil in this part of the oil field.



So, after the start of production and injection at the field, an unprecedented movement has begun for thousands of years - reservoir pressure is redistributed, causing filtration (recall, the word ‚Äúfiltration‚Äù can be safely replaced with ‚Äúoverflows‚Äù) of the contents from places with high pressure to places with less pressure, changing, thus, at all points, oil saturation, water saturation and gas saturation. If you suddenly stop production, then after a while the reservoir pressure will equalize, only one gravity will remain at the field and the same slow processes of restoration of equilibrium will begin again: heavier water - down, lighter gas - up, oil - between them. The cocktail is stratified again.



Physico-chemical properties of oil, water and gas



The physicochemical properties of the fluids, that is, of everything that can flow and filter through the rock in the field, greatly affect how this filtration occurs. We have already seen that the filtration rate is directly proportional to the permeability of the rock and inversely proportional to the viscosity. In conditions where at some point in the field there is water, gas, and oil, they are all filtered at the same time in accordance with the general absolute permeability and intrinsic viscosity.



But it turns out that when they are all in one place, in the same pores, they begin to interfere with each other, and the filtration rate of all of them depends not only on the absolute permeability of the rock and intrinsic viscosity, but also on the amount of other fluids available. For example, experiments show that when oil and water are in the same pores, the oil filtration rate at a ratio of 10: 1 can be many times greater than the oil filtration rate at a ratio of 1:10.







I understand the formulas are scary, but still, let me explain them on my fingers. Earlier, we thought that everything flows in proportion to permeability and pressure drop and inversely to viscosity. It turns out that when there is water and oil, it is necessary to consider separately the speed of water and the speed of oil, each with its own viscosity. In this case, the permeability in the formula for both oil and water consists of some general constant permeability determined by the rock and an additional factor, which has its own water and oil, and this factor depends on the amount of water.



In other words, when there is little water, it almost does not prevent the oil from being filtered, but when there is already a lot of water, the remaining oil practically ceases to be filtered. It looks as if an additional factor called the relative phase permeability coefficient is superimposed on the rock permeability coefficient, which for each fluid (water, oil, gas) has its own and, moreover, depends on saturation (fraction of water, oil and gas).







As a result, there is an absolute permeability of the rock, which depends only on the rock, and there is a relative permeability for water, oil, gas, which depends on their proportion, and all this is displayed on graphs similar to the one I quoted above. How to read such a picture? There is no water saturation of less than 20% in this hypothetical deposit, that is, water is always at least 20%. With such a water content, its relative phase permeability is zero, that is, there is water, it is 20%, but it does not flow anywhere, only oil flows. On the other hand, when water becomes 75% (and oil remains, respectively, only 25%), the relative phase permeability of the oil drops to zero, which means that the oil is no longer flowing anywhere, and the remaining 25% cannot be picked up from the pores.



The density of oil, water, and gas affects their initial distribution in the field. In full accordance with the law of Archimedes, less dense gas and oil are located in the upper part of the field, and more dense water - in the lower. In the process of developing the field, all these effects, of course, continue to operate, although they may not keep pace with the events. It is one thing to bubble a gas from the bottom of the glass to the surface, and quite another to leak from the lower layer to the upper through the small pores of the rock, where water and oil held by capillary forces are not eager to let some bubbles pass through.



Another feature of oil is its ability to dissolve hydrocarbon gas (most often methane). Tens and hundreds of cubic meters of gas can be dissolved in one cubic meter of oil, depending on pressure. The ratio of the volume of gas released from oil during its production into the white light and under normal conditions, to the volume of the oil from which it was extracted, is called gas content. If you produce oil with a gas content of 100, then when producing 1 cubic meter of oil, you will also get 100 cubic meters of gas in the gas that were dissolved in it and which are extracted from it.







Pay attention and never confuse: gas content is the volume of gas dissolved in a unit volume of oil, and gas saturation is a fraction of the pore volume of a rock occupied by free gas. There is one more indicator: gas solubility is the volume of gas that can potentially dissolve in a unit volume of this oil at a given pressure.

Solubility - how much gas can dissolve in oil at a given pressure, and gas content - how much gas is actually dissolved. It is clear that the second can never exceed the first. If you put oil in a barrel without dissolved gas, then the gas content of this oil will be zero. If you then begin to pump gas into this barrel under a pressure of 200 atmospheres, then the gas will begin to dissolve in oil without residue, and the gas content of oil will increase. As soon as the gas content of oil reaches the solubility of the gas at a given pressure, the dissolution process will stop and the remaining gas will begin to accumulate in the upper part of the barrel.



Oil in which the maximum possible amount of gas is dissolved at the current pressure is called saturated oil, and this pressure is called saturation pressure. The saturation pressure for a given oil is such a pressure, when lowering it, the gas dissolved in it begins to be released from the oil. If we assume that there is no more gas and begin to increase pressure, then nothing will happen to the oil (although it will cease to be saturated). If you start, on the contrary, to lower the pressure, then as soon as the pressure falls below the saturation pressure, the gas dissolved in it will start to be released from the oil, and the gas content will begin to decrease (and the gas saturation of the rock in which all this happens will begin to increase).







In the case of a deposit, everything happens in a similar way, but in different directions, depending on what happened in the beginning and what happens. If the field had a gas cap at a pressure of 200 atmospheres, this means that the oil has already taken all the gas that it could dissolve and is in a saturated state. If you start to pump water into the field and increase reservoir pressure, then the solubility of gas (the ability of oil to contain gas) begins to grow, and gas immediately takes this opportunity and begins to dissolve in oil (though this does not happen quickly). So increasing pressure can theoretically achieve that the entire gas cap in the field is dissolved in oil. Assume that this happens at 250 atmospheres, and we continue to pump, what will happen when the reservoir pressure rises, say, to 300 atmospheres? Never mind,there wasn‚Äôt gas, and never will be. But oil, as it were, ‚Äúremembered‚Äù at what pressure the last cubic meter of gas dissolved in it ‚Äî this pressure is called the saturation pressure.



If then we start to extract oil and reduce the pressure from 300 to 290, 280, 270 atmospheres, then nothing will happen, but as soon as the pressure drops to a saturation pressure of 250 atmospheres, the gas will begin to be released again, and not in the cap, but in the entire volume, and fast.



If a field with a reservoir pressure of 200 atmospheres had no gas cap from the very beginning, does this mean that there is no dissolved gas in this oil? No, it does not mean - there is always dissolved gas in oil, simply because of the conditions of its formation. For such oil, it is important to know what its gas content and saturation pressure are, because if during the development process the reservoir pressure drops below the saturation pressure, gas will start to be released from the oil throughout the entire volume of the field, and this gas will interfere very much with normal oil filtration. wells.







To whom it is difficult to understand the essence of all these ongoing processes with dissolution, solubility and saturation pressure, I propose to imagine a bottle of soda water just bought. In it, obviously, under pressure there is water with dissolved carbon dioxide and a small amount of carbon dioxide under the cover, while they are in equilibrium: the gas no longer dissolves, but also does not stand out from the water. You can hardly carry out this experiment in reality, so you have to do it mentally: if you squeeze the bottle, increasing the pressure in it, you can ensure that all the gas from under the lid dissolves and only water is in the bottle. But the reverse experiment is easy to carry out: if you slightly unscrew the lid, bleed part of the gas and screw the lid back on, then the pressure under the lid and in the water will decrease.And since before that water and gas were in equilibrium, now we lowered the pressure below the saturation pressure, and gas from the water will begin to stand out, and over the entire volume immediately, until a new equilibrium is established.



In addition, it is important to know that when gas is dissolved in oil, the volume of this oil increases. Conversely, when producing 1 cubic meter of oil from the reservoir, when we raise it to the surface and dissolved gas is released from it, its volume will decrease in accordance with the so-called volumetric coefficient. When oil is raised to the surface, several phenomena occur at once affecting its volume in different directions: pressure drops (oil increases volume in accordance with its compressibility coefficient), temperature drops (oil decreases volume in accordance with its coefficient of thermal expansion), dissolved gas (volume and mass of oil are reduced). All this together is combined into a single volume factor. If it is equal, for example, 1.2, then to get 1 cubic meter on the surface, you need to take 1.2 cubic meters from the reservoir.



Both viscosity and volume coefficient, of course, depend on pressure and gas content: the higher the gas content (at the same pressure), the higher the volume coefficient and the lower the viscosity. The higher the pressure (at the same gas content), the higher the viscosity and the lower the volumetric coefficient.
