History of unmanned vehicles



Easter Sunday 1900, pictured is Fifth Avenue, New York. Among the sea of ​​horse-drawn carriages, it is hardly possible to see the only car in the left lane. Only 13 years will pass, and we will see a similar photo, with the only difference being that among the many cars there will be only one horse drawn cart.



Part one


Currently, the automotive industry is undergoing three revolutions at the same time: the electrification of the transmission, the connection of passenger vehicles to a single network and the replacement of drivers with autonomous driving systems. These processes not only go in parallel, but also significantly affect the automotive industry, just as the revolution at one time pushed to replace horse-drawn carts with self-propelled vehicles. This is the first part of a short blog series that attempts to cover the history of drones, from concept to production.

First developments


Inventions in the field of avionics and radio engineering inspired the creation of the first automated street vehicles: in 1914, the first autopilot of an airplane on a gyroscopic stabilizer was demonstrated in France :



The first autopilot on a biplane.

In the 1920s, a radio control for ships appeared: the



Natalie unmanned torpedo boat.

The RC car was first introduced by RCA in October 1921 in Dayton, Ohio.



The three-wheeled vehicle was controlled wirelessly from radio equipment on another vehicle. A similar radio-controlled car was demonstrated in 1925.on Fifth Avenue in New York. The unmanned vehicle was closely followed by a second car, in which there were radio transmitters and an operator.

Fantasy


The fantasy of unmanned vehicles is almost as old as the debut of the first car itself. And it all started with ordinary fiction.



In 1918, a tram that was not controlled by a driver was already drawn in one magazine with the caption: “A motorist’s dream: a car that is controlled by a set of buttons.” The article said that “... in the future, a car with a steering wheel will also become obsolete, as a car with a hand pump for gas or oil has become obsolete today!”

In 1933, Popular Mechanics reported that “they went into automation”claiming that recent inventions, such as the automatic opening of garage doors and power steering, were the first steps towards fully automated vehicles; “You can convert the car so that it will start on its own, open the garage door and move out of the carriageway.”


In the 1935 film “The Safest Place,” Chevrolet predicted that “if a manufacturer can equip each car with an automatic driving mechanism, the car will always do exactly what it should do when driving on the road. With this control, the car will not be able to drive off the side of the road without giving a signal and without considering oncoming traffic. With this control, the car will drive straight, and not tack in the stream of cars. With this control, he will always be rebuilt in the right lane before turning. Will always stop at the stop sign. With an automatic driving mechanism, the car will slow down before breaking the movement. He will not cut cars before a dangerous turn and will always stop before the railway crossing. ”

In 1938, "Popular Science" in an article“Motorways of the Future” predicted that after 50 years, accidents would not be possible, communication between cars would be via infrared, and electrical cables would control the speed of the car and eventually take full control of the car.



At the 1939 World's Fair in New York, General Motors, on display at Futurama, presented its vision for 1960: automated vehicles on multi-lane highways. This happened 17 years before the Federal Multi-Lane Freeway Act allowed the construction of a 41,000-mile interstate highway network across the United States.


The video “Towards New Horizons”, a 1939 film by Futurama, talks about futuristic utopian thinking on the eve of World War II that General Motors predicted. It captures the exposition of “Futurama” from the pavilion “Towards New Horizons” by General Motors at the world exhibition, which has turned its gaze to the “wonderful world of 1960”.

In 1953, Mechanix Illustratedasked the question: “Why do not we have ... anti-emergency roads ... With autopilots to take control instead of exhausted or injured drivers? If the inventor can offer the driver an autopilot for the car, then just think about the colossal value of such a device. The human factor will be excluded from the driving process. Our roads will become virtually trouble-free. “The article considered a system based on a magnetic detector for controlling lateral movement and a radar for maintaining distance.

In 1956, American energy companies toutedthat “one day your car will be able to race along the electric super-highway, its speed and taxiing will be automatically controlled by electronic devices built into the road. Highways will be safe thanks to electricity! No traffic jams ... no accidents ... no driver fatigue. ”


In 1956, for the Motorama Motor Show, General Motors released the musical short film Key to the Future, which expected unmanned vehicles to drive in the distant future of 1976. In 1958, Popular Science magazine wrote that "in your future, the car will be driven by black boxes, and you just have to watch," GM and RCA are developing.



Firebird III at Century 21 Exposition, Seattle, 1962. The car should was controlled by a road cable, and the interaction with the infrastructure would control lateral movement.Firebird

IV was not a functional vehicle like its predecessors, but a concept focused on the day whencars will drive along the highway and not be controlled by the driver, but by automatically programmed guidance systems that will ensure absolute safety when speed is more than 2 times that allowed today. As soon as the car leaves the highway, control will be transferred to the driver, and he will already drive the car through the streets.

In 1967, Popular Science magazine reported that “the United States funded a study worth $ 100,000. "The design of the commuter car of tomorrow is being developed at the world famous laboratory - and this is an electric tracked vehicle following the route to the city and back."

Pop culture


Walt Disney took imagination to a new level.


The episode "US Magic Highway" from the 1950s television series "Disney's Magic World" talked about the appearance of radar sensors, night vision, a motion guidance system and other futuristic features of vehicles. Cars were still portrayed as creatures capable of thinking and feeling, making their own decisions: at the end of 1968 Disney created Herbie , in the film The Love Bug the car had "its own mind and drove itself."


Perhaps the most capable fictional robot car in the 1980s was KITT from the television series Knight Rider.


KITT controlled himself and possessed a scanner that allowed him to “see” the environment. More unmanned vehicles appeared in the 1983 Christine movies, in Batman 1989, Total Recall 1990, Demolition Man 1993, the Fifth Element 1997 and I, Robot 2004.

The era of unmanned vehicles began 100 years ago. Inspired by advances in gyroscopic stabilizers, radio and radar technology, magazines, newspapers and films paint cars that could drive themselves. Many of these sources expected technology to emerge in about 20 years, (a common time constant in the history of automated vehicles). While the study of unmanned technologies began in the late 1950s - which will be discussed in the next part - it took another 50 years to overcome the mysterious 20-year barrier.

Part Two: Research and Development


Research on the creation of unmanned or robotic vehicles began in the late 1950s. Jameson Wetmore describes in detail the 60-year history of the creation of automated road systems in America and the ideas behind them. You can read it here and here . In 1958, General Motors and Radio Corporation of America (RCA) jointly tested automated road prototypes equipped with radio-controlled speed and taxiing.



As they wrote in 1960 , magnets embedded in vehicles were attracted by a steel cable laid under the road, and control towers monitored the general traffic flow. The onset of a new revolution seemed so inevitable that inIn 1960, the New York Times wrote that "we will come to full use in 15 years."


First constructed around 1960, Stanford Cart was designed to explore the possibility of controlling a lunar rover from Earth. Over the next decades, a group of graduate students finalized the cards. The main improvement was made by Hans Moravek . He equipped the cards with stereo vision, which allowed him to independently move around the room filled with chairs - it took only 5 hours - 1979 .


Around the same time, Nils Nilsson developed Shakey at the Stanford Research Center for Artificial Intelligence . This was the first mobile robot capable of perceiving the environment through the “eye” of a television camera and with the help of whisker tentacles. Shakey also had an action plan to complete the task. Shakey was succeeded by the Rover Lunokhod (CMU) , developed at Carnegie Mellon University.



The first environmental-friendly automated car was developed in 1977 at the University of Tsukuba's Engineering Laboratory . The research team has been developing unmanned vehicles for 20 years. This 1994 document refers to a vehicle designed by Toyota. With the help of one camera, the car was able to drive along the 50 m marking at a speed of 50 km / h.



Developed in the 1980s, the autonomous ground vehicle DARPA (ALV) was an eight-wheeled robot 12 feet high with several sensors, which was tasked with moving from point A to point B, climbing hills without human help. The experiment took place outside of Denver in 1985 .



In the 1980s, Professor Ernst Dickmanns and his research team at the Military University of Munich built the world's first robotic street vehicles that, using computer vision and probabilistic algorithms, were able to drive 20 km on an empty road, reaching speeds of up to 96 km / h .

This study continued inthe European state-funded project EUREKA- “Prometheus” , (PROgraMme for the European movement with the highest efficiency and unprecedented security), which was carried out from 1987 to 1994. It included many European participants, and government funding was significant.

In 1993, Bosch introduced navigation systems and cameras capable of recognizing lanes developed as part of the Prometheus project.


Then it was found that “the meaningful connection of a global digital map with local sensory information is the key to targeted autonomous driving.”

The final demonstration of the Prometheus project took place on the A1 highway in Paris , where Professor Dickmanns' VAmP drove more than 1000 km, reaching a speed of 130 km / h.


He automatically skipped the slower cars in the left lane and simultaneously tracked up to twelve other cars. In 1995, the team traveled 1,758 km from Munich to Denmark and back at a speed of 175 km / h , longitudinal and lateral control was carried out using computer vision. The largest section, which managed to pass in autopilot mode, amounted to 158 km. Professor Diekmans talks about the development of technology in this area from the 1950s to the present at the conference in 2011.





Also in 1995, developed in CMU machine Navlab 5 drove on autopilot, but with manual control of the lateral movement, from Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, in San Diego, California, as part of the tour“No Hands Across America . ” This 1998 History Channel video briefly describes the activities of CMU Navlab in the 1990s:


In 1996, in Italy, at the University of Parma, Alberto Brodzhi founded the ARGO project . In 1998, as part of the Automatico tour , the MilleMiglia car drove 2000 km in autopilot mode. The team published the report in this video 10 years later.


In 1997, the U.S. Department of Transportation's National Automated Automotive Systems (NAHS) Research Program ended with unmanned vehicles, buses, and trucks driving along I-15 in San Diego, California.



Lateral movement control was carried out using computer vision and magnets built into the road. The distance between the cars was regulated by radar or lidar sensors .

The German research project "Autonomes Fahren" , which took place from 1997 to 2000, was attended by Volkswagen, Bosch, the Technical University of Braunschweig and other companies.



As part of the project on test sitesVolkswagen conducted test drives of manufactured cars . The cars were equipped with lidars, cameras, radars, GPS, a digital route map.



So that the tested cars could drive without a test driver on a closed route in mixed traffic among other vehicles, they put electromechanical robot drivers in them , which powered three pedals, shifted gears and steered the steering wheel.

The DARPA Grand Challenge is a series of unmanned vehicle competitions sponsored by the Advanced Defense Research Projects Agency (DARPA) .



The law of the United States national defense of 2001 statesthat “the purpose of the armed forces is to ensure the use of unmanned, remotely controlled technologies, so that ... by 2015, one third of the operational ground combat vehicles of the Armed Forces are unmanned."

Given the tight schedule, DARPA decided that competition with a prize pool would be a quick economic exit and the best incentive to develop innovation and expand the research community of unmanned technologies. The first event took place in 2004. Only 15 teams were selected for the final race. Self-driving unmanned vehicles attempted to follow the 142-mile route through the Mojave Desert (Nevada), along dirt roads, plains, and mountain passes.


Teams recognized GPS waypoints just 24 hours before the event. The CMU succeeded the most by driving 7.32 miles (less than 5% of the total route). However, as Wired correctly pointed out , the first of the three Grand Challenge events has shaped the drone developer community as we know it today.



DARPA held a re-competition in 2005 (DARPA Grand Challenge). Five teams covered 132 miles , and all but one of the 23 finalists covered 7.32 miles, ahead of the best result of 2004.


The Stanley robot car , designed by Sebastian Trun and his team at Stanford University , arrived at the finish line first, following the route in 6 hours 54 minutes. Sebastian later described his work at Stanford on Stanley in this article and in this conversation:


Two cars developed at Carnegie Mellon University , Sandstorm and Highlander, arrived just minutes after Stanley. Twelve years later, the team accidentally discovered that the filter between the engine control module and the fuel injectors (one of only two electronic parts in the 1986 Hummer engine) was broken and resulted in a loss of engine power.


The 2007 DARPA Urban Challenge , DARPA relocated to an urban area, the site of George's now closed air base in Victorville, California. Teams had to go through the city, driving through four-lane intersections, blocked roads or parking lots, in mixed traffic with drones and people-driven vehicles. More than 90 teams have applied to participate in the Urban Challenge. 53 teams took part in the selection, 36 were invited to the semifinal event, and only 11 took part in the final race.


DARPA considered it necessary that people and DARPA representatives, who evaluate the performance of robots, be in the stream of cars next to the robots. For the first time a traffic jam from unmanned vehicles was formed at the event, as well as the first, albeit insignificant, collision of two drones.


The race was won by a Carnegie Mellon University car - Boss . The Junior of Stanford University came for him .



Junior at Stanford University and Victor Tango of the Virginia Polytechnic at the 2007 DARPA Urban Challenge.



Carnegie Mellon University's Boss car crosses the finish line.

After the Urban Challenge, several more, mostly research, events were held. Among them, the Stadtpilot project at the Technical University of Braunschweig , aimed at driving in an urban environment, including changing lanes, parking and inclusion in a moving stream. In 2010, the Vislab Intercontinental Autonomous Challenge projectin which a convoy of four drones, following the driving car, drove 13,000 km from Parma (Italy) to Shanghai (China). In 2012, a racing drone was built for the Pikes Peak race at Stanford .


The Grand Cooperative Driving Challenge is a competition held in Europe in 2011 and 2016, in which cars had to follow the leading car, react to its vibrations, jointly start at traffic lights and join in a column.

The Urban Challenge marked the transition from research to industrial development - which we will cover in the next part. The mysterious 20-year-old barrier was finally overcome.
