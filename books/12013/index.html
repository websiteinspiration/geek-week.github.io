Recommendation

Science journalist Ellen Ruppel Shell offers many insights in this terse, but engaging overview of the discount industry, starting with the image of shoppers browsing mindlessly through discount store aisles filled with shoddy merchandise. Her mix of history, economics and psychology delivers a disturbing portrait of the discount industry from the industrial era to the present day. Shell, who acknowledges that she is a bargain hunter, too, never gets preachy. BooksInShort recommends Shell‚Äôs treatise to shoppers, economists and executives in the retailing and manufacturing industries.

Attention Shoppers

Innovations in Commerce

Before the Industrial Revolution, many products were difficult to obtain because production was a slow, tortuous process, but modernization made creating products easier and cheaper.

Frank W. Woolworth of upstate New York opened the first ‚Äúfive-and-dime‚Äù store in 1878. He based his concept on his experience as a salesclerk. He had noticed that shoppers needed very little sales help when they browsed displays of items selling for a nickel each.

In 1895, Richard Warren Sears launched the Book of Bargains, the first Sears catalog, establishing a mail-order business for low-priced goods that was accessible to the general public. Discount Backlash and Boom

Chain stores and low-priced vendors succeeded in delivering affordable products to average Americans, but critics soon spotted flaws in the discount chain stores. Supermarket Models

Discount retailers sampled and borrowed the business strategies used by supermarket chains. Popular grocery stores provided self-service displays, shopping carts and centralized checkout lanes. Low prices became the pivotal factor in the discount stores‚Äô strategies for selecting, distributing and marketing.
