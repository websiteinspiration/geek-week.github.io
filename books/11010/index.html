The Advantages of E-Learning

Corporations are always looking for new and better ways to deliver training, and even small businesses want to find ways to keep their staffs up-to-date. E-learning offers powerful possibilities for both groups. Once you develop an e-learning program, it is always available. Participants don‚Äôt have to retain as much content as they do in a traditional classroom setting, because they can always review the course to refresh their memories. Trainers can develop courses that meet specific corporate needs or audiences.

‚ÄúFor more than a decade, e-learning has been touted as the next big thing in training. Yet most organizations are still trying to figure out how to make it work.‚Äù

Calculating the return on investment for an e-learning course is relatively easy: Compare the cost of developing the e-learning course (plus technical support costs) to the cost of the in-person training it would replace. Often, an e-learning course works out cheaper. Before engaging your firm in e-learning training, set definite, concrete goals that you want the training to achieve within a given time parameter. E-Learning Design

E-learning depends on the Internet, and the rules governing Web site design apply to e-learning as well. For example:

  • Users should be able to retrieve the information they need ‚Äúwithin three clicks.‚Äù

  • The Web site should enable users to provide feedback.

E-learning programs can be either synchronous or asynchronous. Synchronous e-learning takes place in real time. In asynchronous learning, the content is available on the Internet for students to access at will, and students determine the pace of their e-learning. ‚ÄúRapid e-learning‚Äù courses, with content you can continually update, use words alone for communication and are inexpensive. Just one or two people working for a few days can develop such courses. The average user needs one minute to read a page of content. Divide courses into 15‚Äì20 page blocks, which equal 15‚Äì20 minute units. Organize courses on four levels:

For course navigation, provide both a tour that guides users through the entire course in a stream, and a ‚Äústep-by-step‚Äù approach that lets users view each component, as in a flow chart.

Using Images and Sound

Poorly used images distract learners and slow their progress. Graphics come in a range of formats. The more colors and the higher the resolution, the slower the image will load. Use sound sparingly. Audio can be distracting, and most learners retain less from an audio format.
