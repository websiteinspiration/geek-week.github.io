Recommendation

This copiously researched, vivid account covers the rise of one of the world‚Äôs most successful entertainment companies. Experienced journalist David A. Price fills Pixar‚Äôs history with implied lessons about patience in management and running a creative company, but he doesn‚Äôt seem much interested in writing a how-to business book, so he sticks to the historic narrative and draws few conclusions. Take-Aways

  • Pixar began in 1986. A vision of making computer-animated movies drove its leaders long before any such thing was technologically possible.
  • Pixar‚Äôs principals had suffered recent business failures before they joined the firm.
  • The chance to do ‚Äúcool‚Äù work can lure top talent just as well as ‚Äì or better than ‚Äì money.
  • Disney declined many times to hire Pixar‚Äôs talent or acquire the company.
  • Pixar kept its team together while working for bosses, including George Lucas and, initially, Steve Jobs, who were uninterested in animated movies.
  • Under Ed Catmull and John Lasseter, Pixar combined cutting-edge technology with classic animation principles.
  • After years of losses, Pixar owner Steve Jobs became a billionaire when it went public.

Catmull hired him, but since Lucas had no interest in computer animation, Lasseter‚Äôs title was ‚ÄúInterface Designer.‚Äù His job was to work on a computer-generated short film, The Adventures of Andre and Wally B. ‚ÄúDisney had observed that giving lifelike qualities to inanimate objects held comic potential.‚Äù

Like Lucas, Jobs was not interested in movies. He wanted to democratize technology and change the world. Smith helped Pixar secure a contract with Disney to develop the Computer Animation Production System, a revolutionary method for using computer tools on traditional animation.
